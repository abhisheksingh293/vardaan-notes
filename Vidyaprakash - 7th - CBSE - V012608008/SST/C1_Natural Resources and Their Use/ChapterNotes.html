Natural Resources and Their Use

1. Introduction to Natural Resources

The Earth provides a variety of materials essential for survival, such as rocks, soil, minerals, natural vegetation, and animals. These naturally occurring materials that help satisfy human needs are called natural resources.

Concept

When does nature become a resource?

The materials found in nature are often referred to as "gifts of nature." However, they only become true resources when human beings develop the technology, recognize their need, and utilize the time required to convert these gifts into valuable and usable forms.

2. Classification of Natural Resources

Natural resources are diverse and are classified into several categories based on different criteria:

A. Based on Origin

  • Biotic Resources: Resources obtained from the biosphere, such as plants, animals, and human beings.
  • Abiotic Resources: Resources composed of non-living things, such as soil, rocks, and minerals.

B. Based on Development

  • Actual or Developed Resources: These are resources whose exact quantity and quality are known, and they are currently being used. Their development relies heavily on available technology.
  • Potential Resources: These are resources whose entire quantity may not be known and which are not being used at present. They possess the potential to be used in the future once appropriate technology is developed.
Fact

Actual Resource Example: Windmills generating energy in the Netherlands and Tamil Nadu.
Potential Resource Example: The uranium reserves found in Ladakh, which hold great potential for future energy generation.

C. Based on Distribution

  • Ubiquitous Resources: Resources that are found everywhere on Earth, such as air and sunlight.
  • Localized Resources: Resources found only in specific or certain places, such as gold, silver, and copper deposits.

D. Based on Renewability

  • Renewable Resources: Resources that can be renewed or replenished naturally over a short period of time, such as solar energy and wind energy.
  • Non-renewable (Exhaustible) Resources: Resources that have a limited stock and take thousands or millions of years to form, such as minerals and fossil fuels.

3. Groundwater: A Vital Resource

A significant portion of the rain that falls on the Earth seeps into the soil and is stored beneath the surface. This is known as groundwater. It is a critical resource heavily utilized for agriculture, industry, and daily consumption.

Important

Overexploitation warning: Excessive extraction of groundwater, especially for irrigation and industrial use, is leading to a severe depletion of the water table. Responsible usage is required to ensure this resource remains available for future generations.

4. Responsible Use and Sustainable Alternatives

Due to the fast depletion of non-renewable resources and environmental degradation, there is a global shift towards sustainable alternatives and practices.

A. Renewable Energy Initiatives

Switching from fossil fuels to renewable energy (like solar and wind) is crucial for controlling pollution and ensuring long-term energy security.

  • International Solar Alliance (ISA): India has taken a strong leadership role in promoting solar energy globally. The ISA aims to achieve the "Towards 1000" strategy by 2030, which includes installing 1000 GW of solar energy capacity worldwide.
  • Bhadla Solar Park: Located in Jodhpur, Rajasthan, it is currently the world's largest solar park, producing immense amounts of clean energy.

B. Organic Farming

Organic farming eliminates the use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides, using organic manure and natural techniques to maintain soil health and minimize environmental damage.

Fact

Sikkim's Achievement: In 2016, Sikkim became the first state in the world to be declared a fully organic state, setting a global benchmark for sustainable agriculture.

THE LEGISLATURE

1. Organs of the Government

The Constitution of India is a vital document that outlines the rules and procedures for governing the country. It ensures the rights and duties of citizens are protected. In India, the head of the State is the President.

India has three main organs of the government:

  • Legislature: It is the law-making body. It controls the Executive's finances and questions the laws passed.
  • Executive: It implements the laws and runs the government.
  • Judiciary: It interprets laws and ensures justice for all citizens.
Concept

What is the Union Legislature?

The Union Legislature, also called the Parliament, is responsible for making laws for the country. It consists of the President and two Houses: The Lok Sabha (Lower House) and the Rajya Sabha (Upper House).

2. Division of Powers Between the Centre and States

The Legislative powers have been divided between the Union and the states. Subjects have been listed under three legislative lists:

Fact
  • Union List: Matters over which only the Centre can make laws. (Examples: Defence, Citizenship, Railways, Finance, Foreign Affairs).
  • State List: Matters over which only the States can make laws. (Examples: Public health, Agriculture, Fisheries, Water supply).
  • Concurrent List: Matters over which both the centre and states can make laws. (Examples: Education, Trade unions, Forests, Population control).

3. The Lok Sabha (House of the People)

The Lok Sabha is the Lower House of the Parliament. People directly elect members from constituencies all over the country.

Composition and Strength

  • The maximum strength of the Lok Sabha can be 550 members.
  • 530 representatives are elected from states.
  • 20 representatives are elected from union territories.
  • As of 2024, out of the 543 members of the Lok Sabha, 524 represent the states, while 19 represent the union territories.

Qualifications & Tenure

To become a Member of Parliament (MP) in the Lok Sabha, a candidate must meet these criteria:

  • Must be a citizen of India, a registered voter, and mentally sound.
  • Must be at least 25 years of age.
  • Must not hold an office of profit under the government.
  • Must not be an insolvent or bankrupt.
  • Must not be a proclaimed criminal.

The tenure of the Lok Sabha is normally five years. However, it can be dissolved earlier by the President.

Important

Procedure for Election: Voting is done by secret ballot. The voting age of a person in India is 18 years or above (Universal Adult Franchise). India uses Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) for elections.

The Speaker

The Speaker is the presiding officer of the Lok Sabha. He/She maintains order and supervises the work of the House. The Speaker is elected from among the members of the Lok Sabha and may cast a vote only in case of a tie.

4. The Rajya Sabha (Council of States)

The Rajya Sabha is the Upper House of the Parliament. It represents the states of India and is a permanent body (it cannot be dissolved).

Composition and Strength

  • The maximum strength of the Rajya Sabha is 250 members.
  • 238 members represent the states and union territories. They are elected by the members of the State Legislative Assemblies.
  • 12 members are nominated by the President. They are distinguished individuals from the fields of art, literature, science, or social service.

Tenure & Eligibility

  • To become a member, one must be at least 30 years of age.
  • Members are elected for a period of six years.
  • One-third of its members retire every two years, making it a Permanent House.
  • The Vice President is the presiding officer of the Rajya Sabha.

5. Functions of the Parliament: Law-Making Procedures

Until a proposal is passed by both Houses and receives the consent of the President, it is referred to as a bill. After it is passed, it becomes an Act (law).

Types of Bills

  • Ordinary Bills: Can be introduced in either the Lok Sabha or the Rajya Sabha. It can be introduced by a minister or a private member.
  • Money Bills: Can be introduced only in the Lok Sabha. The Speaker decides if a bill is a money bill. It is sent to the Rajya Sabha for discussion, but they only have 14 days to accept it or offer recommendations. The President's consent is a mere formality as prior approval is taken.
Process Flow

Draft Bill ➔ Introduced in House ➔ Debated and passed by both Houses ➔ Sent to President for assent ➔ Becomes an Act.

6. The State Legislature

India has 28 states and eight union territories. Each state is divided into smaller constituencies. State legislatures can be:

  • Unicameral: Having only one House. Most states in India have unicameral legislatures.
  • Bicameral: Having two Houses. Only some states have this, such as Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Maharashtra.

Houses of the State Legislature

  • Vidhan Sabha (Legislative Assembly): The Lower House. People directly elect the Members of Legislative Assembly (MLAs). Total members range between 60 and 500. The term is five years, and the qualification age is 25.
  • Vidhan Parishad (Legislative Council): The Upper House. Members are indirectly elected and nominated (MLCs). Total members cannot be more than one-third of the Vidhan Sabha.
Key Role

The Governor: The Governor plays an important role in the state legislature. All state bills go to the Governor for his/her assent. The Governor also has the power to summon or dissolve the Legislative Assembly.

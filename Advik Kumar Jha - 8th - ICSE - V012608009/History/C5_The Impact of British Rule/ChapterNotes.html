THE IMPACT OF BRITISH RULE

1. Introduction: The Exploitative Nature of British Rule

Before the arrival of the British, Indian villages were primarily self-sufficient. Agriculture and village handicrafts went hand-in-hand. However, the British conquest fundamentally altered India's economy. While the Queen's Proclamation of 1858 promised progress and welfare for Indians, in reality, British policies mainly served their own industrial and commercial interests. The broader economic and social impact of British rule was deeply exploitative.

2. British Land Revenue Systems

To maximize their income, the English East India Company introduced successive land revenue policies. These policies enriched the British and the Zamindars, while the peasants suffered severe exploitation and poverty.

The Permanent Settlement (1793)

  • Introduced by Lord Cornwallis in Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa.
  • Zamindars (landlords) were recognised as the permanent owners of the land as long as they paid a fixed revenue to the British.
  • If the Zamindar failed to pay the revenue on time, his land was auctioned off.
  • Impact: It created a loyal class of Zamindars for the British but left the peasants at the mercy of landlords, leading to extreme exploitation.

The Ryotwari System (1820)

  • Introduced by Thomas Munro in parts of Madras and Bombay presidencies.
  • The revenue settlement was made directly with the Ryot (cultivator/farmer).
  • Impact: Although it eliminated middlemen (Zamindars), the tax rates set by the British were excessively high. If crops failed, farmers had to take loans from moneylenders, trapping them in a cycle of debt.

The Mahalwari System (1822)

  • Introduced by Holt Mackenzie in the North-Western Provinces, parts of Central India, and Punjab.
  • The revenue was assessed on the entire Mahal (village or estate) rather than individual farmers. The village headman was responsible for collecting the tax.

3. Commercialisation of Agriculture

The British deliberately changed the cropping pattern in India. There was a forced shift from growing food grains (like wheat and rice) to cash crops (like cotton, jute, indigo, and tea) which were needed as raw materials for industries in England.

Fact

Impact of Commercialisation:

  • Farmers were forced into rope cultivation of crops like tea, indigo, jute, and cotton, which were sold in European markets.
  • Because farmers were forced to grow non-food crops, there was a massive shortage of food grains.
  • This direct interference, coupled with British apathy, resulted in frequent and devastating famines, leading to the death of millions.

4. Impact on Traditional Industries (De-industrialisation)

Before British rule, India was a leading exporter of fine cotton and silk textiles. The British Industrial Revolution ruined the traditional Indian handicraft industries.

  • The British imposed heavy taxes on Indian goods entering Britain, making them too expensive.
  • Conversely, machine-made, duty-free British goods flooded the Indian markets. These were cheaper than hand-made Indian goods.
  • Result: Millions of Indian weavers and artisans lost their livelihoods. They were forced to abandon their crafts and move to agriculture, putting immense pressure on agricultural land.
Concept

The Drain of Wealth Theory (1867)

Dadabhai Naoroji, in his book 'Poverty and Un-British Rule in India', introduced the "Drain of Wealth" theory. He scientifically explained how the British were continuously draining India's wealth and resources to Britain without giving any adequate economic or material return to India. This constant drain was identified as the primary cause of India's poverty.

5. Educational Policy of the British

Initially, the East India Company did not care about educating Indians. However, as their empire expanded, they needed cheap clerks to run their administration.

English Education Act (1835)

Based on Macaulay's Minute, the British decided to spend funds exclusively on Western education through the medium of the English language. Lord Macaulay famously stated that the goal was to create a class of people who were "Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect."

Wood's Despatch (1854)

Often called the Magna Carta of English Education in India, this document laid the foundation of the modern educational system.

  • It recommended setting up primary schools in villages, high schools in towns, and establishing Universities.
  • Departments of Education were set up in all provinces.
Important

Positive Impacts of British Education:

Although the British introduced English education for their own administrative convenience, it had unintended positive consequences. It exposed Indians to modern Western ideas of democracy, liberty, and nationalism. Certain laws also promoted the opening of schools and supported the social upliftment of women. This ultimately laid the intellectual foundation for the Indian National Movement.

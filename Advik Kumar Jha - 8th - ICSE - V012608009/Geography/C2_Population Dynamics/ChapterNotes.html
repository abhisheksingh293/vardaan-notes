POPULATION DYNAMICS

1. Introduction to Population Dynamics

Population refers to the total number of people inhabiting a specific area. Population and resources are highly unevenly distributed across the world and are closely related. Rapid growth in population threatens the sustainability of limited natural resources.

Fact

Demography: It is a social science that entails the statistical study of human populations, including size, structure, and distribution.

Growing Population of the World

  • At present, the population of the world is nearly 7.9 billion (as of 2022).
  • The current population growth rate is nearly 1 per cent per year.
  • The global population is expected to reach 9.7 billion in 2050 and could peak at nearly 11 billion around 2100.
  • The speed of population growth peaked between 1962-65 but has slowed down by more than half since then due to reduced fertility rates.

2. Population Distribution and Density

Population distribution refers to how the population is spread across the world. Strikingly, 7.9 billion people are concentrated in only 20 per cent of the Earth's land surface that is suitable for human habitation.

Important

Top 3 Most Populated Countries (2022):
1. China (1,426 million)
2. India (1,412 million)
3. USA (337 million)

Population Density

Density of population means the number of people living per square kilometre. It is calculated as: Density = Total Population ÷ Total Area.

The average density is much higher in less developed countries (697 persons/sq. km) compared to developed countries (238 persons/sq. km).

Areas of High Density

Regions with vast fertile lands, ample water supply, suitable climate, and thriving economic activities attract dense populations.

  • Asia: The riverine plains of Asia, such as the Ganga Plains in India, hold the highest population density.
  • Europe: Industrial north-west Europe (France, UK, Germany, Denmark) due to cool climate, industrial growth, and diverse natural resources.
  • Americas: The fertile, temperate regions of the US, Mexico, and Brazil. The US attracts high immigrant populations due to technological advancement and job prospects.
  • Africa: The rich Nile Valley, Mediterranean Coast, Guinea Coast, and coastal South Africa.

Areas of Moderate and Low Density

  • Moderate Density: Central USA, Tropical western Africa, Russia, Eastern Europe, Deccan Plateau of India, and Central China.
  • Low Density (Sparsely Populated): Arid hot and cold deserts, mountainous regions, and dense equatorial forests. Examples include Mongolia (lowest density of 2 persons per sq. km), Iceland, Namibia, northern Canada, and the Australian outbacks. These areas suffer from extreme climates, lack of arable land, and poor transportation.

3. Overpopulation and Underpopulation

Concept

Optimum Population: This is a state where the available resources in a given area are just adequate to support a certain number of people. It provides a perfect balance, ensuring high standards of living and sufficient resources for everyone.

Overpopulation

Overpopulation occurs when a very large number of people exist for the available resources in a region. This is caused by a sudden population increase or rapid depletion of resources. India, Bangladesh, and China are examples.

Impact of Overpopulation:

  • Depletion of Resources: Overutilisation creates water scarcity, food crises, and starvation. We must adopt sustainable development (judicious use of resources without compromising future generations' needs).
  • Unemployment: Stiffer competition for fewer available jobs.
  • Poor Living Conditions: Growth of slums and unhygienic conditions due to lack of land.
  • Price Rise: High demand and low supply increase the cost of living.
  • Environmental Degradation: Burning of fossil fuels and deforestation lead to global warming and loss of biodiversity.

Underpopulation

Underpopulation is a state where the number of people living in a region is so small that the resources are not fully utilised. It results from a high death rate, low birth rate, or high emigration. Example: The Prairies of North America.

Impact of Underpopulation:

  • Negative: Yields an insufficient skilled labour force, leading to poor resource utilisation and poor economic growth.
  • Positive: Results in better job opportunities, sufficient resource availability, and reduced levels of pollution due to less fierce competition.

4. Factors That Affect Population Change

Population change is determined by birth rate, death rate, and migration. The Census (official enumeration of population conducted every 10 years) provides accurate data for these factors.

  • 1. Crude Birth Rate: The number of live childbirths per 1000 persons in a given area per year. Birth rates are higher in developing countries (need for helping hands) and lower in developed countries (higher living standards). Lowest: Monaco, Japan. Highest: Niger, Angola.
  • 2. Crude Death Rate: The number of deaths per 1000 persons in a given area in a year. It is high in Sub-Saharan Africa (high child mortality) and also in some developed countries (due to a large aging population).
  • 3. Population Growth Rate: Compares the average annual percentage change in population, resulting from a surplus of births over deaths and the net balance of migrants.
  • 4. Migration: The movement of people with the intention of settling permanently.
    • Emigrants: People who move out of a country.
    • Imigrants: People who come into another country to live.
    • Migration can lead to cultural enrichment and new cities, but it can also cause racial tension or a brain drain (extensive transfer of skilled human resources to another country).

5. Composition of Population

Sex Ratio

Sex ratio refers to the number of females per 1000 males in a population. It indicates equality between genders in society. According to the 2011 Census of India, the overall sex ratio was 940. Kerala has the highest (1084), while Daman and Diu have the lowest (710). Discrepancy persists due to female foeticide and discrimination.

Age Composition

This categorizes people into three main groups, signifying the rate of resource use and work capacity:

  1. Children (0-14 years): Dependent population.
  2. Working age (15-59 years): Productive population.
  3. Old age (Above 59 years): Dependent population.

Population Pyramid

A population pyramid is a graphical representation of the population structure based on age and gender. The male population is on the left, female on the right. Age groups are on the Y-axis.

  • Developing Country (e.g., Bangladesh): Broad base (high birth rate), tapering top (high mortality rate, low life expectancy). Indicates a lack of medical care and education.
  • Developed Country (e.g., USA): Almost rectangular base (low birth and death rates). Thicker top shows longer life expectancy and a greater number of dependent old people. Women tend to outlive men. Indicates a high standard of living.

Rural and Urban Population

There is a massive global trend towards urbanization. More than half the world's population currently lives in urban areas, and it is expected to surpass 6 billion by 2045. This causes problems related to housing, transport, and employment.

India and China have the largest rural populations globally due to their massive agrarian economies.

URBANISATION

1. Introduction to Urbanisation

Urbanisation refers to the process of an increase in urban population and urban areas (towns and cities) in a country. This process creates significant social, economic, cultural, and environmental changes. The rate of urbanisation today is more rapid in the developing countries of the eastern part of the world.

2. Causes of Urbanisation

Several push and pull factors contribute to the rapid growth of urban centers:

  • Industrialisation: The rise of industries generates employment. People migrate to live closer to their workplaces to reduce commuting costs, resulting in better roads, transport, and infrastructure.
  • Social Factors: Improved living conditions, better educational and health facilities, and a rise in social status motivate people to move to cities.
  • Lower Agricultural Income: When everyday sustenance becomes a challenge due to lower farming incomes, people move to cities for better jobs.
  • Modernisation: The cycle of urbanisation offers a sophisticated lifestyle, comfortable housing, and advanced means of communication, luring people from less developed areas.
  • Development of Technology: The spread of education, globalisation, and technology (like the Internet and cell phones) transforms villages situated closer to cities, making them urbanised.
Important

Impact of the Green Revolution

In India, the introduction of mechanised farming during the Green Revolution led to increased unemployment in rural areas. Many farmers who lost their means of livelihood migrated to urban areas in search of new jobs.

3. Impacts of Urbanisation

Positive Impacts

  • Increase in improved housing facilities and higher standards of living.
  • Growth of educational, health, transport, and communication facilities.
  • Expansion of the service sector, leading to higher incomes and a variety of job prospects.
  • Easy availability of a large labour force for industries.

Negative Impacts

  • Squatters and Slums: A large concentration of population leads to a lack of proper housing, resulting in cramped homes with poor sanitation, ventilation, and drinking water facilities.
  • High Cost of Living: Overpopulation and a dearth of land cause a sharp rise in land value and the cost of living.
  • Crime and Pollution: As population surges, crime rates often rise. Overcrowded cities suffer from severe traffic congestion, along with air, water, and land pollution.
  • Stressful Urban Life: The busy work life leaves little time for leisure, leading to mental strain and an increase in lifestyle diseases.
Fact

Real World Context

Dharavi in Mumbai is known as the largest slum in Asia, highlighting the severe housing crisis caused by rapid urbanisation. Additionally, the stress of modern city life is linked to an increase in lifestyle diseases such as diabetes and migraines.

4. Ways to Reduce the Negative Impact

Proactive steps must be taken to ensure sustainable urban living:

  • Slums: Provide legitimate rehabilitation solutions for slum-dwellers within the community, rather than moving them to remote locations with low employment opportunities.
  • Traffic Congestion: Promote alternative transportation such as bicycles, car-sharing, carpooling, and improved public transit.
  • Pollution and Waste: Encourage the use of reusable bottles and bags while strictly avoiding single-use food and drink containers.
  • Stress: Promote greenery and relaxation spaces—a walk in the park or potted plants on balconies can significantly help the mind recover.

5. Satellite Cities vs. Smart Cities

Satellite Cities

Satellite cities emerge to supply resources and fulfill the needs of the main metropolitan city. They act as reasonably priced residential hubs for the labor force working in the main city.

  • They help in decongesting the main city while providing better housing and less polluted environments.
  • Unlike typical suburbs, satellite towns have their own independent municipality and are self-sufficient.
  • Examples in India: Gurugram, Noida, and Faridabad (for Delhi); Navi Mumbai (for Mumbai); Yelahanka (for Bengaluru); Pimpri (for Pune).
Concept

Smart Cities

Smart cities are a new concept in urban development that use urban informatics and technology to improve the efficiency of services and quality of life. They utilize ICT (Information Communication Technology) and the Internet of Things (IoT) to seamlessly manage water, waste, power supply, and medical facilities.

Key Features of Smart Cities

  • Aims to address global challenges like global warming, climate change, and overpopulation.
  • Provides sustainable, smart movement, energy, and governance through direct contact with the government.
  • Global Examples: Amsterdam, Barcelona, Stockholm, New York.
  • Indian Context: The Government of India has selected nearly 90 cities for this development. The first 20 cities, termed Light House Cities, include Jaipur, Bhubaneswar, Kochi, Surat, and Guwahati.

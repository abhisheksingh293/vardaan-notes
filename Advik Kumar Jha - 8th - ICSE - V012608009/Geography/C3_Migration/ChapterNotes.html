MIGRATION

1. Introduction to Migration

The movement of people from their native land to another destination is called migration. Human beings have migrated from one place to another since evolutionary times. This process is generally controlled by social, cultural, economic, political, and environmental factors.

Concept

Key Terminology

  • Emigrant: A person who leaves their own country in order to live in another country.
  • Immigrant: A person who arrives in a new country to settle permanently.

2. Types of Migration

Migration plays an important role in changing the size and composition (age, sex, and occupation) of a population. It can be classified into several types:

Internal vs. External Migration

  • Internal Migration: Movement of a large number of people from one state to another within the same country due to disturbed political, social, economic, or climatic conditions.
  • External (International) Migration: Movement of people from one country to another. This is often seen among highly skilled people moving in search of better work opportunities (e.g., migrating from East-Asian countries to the Western world).

Short-term vs. Long-term Migration

  • Short-term Migration: Moving to escape a temporary disaster (flood, earthquake) or for seasonal occupational purposes (like lumberjacks moving to Taiga forests during winter).
  • Long-term Migration: Driven by the need for permanent settlement, better occupational opportunities, education, or higher living standards.

Directional Migration

  • Rural to Urban: The most common form of migration. People move from villages to cities in search of employment, higher incomes, and better facilities.
  • Urban to Rural/Suburban: Occurs when a city's population outgrows its carrying capacity, leading to congestion and pollution. People move to suburbs and commute to work.
  • Urban to Urban: Common in highly urbanized countries where people move between cities strictly to improve their economic and employment prospects.
  • Rural to Rural: Common in agricultural nations like India and Bangladesh. Farmers move to newly reclaimed lands or areas with better irrigation and land reforms.
Fact

Demographics of Rural to Urban Migration

The demographic profile of migrants varies across the world. In Latin America, women outnumber men in migrating due to industrial jobs in free trade zones. In Africa (e.g., Kenya), men mostly migrate for higher wages while women manage domestic duties. In India and China, female migration is heavily influenced by movement after marriage.

Special Categories of Migration

  • Climate Refugees: People forced to move when their homes are threatened by climatic catastrophes (e.g., people fleeing sinking South Pacific Islands like the Carteret Islands).
  • Miscellaneous (Political): Movement forced by government decisions or political unrest. (e.g., Indonesia redistributing population from Java to Borneo, or Afghan nationals fleeing the Taliban).
  • Forced Migration: Happens during disaster or warfare (e.g., Ukrainian civilians fleeing to Europe in 2022).
  • Voluntary Migration: Moving to highly developed countries (HDC) from less developed countries (LDC) by choice for better education and lifestyle.

3. Impact of Migration on Society

Migration affects both the source country (where people leave) and the host country (where people arrive). The impacts can be socio-economic, cultural, and environmental.

Positive Impacts

  • Growth of New Cities: Leads to the development of satellite towns and urban sprawl (e.g., Navi Mumbai, Gurugram, Noida).
  • Resource Development: Countries like Canada and Australia develop their vast resources with the help of an immigrant workforce.
  • Cultural Exchange: Leads to the creation of multi-ethnic societies and increased understanding of diverse cultures.
  • Remittances: Migrants send money back to their home country, financing improved education, health facilities, and economic growth.

Negative Impacts

  • Squatter Settlements: Rapid influx leads to a lack of housing, resulting in slums with poor sanitation (e.g., Dharavi in Mumbai).
  • Rising Costs and Congestion: High demand raises land value and the cost of living. Overcrowding leads to severe traffic, as well as air, water, and land pollution.
  • Brain Drain: The home country loses its highly skilled human capital.
  • Social and Geopolitical Strain: Can cause conflict, racial discrimination, and immense pressure on the host country's educational and healthcare resources.

4. Brain Drain (Human Capital Flight)

Brain Drain is the emigration of highly trained, talented, and productive people from their home country to another country in pursuit of higher salaries, advanced studies, and better work environments. This predominantly occurs from developing nations to developed nations.

Causes of Brain Drain

The movement is triggered by a combination of factors that push people away from their home country and pull them towards a foreign destination.

  • Push Factors (Negative conditions at home): Stiff competition for employment, poor research facilities, poor economic development, poor working conditions (long hours, low wages), and lack of intellectual stimulation.
  • Pull Factors (Positive conditions abroad): High-paying jobs, the prestige of world-class institutions, stable government policies, intellectual freedom, modernized education systems, and better economic standards.
Important

Impacts of Brain Drain

Negative: The home country suffers a massive economic loss. There is a severe shortage of skilled professionals (like doctors and engineers) catering to critical local services. The number of potential future entrepreneurs also sharply decreases.

Positive: Migrants acquire world-class skills that they can eventually bring back. More significantly, they send back substantial earnings called remittances, which boost the home country's economy. Some nations even fund students to study abroad under the condition they return to serve the country (Reverse Brain Drain).

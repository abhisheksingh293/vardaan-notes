Chapter 3: Cell - The Structure And Functions

1. Discovery of the Cell

The branch of biology which deals with the study of cells is called cytology. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living things.

  • Antonie Van Leeuwenhoek (1632-1723): Developed a simple microscope using a single biconvex lens. He was the first to see living cells (blood cells in a frog) but did not call them "cells".
  • Robert Hooke (1665): Developed a compound microscope using two lenses. He examined a thin slice of dead cork and observed a cluster of box-like cubicles. He coined the term "cell" because they reminded him of the cells of monks living in a monastery.
Concept

Living Cell & Protoplasm: Hooke only saw the walls of dead cells. A cell is only considered living when it contains a jelly-like substance called protoplasm. Protoplasm is the living substance of the cell, made up of the cytoplasm and nucleus.

2. Cells: Numbers, Shapes, and Sizes

Number of Cells

  • Unicellular Organisms: Single-celled body. Examples: Bacteria, Yeast, Amoeba, Paramecium, Chlamydomonas.
  • Multicellular Organisms: Body made up of millions and billions of cells. Examples: Rose, Neem, Earthworm, Human, Lion.

Cell Shape

Cell shape is largely determined by its specific function:

  • Spherical: Red blood cells (disc-shaped to hold oxygen and flow easily).
  • Oval: Chlamydomonas (unicellular green algae).
  • Cylindrical: Spirogyra, Cardiac muscles (for easy contraction/relaxation).
  • Amoeboid (Irregular): Amoeba, White blood cells (have no definite shape).
  • Cubical/Rectangular: Leaf cells (plants have varied shapes including polygonal and tubular).
  • Unique Shapes: Slipper-shaped (Paramecium), Spindle-shaped (Smooth muscles).
Fact

Extremes in Size: The longest cells in the human body are the nerve cells (up to 3 meters). The smallest cells are found among bacteria (0.2-0.5 micrometre). The largest single cell is the ostrich egg.

3. The Cell Theory

Three scientists—Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow—formulated the cell theory which states:

  • Every living organism is made up of one or many cells.
  • The cell is the structural unit of all living organisms.
  • The cell is the functional unit of all living organisms.
  • All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

4. The Cell as a Functional Unit

Every function inside a living organism is the result of cellular activity:

  • In Plants: Root cells absorb water and minerals; Leaf cells manufacture food through photosynthesis using chlorophyll.
  • In Animals: Muscle cells help in movement; Nerve cells conduct messages; Gland cells secrete enzymes; Skin cells protect the body and regulate temperature.

5. Structure of a Cell

A typical animal or plant cell has three essential parts:

1. Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

It surrounds the cell. It is very thin, delicate, and flexible. It is a living structure present in both plant and animal cells. It protects the cell and is selectively permeable, allowing only certain molecules to pass in and out.

2. Cell Wall (Only in Plant Cells)

Found only in plant cells, it lies outside the cell membrane. It is made of cellulose, is a non-living structure, and gives shape and rigidity to the cell. It is freely permeable.

3. Cytoplasm

A semi-liquid (viscous), colourless, and translucent substance found between the nucleus and the cell membrane.

4. Nucleus

The nucleus is a small spherical body located towards the center of the cell. It regulates and coordinates all life processes of the cell and plays an important part in cell division. It consists of:

  • A porous nuclear membrane enclosing a dense nucleoplasm.
  • A darker body inside called the nucleolus.
  • Chromosomes, which contain units called genes responsible for transmitting characteristics from parents to offspring.
Important

Chromosomes: The number of chromosomes is definite in each species. For example, every cell of the human body has 46 chromosomes (occurring in 23 pairs), while a pea plant has 14 chromosomes (7 pairs).

6. Cell Organelles

With electron microscopes, tiny structures inside the cytoplasm were revealed, called cell organelles (like Endoplasmic reticulum, ribosomes, mitochondria, golgi bodies, lysosomes, centrosome). The syllabus focuses on:

Plastids (Only in Plant Cells)

  • Chloroplasts: Green plastids that trap solar energy for photosynthesis.
  • Chromoplasts: Contain yellow and red pigments. They impart varied colours to flowers and fruits to attract insects for pollination.
  • Leucoplasts: Colourless plastids occurring in seeds. They store starch, fats, and proteins.

Vacuoles

These are non-living inclusions in the cytoplasm bound by a membrane. They are filled with water and various substances in solution called cell sap.

7. Differences Between Plant and Animal Cells

  • Plant Cell: Usually larger in size. Has a definite cell wall. Cytoplasm is not as dense (often just a thin layer). Vacuoles are prominent but fewer in number (concerned with storage). Contains plastids.
  • Animal Cell: Usually smaller in size. No cell wall present. Cytoplasm is denser, granular, and fills almost the entire cell. Vacuoles are small and numerous (concerned with excretion/secretion). Does not contain plastids.

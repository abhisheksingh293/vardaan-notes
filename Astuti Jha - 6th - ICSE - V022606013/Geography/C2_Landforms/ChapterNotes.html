LANDFORMS

Concept

Landforms refer to the natural physical features of the solid part of the Earth's surface. Examples include mountains, plateaus, plains, and valleys.

1. Formation of Landforms

The surface of the Earth is constantly changing due to various forces. These forces are broadly divided into two processes:

A. Endogenous (Internal) Processes

These are the forces that originate inside the earth and bring about changes on its surface. They lead to the upliftment and sinking of the earth's surface. They are divided into:

  • Sudden Movements: Occur rapidly and bring sudden changes, e.g., Earthquakes and Volcanoes.
  • Slow Movements: Take thousands of years to show effects. They include:
    • Vertical / Epeirogenic Movements: Cause upliftment or subsidence of large parts of the earth's crust.
    • Horizontal / Orogenic Movements: Compressive forces that result in the folding or faulting of rock strata, eventually forming mountains.

B. Exogenous (External) Processes

These are the external forces operating on the surface of the earth. These continuously wear down the high landscapes and rebuild the low-lying ones through agents like running water, wind, and moving ice (glaciers).

2. Types of Landforms

I. Mountains

A mountain is an uplifted portion of the earth's surface that is much higher than its surrounding area. Mountains generally have steep slopes and a peak. They are classified into three major types based on their formation:

1. Fold Mountains:

Formed due to the horizontal/compressional forces acting on the earth's crust. These forces cause wrinkles or folds in the layers of rocks. The uplifted folds are called anticlines, and the downward folds are called synclines. Fold mountains are further classified as:

  • Young Fold Mountains: Formed relatively recently. They have high, pointed peaks and steep slopes. Examples include the Himalayas (Asia), Alps (Europe), Rockies (North America), and Andes (South America).
  • Old Fold Mountains: Formed millions of years ago. Their peaks have been rounded off and their height reduced due to continuous erosion. Examples include the Aravallis (India), Appalachians (North America), and Urals (Europe/Asia).
Fact

The Andes in South America form the longest continental mountain range in the world, stretching along the western coast of the continent.

2. Block Mountains:

Formed when cracks or faults occur in the earth's crust due to tension forces. When the land between two parallel faults is pushed upward, it forms a Block Mountain (also known as a Horst). They characteristically have flat tops and steep, fault-scarp sides.

  • Examples: The Black Forest and Vosges mountains in Europe, and the Vindhyas in India.

3. Volcanic Mountains:

Formed due to volcanic eruptions. Deep from the earth's interior, hot molten rock called magma erupts through a vent onto the surface (as lava) and accumulates over time to form a cone-shaped mountain.

  • Examples: Mount Kilimanjaro in Africa and Mt. Fujiyama in Japan.

II. Valleys

A valley is a low area of land between hills or mountains, typically with a river or stream flowing through it. A prominent type is the Rift Valley.

Rift Valley: Formed by faulting. When the block of land between two parallel faults sinks down, it forms a long, narrow trough known as a rift valley (or Graben). They have steep parallel walls.

  • Examples: The Rhine Valley in Europe, the Narmada Valley in India, and the Nile Valley in Africa.
Important

Block Mountains and Rift Valleys are closely related; both are the result of faulting (cracking of the earth's crust), where one part gets uplifted (mountain) and the other sinks (valley).

III. Plateaus

A plateau is an elevated area with a more or less flat top. It is also called a tableland. They have steep slopes on one or more sides. Based on their location and formation, they are of three types:

  • Intermontane Plateau: Surrounded by mountain ranges on all sides. Example: The Plateau of Tibet (the highest plateau in the world).
  • Piedmont / Border Plateau: Located at the foot of a mountain range on one side and a plain or ocean on the other. Example: The Patagonian Plateau in South America.
  • Volcanic Plateau: Formed by the spread of successive layers of fluid lava over a large area. Example: The Deccan Plateau in India.

Significance: Plateaus are extremely rich in mineral deposits. Mining is a primary occupation here. If the soil is fertile (like the black soil of the Deccan), it supports farming.

IV. Plains

Plains are extensive, relatively flat, and low-lying areas of land. They are broadly classified into:

  • Structural Plains: Formed by the upliftment of a part of the sea floor or continental shelf.
  • Erosional Plains: Formed when mountains and hills are worn down to a flat surface by agents of erosion.
  • Depositional Plains: The most common type. Formed by the deposition of silt and materials brought down by rivers. Example: The Gangetic Plains of India and the Plains of North America.

3. Landforms and People (Impact on Human Life)

The type of landform heavily influences the lifestyle, occupation, and population density of a region:

  • Life in the Mountains: The terrain is rugged, the climate is harsh, and the soil is usually thin and infertile. Consequently, agriculture is difficult, and transport networks are hard to build. Therefore, mountain areas are generally thinly populated.
  • Life in the Plains: Plains provide level land, fertile soil (especially depositional plains), and an abundant water supply. This makes them ideal for agriculture, setting up industries, building houses, and developing dense transport networks (roads, railways). Hence, river plains are the most densely populated regions of the world.

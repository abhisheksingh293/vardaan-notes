THE INDUS VALLEY CIVILIZATION

1. Discovery and Introduction

Like the Egyptian and Mesopotamian civilizations, the Indian subcontinent witnessed a highly developed civilization along the banks of the river Indus. Its discovery pushed back the history of India by almost 2000 years. Prior to this, it was believed Indian history began with the coming of the Aryans in 1500 BC.

The Indus Valley Civilization was first discovered in 1920-21 during excavations conducted by two prominent archaeologists:

  • Rakhal Das Banerji at Mohenjo-Daro
  • Daya Ram Sahni at Harappa
Concept

Chalcolithic Age: The Indus Valley Civilization belonged to the Chalcolithic Age. This was an age when copper and bronze came to be extensively used for making utensils and weapons, along with the continued use of stone tools.

2. Nomenclature and Extent

Naming the Civilization

Initially, remains were only found confined to the river Indus and its tributaries (Ravi, Chenab, Beas, Sutlej, and Jhelum). Hence, it was called the Indus Valley Civilization. Later, because Harappa was the first city to be discovered and its features matched other sites, it also came to be known as the Harappan Civilization.

Extent and Boundaries

The civilization flourished roughly between 2600 BC and 1900 BC. Excavations over the last fifty years have revealed about 1400 settlements across the Indian subcontinent (nearly 925 in India and 475 in Pakistan).

Fact

Covering a total area of about 12,50,000 sq. km, the Indus Valley Civilization was the largest civilization of the ancient world.

The vast boundaries of this civilization spanned across:

  • West: Sutkagen Dor in Baluchistan
  • East: Alamgirpur in Uttar Pradesh
  • North: Manda in Jammu & Kashmir
  • South: Diamabad in Maharashtra

3. Main Features and Town Planning

The Indus Valley cities were known for their remarkable urban planning and architecture, featuring well-organized structures.

  • Streets and Drainage System: The streets were laid out in a grid pattern. The drainage system was highly advanced, with covered drains running alongside streets to ensure proper sanitation.
  • The Great Bath: Found in Mohenjo-Daro, this was a large, public rectangular tank likely used for religious bathing or rituals.
  • The Great Granary: Massive structures used for storing surplus grains securely.
  • Assembly or Town Hall: Large pillared halls likely used for public meetings or administrative purposes.

4. Means of Livelihood

The Harappan people engaged in a highly diversified economy to sustain their cities. Their primary means of livelihood included:

  • Agriculture: Growing crops to produce a food surplus.
  • Domestication of Animals: Rearing cattle, sheep, and goats.
  • Crafts and Trade: Extensive work in brick-making and masonry, pottery-making, spinning, and seal-making.
  • Metalwork and Jewellery: Bronze-smiths created tools and weapons. Highly skilled artisans engaged in intricate bead-making and jewellery making.

They also had a thriving system of Trade and Commerce, both within their territories and with contemporary civilizations like Mesopotamia.

5. Religion, Script, and Decline

Religious Beliefs

Excavations have revealed elements of their religious life, including the worship of the Mother Goddess, a male deity often identified as Pashupati (Shiva), as well as the reverence of specific trees and animals.

The Script

Important

The Harappans developed a distinct pictographic script. However, despite decades of research by historians and linguists, this script remains undeciphered to this day.

Decline

The civilization began to decline around 1900 BC. Historians suggest various reasons for its end, including natural disasters (like floods or earthquakes), changes in the course of the Indus river, ecological degradation, or external invasions.

THE MESOPOTAMIAN CIVILIZATION

1. Introduction and Origin

Like the Indus Valley and Egyptian Civilizations, the Mesopotamian Civilization flourished in a highly fertile river valley. It is recognized by historians as one of the earliest civilizations in human history.

Concept

The term Mesopotamia is derived from two Greek words meaning "the land between the rivers." This civilization developed in the fertile region between the rivers Tigris and Euphrates (present-day Republic of Iraq).

The southern part of this land was called Sumer, and its inhabitants were known as the Sumerians. They created the first urban civilization in this region. Later, other empires like the Akkadian and Babylonian empires also flourished here.

2. Chief Features of the Civilization

Society

The Mesopotamian society was highly stratified and divided into three main classes:

  • Upper Class: The King, his royal family, priests, and high-ranking government officials.
  • Middle Class: Farmers, artisans, traders, and merchants.
  • Lower Class: Slaves (usually prisoners of war).

The King and His Government

The King was the head of the state and was considered the representative of God on Earth. He was responsible for the governance of the empire, collecting taxes, and protecting the citizens.

Important

Hammurabi's Code: King Hammurabi of Babylon is famous for compiling one of the earliest known written codes of law. These laws covered trade, family, labor, and property, establishing the strict principle of "an eye for an eye, and a tooth for a tooth."

Means of Livelihood

  • Agriculture: It was the main occupation. Because the flooding of the rivers was unpredictable, the Mesopotamians built complex systems of canals, dikes, and dams for irrigation.
  • Trade and Commerce: They had flourishing trade relations with other civilizations, including the Indus Valley. They exchanged agricultural surplus and manufactured goods for metals, timber, and stone.

3. Religion

The Mesopotamians were polytheistic, meaning they worshipped many gods. They primarily worshipped the forces of nature like the sun, moon, and wind.

Fact

To honour their gods, the Mesopotamians built large, multi-tiered temples called Ziggurats. These structures were placed on high platforms and often served as an observatory, a school, and a granary.

4. Major Achievements

Art and Architecture

They were masterful builders. The Mesopotamians introduced important architectural features to the world, notably the use of arches, columns, and domes in their buildings.

Writing System

The Sumerians developed one of the earliest systems of writing known as Cuneiform. It consisted of wedge-shaped marks pressed into wet clay tablets using a reed stylus. Once dried or baked, these clay tablets became permanent records.

Science and Mathematics

Their contributions to science, specifically astronomy and mathematics, are widely recognized today:

  • They developed a number system based on 60. This is why we still divide an hour into 60 minutes and a minute into 60 seconds.
  • They divided the circle into 360 degrees.
  • They studied the stars and developed a highly accurate lunar calendar consisting of 12 months.

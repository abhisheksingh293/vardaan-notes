The American War of Independence

1. Nationalism

Concept

Nationalism means loyalty to a nation. It is a way of thinking that says groups of humans, like ethnic groups, should be free to rule themselves.

  • Nationalism is also known as patriotism.
  • It gives people a sense of belonging to a national community.
  • The American and French Revolutions (late 18th century) are considered powerful manifestations of nationalism.
  • The 19th century is known as the Age of Nationalism in Europe.
  • In Asia and Africa, nationalism rose in the 20th century.

2. The American War of Independence

The American Revolution was a political and military struggle where 13 British colonies in America rebelled against the Imperial rule of Britain.

  • Duration: 1775 to 1783.
  • British Monarch: King George III.
  • Key Causes: Heavy taxes and restrictions on business and commerce.
  • Outcome: Creation of the United States of America as an independent and democratic state.
Fact

The American War of Independence was the first movement in which people asserted the right to rule themselves.

3. The British Colonies in America

After the discovery of the Americas in the 15th century, European countries like France, Holland, Spain, and Britain established colonies.

  • By the middle of the 18th century, Britain established 13 colonies along the Atlantic coast.
  • These included: Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, Connecticut, New York, Pennsylvania, Delaware, New Jersey, Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia.
  • Population: Independent farmers, people seeking religious freedom, and traders.
  • Economy: The north focused on fishing and shipbuilding. The south had large plantations of tobacco and cotton, grown using slave labour brought from Africa.
  • Governance: Each colony had a local assembly of elected representatives, but they were ruled by British-appointed Governors whose main task was to promote British interests, not the colonists'.

4. Causes of the American Revolution

4.1 Economic Exploitation

Britain's policies hindered the economic development of the American colonies.

  • Colonists were forbidden to use non-British ships for trade.
  • Products like tobacco, cotton, and sugar could only be exported to Britain.
  • Heavy duties were imposed on goods imported from other places.
  • Colonies were forbidden to start certain industries (e.g., iron works, textiles) and were forced to import these from Britain.

4.2 Restriction on Expansion of Colonies

The British angered colonists by preventing them from moving west into new lands, wanting to keep direct trade links with native Americans.

4.3 Imposition of New Taxes

Important

To recover debts from the Seven Years' War (1756-63) against France, the British government levied heavy taxes on the colonies.

  • Sugar Act (1764): Taxed sugar imports, affecting local traders.
  • Stamp Act (1765): Imposed stamp taxes on all business transactions and legal documents. Aroused violent resentment and led to a boycott of British goods.
  • Townshend Acts (1767): A series of acts (Revenue Act, Commissioners of Customs Act, Indemnity Act, New York Restraining Act) imposing taxes on imports of tea, paper, glass, and paint.
Concept

"No Taxation Without Representation": The colonists declared that the British Parliament had no right to levy taxes on them because they had no representatives in the Parliament. Only their own assemblies had the right to raise money.

4.4 Influence of Philosophers

Colonists were inspired by the writings of 17th-century English philosophers like John Locke, James Harrington, and John Milton, who believed in fundamental rights and restricted government interference.

  • Thomas Jefferson asserted the right to rebellion.
  • Thomas Paine detested British societal inequalities and supported independence.

5. Beginning of the American Revolution

5.1 The Boston Tea Party (1773)

The British passed the Tea Act, giving the East India Company a monopoly on the tea trade in America.

  • In protest, a group of colonists disguised as Native Indians raided British ships in Boston harbour and dumped 342 chests of tea into the water.
  • This event hastened the American War of Independence.

5.2 Intolerable Acts (1774)

To punish the colonies for the Boston Tea Party, the British passed the Intolerable Acts.

  • The Boston Port Act shut down the Boston Port for trade until the destroyed tea was paid for.
  • This angered all colonies, uniting them against British oppression.

5.3 First Continental Congress (1774)

  • Representatives of the 13 colonies met in Philadelphia.
  • They appealed to King George III to remove trade restrictions and stop imposing taxes without consent.
  • The King declared this a mutiny and sent troops, leading the colonies to form local militias.
  • The first battle occurred in 1775.

5.4 The Second Continental Congress & Declaration of Independence (1776)

Important

On July 4, 1776, representatives met in Philadelphia and adopted the Declaration of Independence, announcing separation from Great Britain.

  • Drafted by Thomas Jefferson.
  • Considered the Magna Carta of the American Revolution.
  • Asserted that "all men are created equal" and have the right to "life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness."
  • Stated the people's right to set up their own government.

6. The War of Independence

  • Commander-in-Chief: George Washington led the American forces.
  • Foreign Support: France provided troops, supplies, and funds. Spain and Holland also supported the colonies.
  • End of War: The war ended in 1781 with the defeat of British forces.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): The British formally recognized the independence of the 13 colonies.

7. Birth of the United States of America

  • The 13 independent states united under the Articles of Confederation (1781).
  • The Federal Constitution was written in 1787 and came into effect in 1789, establishing a federal, democratic republic.
  • It was the first written republican constitution in history.
  • George Washington was elected as the first President in 1789.
  • Bill of Rights (1791): Ten amendments were added to guarantee rights like freedom of speech, press, and religion.

8. Consequences of the American War of Independence

  • Global Inspiration: Inspired revolutionaries in Europe (especially France) and encouraged Spanish and Portuguese colonies in South America to rebel.
  • Democratic Government: Established a federal, democratic republic.
  • Social Change: People gained the right to vote and freedom of speech, justice, and religion.
  • Economic Impact: Opened new markets for the US, while Britain suffered a huge financial setback from losing the American markets.

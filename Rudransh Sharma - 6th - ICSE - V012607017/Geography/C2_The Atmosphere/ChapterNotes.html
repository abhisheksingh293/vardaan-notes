The Atmosphere

1. Introduction to the Atmosphere

Concept

Atmosphere: The atmosphere is made up of a mixture of gases that form a layer surrounding the Earth. It is held in place by the gravitational force of our planet.

Although the atmosphere extends beyond 1500 km above the Earth's surface, 99 percent of its mass lies within a height of 32 km from the ground. This is because the density of the atmosphere is greater in the lower layers, as the upper layers compress them into a dense mass.

Composition of the Atmosphere

The atmosphere is a vital gaseous envelope. Its composition includes:

  • Nitrogen: The most abundant gas, accounting for about 78% of the total volume.
  • Oxygen: Forms 21% of the atmosphere and is essential for respiration.
  • Other Gases: The remaining 1% consists of minute quantities of carbon dioxide, hydrogen, helium, ozone, and argon.
  • In its lower layers, the atmosphere also contains variable amounts of water vapour and suspended solid particles such as dust, smoke, and pollen.

2. Structure of the Atmosphere

Based on differences in composition, temperature, density, and other properties, the atmosphere is divided into five distinct layers:

1. Troposphere

  • The lowest layer of the atmosphere. Its thickness varies from about 8 km at the poles to 18 km above the Equator.
  • It is the densest layer, containing 75% of the total mass of the atmosphere.
  • Contains the air we breathe, almost all the dust particles, and water vapour.
  • All weather changes (clouds, rain, fog, storms) occur here.
  • Temperature decreases with height, a phenomenon known as the normal lapse rate.
  • High-speed winds called jet streams blow in the upper limits of the troposphere, heavily influencing surface weather.

2. Stratosphere

  • Lies above the troposphere, extending up to 50 km from the Earth's surface.
  • Provides ideal flying conditions for aircraft because the absence of water vapour keeps it free from weather disturbances and turbulence.
  • Contains the ozonosphere (ozone layer), which absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiations emitted by the Sun.
  • The upper limit is marked by the stratopause.

3. Mesosphere

  • Lies above the stratosphere, extending from 50 km to 80 km.
  • It is the coldest atmospheric layer, with temperatures dipping to -90°C.
  • Meteors slow down and burn up here due to friction when they enter from space.

4. Thermosphere (Ionosphere)

  • Extends up to a height of around 400 km.
  • Temperature rapidly increases with height, making it the hottest layer.
  • Contains electrically charged particles called ions. This layer reflects radio waves back to Earth, making radio communication possible.
Fact

Auroras occur in the Thermosphere! These are bright, beautiful bands of light (like the Aurora Borealis in the north) caused by high-energy particles from the Sun interacting with atoms in the ionosphere.

5. Exosphere

  • The uppermost layer of the atmosphere that gradually merges with interplanetary space.
  • The air is extremely thin and composed of very light gases like hydrogen and helium.

3. Importance of the Atmosphere

The atmosphere makes life on Earth possible in several ways:

  • Provides oxygen for humans and animals to breathe, and carbon dioxide for plants.
  • Water vapour enables various forms of precipitation (rain, snow).
  • The ozone layer prevents harmful UV rays from reaching the Earth.
  • Meteors burn up before hitting the surface.
  • Acts as a blanket, trapping heat (Greenhouse Effect) to maintain an optimum temperature for all living beings.

4. Greenhouse Effect and Global Warming

Concept

Greenhouse Effect: During the day, the Earth heats up. At night, it cools by releasing heat (terrestrial radiation). Some of this heat is absorbed by Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) like carbon dioxide and water vapour, preventing the Earth from freezing at night.

Global Warming: An unnatural increase in the concentration of GHGs and Ozone Depleting Substances (ODS) traps excessive heat, leading to a gradual, dangerous rise in global temperatures.

Causes of Global Warming

  • Burning of Fossil Fuels: Coal and petroleum release massive amounts of carbon dioxide (the most significant GHG contributor).
  • Industrial & Vehicular Emissions: Release harmful gases like oxides of nitrogen, sulphur, and methane.
  • Deforestation & Mining: Trees act as a "carbon sink" (absorbing CO2). Cutting them down increases CO2 levels in the air.
  • Rearing Farm Animals: Animals like goats, sheep, and buffaloes produce methane during digestion, which is released through their dung.
  • Chemical Fertilisers: Release nitrogen oxide into the atmosphere.

Impact of Global Warming

  • High evaporation rates dry up soil and surface water, affecting crop production and causing severe droughts.
  • Heatwaves cause health issues and increase the spread of vector-borne diseases (malaria, dengue) and water-borne diseases (cholera).
  • Frequent forest fires and erratic rainfall patterns (more cyclones and hurricanes).
  • Melting of glaciers and permanent ice sheets causes sea levels to rise, submerging coastal areas and creating climate refugees.
  • Destruction of marine ecosystems, including coral reefs, and loss of biodiversity.

Ways to Reduce Global Warming

  • Switch from fossil fuels to renewable sources of energy (wind, solar, hydro), which are non-polluting.
  • Plant more trees (afforestation) to increase the Earth's carbon sink.
  • Use eco-friendly gadgets and vehicles (e.g., electric cars, replacing incandescent bulbs with energy-efficient CFL bulbs).
  • Develop Green Cities (Eco-cities) that strive to lessen environmental impacts by reducing waste, encouraging recycling, and lowering pollution.
  • Adopt the 3Rs (Reduce, Recycle, Reuse) strategy for sustainable development.

5. The Ozone Layer and Its Depletion

The ozone layer in the stratosphere acts as a shield against the Sun's harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation.

Causes of Ozone Depletion

Human activities release Ozone Depleting Substances (ODS), primarily Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs). When CFCs reach the stratosphere, UV radiation causes a chlorine atom to break away. This free chlorine atom hits an ozone molecule, splitting it apart and destroying the ozone layer.

CFCs are released from disposal of junk refrigerators, air conditioners, aerosols, fire extinguishers, and jet aircraft.

Impact of Ozone Depletion

  • Increased UV rays cause skin cancer, eye cataracts, and suppress human immunity.
  • Adversely affects plant growth (stunted growth).
  • Penetrates water and destroys aquatic plants and animals.
Important

The Montreal Protocol: In September 1987, several countries met in Canada and agreed to the Montreal Protocol to reduce the production of ODS. As a result, the ozone hole is slowly recovering. September 16 is celebrated worldwide as World Ozone Day.

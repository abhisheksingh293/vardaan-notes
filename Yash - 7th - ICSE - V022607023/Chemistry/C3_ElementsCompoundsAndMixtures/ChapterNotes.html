ELEMENTS, COMPOUNDS AND MIXTURES

1. Atoms and Molecules

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists in three physical states: solid, liquid, and gas.

Concept

An atom is the smallest particle of an element that exhibits all the properties of that element. Atoms may or may not exist independently but participate in every chemical reaction. Examples: C (Carbon), H (Hydrogen), O (Oxygen).

Concept

A molecule is the smallest particle of a substance that can exist freely and retain the characteristics of that substance. It is formed when atoms combine together.

Characteristics of Atoms

  • Atoms of most elements are very reactive and generally do not exist as free atoms.
  • Atoms of a specific element are identical (alike in shape, size, and mass).
  • An atom maintains its properties through all physical and chemical changes.

2. Elements

A substance made of only the same kind of atoms is called an element. Atoms cannot be broken down into simpler substances.

Fact

Currently, about 118 elements are known, out of which 92 occur naturally in the earth's crust. The rest have been synthesized by scientists in laboratories.

Characteristics of Elements

  • It is a pure substance made up of only one type of atom.
  • It cannot be separated into simpler pieces except during nuclear processes.
  • The smallest unit is an atom, which displays all characteristics of that element.
  • Elements can be found in nature in a free state or combined as compounds.

Elements exist in all three states of matter:

  • Solid: Gold, silver, iron, copper, lead, aluminium, zinc, sodium, carbon.
  • Liquid: Mercury and bromine.
  • Gas: Oxygen, neon, nitrogen, helium, hydrogen, chlorine, fluorine.

3. Classification of Elements

Elements are classified into four main groups based on their physical and chemical properties:

A. Metals

  • All metals (except mercury, which is liquid) are solids at room temperature.
  • Usually lustrous (shiny surface).
  • Ductile (can be drawn into wires).
  • Malleable (can be hammered/rolled into thin sheets).
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Examples: Sodium (Na), Magnesium (Mg), Calcium (Ca), Copper (Cu), Potassium (K), Silver (Ag), Gold (Au), Aluminium (Al).

B. Non-metals

  • Generally liquids and gases at room temperature (some are solids like carbon and sulphur).
  • Have dull surfaces.
  • Non-malleable (cannot be easily hammered into shapes) and non-ductile.
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Examples: Hydrogen gas (H₂), Oxygen gas (O₂), Nitrogen gas (N₂), Sulphur solid (S), Carbon solid (C).

C. Metalloids

  • Also known as semi-metals.
  • They show properties that are intermediate between metals and non-metals.
  • Examples: Arsenic (As), Boron (B), Silicon (Si).

D. Noble or Inert Gases

  • Elements belonging to group 18 of the Modern Periodic Table.
  • They are chemically unreactive (inert).
  • Examples: Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar), Krypton (Kr), Xenon (Xe), Radon (Rn).

4. Compounds

When atoms of two or more different elements combine chemically in a fixed proportion, they form compounds.

Important

Atoms of the same element combining together form a molecule of an element, NOT a compound. E.g., O₂ is a molecule of an element, while H₂O is a compound because it has atoms of two different elements (Hydrogen and Oxygen).

Examples of Compounds: Hydrogen chloride gas (HCl), Sodium chloride solid (NaCl), Nitrogen dioxide gas (NO₂), Zinc sulphide solid (ZnS), Magnesium chloride solid (MgCl₂).

Characteristics of Compounds

  • A compound is a pure substance.
  • The components are present in a fixed, definite ratio.
  • It has a homogeneous composition (uniform structure throughout).
  • The properties of a compound are entirely different from the properties of its constituent elements. (e.g., Sodium is a highly reactive metal, Chlorine is a poisonous gas, but Sodium Chloride is edible table salt).
  • Elements in a compound cannot be separated by physical methods.
  • Energy is either released or absorbed during compound formation.

5. Symbols of Elements

The abbreviation of the name of an element to its initial letter or letters is called the symbol of the element.

Rules for writing chemical symbols:

  1. The symbol is often the first letter of its name (e.g., H for hydrogen, C for carbon).
  2. If names begin with the same letter, the first two letters are used (e.g., Ar for argon, Al for aluminium).
  3. Some symbols come from their Latin names:
    • Mercury (Hydrargyrum) → Hg
    • Copper (Cuprum) → Cu
    • Gold (Aurum) → Au
    • Sodium (Natrium) → Na
    • Potassium (Kalium) → K
    • Silver (Argentum) → Ag
    • Iron (Ferrum) → Fe
    • Lead (Plumbum) → Pb
  4. Some symbols use the first letter followed by a letter with a strong sound (e.g., Mg for magnesium, Cl for chlorine).
Concept

Significance of a Symbol: It represents the name of the element and denotes exactly one atom of that element.

6. Atomicity of an Element

Atomicity is defined as the number of atoms present in one molecule of an element.

  • Monoatomic molecules (1 atom): Noble gases like Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar).
  • Diatomic molecules (2 atoms): Hydrogen (H₂), Nitrogen (N₂), Oxygen (O₂), Chlorine (Cl₂).
  • Triatomic molecules (3 atoms): Ozone (O₃).
  • Polyatomic molecules (>3 atoms): Phosphorus (P₄), Sulphur (S₈).

7. Valency

Valency is a whole number that indicates the combining capacity of an element's atom with other atoms to form a compound. It is defined as the number of electrons donated or accepted by an atom during chemical combination.

  • Electropositive valency: Atom donates electrons (e.g., Sodium Na is +1).
  • Electronegative valency: Atom accepts electrons (e.g., Chlorine Cl is -1).

Types based on Valency:

  • Monovalent (Valency 1): Fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, sodium, potassium.
  • Divalent (Valency 2): Oxygen, magnesium, sulphur, calcium.
  • Trivalent (Valency 3): Boron, nitrogen, aluminium, phosphorus.
  • Tetravalent (Valency 4): Carbon, silicon, lead.

8. Chemical Formulae of Compounds

The symbolic representation of a molecule of a compound is called its chemical formula or molecular formula.

It shows the atoms (and their quantities as subscripts) present in one molecule.

Important Formulae:

  • Water: H₂O
  • Common Salt (Sodium chloride): NaCl
  • Carbon dioxide: CO₂
  • Ammonia: NH₃
  • Marble / Calcium carbonate: CaCO₃
  • Baking soda: NaHCO₃
  • Sand / Silicon dioxide: SiO₂
  • Quick lime (Calcium oxide): CaO
  • Caustic soda (Sodium hydroxide): NaOH
Concept

Significance of a Formula: It represents the name of the compound, one molecule of the compound, the names of the elements involved, and the exact number of atoms of each element in one molecule.

9. Mixtures

If two or more substances are mixed together in any proportion, do not undergo any chemical change, and retain their own properties, the result is a mixture.

Characteristics of a Mixture

  • Composed of substances mixed in any ratio without chemical reaction.
  • Retains the properties of its constituent components.
  • Components can be separated by simple physical methods.
  • No energy change (heat/light) occurs during its formation.

Types of Mixtures

A. Homogeneous Mixtures

A mixture in which the composition is the same (uniform) throughout. The components cannot be seen separately.

  • Examples: Salt in water, sugar in water, air (mixture of gases), alloys (brass, bronze, steel).

B. Heterogeneous Mixtures

A mixture that consists of physically distinct parts, each with different properties. The composition is not uniform.

  • Examples: Sand and water, iron filings and sulphur powder, oil and water.
Important

Difference between Iron-Sulphur Mixture vs. Compound:
- Mixture (Fe + S): Iron can be separated using a magnet. Sulphur dissolves in carbon disulphide.
- Compound (Iron Sulphide, FeS): Formed by heating. Iron cannot be separated by a magnet. Sulphur does not dissolve in carbon disulphide. The properties change entirely.

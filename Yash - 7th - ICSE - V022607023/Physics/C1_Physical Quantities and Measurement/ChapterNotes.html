PHYSICAL QUANTITIES AND MEASUREMENT

1. Introduction to Measurement

Measurement has always been a vital component of science. Throughout history, great minds like Archimedes, Isaac Newton, and Galileo Galilei discovered fundamental laws regarding mass, volume, density, and speed that made measuring in science uniform and accessible.

Important

The International System of Units (SI units) was adopted by the General Conference on Weights and Measures in 1960. This system ensures uniformity in measurement across the world, completely avoiding the confusion caused by earlier, non-standard units like handspans, footsteps, and cubits.

2. Fundamental Quantities

Fundamental quantities are the base quantities that do not depend on any other physical quantity. The SI system recognizes seven fundamental quantities:

  • Length: Metre (m)
  • Mass: Kilogram (kg)
  • Time: Second (s)
  • Temperature: Kelvin (K)
  • Luminous Intensity: Candela (cd)
  • Electric Current: Ampere (A)
  • Amount of Substance: Mole (mol)

3. Measuring Area

Concept

Area is defined as the space enclosed within the boundary of a two-dimensional figure. The SI unit of area is the square metre (m2).

Larger spaces, such as agricultural fields, are measured in hectares (ha) or square kilometres (km2). Smaller objects like a book cover are measured in cm2 or mm2.

Area of Regular Surfaces

We can calculate the area of regular geometric shapes using standard mathematical formulae:

  • Rectangle: Length × Breadth
  • Square: Side × Side
  • Triangle: 1/2 × Base × Height
  • Circle: π × (Radius)2

Area of Irregular Surfaces

For irregular shapes (e.g., a leaf), standard formulas cannot be used. We trace the outline of the irregular surface onto a graph paper. The area is estimated by counting the number of complete squares and squares that are more than half-covered. Squares that are less than half-covered are ignored.

4. Measuring Volume

Volume is defined as the amount of space occupied by a three-dimensional body. The SI unit of volume is the cubic metre (m3).

Volume of Liquids

The volume of liquids refers to the capacity of the container they are held in. It is most commonly measured in litres (L) and millilitres (mL). Instruments like measuring beakers and measuring cylinders are used to get accurate readings.

Key Conversions: 1 L = 1000 mL = 1000 cm3. Also, 1 m3 = 1000 L.

Fact

When measuring liquid volume in a cylinder, you will notice the surface is slightly curved. This is called the meniscus. Water forms a concave meniscus, whereas dense liquids like mercury form a convex meniscus. Always keep your eyes perfectly horizontal with the bottom of a concave meniscus (or the top of a convex one) to prevent reading errors.

Volume of Solids

  • Regular Solids: Calculated using formulas (e.g., Cube = Side3, Cuboid = Length × Breadth × Height).
  • Irregular Solids: Measured using the water displacement method. An irregular solid is completely submerged in a measuring cylinder containing water. The displaced water is equal to the object's volume (Volume = Final reading - Initial reading).

5. Measuring Density

Concept

Density depicts how tightly molecules are packed in a substance. It is defined as the mass per unit volume of a body. An object with a higher mass for the same volume is considered denser.

Formula: Density (ρ) = Mass (m) / Volume (V)

Units: The SI unit of density is kg/m3. The CGS unit is g/cm3. To convert kg/m3 to g/cm3, divide by 1000.

Determination of Density

  • Regular Solid: Determine mass using a balance. Calculate volume using standard formulae. Divide mass by volume.
  • Irregular Solid: Find mass using a balance. Find volume using the water displacement method. Calculate density using the formula.
  • Liquid: Measure the mass of an empty beaker. Pour the liquid and measure the total mass. Subtract to find the mass of the liquid. Determine the liquid's volume using a measuring cylinder, then calculate density.

6. Measuring Speed

When the position of a body changes with respect to its surroundings over time, it is said to be in motion. Speed gives us an idea of how fast or slow that body moves.

Important

Speed is defined as the distance travelled by a moving body in unit time.
Formula: Speed = Distance Travelled / Time Taken

The SI unit of speed is metre per second (m/s). Another widely used unit is kilometre per hour (km/h).

  • To convert km/h into m/s: Multiply the value by 5/18.
  • To convert m/s into km/h: Multiply the value by 18/5.

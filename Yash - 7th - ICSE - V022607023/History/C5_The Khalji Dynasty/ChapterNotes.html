THE KHALJI DYNASTY

Fact

Timeline of the Khalji Dynasty:

  • 1290 CE: Jalaluddin Khalji ascends the throne.
  • 1296 CE: Alauddin Khalji captures the throne.
  • 1316 CE: Death of Alauddin Khalji.
  • 1320 CE: End of the Khalji Dynasty.

1. Jalaluddin Firoz Khalji (1290 - 1296 CE)

After the death of Balban, the Delhi Sultanate entered a period of instability. In 1296 CE, a new ruling family took charge:

  • He was the founder of the Khalji Dynasty.
  • He ascended the throne at the age of seventy in 1290 CE.
  • He inherited a disordered kingdom where the Sultan's authority was no longer feared.
  • He was assassinated by his ambitious nephew and son-in-law, Alauddin Khalji, who subsequently captured the throne.

2. Alauddin Khalji (1296 - 1316 CE)

Alauddin initially served as the governor of Awadh and Kara. He gained immense popularity and wealth by defeating Raja Ramchandra of Devagiri in the Deccan and later distributed this wealth among the nobles to win them over.

Military Expansion

Alauddin built an extensive empire, extending from the Brahmaputra in the East to the Arabian Sea in the West, and from the Himalayas down to Madurai.

  • North India: Successfully led conquests of Gujarat and Malwa (1297 CE). He also captured key forts in Rajasthan, including Ranthambore and Chittor (1303 CE).
  • South India: Expeditions were led by his trusted general, Malik Kafur (also known as Hazar Dinari). They captured Dwarasamudra and Madurai.
  • Deccan Strategy: Realising the South was difficult to rule directly, Alauddin made the regional rulers his vassals, forcing them to pay annual tributes to maintain his vast military.
Important

Defense against Mongols: Alauddin faced over a dozen aggressive Mongol invasions. He successfully safeguarded his empire by strengthening border forts and entrusting defense responsibilities to experienced generals like Zafar Khan and Malik Kafur.

Administration & Control

Alauddin was known as a strict and highly efficient administrator who took extreme measures to ensure absolute power:

  • He actively curbed the power of his nobles by confiscating lands and wealth previously granted to them.
  • He discouraged social gatherings, extravagant marriages, and the public drinking of wine to prevent conspiracies.
  • He established a powerful and covert network of spies to immediately report any signs of rebellion.

Military Reforms

Concept

To strengthen his large standing army (an army permanently on the payroll), Alauddin introduced two major organizational reforms:

  • Chehra: A system of maintaining strict identity cards detailing the physical description of soldiers.
  • Dagh: The practice of branding military horses to ensure good quality horses weren't swapped with inferior ones.

Economic Reforms

To feed and pay his massive army without emptying the royal treasury, Alauddin implemented strict economic measures:

  • Taxes were collected directly in cash.
  • The state's share of agricultural produce was heavily raised to half, particularly in the fertile Doab region (between the Ganga and Yamuna rivers).
  • Price Control: Essential goods had rigidly fixed prices. Markets were supervised daily by appointed officers.
  • Grain was stored in government granaries. Any defaulters or hoarders were dealt with severe punishments.

3. Cultural Contributions & Architecture

Despite being a strict ruler, Alauddin's reign marked a period of cultural growth. He was highly religious but ensured religion did not interfere with state matters.

  • He patronised scholars and poets, the most notable being Amir Khusrau.
  • Amir Khusrau, celebrated as the Father of Qawwali, authored significant literary works such as Khazain-ul-Futuh (detailing Alauddin's conquests) and Nuh-Sipihr (praising Qutb-ud-din Mubarak Shah).

Key Architectural Monuments Built:

  • Alai Darwaza: An exquisite gateway to the Qutb Minar.
  • A prominent madrasa (college) near the Qutb Minar in Delhi.
  • Siri Fort and the grand Palace of a Thousand Pillars (in Warangal).
  • Hauz Khas: A large royal water reservoir built for the city's needs.

4. Decline of the Dynasty

  • Following Alauddin's death in 1316 CE, his son Qutb-ud-din Mubarak Shah ascended the throne and ruled for four years.
  • Mubarak Shah proved to be a weak successor, which eventually led to the collapse of the Khalji Dynasty in 1320 CE.
  • The power vacuum paved the way for the rise of a new ruling family: The Tughluq Dynasty.

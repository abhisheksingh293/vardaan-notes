The Advent of Christianity

1. Introduction to the Medieval Period

Historians divide history into ancient, medieval, and modern periods. The transition between these periods was gradual and did not occur uniformly across the world.

  • In India, the Medieval Period extended roughly from 800 CE to 1800 CE.
  • In Europe, the Medieval Period stretched from about 600 CE to 1500 CE.
  • This era witnessed significant global developments: Christianity spread across Europe, the Arabs and Turks rose to power, and Europeans began traveling to India for trade.

2. The Birth and Teachings of Jesus Christ

Christianity was founded by a Jew named Jesus, born around 4 BCE in Bethlehem, Judaea. He was raised in Nazareth (Palestine).

Concept

Messiah: A leader (usually religious) who brings hope for the people. Christians consider Jesus Christ to be the Messiah and the Son of God.

The Core Teachings

Jesus began preaching his word around the age of 30. His teachings heavily focused on love, equality, and compassion. While his teachings clashed with traditional Jewish leaders, his ability to heal and perform miracles attracted many followers. Some of his primary teachings included:

  • Live a simple living life.
  • Treat all people equally.
  • Love God unconditionally.
  • Love your neighbors and forgive your enemies.
Fact

The Holy Bible: The sacred text of Christianity consists of the Old Testament and the New Testament. The teachings and life of Jesus are recorded in the New Testament within the Gospels of his disciples: Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John.

3. Crucifixion and Resurrection

During this time, Palestine was under Roman rule. Jewish leaders persuaded the Romans that Jesus was a threat to their power and religion. As a result, Jesus was arrested, charged with rebellion, and crucified on Good Friday (between 30-33 CE).

According to Christian belief, Jesus was resurrected (rose from the dead) three days later. This miraculous event is celebrated as Easter. The belief in Jesus's resurrection gave hope to his followers and became the central foundation of the Christian faith.

4. Spread of Christianity

The Roman Emperor Constantine adopted a tolerant policy towards Christians. He famously placed the Greek initials for Christ on his shields and banners. He built a new capital named Constantinople (present-day Istanbul, formally Byzantium) filled with Christian architecture.

By the 4th century CE, Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire. Largely due to the efforts of St. Paul and other apostles, it spread rapidly beyond Europe into regions like Armenia and Persia.

5. Division and Decline of the Roman Empire

After the death of Roman Emperor Theodosius, the vast Roman Empire was divided between his two sons:

  • Honorius: Ruled the Western empire from Rome.
  • Arcadius: Ruled the Eastern empire from Constantinople.
Important

Reasons for the Decline of the Roman Empire:
1. Cultural Conflicts: Christianity's intolerance for older religions caused unrest (e.g., Emperor Theodosius ended the Olympic Games because they honored the Greek God, Zeus).
2. Military Disloyalty: Soldiers' loyalty shifted from the emperor to their local military leaders.
3. Economic Weakness: Severe economic problems weakened the empire.
4. Barbarian Invasions: Constant invasions from outside tribes.

Threats and Invasions

The most prominent cause for the decline of the Western Roman Empire was invasions from "barbarian" tribes:

  • The Goths: A nomadic Germanic tribe fleeing the Huns. Under their young king Alaric, the West Goths attacked the empire multiple times, even plundering Rome.
  • Other tribes included the Vandals, Angles, Saxons, Franks, and Lombards. For instance, the Angles and Saxons settled in Britain, and the Franks established themselves in what became France.
  • In 476 CE, Romulus, the last western Roman emperor, was overthrown by a Germanic leader, marking the ultimate fall of the Western Roman Empire.

Meanwhile, the Eastern Roman Empire, known as the Byzantine Empire, survived for nearly a thousand more years with its capital at Constantinople. It preserved Roman legacy, used Greek as its main language, and became the center of the Orthodox Church.

6. The Crusades (1095-1291 CE)

During the Medieval Period, the Turks (like the Khazars and Seljuks) rose to power. In the 11th century, they captured the holy land of Jerusalem. This led to religious wars known as the Crusades.

The Crusades were a series of religious wars between the Christians and the Muslims aimed primarily at recovering holy sites considered sacred by both. The wars were sanctioned by the Latin Church, with Pope Urban II declaring that fighting in a crusade would ensure immediate forgiveness of sins.

Results of the Crusades:

  • Christians learned about gunpowder from Muslims, changing European warfare.
  • Knowledge of science, medicine, and architecture from the Islamic and Greek worlds enriched Europe, paving the way for the Renaissance.
  • Arab inventions like algebra and optics spread to Europe and were introduced as subjects in European universities.
  • Increased trade led to the growth of towns. Merchants began supporting stronger kings, which weakened the position of feudal lords.

7. Christian Monastic Orders and the Church

The fall of Rome left a massive political and social void in Europe, which Christianity began to fill. By the 5th century CE, Christian churches had been set up all over Western Europe.

Hierarchy of the Church

  • Priests: Formed the lowest rung, performing religious services, prayers, and marriages.
  • Bishops: Higher in rank, oversaw churches in entire regions (e.g., Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem).
  • The Pope: The Bishop of Rome eventually became the supreme spiritual head of Christians.

Over time, the Christian Church split into different branches: the Roman Catholic Church, the Eastern Orthodox Church, and later, the Protestant Church.

Monks and Nuns

Some priests chose to live strictly as ascetics in monasteries, dedicating their lives to prayer, study, and service. They were known as monks. Women who gave up worldly life were known as nuns and lived in convents or nunneries.

Impact of Monasteries

  • Centers of Learning: Monasteries became vital centers of education. Monks and nuns studied and taught literature, art, science, and religion.
  • Preservation of Knowledge: Monks painstakingly hand-copied texts onto parchment, preserving ancient knowledge.
  • Birth of Universities: Churches eventually grew into full universities, like Oxford and Cambridge.
  • Social Welfare: Monasteries functioned as hospitals and shelters, educating children, treating the sick, and helping the poor.
  • Artistic Flourish: Skills like architecture, woodworking, and glass painting flourished inside monasteries.

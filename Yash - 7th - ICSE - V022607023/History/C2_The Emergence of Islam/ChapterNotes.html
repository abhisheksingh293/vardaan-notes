The Emergence of Islam

1. The Early Arab Society

Before the emergence of Islam, the people of Arabia were largely divided into different tribes. The early Arabs worshipped nature and various idols. A central religious focal point was a sacred black stone housed in Mecca.

Fact

The Kaaba: Located in Mecca, it is a cubic structure made of grey basalt block. It is approximately 50 feet long, 35 feet wide, and 43 feet high, completely covered by a black cloth adorned with gold and silver trim.

2. Prophet Muhammad and the Birth of Islam

Prophet Muhammad was born in Mecca in 570 CE. Disturbed by the moral decline and idol worship around him, he spent long hours in deep prayer and reflection.

  • At the age of 40, Muhammad began receiving divine revelations from the Archangel Gabriel.
  • He preached the foundation of a new faith known as Islam, which means "surrender to the will of one God (Allah)."
  • He stressed democratic ideals, social equality, and compassion, which heavily appealed to the common people.
  • The holy book of Islam, containing the divine revelations, is the Quran.
Concept

Islam & Muslims: The religion is called Islam, and the followers of this faith are called Muslims.

3. The Five Pillars of Islam

According to the faith, every Muslim is required to observe five essential duties known as the Five Pillars of Islam:

  • Shahada: Belief in one God (Allah) and that Muhammad is His prophet.
  • Salat: Praying five times a day facing Mecca.
  • Sawm: Fasting during the holy month of Ramadan.
  • Zakat: Giving charity to the poor and needy.
  • Hajj: Making a pilgrimage to Mecca at least once in a lifetime.

Additional Conduct: Muslims are also strongly discouraged from drinking wine, eating pork, and gambling.

4. The Spread of Islam and The Hijrat

Muhammad's teachings faced massive opposition from traditional orthodox Arabs in Mecca who worshipped idols. Because of severe hostility, Muhammad and his followers were forced to flee.

Important

The Hijrat (622 CE): Muhammad's migration from Mecca to the city of Medina in 622 CE is known as the Hijrat (or Hijra). This marks the official beginning of the Islamic lunar calendar, known as the Hijri Calendar.

In Medina, Muhammad secured massive support, built an army, and successfully returned to Mecca. He defeated his opponents and gradually united the various Arab tribes under one common religious and social code. Prophet Muhammad passed away in 632 CE.

The Caliphate

After Muhammad's death, his successors were known as Caliphs (or Khalifas). Under the Caliphs, Islam expanded rapidly. Over the centuries, through dynasties like the Umayyads and Abbasids, the Arab empire spread across the Middle East, North Africa, Spain, and Persia.

5. Impact and Contributions of Islam

The rise of the Islamic Empire brought about significant changes and advancements in various fields, heavily influencing the Medieval Period globally.

Trade and Economy

  • The Arabs controlled highly lucrative trade routes linking the Mediterranean Sea with China and India.
  • They traded extensively in silk, spices, and jewellery from the East.
  • The capital city, Baghdad, became a booming, rich city and a central meeting point for global trade.

Science, Mathematics, and Intellectual Progress

The Abbasid period is especially known as a golden age of learning. Scholars at Baghdad translated knowledge from Greek, Persian, Egyptian, and Sanskrit into Arabic.

  • Mathematics: They adopted the decimal system and the concept of zero from India, and learned geometry from the Greeks. They introduced mathematical terms to Europe, such as algebra.
  • Science: Arabs made monumental progress in astronomy, medicine, pharmacology, and chemistry.
  • Literature: Literature, poetry, and history flourished under Islamic patronage.

Art and Architecture

Islam introduced a completely distinct artistic style since depicting human figures was generally discouraged:

  • Arabesque: Beautiful, intricate geometric and floral patterns used for decoration.
  • Calligraphy: The art of beautiful handwriting, frequently used to intricately decorate the Quran and building walls.
  • Architecture: Islamic architecture introduced signature building features like large domes, minarets, and arches.

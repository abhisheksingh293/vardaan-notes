MOTION

1. Rest and Motion

In our daily lives, we see some objects moving while others remain stationary. Based on this observation, objects can be classified into two states:

  • Motion: An object is said to be in motion if its position changes with respect to its surroundings and time (e.g., flying birds, moving cars).
  • Rest: If an object does not change its position with respect to its surroundings with time, it is said to be in a state of rest (e.g., houses, trees).
Concept

Rest and Motion are Relative Terms: An object may appear to be in motion to one observer while appearing at rest to another. For example, to a passenger in a moving car, the trees outside appear to be moving backwards, but to a person standing outside, the trees are at rest and the car is moving.

2. Types of Motion

Objects can undergo different types of motion based on the path they follow:

Translatory Motion

When a body moves in a line such that all its parts move through the same distance in the same time interval, it is called translatory (or linear) motion. It is of two types:

  • Rectilinear Motion: Motion along a straight line (e.g., a falling apple, a car on a straight road).
  • Curvilinear Motion: Motion along a curved or circular path (e.g., a discus in a discus throw).

Other Important Types of Motion

  • Circular Motion: When an object moves around a fixed point in a circular path. The distance from the fixed point remains constant (e.g., a child on a merry-go-round, the moon around the Earth).
  • Rotatory Motion: When a body spins about a fixed axis without changing its radius of motion (e.g., movement of a fan, a giant wheel). Unlike circular motion, different parts of a rotating body cover different distances.
  • Oscillatory Motion: The back-and-forth (to and fro) movement of a body about its mean (rest) position (e.g., a pendulum clock, a swinging child).
  • Vibratory Motion: A type of oscillatory motion where only a part of the body moves back and forth, not the entire object. This rapid movement often produces sound (e.g., guitar strings, tabla surface).
  • Periodic Motion: Motion that repeats itself exactly after a fixed, regular interval of time (e.g., the Earth revolving around the sun, movement of clock hands).
  • Non-periodic Motion: Motion that does not repeat itself after regular time intervals (e.g., running, heartbeat while exercising).
  • Multiple Motion: When a body exhibits more than one type of motion simultaneously (e.g., the Earth rotates on its axis and revolves around the sun).
  • Rolling Motion: A special combination of both rotatory and translatory motion (e.g., a ball rolling on the ground, bicycle wheels moving forward).
Fact

The Simple Pendulum: It consists of a heavy mass (bob) suspended by a string from a rigid support. One complete to-and-fro motion from its mean position is called one oscillation. The time taken to complete one oscillation is its time period (T), measured in seconds (s). The motion of a simple pendulum is strictly periodic.

3. Distance and Displacement

To measure motion accurately, we need to understand distance and displacement:

  • Distance: The total length of the actual path covered by an object, regardless of its direction. It is denoted by 'd'. The SI unit is the metre (m).
  • Displacement: The shortest possible straight-line distance from the initial position to the final position in a particular direction. It is denoted by 's'. The SI unit is also the metre (m).
Concept

Scalar vs Vector Quantities:
• A Scalar Quantity has only magnitude and no direction (e.g., Distance, Speed, Mass, Volume).
• A Vector Quantity has both magnitude AND direction (e.g., Displacement, Velocity, Weight).

4. Uniform and Non-uniform Motion

Depending upon the distance travelled, time taken, and the direction of motion, the motion of a body can be broadly divided into uniform or non-uniform motion.

Uniform Motion

If a body covers equal distances in equal intervals of time, it is said to exhibit uniform motion.

  • Example: A motorbike moving along a straight road that covers a distance of 15 metres every second.
  • Speed: An object in uniform motion always has a constant speed.

Non-uniform Motion

If a body covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time, it is said to exhibit non-uniform motion.

  • Example: A car moving on a busy road that covers 10 metres in the first 3 seconds, 15 metres in the next 3 seconds, and 20 metres in another 3 seconds.
  • Direction change: A body is also considered to be in non-uniform motion if it covers equal distances in equal intervals of time, but its direction is not constant.
  • Speed: The speed is not constant; it keeps changing.
Concept

Average Speed: Because the speed of an object in non-uniform motion keeps changing, we calculate its average speed.

Average speed is the ratio of the total distance travelled by the object to the total time taken during the journey.
Average Speed = Total Distance Travelled ÷ Total Time Taken

Speed and Velocity

  • Speed: The distance travelled by a body in unit time. It is a scalar quantity. (Speed = Distance ÷ Time). The SI unit is m/s.
  • Velocity: Speed with a specified direction. It is defined as the displacement in unit time. It is a vector quantity. (Velocity = Displacement ÷ Time). The SI unit is also m/s.

5. Mass and Weight

In physics, mass and weight are two very different concepts:

  • Mass (m): The quantity of matter contained within a body. It is a scalar quantity, always remains constant, and can never be zero. It is measured with a beam balance, and its SI unit is the kilogram (kg).
  • Weight (W): The force with which the Earth attracts a body towards its center due to gravity. It is a vector quantity (acts downwards) and varies from place to place. It can be zero in the absence of gravity. It is measured with a spring balance, and its SI unit is the newton (N).
Important

Formula for Weight: Weight = Mass × acceleration due to gravity (W = m × g).
Another common unit for weight is the Kilogram-force (kgf).
1 kgf = 9.8 N (often rounded to 10 N for simpler calculations).

Topographical Maps

1. Introduction to Topographical Maps

Maps are fundamental tools for geographers. They act as a communication system where a cartographer (the map maker) visually represents information on a two-dimensional surface using specific symbols, effectively communicating with the map reader.

Concept A topographical map is a large-scale map that details accurate and scientific representations of the Earth's physical, socio-cultural, and political features on the same map.

Key Features Represented:

  • Relief: Mountains, valleys, contours, cliffs, and depressions.
  • Vegetation: Wooded/cleared areas, orchards, and vineyards.
  • Hydrography: Lakes, rivers, streams, and swamps.
  • Socio-Cultural: Roads, buildings, railways, administrative boundaries, and settlements.

Because these maps are drawn on true coordinates (Graticule - a network of latitudes and longitudes), the absolute and relative position of any feature can be accurately determined.

2. History of Topographical Maps

Before satellites, mapping was a tedious job primarily relying on the triangulation method. The British East India Company was heavily involved in exploring and mapping the Indian subcontinent to locate its rich resources.

Fact The Survey of India was initiated in 1767. It is the oldest scientific department of the Government of India, currently headquartered in Dehradun under the Department of Science and Technology.

3. Importance and Uses of Topographical Maps

Topographical sheets are highly versatile and widely used because of the deep level of detail they provide. They are drawn on a grid of 4° latitudes and 4° longitudes, allowing them to be joined together like a mosaic to map larger regions.

  • Military: Originally used to assist in battle planning, defensive emplacements, calculating distances, directions, and elevations.
  • Civilian & Professional: Used by hikers, campers, town planners, geologists, engineers, highway planners, and forest services.

4. Map Scale

Since maps cannot be equal in size to the Earth, distances must be reduced accurately. A scale is the ratio of the distance between two points on the map to the actual distance between the same two points on the ground.

Types of Scales:

  • Verbal or Statement Scale: Expressed as a direct statement. E.g., 1 cm = 1 km.
  • Graphical or Linear Scale: A straight line subdivided into primary and secondary divisions to represent distances on the Earth. Distances can be read directly off this graduated line.
  • Representative Fraction (R.F.) Scale: Expressed as a mathematical ratio/fraction where the numerator (map distance) is always 1. Both sides must use the same unit. E.g., 1 : 100,000.
Important As the ratio between the map distance and actual distance decreases, the detailing of information increases.

5. Measuring Distance on a Map

To accurately find the actual ground distance using a map, different methods are used depending on the shape of the route:

  • Ruler Method: Used for measuring straight lines. Place the ruler between two points and convert the map distance using the scale.
  • Twine/Thread Method: Ideal for curved roads or rivers. Lay the thread along the bends carefully, measure the thread's length on a ruler, and convert using the scale.
  • Divider Method: Take a small, regular length (e.g., 1 cm) between the divider's legs and turn it continuously along a curved path. Multiply the number of turns by the divider gap length, then convert.

6. Symbols on Topographical Maps

Symbols act as the universal language of map-reading. Standardizing these symbols ensures that maps are easily interpreted worldwide.

Colour Conventions:

  • Black: All boundaries, writings/names, railway lines, dry streams, heights/numberings, and lines of latitudes & longitudes.
  • Blue: Water features containing water (seas, rivers, lakes, wells).
  • Green: Wooded and forested areas, orchards, scrub.
  • Yellow: Cultivated agricultural land.
  • White: Uncultivated/barren land.
  • Brown: Contour lines, sand features (dunes, hills).
  • Red: Grid lines (Eastings and Northings), roads, settlements, huts, and buildings.
Fact Contours are imaginary lines joining places of the same altitude or height above sea level, primarily drawn in brown.

Grid System:

To locate a place accurately, a network of red lines called the grid system is used:

  • Northings: Horizontal lines. Their numbers increase as they move northwards.
  • Eastings: Vertical lines. Their numbers increase as they move eastwards.

WEATHERING AND SOIL FORMATION

1. Rocks: Their Types and Formation

The outer surface of the Earth is known as the crust or the lithosphere. It is a solid and rigid layer made up of a variety of rocks.

Concept

Rocks are naturally formed aggregates of minerals. They do not have a fixed chemical composition. If a rock contains a large quantity of a metal from which it can be profitably extracted, it is called an ore (e.g., Hematite for Iron, Bauxite for Aluminium).

Types of Rocks

Rocks are grouped into three main categories based on their origin:

  • Igneous Rocks: The word igneous is derived from the Latin word ignis meaning fire. They are formed by the cooling and solidification of molten magma or lava. They are hard, compact, and do not contain fossils.
    • Intrusive (Plutonic): Magma cools slowly inside the earth under great pressure, forming large grains (e.g., Granite, Gabbro).
    • Extrusive (Volcanic): Lava cools rapidly on the surface, forming fine/no crystals (e.g., Basalt).
  • Sedimentary Rocks: Eroded rock pieces (sediments) are transported by water, wind, or glaciers and deposited in layers. They are compacted and cemented by minerals like silica. They are soft, porous, prone to erosion, and uniquely contain fossils.
    • Mechanically formed: Sandstone
    • Chemically formed: Rock Salt
    • Organically formed: Limestone, Coal
  • Metamorphic Rocks: The word metamorphose means "to change". When igneous or sedimentary rocks are subjected to immense heat and pressure, they completely change their composition and form.
    • Examples of transformations: Granite changes to Gneiss, Sandstone to Quartzite, Limestone to Marble, Coal to Graphite.

2. Weathering and Its Types

The Earth's surface undergoes a remodeling process known as Gradation. This includes degradation (erosion/wearing away) and aggradation (deposition). The process that makes this easier is called weathering.

Concept

Weathering is the continuous process through which rocks are slowly broken down over time. It involves both the disintegration (physical breakdown) and decomposition (chemical decay) of rocks.

Types of Weathering

  • Physical/Mechanical Weathering: The disintegration of rocks into finer particles without any chemical change. Highly effective in extreme cold or hot/dry places.
    • Exfoliation: Rocks peel off in layers due to continuous expansion (day) and contraction (night).
    • Granular Disintegration: Mineral grains within the rock expand and contract differently, loosening and crumbling the rock.
    • Block Disintegration: Expansion and contraction cause wide cracks, dismantling rocks into large blocks.
    • Frost Action (Freeze & Thaw): Water seeps into cracks, freezes at night (expanding), and exerts immense stress on the rock, eventually splitting it.
  • Chemical Weathering: The decay of rocks causing a change in their chemical composition, mostly driven by rainwater and atmospheric gases.
    • Oxidation: Atmospheric oxygen reacts with minerals (like iron), forming rust and weakening the rock.
    • Carbonation: Rainwater dissolves carbon dioxide to form weak carbonic acid, which dissolves minerals like limestone and marble.
    • Hydration: Minerals absorb water and change structure (e.g., Feldspar turns into Kaolin clay).
    • Solution: Soluble minerals directly dissolve in rainwater and wash away, loosening the insoluble parts.
  • Biological Weathering: Brought about by living organisms. Plant roots exert pressure, burrowing animals (earthworms, moles) loosen the soil, and human activities (mining, quarrying, deforestation) break up rocks.
Fact

Karst Topography: In regions composed of carbonate rocks like limestone or dolomite, physical and chemical weathering dissolve the bedrock, creating a unique landscape full of large cave formations!

3. Soil and Factors Affecting its Formation

Soil is the uppermost layer of the Earth's crust, consisting of mineral particles, organic matter, gases, liquids, and living organisms. It is formed through the slow weathering of rocks.

Five Major Factors of Soil Formation:

  1. Climate: The most important factor. It determines the rate of weathering and affects the type of vegetation in the area.
  2. Living Organisms: Roots hold the soil together, and dead leaves, insects, and animal droppings add humus (decaying organic matter).
  3. Nature of the Bedrock (Parent Rock): Determines the mineral content and chemical composition of the soil.
  4. Relief: Altitude and the slope of the land determine how well the soil drains and accumulates.
  5. Time: It takes thousands of years to form thick soil. Time determines the maturity and thickness of the soil layer.
Important

Sudden movement of disintegrated and weathered rock debris down a slope is called a landslide. When this rock waste moves with flood water, it is known as mass wasting.

4. Soil Profile

A soil profile is a vertical section showing the successive distinct layers (horizons) of the soil from the surface down to the bedrock.

  • Top Soil (O & A Horizons): The uppermost layer consisting of fine particles and rich organic matter known as humus.
  • Subsoil (B Horizon): Located below the top soil. It is more compact and contains materials that have seeped down with water from the top layer.
  • Weathered Rock (C Horizon): A zone of partly weathered rock chunks that contains no humus.
  • Bedrock (R Horizon): The lowest layer of solid, unweathered parent rock which provides the basic mineral content.

5. Soil Conservation

Soil conservation is the prevention of soil loss from erosion or reduced fertility caused by over-cultivation, weathering, and deforestation.

Important Conservation Methods:

  • Afforestation: Planting more trees; tree roots firmly bind the soil to prevent erosion.
  • Terraced Farming: Cutting steps into hill slopes to slow down the flow of water and retain nutrients.
  • Contour Ploughing: Ploughing along the contours of the land to check soil erosion.
  • Crop Rotation: Growing different crops sequentially to maintain soil fertility year-round.
  • Shelter Belts: Planting rows of trees to reduce wind speed, preventing wind erosion.
  • Checking Overgrazing: Providing separate grazing grounds to stop animals from stripping the land bare.
  • Banning Shifting Agriculture (Slash and Burn): Stopping wasteful agricultural methods that destroy forest cover.
  • Mulching & Cover Crops: Covering the surface with leaves, straw, or cover crops to minimize evaporation and protect soil from heavy rain.

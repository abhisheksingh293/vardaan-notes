Heat

1. Heat as Energy

Heat is a form of energy (thermal energy) that is transferred from one body to another due to the existence of a temperature difference between them.

  • Transfer of heat energy always takes place from a body at a higher temperature to a body at a lower temperature.
  • Heat can be converted into other forms of energy. For example, steam (formed by heating water) can lift the lid of a vessel, showing that heat can do mechanical work.
Concept

Heat vs. Temperature

Heat is the total energy of a body due to the motion of its constituent particles (molecules). Temperature is simply an indicator of this energy; it expresses the degree of hotness or coldness of a body. When we add heat to a body, the motion of its molecules becomes faster and its temperature rises.

Fact

In early days, heat was incorrectly considered a weightless and colourless fluid called 'caloric'. Experiments by physicists like Davy, Count Rumford, and Joule proved that heat is actually a form of energy.

2. Effects of Heat

When a body is heated, various types of physical and chemical changes are observed.

A. Physical Changes

  • Change in temperature: Addition of heat raises the temperature; removal of heat lowers it.
  • Change of state: Heat can change matter from one state to another (e.g., solid ice melts into liquid water, and liquid water boils into gaseous steam).
  • Thermal expansion: Most solids, liquids, and gases expand on heating. For example, a metal rim is made slightly smaller than a wooden cart wheel; it is heated to expand and slip onto the wheel, and upon cooling, it contracts and grips the wheel firmly.

B. Chemical Changes

Many chemical changes take place only when the reactants are heated. For example, preparing oxygen in the laboratory requires heating potassium chlorate.

3. Measurement of Temperature

Our sense of touch is unreliable for measuring temperature. Therefore, we use a special instrument called a thermometer.

The Mercury Thermometer

The simplest thermometer is the mercury-in-glass thermometer, which works on the principle of the thermal expansion of mercury. Mercury is used because it expands uniformly, remains liquid over a wide temperature range, does not stick to glass, and is a shining silvery liquid that is easy to see.

Reading a Thermometer

Temperature scales are calibrated using two fixed reference points (lowest and highest temperatures). The two most common scales are the Celsius (°C) scale and the Fahrenheit (°F) scale. The relationship between them is: T(°C) = [T(°F) - 32] × 5/9.

  • Normal Human Body Temperature: 36.9°C (or 98.4°F)
  • Boiling Point of Water: 100°C (or 212°F)
  • Freezing Point of Water: 0°C (or 32°F)
Important

Clinical vs. Laboratory Thermometers

Clinical Thermometer: Used to measure human body temperature. Its range is strictly from 35°C to 42°C. It has a slight bend or kink (constriction) in its capillary tube to prevent the mercury thread from falling back on its own, allowing us to read it conveniently.

Laboratory Thermometer: Used in labs for general purposes. Its range is typically from -10°C to 110°C. It does not have a kink.

4. Transfer of Heat

Heat transfer occurs through three different methods: Conduction, Convection, and Radiation.

A. Conduction

This is the primary method of heat transfer in solids. Heat is transferred from one particle to the next through vibration, without the particles leaving their places.

  • Conductors: Materials that allow heat to go through them easily (e.g., metals like copper, aluminium).
  • Insulators (Bad Conductors): Materials that do not allow heat to pass through them easily (e.g., wood, plastic, air, water, wool, ebonite).

Applications: We use metals for cooking utensils because they conduct heat well. We use plastic/wooden handles, woollen clothes, and double-walled ice boxes because they trap air (an insulator) to prevent heat transfer.

B. Convection

This method of heat transfer occurs in liquids and gases. Heat is transferred by the actual movement of the molecules themselves. These cyclic movements are called convection currents.

Applications: Ventilators, chimneys, and room heaters all rely on convection currents. Global wind patterns are caused by uneven heating of the earth setting up massive convection currents.

Concept

Land and Sea Breezes

  • Sea Breeze (Day Time): Land heats up faster than the sea. The warm air over land rises, and the cooler air from the sea rushes in to take its place.
  • Land Breeze (Night Time): Land cools down faster than the sea. The warmer air over the sea rises, and the cooler air from the land rushes towards the sea.

C. Radiation

Heat transfer by radiation does not require any material medium. It can travel through empty space (vacuum). The heat from the sun reaches the earth through radiation.

  • Heat energy received through radiation is called radiant energy.
  • Dark/Black objects are very good absorbers and radiators of heat.
  • Light/White/Shiny objects reflect most of the heat and are poor absorbers.

Applications: We wear light-coloured clothes in summer to absorb less heat. The bottom of cooking pans is often painted black to absorb heat better. Firemen wear shiny brass caps to reflect heat.

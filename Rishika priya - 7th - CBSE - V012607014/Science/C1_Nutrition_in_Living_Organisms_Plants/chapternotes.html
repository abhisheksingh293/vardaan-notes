Nutrition in Living Organisms—Plants

1. Introduction to Nutrition

Food is essential for all living organisms. The components of food such as carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, and minerals are necessary for our body and are called nutrients.

These nutrients enable living organisms to build their bodies, grow, repair damaged parts, and provide energy to carry out life processes.

Concept Nutrition is defined as the process of obtaining, and utilising, food by an organism.

2. Modes of Nutrition

On the basis of food habits, the modes of obtaining nutrition have been divided into two major categories:

A. Autotrophic Nutrition

It is the mode of nutrition in which organisms can make their own food from simple raw materials. All green plants and some bacteria exhibit this mode and are known as autotrophs. (In Greek, auto = self, trophe = nutrition).

B. Heterotrophic Nutrition

It is the mode of nutrition in which organisms cannot prepare their food on their own and depend on others for it. All animals, humans, and a few non-green plants are heterotrophs. (In Greek, heterone = other).

Fact Euglena is a unique organism that shows both autotrophic and heterotrophic modes of nutrition because it has both plant and animal-like features.

3. Photosynthesis: Food Making Process in Plants

The synthesis of food in plants occurs mostly in their leaves. Because of this, leaves are called the food factories of the plants.

The process by which plants synthesise their food using carbon dioxide and water, in the presence of sunlight and chlorophyll, is called photosynthesis (photo = light, synthesis = to combine).

Important Equation of Photosynthesis:
Carbon dioxide + Water → (in the presence of Sunlight & Chlorophyll) → Carbohydrate (glucose) + Oxygen

Raw Materials Required for Photosynthesis

  • Sunlight: The light and energy that comes from the Sun. It is the ultimate source of energy for all living organisms.
  • Chlorophyll: The green pigment present in the leaves. It helps the leaves to capture solar energy.
  • Water and Minerals: Absorbed by the roots from the soil and transported to the leaves through pipe-like channels called vessels.
  • Carbon dioxide: Taken from the air. It enters the leaves through tiny pores present on the surface called stomata. These stomata are surrounded by special cells called guard cells.

Products of Photosynthesis

The initial product of photosynthesis is a carbohydrate (glucose), which is subsequently converted into starch. Carbohydrates contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen.

Plants also synthesise proteins and fats, which contain nitrogen. Plants absorb usable nitrogenous compounds from the soil, which are converted from gaseous nitrogen by certain soil bacteria.

4. Other Modes of Nutrition in Plants

Some plants do not contain chlorophyll and cannot synthesise their own food. They use a heterotrophic mode of nutrition. These are sub-divided into:

A. Parasitic Nutrition

Plants that derive nutrition from the body of other living organisms (the host) and cause harm to them are called parasites. An example is Cuscuta (amarbel or dodder), which is a yellowish thread-like structure without leaves.

B. Insectivorous Plants

These are plants that feed on insects to meet their nitrogen requirements. For example, in the Pitcher plant, the leaf gets modified to form a pitcher with a lid that traps insects inside where they get digested by juices.

C. Saprotrophic Nutrition

In this mode, organisms feed on dead and decaying matter. They secrete digestive juices directly onto the food to make it soluble before absorbing it (a process called extracellular digestion). Examples include Fungi (like bread mould, mushrooms, yeast) and many bacteria.

5. Symbiotic Relationship

Sometimes two organisms live in close association and develop a relationship that is beneficial to both. This is called a symbiotic relationship.

  • Lichens: A living partnership between a fungus and an alga. The fungus absorbs water and provides shelter, while the alga prepares food by photosynthesis.
  • Rhizobium: A bacterium that lives in the roots of leguminous plants. It converts nitrogen from the atmosphere into a usable form for the plant. In turn, the plant provides food and shelter to the bacterium.

6. Replenishment of Nutrients in the Soil

Plants continuously remove nutrients from the soil as they grow. To ensure the soil remains productive, nutrients must be replenished. This is done by:

  • The decomposition of dead parts of plants and animals.
  • Adding manures and fertilisers to the soil.
  • The action of nitrogen-fixing bacteria like Rhizobium.

Acids, Bases and Salts

1. Introduction

There are wide varieties of materials around us that differ in color, odor, taste, and physical state. We often classify these substances into categories based on their properties.

  • Acids: Materials like lemon, vinegar, and tamarind taste sour. This sour taste is due to the presence of chemicals called acids. The term "acid" is derived from the Latin word 'acidus', which means 'sour'.
  • Bases: Materials like baking soda and soap have a bitter taste and a soapy feel. This is due to the presence of chemicals called bases.
Important

Caution: It is highly dangerous to taste or touch any unknown substance, either at home or in the school laboratory.

2. Classification of Acids

A. Mineral Acids

Acids prepared from the minerals present in the earth are called mineral acids. They are generally strong acids. Examples include:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl)
  • Sulphuric acid (H2SO4)
  • Nitric acid (HNO3)
  • Phosphoric acid (H3PO4)
Fact

Sulphuric acid (H2SO4) is often known as the 'King of Chemicals' because it is widely used in the manufacture of various materials.

B. Organic Acids

Acids which naturally occur and are found in plants and animals are called organic acids. They are generally weak acids. Examples include:

  • Citric acid: Found in orange, lemon, and amla.
  • Tartaric acid: Found in grapes, tamarind, and gooseberries.
  • Lactic acid: Found in sour milk.
  • Acetic acid: Found in vinegar (CH3COOH).
  • Formic acid: Found in the sting of bees, red ants, wasps, and stinging nettle (HCOOH). It causes a shooting pain.
Important

Dilution of Acids: Acids manufactured in factories are highly concentrated (low water content). To make them dilute acids, we must add water. However, we must always dilute concentrated acids by adding acid to water slowly, and never by adding water directly to the acid.

3. Bases and Alkalies

Bases are substances which react with acids to form water and salt. They are bitter in taste and have a soapy feel.

Alkalies

Bases that are soluble in water are called alkalies. Therefore, all alkalies are bases, but all bases are not alkalies.

  • Strong Bases: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) and Potassium hydroxide (KOH).
  • Weak Bases: Ammonium hydroxide (NH4OH), Aluminium hydroxide (Al(OH)3), and Copper hydroxide (Cu(OH)2).

4. Indicators

Because it is dangerous to taste chemicals, we use indicators. An indicator is a substance which shows different colours in an acidic and a basic medium.

  • Litmus: Obtained from lichens (naturally purple). Turns red in an acidic medium and blue in a basic medium. Often used as paper strips.
  • Turmeric: Remains yellow in acidic medium and turns red in a basic medium (e.g., soap rubbing on a turmeric stain).
  • China Rose (Hibiscus): Turns dark pink (magenta) when added to an acid and turns green when added to a base.
  • Phenolphthalein: Colourless in an acidic medium and turns pink in a basic medium.
Concept

Acid Rain

Gases like carbon dioxide, sulphur dioxide, and nitrogen dioxide are released by factories and vehicles. These mix with rainwater to form acids, creating acid rain. Acid rain damages buildings made of marble (calcium carbonate), such as the yellowing of the Taj Mahal.

5. Neutralisation and Formation of Salts

A chemical reaction between an acid and a base is known as a neutralisation reaction. During neutralisation, the acid and the base 'cancel' the effect of each other, resulting in the formation of salt and water.

Examples of Neutralisation Reactions:

  • NaOH (Base) + HCl (Acid) → NaCl (Sodium chloride) + H2O
  • 2KOH (Base) + H2SO4 (Acid) → K2SO4 (Potassium sulphate) + 2H2O
  • Mg(OH)2 (Base) + 2HNO3 (Acid) → Mg(NO3)2 (Magnesium nitrate) + 2H2O

6. Salts

Salts are the products formed from a neutralisation reaction.

Properties of Salts

  1. Most salts are readily soluble in water.
  2. Salts do not conduct electricity in their solid state. However, molten salts and solutions of salts in water conduct electricity well.

Naming of Salts

The salts of different acids are named according to the acid used:

  • Salts of Sulphuric acid are sulphates (e.g., Calcium sulphate CaSO4).
  • Salts of Hydrochloric acid are chlorides (e.g., Sodium chloride NaCl).
  • Salts of Nitric acid are nitrates (e.g., Sodium nitrate NaNO3).
  • Salts of Sulphurous acid are sulphites (e.g., Sodium sulphite Na2SO3).
  • Salts of Carbonic acid are carbonates (e.g., Sodium carbonate Na2CO3).
  • Salts of Acetic acid are acetates (e.g., Sodium acetate CH3COONa).

Classification of Salts

  • Neutral Salts: Formed by the reaction of a strong acid with a strong base. Its solution in water is neutral and does not change litmus paper colour. Example: Sodium chloride (NaCl), KCl, KNO3.
  • Acidic Salts: Formed by the reaction of a strong acid and a weak base. Its aqueous solution is acidic and turns blue litmus red. Example: Aluminium chloride (AlCl3), NH4NO3.
  • Basic Salts: Formed by the reaction of a weak acid and a strong base. Its aqueous solution is basic and turns red litmus blue. Example: Sodium acetate (CH3COONa).

1) Introduction to Chemistry

Meaning of chemistry

Chemistry is the branch of science that studies the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter. It explains why substances behave the way they do and how one substance can be transformed into another.

Why this chapter matters

Before solving chemical reactions, you must know how to count atoms, measure matter, and convert between different representations such as mass, moles, molecules, and concentration.

Board focus: definitions, unit conversions, basic formulae, and direct numerical problems.
JEE focus: mole relation, stoichiometry, limiting reagent, concentration calculations, and multi-step conversions.

2) Measurement in Chemistry

Chemistry depends on measurement. A correct answer must use correct units, correct precision, and correct conversion factors.

SI units used often

  • Mass: kilogram (kg), gram (g)
  • Amount of substance: mole (mol)
  • Length: metre (m)
  • Temperature: kelvin (K)
  • Volume: cubic metre (m³), litre (L), millilitre (mL)

Common relations

  • \(1\,\text{L} = 10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3\)
  • \(1\,\text{mL} = 1\,\text{cm}^3\)
  • \(1\,\text{amu} = 1\,\text{u}\)
  • \(1\,\text{mol} = 6.022\times10^{23}\) entities

Counting in chemistry

Atoms and molecules are too small to count one by one. So chemists use the mole as a counting unit, just like a dozen represents 12 objects.

Avogadro constant: \(N_A = 6.022\times10^{23}\,\text{mol}^{-1}\)
Number of particles: \(N = n\,N_A\)
Amount of substance: \(n = \dfrac{\text{given mass}}{\text{molar mass}}\)
Important: The mole links microscopic particles to macroscopic mass. Almost every numerical problem in this chapter begins with mole conversion.

3) Laws of Chemical Combination

These laws were established experimentally. They are the foundation of stoichiometry and atomic theory.

Law of conservation of mass

Mass is neither created nor destroyed during a chemical reaction.

\(\text{Mass of reactants} = \text{Mass of products}\)

Law of definite proportions

A pure compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed proportion by mass.

Law of multiple proportions

When two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole-number ratios.

Gay-Lussac’s law of gaseous volumes

At the same temperature and pressure, volumes of reacting gases and gaseous products are in simple whole-number ratios.

Board focus: memorise the statement of each law and one simple example.
JEE focus: relate these laws to balancing, formula determination, and gas volume stoichiometry.

4) Dalton’s Atomic Theory

Dalton proposed that matter is made of tiny indivisible particles called atoms. This idea explained the laws of chemical combination, but later experiments showed that atoms are divisible into subatomic particles.

Main postulates

  • Matter consists of extremely small particles called atoms.
  • Atoms of the same element are similar in mass and properties.
  • Atoms of different elements differ in properties and mass.
  • Atoms combine in fixed, simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.
  • Atoms are neither created nor destroyed in chemical reactions.

Limitations

  • Atoms are divisible into electrons, protons, and neutrons.
  • Atoms of the same element can have different masses (isotopes).
  • Atoms of different elements can have the same mass number (isobars).
  • Atoms are not always indivisible in nuclear reactions.

5) Mole Concept, Atomic Mass, Molecular Mass, and Molar Mass

This is the most important calculation block in the chapter. Master these definitions and formulas.

Atomic mass

The average mass of an atom of an element relative to \(\tfrac{1}{12}\)th the mass of one carbon-12 atom.

Molecular mass

The sum of atomic masses of all atoms present in one molecule of a substance.

Formula mass

Used for ionic compounds. It is the sum of atomic masses in the formula unit.

Molar mass

Mass of one mole of a substance. Its numerical value is equal to atomic/molecular/formula mass in grams per mole.

\(n = \dfrac{m}{M}\)
\(M = \dfrac{m}{n}\)
\(N = nN_A\)
\(n = \dfrac{N}{N_A}\)

Examples of molar mass

\(M(\text{H}_2\text{O}) = 2(1) + 16 = 18\,\text{g mol}^{-1}\)

\(M(\text{CO}_2) = 12 + 2(16) = 44\,\text{g mol}^{-1}\)

Number of particles

1 mol of any substance contains the same number of particles.

This is true whether the particles are atoms, molecules, ions, or formula units.

Common conversions

Mass \(\leftrightarrow\) moles \(\leftrightarrow\) particles

Volume of gas at STP is often taken as \(22.4\,\text{L mol}^{-1}\) in school-level problems, but use the exact condition given in the question.

Exam trap: Do not mix up atomic mass unit (u), gram, and gram per mole. Atomic mass is a relative number; molar mass is a measurable mass per mole.

6) Percentage Composition, Empirical Formula, and Molecular Formula

These topics help determine the composition of a compound from experimental data.

Percentage composition

\[\%\,\text{of element} = \frac{\text{mass of element in 1 mol of compound}}{\text{molar mass of compound}} \times 100\]

Empirical formula

The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element present in a compound.

Molecular formula

The actual number of atoms of each element present in a molecule.

Relation

\(\text{Molecular formula} = (\text{Empirical formula})_n\)
\(n = \dfrac{\text{molar mass}}{\text{empirical formula mass}}\)
Board focus: learn the steps for empirical formula from percentage composition.
JEE focus: be comfortable converting percentage data into empirical formula under time pressure.

7) Stoichiometry and Limiting Reagent

Stoichiometry deals with the quantitative relationship among reactants and products in a balanced chemical equation.

How to solve stoichiometry problems

  • Write and balance the equation.
  • Convert the given quantity into moles.
  • Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation.
  • Convert the required quantity back into mass, volume, or particles.

Limiting reagent

The reactant that is completely consumed first in a reaction and therefore limits the amount of product formed.

\(\text{Moles of product} = \text{Moles of reactant} \times \frac{\text{coefficient of product}}{\text{coefficient of reactant}}\)

Excess reagent

The reactant that remains after the limiting reagent is fully used up.

Percent yield

\[\%\,\text{yield} = \frac{\text{actual yield}}{\text{theoretical yield}} \times 100\]
JEE alert: In mixed-reagent problems, always check the limiting reagent first before calculating final product mass.

8) Concentration Terms of Solutions

A solution’s concentration describes how much solute is present in a given amount of solution or solvent.

Term Formula Unit Use
Mass percentage \(\dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} \times 100\) % (w/w) Useful for solid-liquid systems
Volume percentage \(\dfrac{\text{volume of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100\) % (v/v) Useful for liquid-liquid solutions
Mass by volume percentage \(\dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{volume of solution}} \times 100\) % (w/v) Common in laboratories and medicine
Parts per million \(\dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}} \times 10^6\) ppm Used for very dilute solutions
Mole fraction \(\chi_i = \dfrac{n_i}{\sum n_i}\) No unit Useful in gas mixtures and colligative properties
Molarity \(M = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution in L}}\) mol L\(^{-1}\) Temperature dependent
Molality \(m = \dfrac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}}\) mol kg\(^{-1}\) Temperature independent
Board focus: especially remember the difference between molarity and molality.
JEE focus: mole fraction and molarity-molality conversion appear repeatedly in higher-level questions.

9) Significant Figures

Significant figures indicate the precision of a measured value. They are important in numerical answers and in rounding final results.

Rules

  • All non-zero digits are significant.
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant.
  • Leading zeros are not significant.
  • Trailing zeros are significant only if a decimal point is present.

Operations

  • In addition/subtraction, match decimal places.
  • In multiplication/division, match the least number of significant figures.
Very important: The final answer should not be over-precise beyond the precision of the given data.

10) Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is the method of using units to check and convert quantities. It is one of the safest methods in chemistry calculations.

Uses

  • Conversion of units
  • Checking whether an equation is dimensionally correct
  • Deriving conversion factors
  • Solving multi-step numerical problems

Idea

Multiply by conversion fractions so that unwanted units cancel out and only the required unit remains.

Example: \(\text{g} \to \text{mol} \to \text{particles}\)
\[\frac{\text{given quantity}}{1} \times \frac{\text{desired unit}}{\text{present unit}}\]

11) Exam-Oriented Solved Patterns

Pattern 1: Mass to moles

Question type: How many moles are present in 36 g of water?

Solution: \(M(\text{H}_2\text{O}) = 18\,\text{g mol}^{-1}\)

\(n = \dfrac{36}{18} = 2\,\text{mol}\)

Pattern 2: Moles to particles

Question type: Number of molecules in 0.5 mol of oxygen?

\(N = nN_A = 0.5 \times 6.022\times10^{23} = 3.011\times10^{23}\)

Pattern 3: Empirical formula

Convert % composition into mole ratio, divide by the smallest number, and then convert into the simplest whole-number ratio.

Pattern 4: Limiting reagent

Convert both reactants into moles, compare with stoichiometric coefficients, identify the reactant that finishes first, and calculate product from it.

Board style questions: direct conversions, percentage composition, and standard formula-based numericals.
JEE style questions: mixed stoichiometry, gaseous volume ratios, limiting reagent, and concentration-based calculation chains.

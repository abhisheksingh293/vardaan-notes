Life Processes in Plants

1. Introduction to Plant Growth

Plants, like animals, are living organisms. They share common characteristics of all living things: they grow, need food, respire, and respond to changes in their surroundings. However, plants do not "eat" food like animals; instead, they require certain factors—sunlight, water, and minerals from the soil—to prepare their own food and grow properly.

2. Photosynthesis: The 'Food Factory'

Leaves are considered the "food factories" of the plant because all green parts of a plant, primarily the leaves, perform the vital function of preparing food.

Concept

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants synthesise their food (in the form of glucose) using carbon dioxide, water, and minerals in the presence of sunlight.

The Equation of Photosynthesis

Plants use solar energy to convert raw materials into food. The process releases oxygen as a vital by-product:

  • Equation: Carbon dioxide + Water + Sunlight → Glucose + Oxygen
  • The produced glucose is often stored in various plant parts in the form of starch. The presence of starch in a leaf is the primary test to verify that photosynthesis has occurred.

Conditions Essential for Photosynthesis

  • Sunlight: The ultimate source of energy. Without solar energy, the process cannot take place.
  • Chlorophyll: A green-coloured pigment present in the chloroplasts of leaves. This pigment is responsible for trapping the solar energy needed for the reaction.
  • Carbon Dioxide (CO2): Plants take in CO2 from the surrounding air through their leaves.
  • Water and Minerals: Absorbed by the plant's roots (specifically by the root hair) from the soil and transported up to the leaves.
Fact

Stomata (singular: stoma) are tiny openings or pores present mostly on the surface of leaves. They are guarded by kidney-shaped guard cells. These pores are responsible for the vital exchange of gases—allowing carbon dioxide in and letting oxygen out.

3. Transportation in Plants

For a plant to survive and perform photosynthesis, water must be transported from the roots to the leaves, and the prepared food must be moved from the leaves to all other parts of the plant. This movement occurs through a well-developed network known as the vascular system.

The Vascular System

The vascular system consists of two highly specialized, tube-like tissues:

  • Xylem: A tissue that carries water and dissolved minerals upwards from the roots to the stem, branches, and leaves.
  • Phloem: A tissue made of cells arranged in long tubes that transports the synthesized food (glucose) from the leaves downwards and outwards to other parts of the plant.
Important

The specific process of transporting prepared food through the phloem to different parts of the plant is scientifically known as translocation.

4. Respiration in Plants

Once food is transported to different parts of the plant, it must be converted into usable energy. This happens through the process of respiration.

  • During respiration, food (glucose) is broken down in the presence of oxygen to release energy.
  • Equation: Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy
  • The released energy is utilized for various life processes, including growth and development.
  • Note: Unlike photosynthesis, which only occurs in green parts containing chlorophyll, respiration takes place in all parts of the plant, whether they are green or non-green.

Transport in Plants

1. Introduction to Transportation in Plants

Plants, just like animals, require a systematic mechanism to transport essential life-sustaining substances from one part of their body to another. The primary substances that need transport are:

  • Water and Minerals: Absorbed from the soil through the root system and transported upwards to the aerial parts (stem and leaves).
  • Food (Glucose): Synthesized in the leaves through the process of photosynthesis and distributed downwards and upwards to all living cells, including roots.

The process by which substances absorbed or synthesized in one part of the plant are moved to other parts of the plant is known as Transportation.

2. The Conducting Tissues (Vascular Bundle)

Transportation in higher plants is carried out by a specialized conducting system consisting of two main tissues: Xylem and Phloem. Together, they constitute the Vascular Bundle.

2.1 Xylem (The Water Carrier)

The primary function of the xylem is to create a tubular passage to transport water and dissolved mineral salts from the roots to the aerial parts of the plant. It flows in a unidirectional manner (only upwards).

The xylem tissue consists of four types of cells:

  • Xylem Tracheids: Elongated, dead cells with tapering ends. Their thick walls have lateral pores. They conduct water upwards and provide mechanical strength.
  • Xylem Vessels: Long, tube-like structures open at both ends and placed one above the other to form a continuous channel. They also provide mechanical support.
  • Xylem Parenchyma: Small, thick-walled living cells. They store food and assist in conducting water. This is the only living part of the xylem.
  • Xylem Sclerenchyma (Wood Fibres): Dead, thick-walled, long, and narrow cells with tapering ends. They exclusively provide mechanical support.
Concept Ascent of Sap is the upward movement of water and dissolved minerals (called plant sap) from the roots to the topmost leaves through the xylem vessels.

2.2 Phloem (The Food Carrier)

Phloem transports food (manufactured by the leaves in the form of sucrose/glucose) to all other parts of the plant, including the roots. This process is called the translocation of solutes. The transport here is bidirectional (moves both upwards and downwards).

The phloem tissue is made up of four types of cells:

  • Sieve Tubes: Cylindrical living cells that lack a nucleus. Arranged vertically end-to-end with perforated end walls called sieve plates. Food material passes through these pores.
  • Companion Cells: Living, thin-walled elongated cells attached directly to the sides of sieve tubes. They assist the sieve tubes in food conduction.
  • Phloem Parenchyma: Thin-walled living cells responsible for the storage of food.
  • Phloem Fibres (Sclerenchyma): Dead, elongated fibres that provide mechanical strength to the plant.
Important Differences to Remember: Xylem conducts water (unidirectional) and is mostly made of dead cells. Phloem translocates food (bidirectional), requires energy expenditure, and is mostly made of living cells.

3. Absorption of Water by Roots

The root system anchors the plant and absorbs water/minerals. The main root gives off lateral branches, which bear millions of fine, microscopic outgrowths called root hairs.

3.1 Special Features of Root Hairs

Root hairs are perfectly adapted for maximal water absorption due to three specific features:

  • Large Surface Area: The massive number of root hairs creates an enormous surface area. Greater surface area equals greater absorption.
  • Concentration Gradient: The root hairs contain cell sap which has a higher concentration of solutes (salts) than the surrounding soil water. This draws water inward.
  • Semi-permeable Membrane: The outer cell wall is freely permeable (allows everything through), but the inner cell membrane is semi-permeable. It strictly allows small water molecules to pass while blocking larger molecules, facilitating osmosis.

4. Movement of Molecules in Plants

Molecules move inside a plant system via three primary mechanisms:

4.1 Diffusion

The free movement of molecules of a gas, liquid, or solid from a region of their higher concentration to a region of their lower concentration until they are uniformly distributed. Example: Soil water and minerals moving into the root hair, or the exchange of O₂ and CO₂ during photosynthesis.

4.2 Osmosis

The precise movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration (dilute solution) to a region of lower water concentration (concentrated solution) entirely across a semi-permeable membrane.

Concept When a plant absorbs water from the soil, it does so primarily through osmosis because the soil holds a higher concentration of water compared to the concentrated cell sap inside the root hair.

4.3 Active Transport

While water enters via osmosis, minerals are often already more concentrated inside the root than in the soil. Moving these minerals from a lower concentration (soil) to a higher concentration (inside the root) forces the plant to move against the concentration gradient. This requires the expenditure of cellular energy (ATP) and is termed Active Transport.

Fact Root Pressure is the hydrostatic pressure developed within the roots due to the continuous osmotic inflow of water. This pressure actively pushes the plant sap upward into the xylem vessels.

5. Transpiration

Plants absorb immense amounts of water, but only a tiny fraction (about 1-2%) is utilized for photosynthesis and retaining cellular shape. The massive remaining bulk must be eliminated.

Transpiration is defined as the loss of water in the form of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant (primarily through the microscopic stomata present on the leaves).

5.1 Factors Affecting the Rate of Transpiration

  • Sunlight: Speeds up transpiration. Stomata open wider during the day to allow CO₂ in for photosynthesis, incidentally letting more water vapour escape.
  • Temperature: Higher temperatures on hot summer days increase the rate of evaporation, thereby increasing transpiration.
  • Wind: Faster wind speeds blow away the moisture-laden air around the leaf, allowing faster evaporation and thus faster transpiration.
  • Humidity: Decreases transpiration. If the surrounding air is already fully saturated (humid) with moisture, it cannot accept more water vapour from the plant.

5.2 Importance of Transpiration

  • Cooling Effect: Just as sweating cools animals, the evaporation of water from the leaves utilizes the plant's own latent heat, naturally cooling the plant during hot weather.
  • Maintains Sap Concentration: By eliminating excess water, transpiration prevents the plant's internal sap from becoming too dilute, ensuring continued absorption of fresh minerals from the soil.
  • Transpirational Pull: As water evaporates from the leaves, it creates a powerful suction force in the xylem vessels. This "pull" literally drags water upward from the roots to the top of tall trees (sometimes over 100 meters high!).

6. Importance of Minerals (Nutrients)

Plants require specific nutrient elements from the soil to grow well and complete their life cycle. These are broadly categorized into two groups:

6.1 Macro-nutrients (Required in large amounts)

  • Nitrogen (N): Major constituent of all proteins. Deficiency causes yellowing of leaves and wrinkling of cereal grains.
  • Phosphorus (P): Constituent of the cell membrane and certain proteins. Deficiency causes purple/red spots on leaves and delays seed germination.
  • Potassium (K): Abundant in growing tissues and controls the opening and closing of stomata. Deficiency results in poor growth and a reduced rate of transpiration.

6.2 Micro-nutrients (Required in trace amounts)

  • Iron (Fe): Constituent of some proteins. Deficiency leads to the severe yellowing of leaves.
  • Manganese (Mn): Constituent of essential enzymes. Deficiency causes yellowing of leaves accompanied by grey spots.
  • Zinc (Zn): Activates enzymes and forms plant hormones. Deficiency causes stunted plant growth, deshaped leaves, and yellowing.

EXPANSION OF THE BRITISH RULE

1. Arrival of European Trading Companies

In the 16th and 17th centuries, several Western European powers—the Portuguese, Dutch, French, and British—came to India. Their primary goal was to capture overseas territories to establish profitable markets for trade and revenue.

  • The Portuguese: Following the sea route discovered by Vasco da Gama, they arrived on the Malabar Coast. Under Alfonso de Albuquerque, they conquered Diu in 1509 CE and Goa in 1510 CE. However, their influence soon waned.
  • The Dutch: Traders from the Netherlands who set up centers in Surat, Broach, and Masulipatnam. Their main focus remained on the Indonesian Spice Islands.
  • The French: The French East India Company set up early settlements at Pondicherry, Chandernagore, and Mahe. Governor Dupleix's ambition to create a French Empire in India led to direct conflict with the British.
  • The British: The English East India Company (EEIC) was formed in 1600. They set up their first factory at Surat in 1612. Sir Thomas Roe visited Emperor Jahangir's court to obtain trade concessions. By building commercial ties with Indian merchants, they amassed immense wealth.

2. Anglo-French Rivalry (The Carnatic Wars)

In the 18th century, intense rivalry between France and Britain in India led to a series of three wars known as the Carnatic Wars.

Fact
  • First Carnatic War (1744-1748): Began when French forces captured Madras. It was returned to the British after a European peace treaty.
  • Second Carnatic War (1748-1754): Fought over succession disputes in Hyderabad and the Carnatic. The French were defeated, and Governor Dupleix was recalled.
  • Third Carnatic War (1756-1763): The British conclusively defeated the French at the Battle of Wandiwash in 1760. French political power in India was effectively destroyed.

3. Conquest of Bengal

Bengal was a wealthy province, and the English wanted to control its trade. Nawab Siraj-ud-Daulah opposed the British activities and attacked Fort William in Calcutta in 1756.

The Battle of Plassey (1757)

On 23 June 1757, the forces of Siraj-ud-Daulah clashed with the British army led by Robert Clive at Plassey. The British were secretly supported by Mir Jafar, the Nawab's commander-in-chief. Due to this betrayal, Siraj was defeated and killed. Mir Jafar was made a puppet Nawab, and the British began exploiting Bengal's economy.

The Battle of Buxar (1764)

When Mir Qasim (who replaced Mir Jafar) rejected British dominance, he allied with Shuja-ud-Daulah (Nawab of Awadh) and Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor). This combined force was heavily defeated by the British at Buxar. As a result, the Company gained Diwani rights (the right to collect revenue) over Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa.

Concept

The Dual Government (1765)

After acquiring Diwani rights, the British introduced the Dual Government system in Bengal. Under this system, there were two rulers: the Nawab and the Company. The Nawab was responsible for the administration and public welfare, but had no money. The Company controlled all revenue collection, but took no responsibility for the people. This exploitative system ultimately contributed to the disastrous Bengal Famine of 1770.

4. Expansion Through Other Regional Wars

To establish a firm foothold over the rest of the subcontinent, the British engaged in a series of conflicts with major regional powers.

Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775-1818)

After the Third Battle of Panipat (1761), the Marathas were divided into five centers of power: Peshwa, Gaikwad, Bhonsle, Sindhia, and Holkar. The British fought three wars against them, taking advantage of their disunity. Eventually, the Peshwa was reduced to a pensioner under British authority.

Anglo-Mysore Wars

Mysore emerged as a powerful force under Hyder Ali and his son, Tipu Sultan.

  • Hyder Ali defeated the British in the First Anglo-Mysore War (1769).
  • Tipu Sultan modernised his army and industries. However, during the Third War, Lord Cornwallis forced him to sign the Treaty of Seringapatam (1792), surrendering half his territory.
  • Tipu Sultan died fighting the British in the Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799).

Anglo-Sikh Wars

Under Maharaja Ranjit Singh, the Sikhs strongly resisted British control. However, after his death in 1839, the British fought two Anglo-Sikh Wars. Lord Dalhousie decisively defeated the Sikhs, and Punjab was annexed in 1849.

5. British Policies of Annexation

Besides outright wars, the British used diplomatic strategies and unfair policies to swallow Indian states.

Important

1. Subsidiary Alliance (Lord Wellesley):

Under this policy, Indian rulers were forced to accept British "protection." In return, they lost their independence. They had to:

  • Maintain British troops at their own cost.
  • Accept a British resident at their court.
  • Refrain from making alliances with other European powers.
States like Awadh, Hyderabad, and Mysore were forced to sign this alliance.

2. Doctrine of Lapse (Lord Dalhousie)

Introduced by Lord Dalhousie in 1848, this unfair law stated that if an Indian ruler died without a biological male heir, his kingdom would "lapse" or automatically pass to the British. The British rejected the Indian tradition of adopting heirs. Due to this policy, states like Satara, Nagpur, and Jhansi were annexed.

3. Direct Annexation

If no other method worked, the British simply annexed territories by force. Awadh was annexed in 1856 by Lord Dalhousie under the false pretext of "misgovernance" by its loyal ruler, Nawab Wajid Ali Shah. This act made the British highly unpopular.

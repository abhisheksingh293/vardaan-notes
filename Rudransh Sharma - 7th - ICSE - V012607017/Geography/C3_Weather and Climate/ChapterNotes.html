Weather and Climate

1. Weather vs. Climate

Not all days of a year feel the same. Changes in hotness, coldness, cloud cover, and rainfall play a major role in how we lead our lives.

Concept

Weather: It is defined as the atmospheric condition of a certain place at a particular point of time. All weather changes take place in the lowest layer of the atmosphere (the troposphere).

Climate: It refers to the sum total of the average weather conditions that prevail at a particular place calculated over a long period of time (usually 30-35 years).

Key Differences

  • Duration: Weather changes over a short period (even within a day). Climate is stable and permanent over a long period.
  • Area: Weather is determined over a small or local area. Climate is calculated for a large area (a state, country, or continent).
  • Nature: Weather is highly dynamic and erratic. Climate helps determine broad themes like natural vegetation regions and cropping patterns.

2. Elements of Weather

The components essential for determining the weather and climate of a region are known as the elements of weather.

Temperature

Temperature is termed as the degree of hotness or coldness of air. It varies from place to place due to:

  • Latitudinal Position: Lower latitudes (near the Equator) are generally warmer than higher latitudes (near the Poles).
  • Altitude: The higher the altitude, the lower the temperature. This is why hill stations are cooler than plains.
  • Distance from the Sea: Coastal areas enjoy an equable (moderate) climate, while inland areas experience extreme temperatures.
  • Ocean Currents: Warm ocean currents increase the temperature of coastal areas, while cold currents decrease it.
  • Wind: Winds blowing from colder regions lower the temperature, while those from warmer regions raise it (e.g., the hot, dry 'Loo' in summer).
  • Vegetation Cover: Forested areas provide shade and reduce the temperature.

Atmospheric Pressure

This is the pressure exerted by the column of air at any point on the Earth's surface. It depends on altitude, temperature, moisture, and Earth's rotation.

  • As altitude increases, pressure drops because the air becomes thinner (less dense).
  • High Temperature = Low Pressure: Warm air expands, rises, and exerts less pressure.
  • Low Temperature = High Pressure: Cold air is denser and sinks, creating high pressure.
  • Low pressure is associated with unsettled, stormy weather, while high pressure brings calm, fair weather.

Wind

Moving air is called wind. Wind always blows from a region of high pressure to low pressure.

  • Coriolis Force: Due to the Earth's rotation, winds deflect to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.
  • Wind velocity depends on the difference in atmospheric pressure. A large difference causes high-speed winds.

Humidity

Humidity is the amount of moisture (water vapour) content in the air. Warm air can hold more moisture than cold air.

Fact

Absolute vs. Relative Humidity:
• Absolute Humidity: The actual amount of water vapour present in a unit volume of air.
• Relative Humidity: The ratio (expressed as a percentage) of the actual water vapour present compared to the maximum amount the air could hold at that temperature.

Precipitation

When clouds become too heavy, water falls back to the Earth. Precipitation includes rain, sleet, hail, and snow. There are three types of rainfall based on mechanism:

  • Convectional Rainfall: Heated air rises, expands, cools, and condenses.
  • Orographic Rainfall: A physical barrier (like a mountain) forces moisture-bearing winds to rise and rain.
  • Cyclonic Rainfall: Warm and cold air masses meet to cause rain.

Cloud Cover

Clouds are formed by the condensation of water vapour around minute dust particles (condensation nuclei). The main types are:

  • Cirrus: High-level, thin, feathery, needle-like clouds indicating fair weather.
  • Cumulus: Medium-level, fluffy clouds resembling cauliflower heads.
  • Stratus: Low-level, layered clouds that can produce drizzle.
  • Nimbus: Dark, grey rain-bearing clouds that cause heavy rain and thunderstorms.

3. Weather Forecast and Its Importance

Weather forecasting is the prediction of weather conditions for a given place based on data collected by meteorological departments and artificial satellites. It is crucial for:

  • Ocean navigation and aviation (guiding flights and ships safely).
  • Alerting people about extreme weather conditions (cyclones, floods).
  • Guiding farmers to prepare their cropping patterns.
  • Military operations.

4. Weather Instruments

Meteorologists use specific scientific instruments to collect weather data:

  • Thermometer: A Six's maximum and minimum thermometer measures the highest and lowest temperatures of the day.
  • Rain Gauge: Measures the amount of rainfall (in mm or cm) using a metallic cylinder and a funnel. It is placed in an open area to avoid splashing.
  • Barometer: Measures atmospheric pressure in millibars (mb). A Mercury barometer uses liquid mercury, while an Aneroid barometer contains no liquid and is portable.
  • Hygrometer: A Wet and Dry Bulb Thermometer is used to measure relative humidity based on the rate of evaporation.
  • Wind Vane: Determines the direction from which the wind is blowing.
  • Anemometer: Measures the speed of the wind in kilometres per hour using spinning cups.
Important

Stevenson's Screen: To get accurate "shade temperatures" without direct sunlight or rain interference, thermometers are placed inside a wooden box on stilts with louvered doors, known as Stevenson's screen.

5. Isotherms and Isohyets

Meteorologists plot data on maps using special lines to understand distribution patterns:

  • Isotherms: Imaginary lines drawn on a map joining places that have equal temperature.
  • Isohyets: Imaginary lines drawn on a map joining places receiving the same amount of rainfall over a unit of time.

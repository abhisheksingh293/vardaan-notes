THE TUGHLUQ AND LODI DYNASTIES

1. The Rise of the Tughluq Dynasty

The death of Alauddin Khalji in 1316 CE signaled a civil war and a scramble for power among rival parties. The last Khalji ruler, Khusrao Khan, was killed in 1320 CE. The nobles, exhausted by the chaos, raised Ghazi Malik to the throne in 1320 CE, who took the title Ghias-ud-din Tughluq, thereby founding the Tughluq Dynasty.

Timeline

1320 CE: Ghias-ud-din Tughluq ascends the throne.
1325 CE: Muhammad bin Tughluq ascends the throne.
1351 CE: Firoz Shah Tughluq ascends the throne.
1398 CE: Invasion of Timur.
1526 CE: The First Battle of Panipat and end of the Delhi Sultanate.

Ghias-ud-din Tughluq (1320–1325 CE)

  • Architecture: He built a massive new city and fort near Delhi, naming it Tughluqabad.
  • Conquests: He carried out successful military campaigns against Pratap Rudra Dev of Warangal (annexing his empire) and also invaded Bengal.
  • Reforms: He reduced the land revenue to one-tenth of the produce, reformed the judicial and police departments, and built canals, gardens, and forts.
  • Death: He died in 1325 CE when a wooden pavilion, built for his reception by his son (Jauna Khan), collapsed in what many believe was a planned accident.

2. Muhammad bin Tughluq (1325–1351 CE)

Upon his father's death, Jauna Khan ascended the throne as Sultan Muhammad bin Tughluq. He was the most highly educated of all the Delhi Sultans. He was a versatile scholar trained in philosophy, astronomy, physical science, and medicine, as well as an accomplished poet and general.

Concept

Ahead of His Time: Muhammad bin Tughluq wanted to bring about massive changes in administration, economy, and transport. His ideas were highly innovative but they failed miserably because they were poorly executed and far ahead of their times.

The Controversial Schemes

A. Taxation in the Doab

To fund his massive army and introduce new crops, he raised the tax in the Doab region (the highly fertile land between the Ganga and Yamuna rivers) to half (50%) of the produce. Unfortunately, a severe famine broke out concurrently. The peasants suffered terrible misery, and although the Sultan later provided liberal relief (free food and loans), the damage to his reputation was done.

B. Transfer of Capital

Realizing that Delhi was highly vulnerable to Mongol attacks, he built a new city at Devagiri in the Deccan, renaming it Daulatabad, and shifted his capital there. This was also to help control the south.

  • The Blunder: Instead of shifting only his government officials, he forced the entire population of Delhi to march 1500 km in the scorching heat.
  • The Result: Thousands died on the journey. Ultimately, Daulatabad proved to be climatically unsuitable, and he eventually ordered the people to return to Delhi, causing untold misery and draining the treasury.

C. Introduction of Token Currency

To overcome a shortage of silver, he introduced a daring scheme: Token Currency. He issued bronze coins that held the same official value as a silver tanka.

  • The Flaw: He failed to make the minting of the new coins a monopoly of the state. As a result, every household turned into an illegal mint.
  • The Collapse: Counterfeit bronze coins flooded the market. Traders refused to accept them, and trade collapsed. The Sultan had to withdraw the currency, exchanging genuine gold/silver from the royal treasury for the fake bronze coins, causing a massive financial loss.

D. Grand Plans of Conquest

He planned to conquer China and Persia. These expeditions failed completely, costing thousands of soldiers' lives and wasting immense amounts of money. Meanwhile, Mongols raided the outskirts of Delhi in 1328 CE.

Disintegration of the Empire: The failure of his schemes caused massive discontent and revolts in Bengal, Warangal, Devagiri, and Gujarat. He died in 1351 CE while trying to suppress a revolt.

3. Firoz Shah Tughluq (1351–1388 CE)

Muhammad bin Tughluq was succeeded by his cousin, Firoz Shah. He was a just and benevolent ruler, though he failed to reconquer lost territories like Bengal and Sind.

Fact

Welfare and Justice: Firoz Shah abolished brutal torture and administered justice strictly according to the Quran. He provided generous financial assistance to the poor and reduced taxes in the Doab region.

The Enthusiastic Builder

No other king of the Delhi Sultanate built as much as Firoz Shah. His public utility works included:

  • New Towns: Founded Firozabad, Fatehabad, Jaunpur, and Firoz Shah Kotla.
  • Institutions: Built 50 madrasas (including the famous Firozshahi Madrasa at Hauz Khas), 40 mosques, 30 colleges, and 100 hospitals.
  • Gardens: Laid out 1,200 new gardens in the vicinity of Delhi.

4. The Invasion of Timur (1398 CE)

After Firoz Shah's death, the empire weakened. This paved the way for Amir Timur (also called 'Timur the Lame'), the head of the Chagatai Turks in Central Asia.

  • Timur marched from Samarkand into India with 92,000 horsemen in 1398 CE.
  • He plundered Delhi mercilessly, leaving behind a trail of blood, tortured bodies, and a severe famine.
  • This invasion dealt a fatal blow to the Tughluq Empire, which it never recovered from, eventually paving the way for the Mughal invasion a century later.

5. The Sayyid and Lodi Dynasties

After Timur left, his appointed viceroy, Khizr Khan, established the Sayyid Dynasty, which ruled Delhi from 1414 to 1451 CE. After them, the Lodi Dynasty took over.

The Lodi Dynasty (1451–1526 CE)

  • Bahlul Lodi (1451–1489 CE): An Afghan governor of Punjab who occupied the throne. He successfully suppressed rebellions and established his authority over Sind and Mewar.
  • Sikander Lodi (1489–1517 CE): Succeeded his father. He is considered the wisest and greatest ruler of the Lodi dynasty. He annexed the Gangetic plain up to Jaunpur and Bihar. He also founded the city of Agra.
  • Ibrahim Lodi (1517–1526 CE): The last ruler of the Delhi Sultanate. He faced massive rebellions (including from Rana Sanga of Mewar). He severely displeased his nobility by replacing senior, experienced commanders with younger ones, ruling with a vengeance.

6. The Dawn of the Mughals (The First Battle of Panipat)

Frustrated by Ibrahim Lodi's arrogance, his own nobles plotted against him. Daulat Khan (the governor of Punjab) and Alam Khan (the Sultan's uncle) invited Babur, the King of Kabul, to invade India.

Important Date

1526 CE - The First Battle of Panipat: Babur accepted the invitation, arrived in India, and defeated and killed Ibrahim Lodi in this fateful battle. This marked the absolute end of the Lodi Dynasty and the Delhi Sultanate, initiating the mighty Mughal Empire in India.

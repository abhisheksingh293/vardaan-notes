THE TURKISH INVASIONS

1. Background to the Invasions

By the 9th century, the great Arab Empire had disintegrated into several independent kingdoms. Among them, two kingdoms emerged prominently: Ghazni and Ghor, both located in present-day Afghanistan.

During the 10th and 11th centuries, North India was divided into many small, constantly warring kingdoms. This political instability and tales of India's fabulous wealth prompted the Turkish invasions in the 11th and 12th centuries, changing the course of Indian history forever.

2. Mahmud of Ghazni (971–1030 CE)

Subuktigin, a Turkish noble and ruler of Ghazni, was succeeded by his son Mahmud in 971 CE. Mahmud was an ambitious ruler who wanted to achieve glory as the greatest conqueror of the world.

Timeline

1001 to 1025 CE: Mahmud of Ghazni attacked India 17 times during this period.

Motives Behind the Invasions

  • Wealth: He had heard about the fabulous wealth of the Indo-Gangetic plains. He plundered India primarily to replenish the treasury of Ghazni.
  • Protection: He needed immense wealth to protect his own kingdom from the expanding kingdoms of Central Asia and to buy mercenaries.
  • Trade: He wanted to control the highly profitable trade routes and commodities, like horses from Arabia.

Important Campaigns

Mahmud was a highly successful general who never suffered a defeat.

  • In 1001 CE, he attacked Punjab and major cities like Ujjain, Gwalior, Kalinga, Kanauj, and Delhi.
  • He targeted and raided rich temple towns such as Nagarkot, Alwar, Thaneshwar, and Mathura.
  • The Somnath Temple Attack: One of his most devastating attacks was on the Somnath Temple in Gujarat. Around 50,000 devotees laid down their lives to defend it, but in vain. The temple lost 1300 kilograms of gold and millions of dinars.
  • Before his death in 1030 CE, he annexed Punjab as the easternmost province of his empire.

Patron of Art and Culture

Although he plundered India, Mahmud was a great patron of art, architecture, and literature in his own country. He used the looted wealth to adorn Ghazni with beautiful mosques, libraries, a museum, and a university.

Concept

Scholars in Mahmud's Court:
• Al Beruni: Spent ten years in India and wrote Tahqiq-i-Hind, a rich source of information about 11th-century India.
• Firdausi: A famous Persian poet who wrote the great epic Shahnama.

3. Mohammad Ghori

After a gap of almost 150 years, Mohammad Ghori, a Turkish ruler of a small kingdom in Afghanistan, attacked North-west India in 1175 CE.

Fact

The Key Difference: While Mahmud of Ghazni was merely a conqueror interested in looting and plundering wealth, Mohammad Ghori wanted to build an empire in India. Hence, Ghori is rightly regarded as the true founder of the Turko-Afghan rule in India.

Rajput Kingdoms during Ghori's Time

When Ghori invaded, North India was ruled by five major Rajput kingdoms:

  1. Rathors of Kanauj
  2. Chauhans of Delhi and Ajmer
  3. Solankis of Gujarat
  4. Paramaras of Malwa
  5. Chandellas of Bundelkhand

Early Conquests: In 1175 CE, Ghori captured Multan. He later defeated the Solankis of Gujarat and conquered Peshawar, Sialkot, and Punjab.

4. The Battles of Tarain

The most crucial battles fought by Mohammad Ghori were against the powerful Rajput ruler, Prithviraj Chauhan.

  • First Battle of Tarain (1191 CE): Mohammad Ghori was heavily defeated by Prithviraj Chauhan and barely managed to escape with his life.
  • Second Battle of Tarain (1192 CE): Ghori returned the very next year with a much larger and better-prepared army. He sought revenge and defeated Prithviraj Chauhan. This victory was a turning point in history as it marked the end of Rajput rule in northern India.

The Story of Kanauj: Prithviraj had eloped with Sanyogita, the daughter of Raja Jaichand of Kanauj. Because of this insult, Jaichand did not help Prithviraj against Ghori. Ultimately, Ghori also defeated Jaichand and annexed Kanauj in 1198 CE.

Expansion by Generals

Ghori made the conquered territories a province of his empire and left his generals in charge:

  • Qutb-ud-din Aibak: Appointed governor, he further expanded the empire by conquering Mathura, Gwalior, Bundelkhand, and Malwa.
  • Bakhtiar Khalji: Another general who successfully conquered Bengal and Bihar.

5. Causes for Turkish Success (and Rajput Defeat)

The defeat of the Rajputs was due to several distinct disadvantages:

  • Weak War Machinery: The Rajputs relied on slow-moving elephants, which were no match for the lightning-fast, horse-mounted Central Asian guerrilla warriors.
  • Disunity and Jealousy: Internal rivalries and hatred (especially between Prithviraj Chauhan and Raja Jaichand) prevented a united front against the invaders.
  • Feudal System: Rajput rulers did not maintain strong, permanent standing armies; they depended heavily on their feudal lords. Clashes among these lords further weakened the kings.
  • Determination: Coming from far-off, harsh lands, the Turko-Afghans fought with extreme desperation and vigor to win and settle.

6. Impact of the Raids

The invasions had profound, long-lasting effects on the Indian subcontinent:

  • Cultural Intermingling: The Islamic culture brought the concept of a single formless God. The interaction between the two cultures led to the emergence of a rich, composite Indo-Islamic culture.
  • New Influences: This composite culture was vividly reflected in the architecture, language, music, and food of the era.
  • Migration: The invasions opened the subcontinent to large-scale migration. Soldiers, traders, and workers came and settled in India, eventually blending into the Indian way of life.
  • Political Shift: It introduced a new political contender to Northern India, eventually leading to the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate.

Death of Ghori: In 1206 CE, Mohammad Ghori was killed by the Khokhars while trying to crush a revolt. Because he had no son to succeed him, a struggle for power broke out among his generals. Qutb-ud-din Aibak emerged victorious, asserted his independence, and laid the foundation of the Slave Dynasty.

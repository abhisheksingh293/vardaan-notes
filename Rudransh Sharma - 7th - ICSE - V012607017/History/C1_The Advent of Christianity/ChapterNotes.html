THE ADVENT OF CHRISTIANITY

1. Introduction to the Medieval Period

History traces the progress of human beings from prehistoric times to the present day. For convenience, historians have divided history into ancient, medieval, and modern periods.

  • The transition between these periods was gradual and not uniform throughout the world.
  • In India: The medieval period stretches approximately from the 8th century to the 18th century.
  • In Europe: The medieval period stretches approximately from 600 CE to 1500 CE.
  • This period witnessed significant events such as the spread of Christianity in Europe, the rise of Arabs and the Turkish Empire, and the advent of Europeans in India for trade.

2. The Advent of Christianity

Christianity was founded by a Jew named Jesus Christ. He was probably born in 4 BCE in Bethlehem, in Judaea. Jesus is considered by Christians as the Messiah and the Son of God.

Concept

Messiah: A leader (usually religious) who brings hope for the people.

Early Life and Preaching

  • Jesus was raised in Nazareth (Palestine) and studied Jewish scriptures.
  • He began his ministry at about 30 years of age after being baptized by his cousin, John.
  • His understanding of the Jewish religion and interpretation of the Law of Moses clashed with traditional Jewish leaders.
  • According to the Gospels written by his disciples, he possessed healing powers and performed miracles, which attracted large crowds and irritated the Jewish leaders.

Teachings of Jesus Christ

Jesus led a simple life and possessed the virtues of tenderness, compassion, and courage. His teachings were simple and can be summarized as follows:

  • He had sympathy for all and treated all people as equals.
  • Our love for God should be unconditional.
  • Good should always triumph over evil.
  • Love for neighbors is extremely important.
  • These teachings are recorded in the four canonical Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John) which form part of the New Testament. The teachings are rooted in the Jewish Bible, which Christians call the Old Testament.

3. Crucifixion and Resurrection

During this time, Palestine was under the control of the Romans.

  • The Jewish community convinced the Romans that Jesus was a threat to them and their religion.
  • The Romans arrested Jesus and charged him with sedition (provocation or inciting rebellion).
  • He suffered death by crucifixion (being fastened to a cross) in 30 CE.
  • The day of his crucifixion is mourned and remembered as Good Friday.
Important Dates

4 BCE: Probable year of the birth of Jesus Christ.
30 CE: Crucifixion of Jesus Christ.

4. Spread of Christianity

Christianity spread rapidly throughout the Roman Empire and beyond, largely due to the efforts of St. Paul and other apostles (messengers specially sent to preach the gospel).

Fact

Emperor Constantine: He followed a policy of tolerance towards Christians and placed the Greek initials for Christ on his shields and banner. He built a new capital at Byzantium, which was called Constantinople (present-day Istanbul).

  • By the 4th century, Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire.
  • Christianity also spread beyond the Roman borders to regions like Armenia and Persia.

5. Division and Decline of the Roman Empire

The vast Roman Empire eventually faced division and decline. After Emperor Theodosius died, the empire was divided between his two sons:

  • Arcadius: Ruled the Eastern Empire with Constantinople as his capital.
  • Honorius: A child of 11, became the ruler of the Western Empire with Rome as his capital.

Causes for the Decline of the Roman Empire

The fall of the city of Rome and the western empire was caused by several internal and external factors:

  • Religious Intolerance: Christianity was less tolerant of other cultures. For example, Emperor Theodosius ended the Olympic Games because they honored the Greek God, Zeus.
  • Military Disloyalty: Roman soldiers were loyal to their military leaders rather than to the Emperor.
  • Failing Economy: A vast empire suffered from economic instability.
  • Heavy Taxation: Unjust and heavy taxes upon the middle class led to civil unrest.
  • Comfort and Laziness: The Romans became lazy and too comfortable with their luxurious lifestyles over time.
  • Barbarian Mercenaries: Romans hired barbarian mercenaries to guard their borders, which backfired when these barbarians decided to invade the empire.

Threats and Invasions

The most prominent cause for the decline was massive invasions by nomadic Germanic tribes.

  • The Goths: Fleeing from the powerful Huns, they sought shelter in Rome but later turned against it. Under their king, Alaric, the West Goths attacked and plundered Rome.
  • Other Tribes: Successive waves of tribes including the Vandals, Angles, Saxons, Franks, and Lombards ravaged the empire. The Angles and Saxons settled in the British Isles, while the Franks settled in France.
  • The Fall: In 476 CE, Romulus, the last Roman Emperor in the west, was overthrown by the Germanic leader Odoacer, who became the first Barbarian ruler of Rome.

Rise of Byzantium

The Eastern Roman Empire survived for another thousand years as the Byzantine Empire. Greek (not Latin) was its main language. Eventually, the Western Roman Empire became the seat of the Roman Catholic Church, while the Eastern Byzantium Empire became the seat of the Orthodox Church.

6. Emergence of the Turks, Mongols, and The Crusades

Parallel to developments in Europe, tribes like the Turks and Mongols (under Genghis Khan) were rising in Asia. Turkish tribes converted to Islam and became a powerful force, eventually capturing the holy land of Jerusalem in the 11th century. This sparked religious wars.

The Crusades (1095 CE - 1291 CE)

The Crusades were a series of religious wars fought in the medieval period between Christians and Muslims.

  • Objective: To secure control of holy sites (especially Jerusalem) considered sacred by both groups.
  • Sanctioned By: The Latin Church, led by figures like Pope Urban II, who promised forgiveness of sins for those who fought.

Results of the Crusades

The Crusades brought about significant and long-lasting changes in medieval Europe:

  • Military Technology: Christians learned the use of gunpowder from the Muslims.
  • Transfer of Knowledge: Scientific, medical, and architectural knowledge was transferred from the Islamic and Greek worlds to the West, paving the way for the Renaissance in Europe.
  • New Inventions: Arab inventions such as algebra, optics, and advanced engineering reached the West and were taught in European universities.
  • Economic and Political Shift: New towns and cities were established. Traders paid taxes to the kings, which helped kings raise large armies. This increased the authority of kings and led to the decline in the power of feudal lords.

7. The Christian Monastic Order

With the fall of the Roman Empire, a void was created in European political and social life. People looked to the Church for stability, leading to an elaborate infrastructure.

Monks and Nuns

  • Some priests chose to live as ascetics (a strict, disciplined life) in monasteries. They were known as monks.
  • Women who gave up worldly life were known as nuns and lived in convents and nunneries.
  • They lived a spartan life (strictly disciplined), shunning luxury, comfort, and marriage.
  • Their day was rigidly divided into three parts: prayer, sleep, and intellectual/manual labor.
  • From the 13th century, some monks chose to travel and preach while living on charity; they were called friars.

Priests and Bishops

The priests formed the lowest rung of the order, performing everyday religious services like baptism and marriages. Bishops supervised the Church. Among the bishops of five important cities (Constantinople, Rome, Alexandria, Antioch, Jerusalem), the Bishop of Rome gained supreme authority and took the title of Pope (meaning 'Father' in Latin).

Impact of Monasteries

Monasteries played an incredibly vital role in medieval Europe:

  • Centers of Learning: They were the primary educational hubs. Monks and nuns studied literature, art, science, and religion.
  • Preservation of Knowledge: Since the printing press was not yet invented, monks copied books by hand on parchment, preserving the knowledge of ancient and medieval civilizations.
  • Birth of Universities: Huge churches built in towns evolved into great universities like Cambridge and Oxford, where secular subjects (history, philosophy, medicine, law) were taught.
  • Social Service: The Church and monasteries looked after the sick, the poor, the destitute, and educated children.
  • Art and Architecture: Monasteries became important centers where architecture, mural painting, woodwork, and glass painting flourished.

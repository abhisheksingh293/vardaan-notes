THE SLAVE DYNASTY

1. The Foundation of the Delhi Sultanate

Mohammad Ghori died in 1206 CE leaving no male heir to inherit his vast territories. A scramble for power broke out among his provincial governors. Qutb-ud-din Aibak, a trusted general of Mohammad Ghori, eventually succeeded to the throne.

Concept

The Slave / Mamluk Dynasty: The dynasty established by Qutb-ud-din Aibak came to be known as the Slave or the Mamluk (an Arabic word meaning 'slave' or 'owned') dynasty. This is because the prominent rulers—Qutb-ud-din Aibak, Iltutmish, and Balban—were originally slaves before they rose to power through military merit.

Important

The Five Dynasties of the Delhi Sultanate:
1. The Slave Dynasty (1206–1290 CE)
2. The Khalji Dynasty (1290–1320 CE)
3. The Tughluq Dynasty (1320–1413 CE)
4. The Sayyid Dynasty (1414–1451 CE)
5. The Lodi Dynasty (1451–1526 CE)

2. Qutb-ud-din Aibak (1206–1210 CE)

Qutb-ud-din Aibak was the first Sultan of the Slave dynasty. He was an able general who successfully suppressed all internal revolts and checked external invaders.

Achievements and Titles

  • Lakh Baksh: Aibak was extremely charitable. He gave away large sums of money, earning him the famous title "Lakh Baksh" (giver of lakhs).
  • Architecture: He built the Quwwat-ul-Islam mosque in Delhi (meaning 'might of Islam') and the Arhai Din Ka Jhonpra mosque in Ajmer.
  • He initiated the construction of the famous Qutb Minar but died before it could be completed.

Death: He did not live long enough to enjoy his victories. He died in 1210 CE after a fatal fall from his horse while playing Chaugan (ancient Polo).

3. Shams-ud-din Iltutmish (1211–1236 CE)

Qutb-ud-din Aibak was succeeded by his slave and son-in-law, Iltutmish. He is widely regarded as the actual consolidator and saviour of the Delhi Sultanate.

Military and Diplomatic Success

  • Conquests: He defeated Yalduz (ruler of Ghazni) and Qabacha (ruler of Multan), pushing them out of Punjab. He also suppressed the Khalji revolt in Bengal and defeated Rajput rulers of Gwalior, Malwa, Ranthambhor, and Mandu.
  • The Mongol Threat: Iltutmish diplomatically saved India from the devastating threat of a Mongol invasion led by Genghis Khan. He did this by politely refusing to give shelter to the fleeing Shah of Persia in 1221 CE, which pleased Genghis Khan who then turned back without attacking India.

Administrative Reforms

  • He secured his claim to the throne by getting approval from the Khalifa (Caliph), the spiritual head of the Islamic world, which greatly enhanced his power and respect among the nobles.
  • Currency: He introduced standard silver coins called the 'tanka'.
Fact

Iqtadari System: To please the Turkish nobles and win their support, Iltutmish introduced the iqtadari system. He divided his empire into territories called 'iqtas' and distributed them among his nobles, who were then responsible for their administration and revenue collection.

Iltutmish as a Builder

Iltutmish was a great patron of art and architecture. He successfully completed the construction of the Qutb Minar, built a beautiful tomb for himself in red sandstone in Delhi, and constructed a grand mosque at Badaun.

4. Razia Sultana (1236–1240 CE)

Before his death in 1236 CE, Iltutmish nominated his highly capable daughter, Razia Sultana, as his successor because none of his sons possessed the qualities of a worthy ruler.

  • Initial Resistance: The orthodox nobles initially refused to be ruled by a woman and placed her incompetent brother, Ruknuddin, on the throne. However, he proved unworthy, prompting the nobles to remove him and allow Razia to ascend.
  • Her Reign: Razia managed to end the chaos and established strong law and order. She was brave and actively led her army in wars.
  • Downfall: The nobles were unhappy taking orders from a woman. Furthermore, her favor for an Abyssinian slave named Yakut led to a rebellion.
  • To pacify the rebellion, she married Altuniya (the Governor of Sirhind and a rebel leader). Together, they marched towards Delhi to regain the throne but were defeated and tragically killed in 1240 CE.

5. Nasir-ud-din Mahmud (1246–1265 CE)

After Razia's death, the empire plunged into chaos and faced Mongol attacks (who attacked Lahore in 1241 CE). In 1246 CE, a powerful group of forty Turkish nobles, known as the Chalisa (The Group of Forty), raised Iltutmish's youngest son, Nasir-ud-din Mahmud, to the throne.

  • Being only 17 years old, Nasir-ud-din left the entire administration in the highly capable hands of his Prime Minister and father-in-law, Balban.
  • Balban faithfully crushed all internal rebellions with a stern hand and built a chain of frontier forts to prevent Mongol attacks. Nasir-ud-din died in 1265 CE.

6. Ghiyas-ud-din Balban (1266–1286 CE)

Balban, originally a slave, ascended the throne in 1266 CE. He virtually controlled the empire for forty years (twenty as Prime Minister and twenty as Sultan).

Consolidation and the 'Blood and Iron' Policy

When Balban became Sultan, he faced defiance from the 'Group of Forty', rebellious Rajput chiefs, and a constant Mongol threat.

  • Blood and Iron Policy: He adopted this ruthless policy to crush the power of rebellious nobles, thieves, and invaders. He showed absolute no mercy towards traitors.
  • He successfully suppressed the revolt of Tughril Beg in Bengal. Tughril Beg and his followers were put to death, and Balban appointed his own son, Bughra Khan, as the Governor of Bengal with a stern warning against future rebellion.
  • He repaired old forts and built new, strong forts along the north-west frontier. He kept a well-equipped standing army to ward off the Mongols.

Conduct of Nobles and Court Etiquette

Balban introduced strict measures to assert his absolute authority and break the power of the nobles:

  • Divine Right of Kingship: Balban promoted the belief that the King was the representative of God on earth, establishing absolute monarchy.
  • Court Discipline: He maintained a lavish court to awe the nobles. Strict silence and discipline were mandatory.
  • Sijda and Paibos: He forced nobles to perform Sijda (kneeling and touching the ground with the forehead) and Paibos (kissing the feet of the Sultan). This horrified orthodox Muslims, as Islamic teachings state that all men are equal and such bowing should only be done before God.

Fall of the Slave Dynasty

Balban was a highly successful ruler who cleared forests, made roads safer, and improved trade and agriculture. He died in 1286 CE. Soon after his death, his inefficient successors failed to maintain the empire. The dynasty collapsed when a noble named Jalaluddin Khalji murdered Balban's grandson, Kaikubad, marking the end of the Slave Dynasty.

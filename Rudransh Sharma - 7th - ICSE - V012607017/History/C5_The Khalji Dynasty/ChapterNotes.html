THE KHALJI DYNASTY

1. Establishment of the Khalji Dynasty

After the death of Balban, the Slave Dynasty fell into chaos as his successors were incapable of maintaining the empire. This instability set the stage for the rise of the second dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate: The Khalji Dynasty.

Fact

Origins of the Khaljis: The Khaljis were originally Turks. However, they settled in a region of Afghanistan called Khalj. After settling there, they adopted Afghan manners and customs before the Mongol invasions pushed them into Hindustan.

Jalaluddin Khalji (1290–1296 CE)

  • Jalaluddin Firoz was the founder of the Khalji dynasty.
  • He was an old man of seventy when he ascended the throne.
  • Although he established a government, his authority was weak, and the crown was no longer feared by the nobles or the public.

2. Alauddin Khalji’s Rise to Power

Jalaluddin's nephew and son-in-law, Alauddin Khalji, was appointed the governor of Awadh and Kara. He was highly ambitious and ruthless.

  • In 1296 CE, Alauddin attacked and defeated Raja Ramchandra of Devagiri in the Deccan, acquiring fabulous riches.
  • When Jalaluddin travelled to Allahabad to welcome his victorious nephew, Alauddin had him treacherously murdered.
  • He then defeated and imprisoned Jalaluddin's two sons (Arkali Khan and Qadar Khan) and proclaimed himself the Sultan in 1296 CE.
  • To suppress opposition and win over the nobles, he generously distributed the vast booty he had looted from Devagiri.
Timeline

1290 CE: Jalaluddin Khalji ascends the throne.
1296 CE: Alauddin Khalji murders Jalaluddin and captures the throne.
1316 CE: Death of Alauddin Khalji.
1320 CE: End of the Khalji Dynasty.

3. Military Expansion and Conquests

Alauddin Khalji established the largest empire in India since the time of Ashoka. His empire extended from the Brahmaputra in the east to the Arabian Sea in the west, and from the Himalayas in the north down to Madurai in the south.

Major Campaigns

  • North India: He conquered Gujarat and Malwa in 1297 CE, and later captured Ranthambhor and Chittor in Rajasthan.
  • The Deccan and South India: Alauddin sent his trusted general, Malik Kafur, to conquer the south. Malik Kafur conquered territories like Dwarasamudra and Madurai.
  • Vassal States: Realizing that the south was too far to rule directly from Delhi, Alauddin made the southern Rajas his vassals. They were forced to acknowledge his supremacy and pay heavy annual tributes.
Concept

Malik Kafur (Hazar Dinari): Malik Kafur was a slave bought for a thousand dinars at the Port of Cambay. Because of his price, he earned the famous title "Hazar Dinari". He became a brilliant military general for Alauddin.

The Story of Padmavati and Chittor

In 1303 CE, Alauddin's passion for the legendary beauty of Queen Padmavati (wife of Ratan Sen, ruler of Chittor) led him to invade Chittor. Rather than surrendering, the queen and the women of the palace chose to commit Jauhar (self-immolation) to protect their honor.

Mongol Invasions

Alauddin faced over a dozen Mongol invasions during his reign. He successfully countered them by directly attacking the Mongols, strengthening frontier fortifications, and putting experienced generals like Zafar Khan and Malik Kafur in charge of border defenses.

4. Administration and Control

Alauddin was not only a conqueror but an exceptionally strict and accomplished administrator. He took severe steps to stamp out all conspiracies among the nobles.

  • Control over Nobles: He kept a strict watch on the nobles. All land and property given to them as gifts or religious grants were confiscated by the state.
  • Social Restrictions: Marriages, parties, and social gatherings amongst nobles were strictly banned without the Sultan's permission to prevent them from plotting rebellions.
  • Prohibitions: The drinking and sale of wine were banned.
  • Spy System: He established an incredibly efficient spy network that reported every minor detail to him.

Military Reforms

To maintain his massive standing army, Alauddin introduced two key military identification systems:

  • Chehra: A detailed descriptive roll or kind of identity card maintained for every soldier.
  • Dagh: The practice of branding horses to ensure that only quality, state-approved horses were used specifically for wars and couldn't be swapped.

5. Economic and Revenue Reforms

To fund his massive military and conquests, Alauddin implemented severe economic and market reforms.

  • Revenue Collection: Special revenue officials were appointed to collect taxes strictly in cash.
  • Tax Increase: In the highly fertile Doab region (between the Ganga and Yamuna rivers), the land revenue was increased to half (50%) of the total produce.
  • Market Control: Alauddin fixed the prices for all commodities required for daily use.
  • Strict Supervision: Officers were appointed to constantly supervise the markets. Any merchant caught short-weighing goods was brutally punished.
  • Granaries: Grains were stored in massive government granaries to ensure supply during times of famine.

6. Art, Architecture, and Literature

Despite his ruthlessness, Alauddin was a great patron of art and architecture. Because the Seljuk Empire in the west was breaking up under Mongol attacks, many artists, poets, and craftsmen sought shelter in Alauddin’s court.

Fact

Amir Khusrau: Also known as the 'Father of Qawwali', he was a brilliant Sufi poet and a disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya. He wrote in Persian and Hindavi. His famous historical and literary works include Duval Rani Khizr Khan, Miftah-ul-Futuh, Nuh-Sipihr, and Khazainul-Futuh (which describes Alauddin's conquests).

Architectural Contributions

Alauddin constructed several significant monuments, primarily in and around Delhi:

  • Alai Darwaza: Built as a beautiful entrance to the Qutb Minar complex.
  • Siri Fort: A new fortress city to protect against the Mongols.
  • Hauz Khas: A large royal water tank/bath.
  • He also built the Palace of Thousand Pillars and a Madrasa near the Qutb Minar.

7. Decline of the Khalji Dynasty

Although deeply religious, Alauddin did not allow religion or the orthodox ulemas (religious scholars) to interfere in the affairs of the state.

After Alauddin's death in 1316 CE, the empire fell into turmoil. His son, Qutb-ud-din Mubarak, held the throne for just four years but was completely ineffective as a ruler. The lack of a strong successor caused the Khalji Dynasty to collapse swiftly, paving the way for the Tughluq Dynasty to seize power in 1320 CE.

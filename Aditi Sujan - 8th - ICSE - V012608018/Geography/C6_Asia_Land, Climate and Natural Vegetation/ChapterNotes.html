Asia - Land, Climate and Natural Vegetation

Introduction

Asia is a continent of great cultures. Three of the world's oldest civilizations—the Indus Valley, the Mesopotamian, and the Chinese—flourished here. Almost all the world's major religions originated in Asia. Geographically, economically, and socio-culturally, Asia is a land of remarkable contrasts.

1. A Land of Extremes

Asia is often called a 'Land of Extremes' due to its vast geographical contrasts:

  • Highest Point: Mount Everest (8,850 m above sea level).
  • Lowest Exposed Point: Dead Sea (424 m below sea level).
  • Coldest Inhabited Place: Oymyakon in Russia (-67.7°C).
  • Hottest Places: Ahvaz in Iran and Tirat Zvi in Israel (both recorded 54°C). The Lut Desert in Iran is considered the hottest place on Earth.
  • Wettest Place: Mawsynram in Meghalaya, India.
  • Driest Places: The deserts of Arabia, Thar, and Gobi receive very scanty rainfall.
  • Population Density: The great river valleys are the most densely populated regions globally, whereas Northern Russia and Mongolia have very low population density.

2. Size, Extent, and Location

2.1 Size and Extent

Asia is the largest of the seven continents, occupying about one-third of the world's total land area. It also houses nearly 60% of the world's population.

  • Latitudinal Extent: 10°S to 80°N.
  • Longitudinal Extent: 25°E to 170°W.
  • The greater part of the continent lies in the Northern and Eastern Hemispheres.
  • Asia and Europe together form one continuous landmass called Eurasia.

2.2 Location and Boundaries

Asia is bordered by oceans on three sides:

  • North: Arctic Ocean.
  • South: Indian Ocean.
  • East: Pacific Ocean.

Separation from other continents:

  • From Europe: Separated by the Ural Mountains, Caspian Sea, Caucasus Mountains, and Black Sea.
  • From Africa: Linked in the south-west through the Sinai Peninsula.
  • From North America: Separated by the narrow Bering Strait in the extreme north-east.

3. Political Divisions

The countries of Asia range in size from tiny nations like Maldives and Singapore to Russia, China, and India, which are among the largest countries in the world.

The countries are grouped based on their locations:

  • East Asia: Japan, North Korea, South Korea, Taiwan, China, Mongolia.
  • North Asia: The Asian part of Russia.
  • Central Asia: Tajikistan, Kyrgyzstan, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan.
  • South-East Asia: Myanmar, Thailand, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, Brunei, Timor-Leste, Philippines.
  • South-Central Asia (South Asia): India, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, Sri Lanka, Maldives.
  • West Asia: Iran, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, UAE, Oman, Yemen, Jordan, Israel, Palestine, Lebanon, Syria, Turkey, Cyprus, Armenia, Georgia, Azerbaijan.
Important

Eurasia: Asia and Europe are part of the same continuous landmass. Russia and Turkey are examples of transcontinental countries that lie in both Asia and Europe.

4. Physiography of Asia

Asia can be divided into five major physical divisions:

4.1 The Northern Lowlands

These are vast lowlands located in the north, bordering the Arctic Ocean.

  • The Siberian Plain: The northern part of these lowlands. It extends from the Ural Mountains in the west to the Bering Sea in the east, making it the greatest continuous plain in the world.
  • It is drained by three large rivers: Ob, Yenisey, and Lena, which flow northwards into the Arctic Ocean.
  • Swamps and Marshes: During winter, the lower courses and mouths of these rivers freeze. In spring, the upper courses melt first, causing water to overflow and form extensive marshes.
  • The Turan Plain: Located south of the Siberian Plain, surrounding the Aral Sea. It is drained by the Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers, which flow into the Aral Sea, forming an area of inland drainage.

4.2 The Central Mountains

To the south of the Northern Lowlands lies an extensive young fold mountain system.

  • The Pamir Knot: Almost all mountain ranges of Asia radiate from a central highland area known as the Pamir Knot.
  • Westward Ranges: Hindu Kush and Sulaiman.
    • The Hindu Kush continues westward as the Elburz Mountains (along the Caspian Sea).
    • The Sulaiman range runs south as the Kirthar Range and then turns west as the Zagros Mountains.
    • The Elburz and Zagros converge at the Armenian Knot, continuing westward as the Pontic and Taurus Mountains into Turkey.
  • Eastward Ranges: Himalayas, Karakoram, Kunlun, Altyn Tagh, and Tien Shan.
    • The Himalayas contain Mt. Everest, the highest peak in the world.
    • The Karakoram range lies north of the Himalayas and contains Mt. K2 (Godwin Austen), the second highest peak.
  • Intermontane Plateaus: Plateaus enclosed by mountain ranges.
    • Plateau of Iran: Between Elburz and Zagros.
    • Plateau of Anatolia: Between Pontic and Taurus mountains.
    • Plateau of Tibet: Between Kunlun and Himalayas. It is the highest plateau in the world, called the 'Roof of the World'.
    • Plateau of Mongolia: Enclosed by mountains, featuring the Gobi Desert.
    • Tarim Basin & Tsaidam Basin: Depressions enclosing the Takla Makan Desert of China.

4.3 The Southern Plateaus

Located south of the Central Mountains, these are stable blocks made of hard crystalline rocks.

  • Plateau of Arabia: Rises steeply along the Red Sea and slopes towards the Persian Gulf. It is a dry desert.
  • Plateau of Peninsular India (Deccan Plateau): Has a steep edge along the Arabian Sea (Western Ghats) and slopes gently towards the Bay of Bengal. Traversed by rivers like Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri.
  • Plateaus of Shan (Myanmar) and Yunnan (China): Drained by the Salween, Mekong, and Si Kiang rivers.

4.4 The Great River Valleys

Formed by rivers bringing huge amounts of alluvium from the snow-covered mountains, giving rise to extensive fertile plains.

  • Tigris-Euphrates Valley (Iraq): Also known as the Mesopotamian Lowland.
  • Indus Valley (Pakistan/India): Formed by the Indus and its five tributaries (Chenab, Jhelum, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej).
  • Ganga-Brahmaputra Valley (India/Bangladesh): A massive, fertile plain.
  • Great Plain of China: Formed by Hwang Ho, Yangtze, and Si Kiang rivers.
  • Other Valleys: Irrawaddy and Salween (Myanmar), Mekong (Indo-China), and Amur Valley (Manchurian Plain).

4.5 The Island Groups

A group of islands is called an archipelago. Asia has many island nations primarily in the east and south-east.

  • Indonesian Archipelago: The largest in the world (Borneo, Sumatra, Java, Sulawesi, Bali, Timor).
  • Indian Islands: Lakshadweep (Arabian Sea), Andaman and Nicobar (Bay of Bengal).
  • Other Islands: Japan, Philippines, Taiwan, Sri Lanka, Bahrain.
Fact

Most of these islands have been formed due to the submergence of fold or volcanic mountains. Consequently, some of these islands contain active volcanoes.

5. Climate of Asia

Being a continent of extremes, Asia experiences a wide variety of climatic conditions.

5.1 Factors Affecting Climate

  • Size and Latitudinal Extent: Spans all three heat zones, ranging from hot equatorial to cold arctic climates.
  • Distance from the Sea: The deep interior lies far from the moderating influence of the sea, resulting in extreme (continental) climates.
  • Relief: East-west mountain ranges obstruct rain-bearing southern winds and block cold northern winds from reaching the south.
  • Winds: Monsoon winds (seasonal reversal of winds) heavily affect rainfall distribution.
  • Ocean Currents: Warm (Kuro Shio) and cold (Oya Shio) ocean currents influence coastal climates.

5.2 Types of Climate, Natural Vegetation, and Wildlife

Climatic Type Areas Climate Characteristics Natural Vegetation Wildlife
Equatorial Indonesia, Malaysia, southern Philippines. Hot and wet; high temperatures, heavy convectional rainfall all year. Equatorial, dark, dense evergreen forests with broad leaves (mahogany, ebony, rubber). Monkeys, sloths, tree frogs, snakes, lizards, crocodiles.
Monsoon India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Vietnam, southern China. Hot summers, cool/dry winters; seasonal reversal of winds brings rain in summer. Tropical deciduous forests (teak, sal, sandalwood, bamboo). Elephants, tigers, leopards, rhinos, monkeys.
Desert South-west (Arabia, Iran, Thar) & cold deserts of Gobi, Tibet, central Asia. Scanty rainfall; hot deserts are extremely hot/cool, cold deserts have extreme temperature ranges. Scrub, thorny bushes, cactus, date palms at oases. Deep roots, thick bark. Camels, desert foxes, hyenas, scorpions, lizards.
Mediterranean Along the Mediterranean Sea (Syria, Turkey, Israel, Lebanon). Hot, dry summers; mild, rainy winters (winter rain). Short evergreen trees with thick bark, deep roots (olive, oak, walnut, fig). Land cleared for agriculture; hence cattle & sheep reared.
Temperate China, Japan, Korea. Warm summers, cold winters; moderate rainfall (more in summer). Mixed evergreen & deciduous forests (oak, magnolia, camellia, bamboo, pine). Giant pandas, deer, antelopes, monkeys, bears.
Steppe (Temperate Grassland) South-western Siberia, eastern Mongolia. Warm summers, cold winters; low rainfall. Extreme temperatures. Short, soft grasses. Much land cleared for agriculture. Antelopes, wild donkeys, horses, wolves.
Taiga Siberia. Warm, short summers; severely cold winters. Low precipitation, mostly snow. Coniferous forests (pine, fir, spruce). Softwood used for paper/pulp. Bears, foxes, lynx, sable, mink, squirrels.
Tundra Beyond the Arctic Circle (northern fringes). Short, cool summers; long, extremely cold winters. Precipitation as snow. Mosses, lichens, stunted shrubs (grow during brief thaw). Reindeer, polar bears, foxes, musk oxen, seals, walruses.

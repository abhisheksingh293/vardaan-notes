Endocrine System and Adolescence

1. Chemical Coordination in Humans

The human body functions smoothly because all its organs work together in a coordinated manner. While the nervous system uses electrical impulses for rapid communication, the body also requires a slower, long-lasting form of control. This is achieved through chemical coordination managed by the Endocrine System.

Concept

What are Hormones?

Hormones are specific chemical messengers secreted directly into the bloodstream by endocrine glands. They are carried by the blood to specific organs (called target organs) to regulate their growth, development, and functioning.

2. Types of Glands

The human body contains two main types of glands based on how they release their secretions:

  • Exocrine Glands: These glands have ducts (tubes) to carry their secretions to specific places. Examples: Salivary glands, sweat glands, tear glands, and liver.
  • Endocrine Glands: These are ductless glands. They do not have tubes. They secrete their hormones directly into the blood surrounding them. Examples: Pituitary, Thyroid, Adrenal glands.

3. Major Endocrine Glands

A. Pituitary Gland

Located at the base of the brain, it is about the size of a pea.

  • Hormone Secreted: Growth Hormone (GH) and several stimulating hormones.
  • Function: Regulates the overall growth of the body.
  • Disorders: Under-secretion causes Dwarfism; Over-secretion in childhood causes Gigantism.
Fact

The pituitary gland is often called the "Master Gland" because its hormones control the functioning of all other endocrine glands in the body.

B. Thyroid Gland

A butterfly-shaped gland located in the neck region, attached to the windpipe (trachea).

  • Hormone Secreted: Thyroxine (requires Iodine for its production).
  • Function: Controls the basal metabolic rate, promoting overall physical and mental growth.
  • Disorders: Lack of iodine in the diet causes under-secretion, leading to an abnormal swelling of the neck known as Goitre.

C. Pancreas

Located just below the stomach. It is a unique gland because it acts as both an exocrine gland (secretes digestive juices) and an endocrine gland.

  • Endocrine Part: A cluster of cells called Islets of Langerhans.
  • Hormone Secreted: Insulin.
  • Function: Regulates the level of sugar (glucose) in the blood.
  • Disorders: Under-secretion of insulin causes Diabetes Mellitus, where blood sugar levels rise dangerously high.

D. Adrenal Glands

A pair of cap-like glands located on the top of each kidney.

  • Hormone Secreted: Adrenaline.
  • Function: Prepares the body to face emergency situations (like fear, anger, or excitement) by increasing heart rate, breathing rate, and blood supply to muscles.
Important

Adrenaline is famously known as the "Fight or Flight" hormone or the "Emergency Hormone".

4. Adolescence and Puberty

Humans undergo a period of rapid physical and psychological changes as they grow from childhood to adulthood.

  • Adolescence: The transitional phase of growth and development between childhood and adulthood. It typically occurs between the ages of 11 and 19 years (often called the "teenage" years).
  • Puberty: The specific time during adolescence when the reproductive organs become functionally active, and the individual becomes capable of reproduction. Girls generally reach puberty a year or two earlier than boys.

5. Changes During Puberty

During adolescence, the body undergoes several noticeable transformations:

Physical Changes

  1. Increase in Height: There is a sudden spurt in height due to the elongation of long bones in the arms and legs.
  2. Changes in Body Shape: In boys, shoulders broaden and muscles grow stronger. In girls, the region below the waist (hips) widens.
  3. Voice Change: In boys, the voice box (larynx) enlarges and protrudes out in the neck region, known as the Adam's apple. Their voice becomes deep and hoarse. Girls' voices become high-pitched.
  4. Activity of Sweat and Sebaceous Glands: The increased secretion from sweat and sebaceous (oil) glands often leads to acne and pimples on the face.
  5. Development of Sex Organs: In males, testes start producing sperms. In females, ovaries mature and start releasing eggs (onset of menstruation).

6. Role of Hormones in Reproduction

The changes occurring during puberty are controlled by sex hormones.

  • Male Sex Hormone (Testosterone): Secreted by the testes. It stimulates the production of sperms and the development of secondary sexual characters in boys (e.g., growth of facial hair, deep voice).
  • Female Sex Hormones (Oestrogen & Progesterone): Secreted by the ovaries. Oestrogen controls the development of female secondary sexual characters (e.g., development of breasts) and the release of eggs.

7. Personal Hygiene and Stress Management

Because of rapid physical changes and hormonal fluctuations, adolescents need to take special care of their health.

  • Nutritional Needs: Adolescence requires a balanced diet rich in proteins, carbohydrates, vitamins, and minerals. Indian adolescents, especially girls, should consume iron-rich foods (like leafy greens and jaggery) to prevent anaemia.
  • Personal Hygiene: Daily bathing is essential because increased activity of sweat glands can cause body odour. Girls need to take special care of hygiene during the menstrual cycle.
  • Physical Exercise: Outdoor games, walking, and playing sports help keep the body fit, healthy, and energized.
  • Managing Stress: Hormonal changes can cause mood swings, stress, or anxiety. It is important to talk to trusted parents or teachers, engage in hobbies, and stay away from harmful habits like smoking or taking drugs.

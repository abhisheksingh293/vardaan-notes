Reproduction in Plants

1. Introduction to Reproduction

The process by which living organisms produce new individuals of their own kind is called reproduction. It is essential for the survival and continuation of a species on Earth. In plants, reproduction mainly occurs in two ways: Asexual Reproduction and Sexual Reproduction.

Concept

Asexual vs. Sexual Reproduction

  • Asexual Reproduction: Involves only one parent. There is no formation or fusion of gametes (sex cells). The new plants are exact copies of the parent plant.
  • Sexual Reproduction: Involves two parents (or two reproductive parts). It involves the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote. Seeds are produced as a result.

2. Asexual Reproduction in Plants

Asexual reproduction can take place through several natural methods in lower plants and microorganisms:

  • Binary Fission: The parent cell divides into two equal halves, each growing into a new individual. Example: Bacteria, Amoeba.
  • Budding: A small bulb-like projection (bud) grows on the parent body, detaches itself, and becomes a new organism. Example: Yeast.
  • Fragmentation: The plant body breaks into two or more fragments, and each fragment grows into a new plant. Example: Spirogyra (a green alga).
  • Spore Formation: The parent plant produces hundreds of tiny, microscopic bodies called spores inside a spore case (sporangium). When the case bursts, spores spread and grow into new plants under favourable conditions. Example: Fungi (Rhizopus/Bread mould), Ferns, Mosses.

3. Vegetative Propagation

Vegetative propagation is a type of asexual reproduction where new plants are produced from the vegetative parts of the plant, such as the root, stem, or leaves.

A. Natural Methods

  • By Roots: Roots of some plants become swollen with stored food and can give rise to new plants. Example: Sweet potato, Dahlia, Asparagus.
  • By Stems: Underground stems are modified for storage and reproduction.
    • Tuber: Has buds called 'eyes' which sprout into new plants. Example: Potato.
    • Rhizome: Thick underground stem with buds. Example: Ginger, Turmeric.
    • Bulb: Fleshy leaves storing food with a short stem. Example: Onion, Garlic.
    • Runners: Stems creeping on the ground that root at intervals. Example: Grass, Strawberry.
  • By Leaves: Leaves of certain plants develop buds on their margins. When these fall on moist soil, they grow into new plants. Example: Bryophyllum, Begonia.

B. Artificial Methods

Farmers and gardeners use artificial methods to quickly grow plants with desirable characteristics.

  • Cutting: A healthy stem with a few leaf nodes is cut and planted in moist soil to grow roots. Example: Rose, Sugarcane, Hibiscus.
  • Layering: A lower branch is bent and covered with soil while still attached to the parent plant. Roots develop, and it is then cut off. Example: Jasmine, Lemon.
  • Grafting: The shoot of a desired plant (called scion) is joined to the rooted stem of another plant of the same species (called stock). Example: Mango, Apple, Guava.
Fact

Tissue Culture (Micropropagation)

This is a modern artificial technique where a small piece of tissue is taken from the growing tip of a plant and placed in a sterile nutrient jelly in a laboratory. The tissue multiplies to form a mass of cells (callus), which is then treated with hormones to grow into hundreds of tiny plantlets. Example: Orchids, Chrysanthemum, Asparagus.

4. Sexual Reproduction in Plants

The flower is the reproductive organ of a plant. A typical complete flower consists of four whorls attached to a swollen base called the thalamus or receptacle.

Parts of a Flower

  1. Calyx (Sepals): The outermost green whorl that protects the flower in the bud stage.
  2. Corolla (Petals): The brightly coloured whorl that attracts insects for pollination.
  3. Androecium (Stamens): The male reproductive part. Each stamen has a long stalk called a filament and a swollen top called an anther (which produces pollen grains containing male gametes).
  4. Gynoecium (Pistil/Carpel): The female reproductive part. It consists of a sticky top called the stigma, a long tube called the style, and a swollen base called the ovary (containing ovules which have the female gamete or egg).

5. Pollination

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same or another flower.

  • Self-Pollination (Autogamy): Transfer of pollen to the stigma of the same flower or another flower on the same plant.
  • Cross-Pollination (Allogamy): Transfer of pollen from the anther of one flower to the stigma of a flower on another plant of the same species.
Important

Agents of Pollination

Cross-pollination requires external agents to carry the pollen:

  • Insects (Entomophily): Flowers are large, brightly coloured, and produce nectar. (e.g., Rose, Sunflower).
  • Wind (Anemophily): Flowers produce huge amounts of light, dry pollen. Stigmas are large and feathery. (e.g., Wheat, Maize, Grass).
  • Water (Hydrophily): Occurs in aquatic plants. Pollen floats on water. (e.g., Hydrilla, Vallisneria).

6. Fertilization and Fruit Formation

Fertilization is the process of fusion of the male gamete with the female gamete (egg) to form a zygote.

  • When a pollen grain falls on a suitable stigma, it germinates and grows a pollen tube down through the style into the ovary.
  • The male gametes travel down the tube and enter the ovule.
  • One male gamete fuses with the egg cell inside the ovule to form the zygote.

Post-Fertilization Changes

After fertilization, the petals, sepals, and stamens wither and fall off. The ovary swells and develops into a fruit, while the ovules develop into seeds. The zygote inside the seed develops into an embryo (the baby plant).

7. Seed Dispersal

If all seeds fall and grow beneath the parent plant, there will be severe competition for sunlight, water, and nutrients. Hence, seeds need to be scattered over a wide area. This process is called Seed Dispersal.

  • Dispersal by Wind: Seeds are light and have wings or hair-like structures (e.g., Dandelion, Madar/Calotropis, Cotton).
  • Dispersal by Water: Seeds or fruits have a spongy or fibrous outer coat to help them float (e.g., Coconut, Lotus).
  • Dispersal by Animals/Humans: Fruits may be fleshy and eaten by animals (seeds passed in droppings), or seeds may have hooks/spines to cling to animal fur (e.g., Xanthium, Tiger's claw, Mango).
  • Dispersal by Explosion: Fruits burst open with a sudden jerk when ripe, scattering seeds far away (e.g., Balsam, Castor, Pea).

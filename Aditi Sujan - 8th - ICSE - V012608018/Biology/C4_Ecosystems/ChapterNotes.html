Ecosystems

1. Introduction to Ecosystem

Our environment consists of both living organisms and non-living things. These components constantly interact with each other to form a stable, self-sustaining system. An ecosystem is defined as a self-sustaining structural and functional unit of the biosphere, consisting of a community of living beings and their physical environment, interacting and exchanging materials between them.

2. Components of an Ecosystem

Every ecosystem, whether a small pond or a massive forest, has two main components: Abiotic (non-living) and Biotic (living).

A. Abiotic Components

These are the non-living physical and chemical factors that influence the survival of organisms. They include:

  • Climatic Factors: Sunlight, temperature, rainfall, wind, and humidity. Sunlight is the ultimate source of energy for all ecosystems.
  • Edaphic Factors: Soil type, soil pH, minerals, and topography (shape of the land).
  • Inorganic/Organic Substances: Water, carbon dioxide, oxygen, nitrogen, proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids found in the environment.

B. Biotic Components

These comprise all the living organisms in an ecosystem. Based on how they obtain their food, they are classified into three groups:

  • Producers (Autotrophs): Green plants, algae, and some bacteria that can manufacture their own food using sunlight through photosynthesis.
  • Consumers (Heterotrophs): Animals that cannot make their own food and depend directly or indirectly on producers. They are further divided into:
    • Primary Consumers (Herbivores): Plant-eating animals (e.g., Deer, Rabbit, Grasshopper).
    • Secondary Consumers (Carnivores): Flesh-eating animals that eat herbivores (e.g., Frog, Fox, Snake).
    • Tertiary Consumers (Top Carnivores): Animals that eat secondary consumers (e.g., Lion, Tiger, Eagle).
    • Omnivores: Animals that eat both plants and flesh (e.g., Humans, Bears, Crows).
  • Decomposers (Saprotrophs): Microorganisms like bacteria and fungi that feed on dead and decaying plants and animals.
Important

The Role of Decomposers

Decomposers are nature's recyclers. By breaking down dead organic matter, they return complex nutrients back into simple inorganic substances in the soil. Without decomposers, the earth would be covered in dead bodies, and plants would quickly run out of essential nutrients.

3. Interdependence: Food Chains and Food Webs

Food Chain

A food chain is a linear sequence of organisms through which food energy is transferred from one organism to another, starting from a producer. It shows "who eats whom."

Example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

Concept

Food Chain vs. Food Web

In nature, a simple linear food chain rarely exists because most animals eat more than one type of food. A network of many interconnected food chains forms a food web. A food web provides alternative food sources, ensuring the stability and balance of the ecosystem.

4. Trophic Levels & Ecological Pyramids

The distinct feeding steps or levels in a food chain are called trophic levels.

  • First Trophic Level (T1): Producers (Plants)
  • Second Trophic Level (T2): Primary Consumers (Herbivores)
  • Third Trophic Level (T3): Secondary Consumers (Carnivores)
  • Fourth Trophic Level (T4): Tertiary Consumers (Top Carnivores)

Pyramid of Numbers

An ecological pyramid is a graphical representation of the relationship between different trophic levels. A Pyramid of Numbers shows the number of individuals at each trophic level. In a typical forest or grassland, the base (producers) has the largest number of organisms, and the numbers decrease progressively towards the apex (top carnivores), forming an upright pyramid.

Fact

The 10% Law of Energy Flow

As energy flows from one trophic level to the next, about 90% of it is lost as heat (used for respiration, digestion, etc.). Only about 10% of the energy is transferred to the next level. This is why food chains usually cannot have more than 4 or 5 trophic levels.

5. Biological Interactions in an Ecosystem

Organisms within a community interact with each other in various ways for survival:

  • Symbiosis (Mutualism): A relationship where both organisms benefit from each other. Example: Lichens (Algae provide food, Fungi provide shelter and moisture).
  • Parasitism: One organism (parasite) benefits at the expense of the other (host). The host is harmed. Example: Ticks on a dog, Cuscuta (dodder) on a host plant.
  • Predation: One animal (predator) hunts, kills, and eats another animal (prey). Example: Lion hunting a zebra.
  • Commensalism: One organism benefits, and the other is neither helped nor harmed. Example: Orchids growing on branches of tall trees for sunlight.

6. Flora and Fauna in a Forest Ecosystem

A forest is a large area covered primarily with trees and undergrowth. It is a highly complex ecosystem.

  • Flora: Refers to all the plant life occurring in a particular region. In a forest, this includes large canopy trees, shrubs, herbs, ferns, creepers, and climbers. They form the backbone of the forest ecosystem.
  • Fauna: Refers to all the animal life in a particular region. Forest fauna is incredibly diverse, ranging from tiny insects and soil worms to birds, reptiles, and large mammals like elephants and tigers.

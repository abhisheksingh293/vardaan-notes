NERVOUS SYSTEM

1. Introduction to Coordination

The nervous system is a complex network in the body responsible for coordinating and controlling various activities. It allows organisms to sense changes in their surroundings, respond to stimuli, and maintain harmony within the body.

Concept

Coordination refers to the process of linking and organizing different parts of the body to work together effectively. It ensures that all body functions harmoniously maintain balance and respond appropriately to internal and external changes.

Types of Coordination

  • Nervous Coordination: Controlled by the nervous system using electrical signals (nerve impulses). It is fast and shows immediate responses. It involves the brain, spinal cord, and nerves. Example: Pulling your hand away from a hot surface.
  • Chemical Coordination: Controlled by hormones released from endocrine glands. These are slower but long-lasting responses suitable for regulating processes such as growth and metabolism. Example: Insulin controlling blood sugar levels.

2. Functions of the Nervous System

The nervous system performs several crucial functions:

  • Performs sensory functions by detecting changes in the environment (light, sound, touch) using organs like eyes, ears, and skin.
  • Processes the information received from sensory organs and decides what the body should do.
  • Controls quick and automatic responses to protect the body from danger (e.g., reflex actions).
  • Controls voluntary actions (actions you control like walking or writing) and involuntary actions (actions that happen automatically like breathing or heartbeat).
  • Maintains overall body balance.

3. Structural and Functional Unit: The Neuron

The nervous system is composed of special types of cells called neurons or nerve cells that carry messages in the form of electrical signals.

Parts of a Neuron

  • Cell body (Cyton): The main part of the cell containing the nucleus.
  • Dendrites: Cytoplasmic extensions of the cell body that receive messages from other neurons.
  • Axon: Sends messages to other neurons or body parts. Its length can vary from a few millimeters to a meter. It ends in branched filaments called terminal branches.

Kinds of Neurons

  • Sensory neurons: Carry messages from sensory organs to the brain or spinal cord.
  • Motor neurons (Efferent Nerves): Carry messages from the brain and spinal cord to muscles, organs, and glands. They play a key role in movement.
  • Interneurons (Association neurons): Contain both sensory and motor fibers. They carry messages in both directions and link sensory to motor neurons.
Fact

Synapse: A synapse is a small junction used for communication between two neurons. Messages are transmitted across this gap using specific chemicals called neurotransmitters. This allows the unidirectional flow of impulses.

4. Nerves and Their Types

A Nerve is formed by a bundle of neurons that transmit signals between the Central Nervous System (CNS) and the rest of the body. They are classified into three types:

  • Sensory nerve: Sends messages from sense organs to the brain or spinal cord (e.g., optic nerve of the eye).
  • Motor nerve: Carries motor signals from the brain or spinal cord to muscles and glands (e.g., nerves going to the muscles of the eyeball).
  • Mixed nerve: Contains both sensory and motor fibers.

5. The Human Nervous System

The human nervous system is broadly divided into two main parts: The Central Nervous System (CNS) and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS).

A. Central Nervous System (CNS)

Often called the central processing unit of the body, it consists of the Brain and the Spinal Cord.

The Brain

The most complex organ, located inside the skull. Its major parts are:

  • Cerebrum: The largest part of the brain. Divided into left and right hemispheres. It controls higher mental functions like memory, thinking, reasoning, emotions, and voluntary movements.
  • Cerebellum: Means "little brain." Located at the back of the brain. Its main function is to balance the body and coordinate muscular activities.
  • Brain Stem (Medulla Oblongata): A long stem-like structure at the lowest region of the brain. It controls autonomic functions such as breathing, heartbeat, and digestion.

The Spinal Cord

A long pipe-like structure arising from the medulla oblongata, running through the vertebral column. Its cross-section shows a butterfly-shaped grey matter surrounded by white matter.

  • Segments: Divided into 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, and 1 coccygeal segments.
  • Functions: Connects brain to PNS, controls reflex actions, and provides structural support.
Important

Voluntary vs Involuntary Actions: Voluntary actions (like jumping, walking) are produced consciously with the involvement of the brain and spinal cord. Involuntary actions (like digestion, sneezing) take place without consciousness or willingness.

B. Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

Includes all the nerves of the body outside the brain and spinal cord. It is divided into two divisions:

  • Somatic Nervous System: Transfers impulses from the CNS to skeletal muscles. It consists of 12 pairs of cranial nerves (emerging from the brain) and 31 pairs of spinal nerves (emerging from the spinal cord).
  • Autonomic Nervous System: Relays impulses from the CNS to involuntary organs and smooth muscles. Further divided into:
    • Sympathetic Nervous System: Prepares the body for violent actions/emergencies (stimulated by adrenaline).
    • Parasympathetic Nervous System: Re-establishes normal conditions after the violent action is over.

6. Reflex Action and Reflex Arc

A Reflex action is a quick, immediate, and automatic response to a stimulus without the involvement of the brain (it is managed by the spinal cord).

The Reflex Arc

A neural pathway that controls a reflex. The path goes from the sensory organ to the spinal cord and back to the muscle, allowing for a faster response.

Pathway Steps:

  • Stimulus: Triggers sensory receptors.
  • Receptor: Detects stimulus and generates an impulse.
  • Sensory Neuron: Carries impulse to the spinal cord.
  • Interneuron (Relay Neuron): Present in the spinal cord, processes information instantly.
  • Motor Neuron: Carries impulse away from the spinal cord to the effector.
  • Effector Organ: The muscle or gland that acts in response.

7. Types of Reflexes

  • Natural or Inborn reflex: Passed from one generation to the next, requiring no prior experience. Examples: Blinking eyes, coughing, sneezing, salivation.
  • Conditioned or Acquired reflex: Learned responses triggered by a previously neutral stimulus. Examples: Developing a fear of driving after an accident, learning to cycle/swim, salivating at the sound of a food bag.

8. Basic Terminology

  • Stimulus: An event or change in the external environment that evokes a reaction.
  • Response: The activity of the body due to the stimulus.
  • Impulse: Relaying of a coded electrical signal from a nerve cell in response to a stimulus.
  • Receptors: A cell or group of cells (sense organs) that receives stimuli.
  • Effectors: Any muscle or gland where the physical or chemical response occurs.

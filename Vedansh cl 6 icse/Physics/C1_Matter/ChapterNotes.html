Matter

1. Introduction to Matter

Look around you; everything you see, feel, or touch is a form of matter. A stone, a book, the air we breathe, and the water we drink are all examples of matter.

Concept

Matter is defined as any substance that occupies space and has mass.

1.1 Characteristics of Matter

  • Occupies Space: All objects take up a certain amount of space, which is called their volume.
  • Has Mass: Every physical object has a measurable quantity of matter, known as mass.
Important

Not everything is matter! Feelings like happiness, sadness, or physical quantities like heat and light do not occupy space or have mass. Therefore, they are not considered matter.

2. Composition of Matter

Matter is not a continuous block but is made up of incredibly tiny particles called molecules.

Concept

A molecule is the smallest particle of a substance that can exist independently in nature and retains all the physical and chemical properties of that substance.

2.1 Key Properties of Molecules

  • The size of a molecule is extremely small, roughly one-millionth of a millimeter.
  • Molecules are composed of even smaller particles called atoms. While atoms are the basic building blocks, they usually cannot exist independently.
  • There are empty spaces between the molecules of matter, known as intermolecular space.
  • Molecules attract each other with a force called intermolecular force.
  • Molecules are always in a state of continuous random motion.

3. States of Matter

Matter exists primarily in three physical states based on the arrangement of its molecules: Solid, Liquid, and Gas.

3.1 Solids

In a solid state, particles are tightly packed together.

  • Shape and Volume: Solids have a definite shape and a fixed volume.
  • Compressibility: They are rigid and cannot be compressed easily.
  • Molecular Arrangement: The intermolecular space is minimal, and the intermolecular forces are very strong. Molecules vibrate about their fixed positions but do not move freely.

Examples: Wood, rock, iron.

3.2 Liquids

In a liquid state, particles are less tightly packed than in solids.

  • Shape and Volume: Liquids have a definite volume but no definite shape (they take the shape of the container).
  • Compressibility: They are negligibly compressible and can flow easily.
  • Molecular Arrangement: Intermolecular spaces are larger than in solids, and intermolecular forces are weaker. Molecules can move within the boundary of the liquid.

Examples: Water, milk, oil.

3.3 Gases

In a gaseous state, particles are very loosely packed.

  • Shape and Volume: Gases have neither a definite shape nor a definite volume. They expand to fill any container they are placed in.
  • Compressibility: Gases are highly compressible.
  • Molecular Arrangement: Intermolecular spaces are maximum, and intermolecular forces are almost negligible. Molecules move rapidly in random directions.

Examples: Oxygen, carbon dioxide, air.

Fact

The differences in the physical states of matter are entirely due to the variation in intermolecular forces and intermolecular spaces.

4. Intermolecular Forces: Cohesion and Adhesion

The forces that hold molecules together play a crucial role in determining the state and behavior of matter.

  • Force of Cohesion: The force of attraction between the molecules of the same substance. For example, the force holding water molecules together in a droplet.
  • Force of Adhesion: The force of attraction between the molecules of different substances. For example, the force that makes water stick to a glass surface.

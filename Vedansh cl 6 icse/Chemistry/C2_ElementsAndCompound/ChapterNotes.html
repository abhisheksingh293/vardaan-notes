ELEMENTS, COMPOUNDS AND MIXTURES

1. Matter and Pure Substances

Any substance that occupies space and has mass is called matter. Matter around us can be categorized into pure and impure substances.

Concept

A pure substance is one that is made up of only one kind of particle (atoms or molecules). It has a uniform composition, definite properties, and cannot be separated into simpler substances by simple physical methods. Elements and compounds are pure substances.

2. Elements

An element is a pure substance that is made up of only one kind of atom. It cannot be broken down into simpler substances.

  • Examples: Carbon, sodium, aluminium, silver, gold, oxygen, hydrogen.

A. Atoms

Atoms are tiny particles of which everything around us is made. An atom is made up of three sub-atomic particles:

  • Protons: Positively charged particles.
  • Electrons: Negatively charged particles.
  • Neutrons: Particles that do not carry any charge.

Protons and neutrons are present inside a small, dense, and hard central core called the nucleus. Electrons move around the nucleus in fixed circular paths called orbits or energy shells.

B. Classification of Elements

Elements can be classified into metals, non-metals, metalloids, and noble gases.

  • Metals: Elements that are generally hard solids (except mercury, which is liquid), have a shiny appearance, and are good conductors of heat and electricity. Examples: Aluminium, iron, copper, gold, silver.
  • Non-metals: Elements that are generally dull in appearance, brittle (if solid), and poor conductors of heat and electricity. They exist as solids, liquids, or gases. Examples: Oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, sulphur. Bromine is the only liquid non-metal.
  • Metalloids: Elements that have some properties of both metals and non-metals. Examples: Silicon, boron, arsenic.
  • Noble Gases: Elements that are quite unreactive and occur in gaseous state in air in traces. Examples: Helium, neon, argon.

3. Symbols of Elements

A symbol is a short representation of an element. J.J. Berzelius suggested using the first letter (or first two letters) of the English or Latin name of the element.

Fact

When two letters are used, the first letter is always a capital letter and the second is a small letter (e.g., Ca for Calcium, Cl for Chlorine, Na for Sodium/Natrium).

4. Molecules

The smallest particle of a substance (element or compound) that can exist independently is called a molecule.

  • Molecule of an element: Formed when similar kinds of atoms combine. Example: Two hydrogen atoms combine to form a hydrogen molecule (H₂).
  • Molecule of a compound: Formed when dissimilar atoms combine together. Example: Two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom combine to form a water molecule (H₂O).

Classification of Molecules (Atomicity)

  • Monoatomic: Molecule made of 1 atom (e.g., Helium He).
  • Diatomic: Molecule made of 2 atoms (e.g., Oxygen O₂).
  • Triatomic: Molecule made of 3 atoms (e.g., Ozone O₃).
  • Tetraatomic: Molecule made of 4 atoms (e.g., Phosphorus P₄).
  • Polyatomic: Molecule made of many atoms (e.g., Sulphur S₈).

5. Compounds

A compound is a substance which forms when two or more elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio by mass.

Important

Properties of a Compound:
1. The constituent elements cannot be separated by physical methods (only by chemical methods like electrolysis).
2. The properties of a compound are entirely different from its constituent elements (e.g., Water extinguishes fire, but its constituents Hydrogen burns and Oxygen supports burning).
3. Elements combine in a fixed ratio by mass.
4. Energy (heat/light) is either absorbed or released when a compound is formed.

Chemical Formula

A molecule of a compound is represented by a chemical formula. It shows the elements present and the exact number of atoms of each element.

  • Water (H₂O): 2 atoms of Hydrogen, 1 atom of Oxygen.
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂): 1 atom of Carbon, 2 atoms of Oxygen.
  • Sodium chloride (NaCl): 1 atom of Sodium, 1 atom of Chlorine.
  • Sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁): 12 Carbon, 22 Hydrogen, 11 Oxygen atoms.

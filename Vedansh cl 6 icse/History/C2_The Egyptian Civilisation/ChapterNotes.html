The Egyptian Civilisation

1. Introduction to Ancient Egypt

The Egyptian Civilisation was one of the earliest civilisations in the world, lasting for over 3,000 years from 3200 BCE to 30 BCE.

Fact

The Gift of the Nile: The civilisation flourished along the banks of the River Nile. Every year, rains caused the Nile to flood, leaving behind rich, black, fertile soil. Without the Nile providing water, fertile soil, and transportation, the civilisation could not have survived.

Key Sources of Information

  • Monuments and Artefacts: Pyramids, objects, and artefacts covered with recently deciphered hieroglyphs are the main sources.
  • Literary Sources: Tomb biographies, letters, pyramid texts, and papyri.
  • The Royal Canon of Turin: A papyrus document written around 1250 BCE that lists over 300 Egyptian rulers and the length of their reigns.

2. Origin and Timelines

By 3300 BCE, villages in the Nile valley grew into towns. In the early stages, Egypt was divided into two parts: Upper Egypt and Lower Egypt. Around 3100 BCE, a pharaoh from Upper Egypt united both parts.

Important

Historians divide the history of Ancient Egypt into three major periods:

  • Old Kingdom (3000 BCE - 2000 BCE): Often referred to as the Age of Pyramids.
  • Middle Kingdom (2000 BCE - 1700 BCE)
  • New Kingdom (1600 BCE - 1100 BCE)

The Pharaonic period lasted until 30 BCE, ending when the last pharaoh, Queen Cleopatra, died.

3. Society and Life

The Class System

Egyptian society was headed by the Pharaoh, who was viewed as the descendant of god on Earth and acted as both political and religious leader. The society was divided into three main classes:

  • Upper Class: Royal family, priests, nobles, government officials, scribes, and doctors.
  • Middle Class: Merchants, manufacturers, and artisans.
  • Lower Class: Farmers and slaves (prisoners of war or debtors).

Note: The class system was not entirely rigid. People could move to a higher position through marriage or job success.

Family and Women

  • Families were headed by the father, but women had far more rights compared to other ancient societies.
  • Women could own land, inherit property, participate in trade, and even seek divorce.
  • Notable women rulers included Queen Hatshepsut, Queen Nefertiti (who ruled alongside Akhenaten), and Queen Cleopatra.

Dress and Lifestyle

People wore simple clothing made of linen (derived from the flax plant). Wealthy individuals wore softer linen, elaborate wigs, heavy jewellery, and used black eye paint called kohl to protect their eyes from the sun. Amusements included singing, dancing, hunting, and board games like Senet.

4. Occupations and Economy

  • Agriculture: The primary occupation. They grew wheat, barley, vegetables, and flax on the fertile black soil. They also planted fruit trees and vineyards.
  • Trade: Used the barter system. Traded gold, papyrus, linen, and grain for cedar wood, ivory, and lapis lazuli through land and sea routes.
  • Art and Craft: Created pottery to store grains and food, built sculptures, and painted inside tombs.
  • Transport: Donkeys and camels were used on land; boats and barges navigated the Nile. They also built the world's first seagoing ships.

5. Art, Architecture & Religion

Architecture and Monuments

Egyptians introduced features like arches, columns, and domes, using clay/mud bricks and stone. The most famous structures are the pyramids, which served as massive tombs for the Pharaohs.

  • The Great Pyramid of Giza: The largest pyramid, built by Pharaoh Khufu (Cheops). It took 10,000 workers about 20 years to build.
  • The Great Sphinx: A mythological figure with a man's head and a lion's body, carved out of a single stone block at Giza.
  • Abu Simbel Temple: Built by Ramesses II, carved directly into a solid rock cliff.
Concept

Religion and Mummification: Ancient Egyptians worshipped many gods, often depicted with animal heads (e.g., Ra the Sun God, Osiris God of the Dead, Anubis patron of embalmers). They strongly believed in life after death. They preserved bodies using herbs, spices, and linen wrappings in a process called mummification. The most famous mummy discovered is that of the boy king, Tutankhamun, found intact in 1922.

6. Achievements of the Egyptians

  • Hieroglyphics: A complex writing system meaning "sacred writing" that used picture and sound symbols.
  • Papyrus: They invented a type of paper made from the tall papyrus reed plant, writing on it with ink made from gum, soot, and water.
  • Solar Calendar: A highly accurate calendar with 365 days, divided into 12 months of 30 days, plus 5 extra holidays.
  • Medicine: Advancements in surgery and mummification gave them vast knowledge of the human body (e.g., blood circulation). The Edwin Smith Papyrus is the oldest known surgical book.
  • Astronomy & Math: Aligned pyramids using stars. Invented the merchet (for surveying) and the water clock. Excelled in fractions, decimals, and calculating area/volume.

7. Decline of the Civilisation

The powerful Egyptian empire began to decline during the reigns of Ramesses II and III due to several reasons:

  • Invasions: Attacks from the "Sea Peoples," tribesmen from Libya, and later Assyrians demolished their stability.
  • Internal Weakness: Weak rulers failed to maintain authority, leading feudal lords to gain control. Internal corruption and quarrels ruined unity.
  • Social Unrest: Ruthless exploitation of the lower class and severe droughts/famines caused massive societal disorder.

The civilisation was brought to an end by the invasion of Alexander the Great in 332 BCE. After his death, the Ptolemaic dynasty ruled until Egypt was annexed by the Roman Empire in 30 BCE.

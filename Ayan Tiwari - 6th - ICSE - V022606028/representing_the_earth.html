REPRESENTING THE EARTH

1. The Globe

The Earth is huge in size and roughly spherical in shape, making it impossible to see the entire planet from a single view. To study it effectively, we use miniature models. A globe is a three-dimensional model of the Earth that depicts its spherical shape. It is considered the best representation of our planet.

Advantages of a Globe

  • It gives the most accurate shape of the Earth.
  • It provides a three-dimensional, realistic view of all the continents and waterbodies.
  • It helps us easily see and understand imaginary lines like latitudes and longitudes.

Limitations of a Globe

  • It is bulky and difficult to carry around.
  • Only a part of the Earth can be seen at a single glance; it must be rotated.
  • It cannot be made for a very small area of the Earth, meaning fine details like small towns and specific landforms cannot be shown properly due to limited space.
Concept

Geodesy and Cartography: The science that studies the shape, size, and different measurements of the Earth is called geodesy. The art of making maps is called cartography, and the expert who generates the maps is known as a cartographer.

2. Maps and Their Utility

A map is a drawing or visual representation of the Earth's surface (or a part of it) on a flat surface, drawn according to a specific scale.

Advantages of Maps

  • They provide much more information than a globe.
  • They give extremely accurate information even for very small areas.
  • They are highly portable, can be easily carried from one place to another, occupy less space, and are easy to preserve.

Disadvantages of Maps

  • The curved surface of the Earth cannot be represented 100% accurately on a flat, two-dimensional surface.
  • Maps of very large areas, especially world maps or continents, inevitably have some distortion in size, shape, or distance.

3. Types of Maps

Maps can be classified based on the size of the scale they use or the purpose they serve.

Based on Scale:

  • Small-Scale Maps: They represent a very large area (like continents or the world) but show fewer details. Example: Atlas maps.
  • Large-Scale Maps: They represent a small area (like a village, town, or city) but show the objects in large size with great detail.

Based on Purpose:

  • Physical or Relief Map: Contains information about natural physical features like mountains, valleys, plains, and rivers.
  • Political Map: Contains information about countries, states, capitals, and national/international boundaries.
  • Thematic Map: Focuses on specific themes or topics like climate, distribution of crops, minerals, vegetation, or population.
  • Topographical Map: Contains highly detailed information about both natural features and human-made features on the Earth's surface.
Fact

Global Positioning System (GPS): Modern devices now use GPS technology. A GPS receiver picks up signals from satellites orbiting the Earth to pinpoint your exact location at any given moment!

4. Essential Features of a Map

To accurately read and interpret maps, five essential features are universally used by cartographers:

  1. Title: Indicates the area and the specific type of information being shown on the map.
  2. Direction: Uses the four cardinal directions (North, South, East, West) and eight intermediate directions (NE, SE, SW, NW). Maps usually feature an arrow pointing to the geographical North, known as the North Line.
  3. Scale: Defined as the ratio between the map distance (measured on the paper) and the corresponding ground distance (the actual distance on Earth).
  4. Conventional Symbols and Colours: Because maps have limited space, internationally accepted signs, shapes, and patterns are used to represent features (e.g., boundaries, rivers, railways).
    • Colours: Blue represents water, Green signifies plains/lowlands, Yellow is for deserts, Brown indicates highlands/mountains, and White represents snow-covered areas.
  5. Key or Legend: Acts as an index, usually located in a corner of the map, to explain the meanings of the various colours, symbols, and patterns used.

5. Sketch and Plan

Besides globes and maps, locations can also be drawn as a sketch or a plan depending on the requirement.

  • Sketch: A rough drawing based entirely on observation and memory. It is not drawn to scale. It helps in understanding relative locations or serves as a quick guide-map to reach a destination.
  • Plan: A highly detailed drawing of a horizontal section of a small area on a large scale. It is based on actual, accurate measurements. Example: A floor plan of a school, house, or office designed by an architect.
Important

While the distance between two straight points on a map is measured using a ruler, the distance along a curved line (like a winding river or a curved road) is accurately measured using a piece of twine or thread!

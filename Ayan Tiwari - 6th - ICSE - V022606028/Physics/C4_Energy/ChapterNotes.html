Energy & Simple Machines

Concept

A Simple Machine is a device that helps us to make our work easier by multiplying our effort, speeding up motion, or changing the direction of force in a desired manner.

1. Why We Need Machines

Our hands are not sufficient to perform all kinds of tasks. We use machines to complete work in lesser time with less effort. Machines are primarily employed for:

  • Lifting Heavy Loads: Applying a small effort to lift something heavy (e.g., using a spoon to pry open a tight lid).
  • Speeding up Motion: Making a task happen faster.
  • Changing Direction: Applying effort in a convenient direction (e.g., a pulley drawing water from a well).
  • Doing Unsafe Work: Handling tasks that are dangerous with bare hands (e.g., lifting burning charcoal with fire tongs).

Important Machine Terms

  • Effort (E): The force applied on the machine to do mechanical work.
  • Load (L): The weight moved or the resistance overcome by the machine.
  • Fulcrum (F): The fixed point or axis around which a machine (like a lever) turns.
  • Input Energy: The work done on the machine by the user.
  • Output Energy: The useful work done by the machine on the load.
Fact

An Ideal Machine is one where Output Energy = Input Energy (100% Efficiency). However, in real life, no machine is 100% efficient. Some energy is always wasted in overcoming friction and moving the weight of the machine's parts.

2. Levers and Their Types

A Lever is a rigid bar which can rotate about a fixed point (Fulcrum). The distance between the Fulcrum and Effort is the Effort Arm, and the distance between the Fulcrum and Load is the Load Arm.

Important

Principle of Lever: Load × Load Arm = Effort × Effort Arm.
The Mechanical Advantage (M.A.) tells us how effectively a machine multiplies our force.
M.A. = Load / Effort = Effort Arm / Load Arm.

The Three Orders of Levers

  • First Order Lever (Class I): The Fulcrum is placed between the Load and Effort. M.A. can be >1, <1, or =1.
    Examples: See-saw, scissors, crowbar, claw hammer, water pump handle.
  • Second Order Lever (Class II): The Load is situated between the Fulcrum and Effort. The Effort Arm is always longer than the Load Arm. Therefore, it is a Force Multiplier (M.A. > 1).
    Examples: Nut-cracker, wheelbarrow, bottle opener, oars of a boat.
  • Third Order Lever (Class III): The Effort is situated between the Fulcrum and Load. The Load Arm is always longer than the Effort Arm. Therefore, it is a Speed Multiplier (M.A. < 1).
    Examples: Sugar tongs, fire tongs, bread knife, fishing rod.

3. Other Simple Machines

Pulley

A pulley is a flat circular disc with a grooved edge, capable of rotating around an axle.

  • Single Fixed Pulley: Attached to a high platform. It only changes the direction of the applied effort to a more convenient one (e.g., pulling downwards to lift water). Ideal M.A. = 1.
  • Single Movable Pulley: Used to multiply force. The load can be lifted with much less effort (ideal M.A. = 2). Often used in cranes.

Inclined Plane & Wedge

  • Inclined Plane: Any sloping flat surface (like a ramp or gang plank). It requires less effort to push a load up an inclined plane than to lift it directly vertically. A winding hill road is a continuous inclined plane.
  • Wedge: A double inclined plane. Used to tear or split apart solid objects. The thinner/sharper the wedge, the easier it penetrates.
    Examples: Axe, knife, chisel, nail, plough.

Screw & Wheel and Axle

  • Screw: A spiral (winding) inclined plane cut onto a cylinder. It provides a better grip than a nail and can support larger loads.
    Examples: Nut and bolt, corkscrew, screw jack.
  • Wheel and Axle: Consists of two cylinders of different diameters joined together. The larger one is the wheel, the smaller is the axle.
    Examples: Door knob, steering wheel, screwdriver, water tap knob.

4. Maintenance and Care of Machines

If a machine is to be used for a long time, it needs proper care:

  • Lubrication: Moving parts rub against each other and wear out due to friction. Regular oiling or greasing reduces friction and prevents energy loss.
  • Painting: Non-movable parts made of iron get rusted easily in moist air. Painting prevents rusting.
  • Covering: Protect machines from dust (which increases wear and tear) by keeping them covered when not in use.

Force

Concept

Force is defined as a push or a pull. It is an external agent which changes or tends to change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body, or changes its direction or shape.

1. Effects of Force

Forces can cause various effects on objects depending on how they are applied:

  • Cause Motion: A force can cause a motion in a stationary object (e.g., pulling a chair).
  • Stop Motion: A force can stop moving objects or slow them down (e.g., applying brakes to a bicycle).
  • Speed Up: A force can make a moving object move faster (e.g., pushing a cart from behind).
  • Change Direction: A force can change the direction of moving objects (e.g., hitting a cricket ball with a bat).
  • Change Shape: A force can change the shape of objects (e.g., stretching a spring or crushing a can).

Note: A force cannot cause any effect on the mass of an object. Mass is an inherent, constant property of matter.

2. Classification of Forces

Contact vs. Non-Contact Forces

  • Contact Forces: Forces which act on a body directly or through a connector (e.g., muscular force, friction).
  • Non-Contact Forces: Forces which do not make direct contact with a body, but act through space (e.g., gravitational, magnetic, and electrical forces).

Consequential Forces

These forces do not exist on their own. A consequential force is called into play only when a force is applied on an object. For example, the force of friction only occurs when a body rolls or slides on another surface.

Forces in Nature

  • Muscular Force: The force produced by the muscles of living beings.
  • Mechanical Force: Forces generated by machines, usually powered by fuels or natural energy.
  • Gravitational Force: The force exerted by the Earth pulling all objects towards its center. Weight is basically the force of gravity acting on a body.
  • Electrical Force: The force with which an electrically charged object can pull or push other charged objects.
  • Magnetic Force: The force by which a magnet attracts or repels objects of iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt.
Fact

Gravitational force is always attractive, while the magnetic and electrostatic forces can be attractive as well as repulsive.

3. Friction and its Effects

Force of Friction is a force which acts at the surface of contact when one body moves upon another body. It always acts in the direction opposite to the direction of applied force.

Types of Friction

  • Static Friction: Comes into play when one body tends to move over another, but the actual motion has not started.
  • Sliding Friction: Comes into play when one body is actually sliding over another. It is less than static friction.
  • Rolling Friction: Comes into play when a body rolls over a surface. It is much smaller than sliding friction, which is why wheels are so important.
Important

Friction is a necessary evil. While it wastes energy, generates heat, and causes wear and tear on machinery and shoes, it is absolutely essential. Without friction, we wouldn't be able to walk, vehicles couldn't stop, and knots would untie themselves.

Reducing and Increasing Friction

  • To Reduce Friction: We use lubricants (oil, grease, talc), ball bearings, polishing surfaces, and streamlining the shapes of vehicles (making them narrow in front and back to move smoothly through fluids).
  • To Increase Friction: We provide deep grooves in vehicle tires, use spiked shoes for athletes, and make grinding stones rough.

4. Units and Measurement of Force

To quantify force, we use standard units:

  • Kilogram Force (kgf): The force required to lift a body of mass 1 kg vertically upward.
  • Gram Force (gf): The force required to lift a mass of 1 g vertically upward. (1 kgf = 1000 gf).
  • Newton (N): The Standard International (SI) unit of force.

Relation: 1 kgf is equal to 10 Newtons. The force required to lift 100 g of mass vertically upward is exactly 1 Newton.

Measuring Force

Force is measured with a Spring Balance. It relies on the extension of a spring which corresponds directly to the pull (force/weight) applied to the hook.

Addition and Cancellation of Forces

A force has both magnitude (strength) and direction.

  • When two forces are applied in the same direction, they add up (Resultant = F1 + F2).
  • When two equal forces are applied in opposite directions, they cancel each other out (Resultant = 0).
  • If opposite forces are unequal, the net force acts in the direction of the larger force (Resultant = F1 - F2).

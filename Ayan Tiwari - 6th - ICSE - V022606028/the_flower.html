THE FLOWER

1. Structure of a Flower

A flower is the reproductive part of a plant. Often, each flower has a stalk called the pedicel. The upper portion of the pedicel gets flattened and enlarged to form the thalamus. A flower generally consists of four sets of whorls arranged in rings on the thalamus:

  • Calyx: The outermost whorl consisting of green, leaf-like structures called sepals. They protect the inner parts in the bud stage.
  • Corolla: The next inner whorl consisting of brightly coloured parts called petals. They attract insects for pollination.
  • Androecium: The third whorl, representing the male reproductive part. It consists of units called stamens. Each stamen has an anther and a filament. The anther produces powdery pollen grains.
  • Gynoecium: The innermost whorl forming the central part. It represents the female reproductive part and consists of a carpel (or pistil). Each carpel has an ovary (basal swollen part), style, and stigma. The ovary contains ovules.

Types of Flowers

  • Complete Flower: Contains all four whorls (calyx, corolla, stamens, and carpel). Examples: Mustard, China-rose, Pea.
  • Incomplete Flower: One or more whorls are missing. Examples: Date palm, Mulberry.
  • Bisexual (Hermaphrodite) Flower: Has both male (stamens) and female (carpels) reproductive parts in the same flower. Examples: Mustard, Lily, Pea.
  • Unisexual Flower: Contains either only male parts or only female parts. Examples: Cucumber, Gourd.
Concept

Gametes: Gametes are the sex cells produced during sexual reproduction. The male gametes (produced in pollen grains) are usually smaller and produced in larger numbers. The female gamete (produced inside the ovule) is larger, with more cytoplasm.

2. Sexual Reproduction and Pollination

Sexual reproduction is the process by which new organisms are produced by the fusion of male and female gametes.

Pollination

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma. It takes place in two main ways:

  • Self-pollination: The transfer of pollen grains from the anthers to the stigma of the same flower, or another flower of the same plant.
  • Cross-pollination: The transfer of pollen grains from the anthers of a flower to the stigma of a flower on another plant of the same species.

Agents of Pollination

  • Insects: Insect-pollinated flowers are large, brightly-coloured, and produce scent and nectar. The pollen grains are sticky. Examples: Dahlia, Rose, Sunflower.
  • Wind: Wind-pollinated flowers are small and inconspicuous. They do not produce nectar or scent. Their pollen grains are light, dry, and produced in large numbers. The stigma is large and feathery. Examples: Wheat, Maize, Grasses.
  • Water: Water currents carry pollen grains from anthers to the stigma. Examples: Hydrilla, Vallisneria.
Fact

Did you know? The largest flower in the world is the Rafflesia (growing in Indonesia, ~1 metre in diameter). To attract flies for pollination, it produces a smell like rotting meat! The smallest flower is the Duckweed (Wolffia), measuring only about 0.5 mm in diameter.

3. Fertilisation and Formation of Fruit

Fertilisation is the fusion of the male gamete with the female gamete. After pollination, the pollen grain germinates on the stigma and a pollen tube develops. It grows downwards through the style into the ovary, carrying the male gametes to fuse with the egg cell inside the ovule. This fusion results in a zygote.

Changes After Fertilisation:

  • The flower loses its bright colour; sepals, petals, and stamens fall off.
  • The ovary increases in size and becomes the fruit.
  • The ovary wall becomes the fruit wall.
  • The ovules develop to form the seeds.

4. Parts of a Fruit

A fruit is the ripened ovary. Fruits are broadly classified as dry fruits (e.g., Pea, Cotton) and fleshy fruits (e.g., Mango, Tomato).

A fruit consists of two main parts: the seeds, and the fruit wall (called the pericarp). In fleshy fruits like mango, the pericarp is divided into three layers:

  • Epicarp: The outer thin, protective skin.
  • Mesocarp: The middle, fleshy, and sweet layer.
  • Endocarp: The inner hard, stony layer that contains the seed.

5. The Seed and Germination

The ovules after fertilisation develop into seeds. A seed consists of an embryo (baby plant), one or two cotyledons (food storage), and a protective seed coat. The embryo contains the plumule (which forms the shoot) and the radicle (which forms the root).

Types of Seeds

  • Dicotyledonous (Dicot) Seeds: Have two cotyledons. Food is stored in the cotyledons. Endosperm is absent. Examples: Gram, Pea, Bean.
  • Monocotyledonous (Monocot) Seeds: Have one cotyledon. Food is stored in the endosperm. The seed coat and fruit wall are fused. Examples: Maize, Wheat, Rice.

Seed Germination

Seed Germination is the process by which the embryo of a seed becomes active and grows into a new plant. It starts with the rapid uptake of water, which activates enzymes to digest stored food.

  • Epigeal Germination: Cotyledons are pushed above the soil because the hypocotyl elongates faster. Examples: Bean, Castor, Sunflower.
  • Hypogeal Germination: Cotyledons remain below the soil because the epicotyl elongates faster. Examples: Maize, Gram, Pea.
Important

Conditions Necessary for Germination:
1. Water: Softens the seed coat and dissolves stored food for the embryo.
2. Oxygen (Air): Required for respiration.
3. Favourable Temperature: A temperature ranging from 25°C to 35°C is essential for the healthy growth of the seedling.

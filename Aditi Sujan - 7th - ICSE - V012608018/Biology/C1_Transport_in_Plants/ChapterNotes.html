Transport in Plants

1. Introduction to Transportation

Plants prepare their own food by a process called photosynthesis, requiring carbon dioxide and water. While carbon dioxide is taken from the atmosphere, water and minerals must be absorbed from the soil by the roots and moved upwards to the leaves.

Transportation in plants is the process by which substances absorbed or synthesized in one part of the plant are moved to other parts. This is carried out by a conducting system consisting of two main tissues: xylem and phloem, which together form the vascular bundle.

2. The Conducting Tissues (Vascular Bundle)

A. Xylem Tissue (Water Transport)

Xylem forms a tubular passage to transport water and mineral salts from the roots to the aerial parts of the plant. It consists of four types of cells:

  • Xylem Tracheids: Elongated dead cells with tapering ends. They provide mechanical strength and conduct water upwards.
  • Xylem Vessels: Tube-like structures open at both ends, placed one above the other to form long channels. They are dead cells that conduct water and provide support.
  • Xylem Parenchyma: Small, thick-walled, living cells. They store food and help conduct water. (Only living part of xylem).
  • Xylem Sclerenchyma (Fibres): Thick-walled, long, narrow dead cells that provide mechanical support.

B. Phloem Tissue (Food Transport)

Phloem transports the food manufactured by the leaves to various parts of the plant (a process called translocation). It consists of four types of cells:

  • Sieve Tubes: Cylindrical cells devoid of a nucleus, arranged in vertical rows. Their end walls are perforated (sieve plates) allowing food material to pass.
  • Companion Cells: Living, thin-walled cells attached to the sides of sieve tubes, helping them in the conduction of food.
  • Phloem Parenchyma: Thin-walled living cells that store food.
  • Phloem Fibres: Dead sclerenchyma fibres that provide mechanical strength.
Important

Differences Between Xylem and Phloem

  • Material Conducted: Xylem conducts water and minerals; Phloem translocates food.
  • Direction: Xylem conduction is unidirectional (upwards); Phloem conduction is bidirectional (upwards and downwards).
  • Nature of Cells: Conducting cells in xylem are mostly dead; conducting cells in phloem (sieve tubes) are living.
  • Energy: Conduction in xylem does not require energy expenditure; Phloem requires expenditure of energy.

3. Water Absorption by the Roots

Roots have a main root and lateral roots that bear fine outgrowths called root hairs. Root hairs are perfectly suited for absorbing water because:

  • They provide a large surface area for greater absorption.
  • They contain cell sap of a higher concentration than the surrounding soil water.
  • They have a freely permeable cell wall and a semi-permeable cell membrane (allowing only water molecules to pass easily).
Concept

Types of Movement in Plants

1. Diffusion: The movement of molecules (gas, liquid, or solid) from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration.

2. Osmosis: The movement of water molecules from their region of higher concentration (dilute solution) to a region of lower concentration (concentrated solution) across a semi-permeable membrane.

3. Active Transport: The movement of molecules (like minerals) from their lower concentration to a higher concentration, which requires energy in the form of ATP.

4. Ascent of Sap & Root Pressure

Ascent of Sap: The upward movement of water and dissolved minerals (called sap) from the roots, through the xylem, to the stem and leaves.

Root Pressure: The pressure developed in the root due to the continuous inflow of water because of cell-to-cell osmosis. This pressure pushes the plant sap upwards into the xylem vessels.

5. Transpiration

Only a little amount of water absorbed is utilized by the plant. The rest evaporates into the atmosphere as water vapour through the stomata present in the leaves. This process is called transpiration.

Transpiration creates a suction force in the xylem known as the transpirational pull, which is crucial for pulling water upwards in very tall trees.

Fact

Factors Affecting the Rate of Transpiration

  • Sunlight: Increases rate (stomata open during the day).
  • Temperature: Increases rate (hot days cause faster evaporation).
  • Wind: Increases rate (blows away water vapour from leaf surface faster).
  • Humidity: Decreases rate (moisture-laden air cannot hold more water).

Importance of Transpiration

  • Cooling Effect: Evaporation utilizes the plant's latent heat, cooling it down on hot days.
  • Maintains Sap Concentration: Prevents the sap from becoming too dilute, ensuring continuous absorption of new water and minerals.

6. Importance of Minerals

Plants absorb nutrient elements (minerals) from the soil to grow well and complete their life cycle. These are divided into two broad categories:

A. Macro-nutrients (Required in larger amounts)

  • Nitrogen (N): Major constituent of all proteins. Deficiency: Yellowing of leaves, wrinkling of cereal grains.
  • Phosphorus (P): Constituent of cell membrane. Deficiency: Purple and red spots on leaves, delay in seed germination.
  • Potassium (K): Involved in opening and closing of stomata. Deficiency: Poor growth and reduced transpiration rate.

B. Micro-nutrients (Required in very small amounts)

  • Iron (Fe): Constituent of some proteins and enzymes. Deficiency: Yellowing of leaves.
  • Manganese (Mn): Constituent of some enzymes. Deficiency: Yellowing of leaves with grey spots.
  • Zinc (Zn): Constituent of plant hormones, activates enzymes. Deficiency: Deshaped leaves, yellowing, stunted plant growth.

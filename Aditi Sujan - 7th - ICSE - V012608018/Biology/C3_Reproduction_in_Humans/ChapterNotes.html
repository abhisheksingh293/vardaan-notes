Reproduction in Humans

1. Introduction to Human Reproduction

Like most highly evolved animals, humans reproduce sexually. This means reproduction requires the involvement of two parents: a male and a female. Both parents produce highly specialized reproductive cells called gametes.

Concept

Gametes and Zygote

  • Male Gamete: Known as the sperm. It is a tiny, motile (swimming) cell produced by the male.
  • Female Gamete: Known as the ovum or egg. It is larger, non-motile, and produced by the female.
  • Zygote: The single cell formed by the fusion of the sperm and the egg during fertilization. It is the first cell of the new individual.

2. The Male Reproductive System

The main function of the male reproductive system is to produce the male gametes (sperms) and deliver them to the female reproductive tract.

Parts of the Male Reproductive System:

  • Testes (Singular: Testis): A pair of oval-shaped primary reproductive organs. They produce millions of sperms and the male sex hormone called testosterone.
  • Scrotum: A pouch-like sac located outside the abdominal cavity that holds the testes.
  • Epididymis: A coiled tube attached to each testis where sperms mature and are stored temporarily.
  • Sperm Duct (Vas Deferens): A narrow tube that carries sperms from the epididymis to the urethra.
  • Accessory Glands: The seminal vesicles, prostate gland, and Cowper's gland secrete nutrient-rich fluids. This fluid mixes with sperms to form a milky liquid called semen. The fluid nourishes the sperms and provides a medium for them to swim.
  • Urethra & Penis: The urethra is a common tube for the passage of both urine and semen. It passes through a muscular organ called the penis, which is used to deposit semen into the female's body.
Important

Why are testes located in the scrotum?

The scrotum hangs outside the main body cavity. This keeps the temperature of the testes about 2°C to 3°C lower than the normal body temperature. This slightly cooler temperature is absolutely essential for the healthy production and survival of sperms.

3. The Female Reproductive System

The female reproductive system is designed to produce eggs, receive sperms, and provide a safe, nourishing environment for a baby to grow before birth.

Parts of the Female Reproductive System:

  • Ovaries: A pair of almond-shaped primary reproductive organs located inside the lower abdomen. They produce female gametes (ova/eggs) and female sex hormones (oestrogen and progesterone). Every month, one mature egg is released by one of the ovaries into the fallopian tube. This is called ovulation.
  • Oviducts (Fallopian Tubes): Two funnel-shaped tubes that receive the egg from the ovary. Fertilization takes place inside the fallopian tubes.
  • Uterus (Womb): A hollow, thick-walled, muscular, pear-shaped organ. If an egg is fertilized, it attaches to the wall of the uterus and develops into a baby here.
  • Vagina: A muscular tube extending from the lower end of the uterus (cervix) to the outside of the body. It receives the sperms during mating and also serves as the birth canal.

4. Fertilization

The process of the fusion of the sperm's nucleus with the egg's nucleus is called fertilization. In humans, internal fertilization takes place.

  • Millions of sperms deposited in the vagina swim upwards through the uterus and reach the fallopian tubes.
  • If an egg is present in the fallopian tube, the sperms surround it.
  • Only one sperm successfully penetrates the egg and fuses with it.
  • The resulting fused cell is called the zygote.

5. Pregnancy and Development of the Embryo

After fertilization, the zygote begins to divide rapidly to form a ball of cells. It travels down the fallopian tube and reaches the uterus.

  • Implantation: The ball of cells embeds itself into the thick, blood-rich inner lining of the uterus. The developing structure is now called an embryo.
  • Placenta & Umbilical Cord: A special disc-like tissue called the placenta develops between the embryo and the uterine wall. The embryo is connected to the placenta by a tube called the umbilical cord. Through this cord, the growing embryo receives oxygen and dissolved nutrients from the mother's blood, and passes back waste products (like carbon dioxide).
  • Foetus: By about the end of 8 weeks, all the major body organs (hands, legs, head, eyes) begin to form, and the embryo starts looking like a human. At this stage, it is referred to as a foetus.
  • Gestation Period: The total time taken for the complete development of the foetus inside the mother's womb is called the gestation period or pregnancy. In humans, it is about 280 days (or 9 months).
Fact

Twins: Identical vs. Fraternal

  • Identical Twins: Formed when a single fertilized egg (zygote) splits into two separate embryos early in development. They share the exact same genes, look exactly alike, and are always the same sex.
  • Fraternal Twins: Formed when two separate eggs are released by the ovaries and get fertilized by two different sperms at the same time. They are like regular siblings born together and can be of the same or different sexes.

6. Birth and Aftercare

When the gestation period is complete, the baby is ready to be born.

  • The muscular walls of the uterus begin to contract rhythmically (labour pains).
  • These strong contractions push the baby downwards and out of the mother's body through the vagina (birth canal).
  • Shortly after the baby is born, the umbilical cord is tied and cut, making the baby independent.
  • The mother’s body produces milk in her mammary glands to feed and nourish the newborn baby (a process called lactation).

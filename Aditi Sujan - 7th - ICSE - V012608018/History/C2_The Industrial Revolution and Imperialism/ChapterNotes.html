THE INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION AND IMPERIALISM

1. Overview of the Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution marked a period of major industrialisation and innovation in the late 18th century and early 19th century. It was a transition from an agricultural-dominated economy to an industrial-dominated economy.

  • Previously, goods were handmade by local craftsmen using simple tools (the domestic system).
  • Increased demand led rich merchants to set up factories and install new machines, leading to large-scale production.
  • The cotton cloth production became the primary industry because raw materials (like water transport) were cheap and efficient.

2. Why the Industrial Revolution Started in Britain

Several crucial conditions converged in Britain, making it the first industrialized nation:

  • Stable Government: Provided political stability, allowing traders the freedom to trade anywhere.
  • Agricultural Revolution: More food was available, leading to population growth and a steady workforce.
  • Climate and Raw Materials: Britain’s cold, damp climate was ideal for raising sheep (textiles).
  • Natural Resources: Britain possessed an abundant supply of coal and iron, essential for fueling and building factories.
  • Strong Navy and Colonisation: A powerful navy expanded the British Empire to places like India and Africa, securing cheap raw materials and markets for finished goods.
  • Collapse of Feudal Economy: Freed peasants and serfs became available to work as labourers in factories.
  • Transportation: A well-developed network of rivers and roads made moving goods cheaper and easier.
Important

Early British Industries: In the 17th century, major industries included Coal mining (North Britain), Tin mining (Cornwall), and Lead mining (Derbyshire).

3. Major Inventions in Britain

New innovations rapidly transformed production methods, especially in the textile industry:

  • Flying Shuttle (1733): Invented by John Kay.
  • Spinning Jenny (1764): Invented by James Hargreaves. A worker could make many threads at once, greatly increasing production.
  • Water Frame: Invented by Richard Arkwright. It used fast-moving rivers to power spindles.
  • Cotton Gin (1793): Invented by Eli Whitney. Cleaned seeds from cotton rapidly; a worker could do the work of 1,000 people.
Fact

The Steam Engine: Invented by James Watt in 1769. It replaced water and horse power, generating energy for factory machines and later powering locomotives and ships.

4. Spread of the Industrial Revolution

During the 19th century, industrialisation spread from Britain to other parts of the world:

  • Belgium: The earliest centre in continental Europe, focusing on coal, iron, textile, and glass.
  • United States: Began in the 1820s but exploded into an industrial power by the late 19th century due to abundant resources and machine tools.
  • France: Started late due to the French Revolution but grew significantly in textile production and railways by the mid-19th century.
  • Germany: Delayed by political unrest but became a world leader in industrial production after its unification in 1871.
  • Russia: Promoted by the Tsarist government (e.g., building the Trans-Siberian line). By 1900, Russia ranked fourth in world steel production.
  • Japan: Modernised rapidly after the 1871 Iwakura Mission. Set up industries like shipbuilding, silk, cotton, and chemicals.

5. Impact of the Industrial Revolution

The revolution brought massive changes, both positive and negative, to global societies.

Positive Impacts

  • Emergence of New Towns: Widespread urbanisation as people migrated from villages to cities (e.g., Liverpool and Manchester).
  • Improvement in Transportation: Railroads allowed manufacturers to transport materials cheaply, creating jobs for miners and railroad workers.
  • Rise of Capitalism: A system where production means are privately owned to earn profits.

Negative Impacts

  • Harsh Working Conditions: Factory workers faced long hours (12-17 hours/day) and unsafe environments.
  • Exploitation of Workers & Child Labour: Owners paid minimal wages. The employment of children skyrocketed due to their cheap labor.
  • Slum Living: Cities became overcrowded. Workers lived in unhygienic conditions, leading to diseases like cholera and typhoid.
Concept

Social Class Divide: Society split into two distinct classes. The Factory Owners (Capitalists) controlled the wealth and lived luxuriously, while the Factory Workers formed the lower class, facing severe exploitation.

Social Movements

  • Labour Unions: By the 1830s, workers organized strikes and unions to demand their rights.
  • Rise of Socialism and Communism: Thinkers like Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels fought for workers' rights, advocating for a classless society where production is collectively owned.

6. Rise of Imperialism

Imperialism means extending the rule or authority of a powerful country over a weaker country (often in Asia or Africa) either through military, economic, or political means.

Causes of Imperialism

  • Need of Markets: European capitalists produced surplus goods and needed new territories to sell them.
  • Requirement of Cheap Labour and Raw Materials: Industrialised nations required rubber, tin, cotton, and manganese.
  • Protective Trade Policy: European countries could not sell goods to each other due to high tariffs, forcing them to look to colonies.
  • Nationalism: Extreme nationalistic pride led countries to acquire colonies for international prestige.
  • Balance of Power: Countries sought new territories to stay economically and militarily level with their neighbors.

7. Impact of Imperialism

Colonial powers treated their subjects as slaves, exploiting natural resources and forcing locals to buy European manufactured goods. This led to poverty, unemployment, and the destruction of indigenous cultures.

Impact of Imperialism on India

Under British rule, the Indian socio-economic and political sphere was heavily impacted:

  • Destruction of Indian Industry: The British forced India to import British goods, causing local cloth, metal, and carpentry industries to collapse.
  • Mismanagement and Famines: The British shifted agricultural focus to cash crops like indigo and cotton instead of food. This caused massive food shortages, leading to millions dying in famines (like the Bengal Famine of 1770).
  • Divide and Rule Policy: The British deliberately broke up strong kingdoms and paid religious leaders to speak out against each other, preventing a unified resistance.
  • Protection from External Enemies: While India was heavily exploited as the "jewel in the crown," the British military did protect India from external threats like France and Afghanistan to safeguard their own assets.

POPULATION DYNAMICS

1. Distribution of Population in the World

The pattern in which people are spread over the Earth is known as population distribution. The world's population is spread very unevenly. Nearly three-quarters of the world's population lives in just two continents: Asia and Africa.

Concept

Density of Population is the measure of the number of people living in one unit area of the Earth. It is commonly represented as people per square kilometre.
Formula: Population Density = Total Population / Total Land Area

Based on density, regions are classified into three types:

  • Areas of High Density: Have favourable climate, fertile soil, level land, and good transport. Examples: Eastern Asia, South Asia, Western Europe.
  • Areas of Medium Density: Transition zones showing improving economic activities. Examples: Central USA, plateau region of India.
  • Areas of Low Density: Harsh climatic conditions and rugged terrain (like mountains, hot and cold deserts, polar regions).

2. Factors Affecting Population of a Place

The population of any place is largely affected by Geographical (Physical), Economic, and Social factors.

Geographical (Physical) Factors

  • Relief: Plains are highly populated because they are suitable for agriculture and building infrastructure. Mountains and plateaus are less suitable.
  • Climate: Moderate climates are most favourable. Extreme climates (too hot, too cold, or too dry) restrict human habitation.
  • Soil & Water: Fertile river valleys (e.g., Ganga plains) and areas near freshwater sources attract dense settlements.
  • Natural Vegetation: Dense, inaccessible forests (e.g., the Amazon Basin) usually have a low population density.

Economic and Social Factors

  • Mineral Resources & Industries: Areas rich in minerals or heavy industrial development attract people due to job opportunities.
  • Transport & Urbanisation: Better infrastructure and transport facilities in urban areas pull populations from rural areas.
  • Government Policies: Forced migrations or specific government schemes can alter population density drastically.

3. Factors Affecting Growth Rate of Population

Population change is driven primarily by birth rates, death rates, and migration.

  • Birth Rate: The number of live births per 1,000 people in a given year.
  • Death Rate: The number of deaths per 1,000 people in a given year.
Important

The difference between the birth rate and the death rate of a country is called its natural growth rate. The massive population increase in the world is mainly due to a rapid increase in this natural growth rate (deaths declining due to medical advances while births remain high).

Migration

Migration is the movement of people across boundaries. It can be:

  • Emigration (Out-migration): People leaving their country (Emigrants).
  • Immigration (In-migration): People arriving in a new country (Immigrants).

4. Understanding Composition of Population

Population composition describes characteristics such as age, sex, literacy, occupation, and rural-urban divide.

Age Composition

  • 0-14 years (Children): Dependent population.
  • 15-64 years (Working): Independent/Earning population.
  • 65+ years (Elderly): Dependent population.

Sex Composition (Sex Ratio)

Sex ratio is the number of females per 1,000 males. In developed nations, it is often in favour of females, whereas in some developing countries (like India), males outnumber females.

Literacy and Rural-Urban Status

Literacy (the ability to read and write with understanding) directly reflects a country's socio-economic development. Globally, there is a massive shift happening towards Urbanisation, with people migrating from rural to urban areas for better opportunities.

5. Population Pyramid

Fact

A population pyramid (or age-sex pyramid) is a bar chart that shows the distribution of a population by age and sex. Males are usually shown on the left and females on the right.

The shape of the pyramid tells a story about the country's growth:

  • Wide Base, Narrow Top (e.g., Nigeria): High birth rates and high death rates. Typical of less developed nations with rapid growth.
  • Uniform Shape (e.g., USA): Low birth and death rates. Indicates a slow-growing, stable population.
  • Narrow Base (e.g., Germany): Death rate exceeds the birth rate, resulting in a negative population growth and an aging population.

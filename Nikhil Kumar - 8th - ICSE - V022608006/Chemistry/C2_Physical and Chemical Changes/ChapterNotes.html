PHYSICAL AND CHEMICAL CHANGES

1. Introduction to Changes

Change is a permanent feature in nature and everyday life. It affects the position, shape, size, colour, taste, state, temperature, composition, or structure of objects around us. Change is a natural law, and everything experiences change over time.

Changes occurring constantly around us include:

  • Cooking of food
  • Drying of clothes
  • Burning of fuels
  • Formation of curd from milk
  • Melting of ice

2. Physical Changes

Concept

A physical change is a temporary and reversible change in which the physical properties of a substance (like state, shape, and size) are altered, but no new substances are formed. The chemical composition of the substance remains strictly the same.

Examples of Physical Changes

  • Interconversion of states of water: When ice (solid) is heated, it changes into water (liquid). Further heating turns it into water vapour (gas). Cooling reverses the process. No new substance is formed.
  • Tearing of paper: Tearing a paper results in altering its shape and size, but the underlying substance remains identical.
  • Dissolving a solid in a liquid: Making a salt solution by dissolving salt in water is a physical change because evaporation of water yields the original salt back.
  • Melting of butter: It changes shape and size without forming new chemical substances.
  • Hammering metals: Beating metals to form thin sheets changes their shape, but the chemical properties remain unaltered.

Properties of Physical Changes

  • No new substances: Only physical appearance or state is altered.
  • Reversible: Most physical changes can be easily reversed (e.g., melting and freezing).
  • No change in mass: The mass of the substance before and after the change remains identical.
  • Energy changes: Only a little heat (if any) is absorbed or given off during a physical change.

3. Chemical Changes

Important

A chemical change is a permanent change in which the chemical composition of a substance is altered, leading to the formation of one or more new substances with entirely different properties. It is an irreversible process.

Examples of Chemical Changes

  • Burning of magnesium ribbon: It burns with a dazzling white flame to form powdery ash (Magnesium oxide), which has entirely different properties from magnesium.
    Magnesium (Mg) + Oxygen (O2) → Magnesium oxide (MgO)
  • Rusting of iron: Iron exposed to air and humidity forms a reddish-brown substance called rust. The chemical properties of rust are completely different from pure iron.
  • Charring of sugar: Heating sugar intensely leaves behind a black powdery substance (charcoal) that cannot be converted back into sugar.
  • Digestion of food: Large food particles are chemically broken down into simpler, absorbable components.
  • Cooking of food: Cooking alters the taste, odour, and chemical composition of the ingredients.

Properties of Chemical Changes

  • New substances: Substances with entirely new properties are formed.
  • Irreversible: The change cannot be reversed by simple physical methods (e.g., you cannot get milk back from curd).
  • Energy exchange: Chemical changes are always accompanied by a significant change in energy in the form of heat, light, or sound (e.g., burning of petroleum).
  • Change in weight: There is usually a change in the weight of individual reacting substances (though total mass is conserved). For example, rusted iron weighs more than the original iron due to the addition of oxygen.

4. Evaporation and its Factors

Evaporation is a physical process by which a liquid at a temperature lower than its boiling point turns into vapour. It is crucial for processes like the drying of clothes and the natural water cycle.

Factors affecting the rate of evaporation:

  • Nature of liquid: Liquids like petrol and kerosene evaporate much faster than water.
  • Temperature: The rate of evaporation increases with an increase in temperature.
  • Surface area: Evaporation is a surface phenomenon. A larger surface area leads to faster evaporation.
  • Humidity: High humidity in the air slows down evaporation, while dry air accelerates it.
Fact

Louis Pasteur (1822-1895) discovered pasteurisation, a process which kills harmful bacteria in milk by heating it to 63°C for 30 minutes and quickly chilling it to 10°C or less. This keeps it fresh for several days without affecting its taste.

5. Changes Involve Interaction and Energy

Interaction: Interaction between substances leads to changes in their properties. For instance, sharpening a pencil with a blade affects both objects. The pencil gets sharpened, and over time, the blade becomes blunt. Woodcutter's axe blunting against wood is another example where the interaction affects both materials.

Energy: Energy initiates change and can be absorbed or evolved as heat, light, or sound.

  • Physical Changes: Processes like the change of state (ice to water) or dissolution usually involve absorbing or releasing heat energy.
  • Chemical Changes: Involve rearranging atoms to create new substances, releasing heat and light (e.g., burning), or absorbing sunlight (e.g., photosynthesis in plants, which is an endothermic reaction).

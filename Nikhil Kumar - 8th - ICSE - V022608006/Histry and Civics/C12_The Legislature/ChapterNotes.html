The Legislature

Concept The Legislature is the law-making organ of the government. In India, at the central level, the legislature is known as the Parliament or Sansad. It consists of the President and two Houses: the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha.

1. The Union Parliament

India follows a Bicameral system of legislature, meaning it has two houses. The Parliament is the supreme legislative body of the Republic of India.

The Lok Sabha (House of the People)

  • Composition: Maximum strength is 552. Currently, it has 543 elected members.
  • Election: Members are directly elected by the people through Universal Adult Franchise.
  • Term: 5 years, but can be dissolved earlier by the President.
  • Presiding Officer: The Speaker, elected by the members of the Lok Sabha.
Fact To be a member of the Lok Sabha, a person must be a citizen of India and at least 25 years of age.

The Rajya Sabha (Council of States)

  • Composition: Maximum strength is 250. 238 are elected by State Legislative Assemblies, and 12 are nominated by the President for excellence in art, science, literature, or social service.
  • Nature: It is a Permanent House; it cannot be dissolved.
  • Term: Each member is elected for 6 years. One-third of the members retire every two years.
  • Presiding Officer: The Vice-President of India is the ex-officio Chairperson.

2. Powers and Functions of Parliament

Legislative Powers

The Parliament makes laws on subjects mentioned in the Union List and Concurrent List. In case of conflict on the Concurrent List, the central law prevails.

Important Residual Powers: Any subject not mentioned in the three lists (Union, State, Concurrent) falls under the jurisdiction of the Parliament.

Financial Powers

The Parliament controls the national finances. No tax can be levied or money spent without its approval. The Annual Budget is presented before the Parliament for its sanction.

Control over the Executive

The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha. Parliament exercises control through:

  • Question Hour: Members ask questions to ministers regarding government policies.
  • No-Confidence Motion: If passed in the Lok Sabha, the entire government must resign.
  • Adjournment Motion: To discuss a matter of urgent public importance.

3. The Law-Making Process (How a Bill becomes an Act)

A Bill is a proposal for a new law. It passes through several stages:

  1. First Reading: Introduction of the bill.
  2. Second Reading: General discussion and clause-by-clause consideration.
  3. Committee Stage: Detailed examination by a selected group of members.
  4. Third Reading: Voting on the final version of the bill.
  5. Approval by the Other House: Sent to the second house for a similar process.
  6. President's Assent: Once both houses pass it, the President signs it, and it becomes an Act.
Concept Money Bills: These can only be introduced in the Lok Sabha with the prior recommendation of the President. The Rajya Sabha can only delay a Money Bill for 14 days.

4. The Speaker of the Lok Sabha

The Speaker is the presiding officer who maintains order and decorum in the House. Key responsibilities include:

  • Presiding over the meetings of the Lok Sabha.
  • Deciding whether a bill is a Money Bill or not.
  • Exercising a Casting Vote in case of a tie.
  • Ensuring that members follow the rules of the House.

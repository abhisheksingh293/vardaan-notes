UNDERSTANDING FORCE AND PRESSURE AROUND US

1. Force

A pull or push acting on a body which tends to change its state of rest or motion, or its shape or size, is defined as a force. Force is a vector quantity; it requires both magnitude and direction to be completely specified.

Effects of Force

  • It can stop a moving body.
  • It can produce motion in a stationary body.
  • It can change the shape and size of a body (e.g., squeezing a toothpaste tube).
  • It can change the direction of motion of a body.
  • It can change the speed of a moving body.
Important Units of Force:
• SI Unit: Newton (N). One newton is the force that increases the speed of a 1 kg body by 1 m/s every second.
• Other Units: Gram force (gf) and Kilogram force (kgf).
• Relation: 1 kgf = 9.8 N (approximately 10 N).

2. Turning Effect of Force (Moment of Force)

When a force is applied to a body pivoted at a point, it causes the body to turn about that point. This turning effect is known as the Moment of Force (or torque).

Concept Moment of Force (M) is the product of the magnitude of force (F) and the perpendicular distance (d) between the point of application of force and the pivoted point.

Formula: M = F × d
SI Unit: Newton-metre (Nm)

Factors Affecting Turning Effect

  • Magnitude of Applied Force: Larger force leads to a greater turning effect.
  • Perpendicular Distance: The greater the distance from the pivot, the greater the turning effect. This is why handles are placed at the far edge of a door!

Applications of Turning Effect

  • A door is pushed near the edge rather than near the hinges to maximize distance and reduce the required force.
  • Unscrewing a tight nut is easier with a long-handled wrench.
  • A potter's wheel is turned using a stick at the outer rim of the wheel.

3. Thrust and Pressure

The effect of a force depends heavily on the area over which it acts. A smaller area of contact results in a greater effect.

Fact Thrust vs. Pressure:
• Thrust: The total force acting exactly perpendicular to a surface. Its SI unit is the Newton (N). It does not depend on area.
• Pressure: The thrust (perpendicular force) acting per unit area of a surface.
Concept Pressure Formula: Pressure (P) = Force (F) / Area of Contact (A)

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa). 1 Pascal = 1 N/m².
Pressure increases if the force acts on a smaller area, and decreases if the force acts on a larger area.

Real-Life Applications of Pressure

  • Camels: Walk easily on sand because they have broad feet, which increases the area and reduces pressure on the soft sand.
  • Knives and Pins: Sharp knives and pointed nails have tiny contact areas, exerting huge pressure that easily cuts or pierces materials.
  • Heavy Machinery: Bulldozers and army tanks use broad steel "caterpillar tracks" instead of wheels to reduce pressure and prevent sinking.
  • Bags & Porters: Shopping bags have wide straps, and porters use round cloth turbans to increase contact area and reduce pressure on the body.

4. Pressure Exerted by Liquids

Liquids exert pressure not just downwards, but on all sides of their container. The pressure exerted on the walls is called lateral pressure. Liquid pressure is measured using a device called a manometer.

Factors Affecting Liquid Pressure

  • Height/Depth of Liquid Column: Pressure increases directly as the depth of the liquid increases.
  • Density of Liquid: Pressure is directly proportional to density (e.g., seawater exerts more pressure than fresh river water).
Important Hydrostatic Paradox: The pressure exerted by a liquid column depends ONLY on the height of the liquid and its density, not on the shape or size of the containing vessel. Additionally, at any given depth, a liquid exerts the same pressure in all directions.

Consequences of Liquid Pressure

  • Dams: The walls of dams are built much thicker at the bottom to withstand the enormous lateral pressure at greater depths.
  • Water Tanks: Built at a height so that the high liquid column provides sufficient pressure to reach all house floors.
  • Deep-sea Divers: Must wear specially designed strong suits to protect their bodies from crushing extreme water pressure at great depths.

5. Pressure Exerted by Gases & Atmosphere

According to the kinetic theory, loosely packed gas molecules are in continuous random motion. They exert force when they strike the walls of a container, creating gas pressure.

Atmospheric Pressure

The Earth is surrounded by an envelope of air extending up to 300 km. The force exerted by this atmosphere on a unit area of the Earth's surface is called atmospheric pressure.

  • Value: At sea level, it is roughly 101,325 N/m² (or 100 kPa or 1 atm).
  • Why we don't feel it: Our bodies do not crush because the blood and fluids inside our bodies exert an internal pressure slightly higher than the atmospheric pressure outside.
  • Variation: Atmospheric pressure decreases with an increase in altitude.

Applications of Atmospheric Pressure

  • Straws & Syringes: Sucking a straw or pulling a syringe piston lowers the pressure inside. The higher atmospheric pressure outside then pushes the liquid up into the straw or syringe.
  • Suction Pads: When pressed against a wall, the air is expelled, creating a vacuum. The high outside atmospheric pressure pins the pad firmly to the wall (Lizards use this same principle on their feet!).
  • Nose Bleeding at Altitudes: Mountaineers experience lower atmospheric pressure at high altitudes, while their internal blood pressure remains the same. This pressure difference forces blood to ooze out of the nose.

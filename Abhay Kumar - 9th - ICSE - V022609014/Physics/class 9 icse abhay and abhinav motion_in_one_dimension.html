MOTION IN ONE DIMENSION

1. Scalar and Vector Quantities

Physical quantities are primarily classified into two broad categories based on whether they require direction to describe them completely:

Concept
  • Scalar Quantities: Physical quantities expressed only by their magnitude (numerical value + unit). They do not require direction.
    Examples: Mass, length, time, distance, density, volume, speed, and temperature.
  • Vector Quantities: Physical quantities that require magnitude as well as direction to express them completely.
    Examples: Displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum, and force.

2. Rest and Motion

A body is said to be at rest if it does not change its position with respect to its immediate surroundings. A body is in motion if it continuously changes its position with respect to its immediate surroundings.

One Dimensional Motion

When a body moves along a straight line path, its motion is said to be one dimensional motion (also called rectilinear motion). Example: A stone falling vertically downwards.

3. Distance and Displacement

Fact

Distinction Between Distance and Displacement:

  • Distance: It is the total length of the path traversed by an object. It is a scalar quantity, depends on the path followed, and is always positive. It can never be less than displacement.
  • Displacement: It is the shortest distance from the initial position to the final position of the body. It is a vector quantity, does not depend on the path, and can be positive, negative, or zero. It is zero if the body returns to its starting point.

Both distance and displacement have the same S.I. unit: metre (m).

4. Speed and Velocity

Speed

The rate of change of distance with time is called speed. It is a scalar quantity.

  • Uniform Speed: Covers equal distances in equal intervals of time.
  • Non-uniform (Variable) Speed: Covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time.
  • Average Speed: Total distance travelled divided by the total time taken.
  • Instantaneous Speed: Speed at any given instant of time (measured by speedometers).

Velocity

Velocity is the distance travelled per second by a body in a specified direction (or rate of change of displacement). It is a vector quantity.

  • A body moving in a circular path with uniform speed has variable velocity because its direction changes continuously.
  • If a body completes a round trip, its displacement is zero; thus, the average velocity is zero, but the average speed is not zero.

5. Acceleration and Retardation

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with time. It is a vector quantity.

  • Formula: Acceleration = (Change in velocity) / (Time interval) or a = (v - u) / t
  • S.I. Unit: Metre per second square (m/s²).
  • Retardation: If the velocity of a body decreases with time, it has a negative acceleration, commonly called deceleration or retardation.
  • Acceleration due to gravity (g): When a body falls freely, its velocity increases at a constant rate of approximately 9.8 m/s². This value does not depend on the mass of the body.

6. Graphical Representation of Linear Motion

Linear motion can be easily analyzed with Displacement-Time and Velocity-Time graphs.

Displacement-Time Graph

  • The slope of a displacement-time graph gives the velocity.
  • A straight line parallel to the time axis indicates the object is stationary.
  • A straight inclined line indicates motion with uniform velocity.
  • A curved line indicates motion with variable velocity.

Velocity-Time Graph

  • The slope of a velocity-time graph gives the acceleration.
  • The area enclosed between the velocity-time graph and the time axis gives the displacement.
  • A straight line parallel to the time axis indicates uniform velocity (zero acceleration).
  • A straight inclined line indicates uniform acceleration (or retardation if the slope is negative).
Important

Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion:

For a body starting with initial velocity (u), moving with uniform acceleration (a), acquiring a final velocity (v), covering a distance (S) in time (t):

  • First Equation: v = u + at
  • Second Equation: S = ut + ½at²
  • Third Equation: v² = u² + 2aS

Note: If a body starts from rest, u = 0. If a body comes to rest (brakes applied), v = 0. If the body is retarding, acceleration 'a' is taken as negative.

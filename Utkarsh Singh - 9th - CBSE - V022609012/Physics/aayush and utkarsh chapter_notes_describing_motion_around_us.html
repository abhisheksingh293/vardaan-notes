DESCRIBING MOTION AROUND US

1. Motion in a Straight Line

Everything in nature is in motion. To study complex motions, we first understand simplified forms like linear motion (motion in a straight line).

Describing Position

To describe the position of an object, we need to specify a fixed point known as the reference point (or origin). The distance and direction of the object with respect to the reference point at any instant describe its position.

  • At Rest: If the position of an object with respect to a reference point does not change with time, it is at rest.
  • In Motion: If its position changes with time with respect to the reference point, it is in motion.
Important

Physical quantities which can be specified by just their numerical value (magnitude) are called scalars. Quantities which require specifying both the direction and magnitude are called vectors.

2. Distance and Displacement

Concept
  • Distance: The total length of the path travelled by an object between the starting and stopping positions. It has only a magnitude (numerical value) and no direction.
  • Displacement: The net change in the position of an object between two given instants of time. The magnitude of displacement is the shortest distance between the initial and final positions. It has both magnitude and direction.

For motion in a straight line, the total distance travelled and the magnitude of displacement are equal only if the object moves without turning back (i.e., it moves in one direction continuously).

3. Average Speed and Average Velocity

Average Speed

It describes how fast or slow an object is moving. It is the ratio of total distance travelled to the total time taken.

Average Speed =
Total distance travelled Time interval

Since distance has no direction, average speed also has no direction.

Average Velocity

It describes how fast the position of an object is changing and in which direction. It is the average rate of change of position.

Average Velocity =
Change in position (Displacement) Time interval
Fact

India's Scientific Contributions: The concept that an object's speed is the distance travelled divided by the time taken dates back to ancient India, as seen in the treatise Aryabhatiya (5th century CE) and further explored in mathematical texts like the Ganitakaumudi (14th century CE).

4. Average Acceleration

The average acceleration of an object over a time interval is the change in its velocity divided by the time interval.

Average Acceleration (a) =
Final velocity (v) - Initial velocity (u) Time interval (t)
  • Positive Acceleration: If the magnitude of velocity increases, acceleration acts in the direction of velocity.
  • Negative Acceleration (Retardation): If the magnitude of velocity decreases, the acceleration acts opposite to the direction of velocity.

5. Graphical Representation of Motion

Graphs provide a visual representation of how position, velocity, and acceleration change with time. All these graphs are plotted with Time on the x-axis.

Position-Time Graph

  • The slope of a position-time graph gives the velocity of the object.
  • A straight line inclined to the time axis represents motion with a constant velocity (uniform motion).
  • A line parallel to the time axis means the object is stationary (at rest).

Velocity-Time Graph

  • The slope of a velocity-time graph gives the acceleration. If the line is parallel to the time axis, acceleration is zero.
  • The area enclosed by the velocity-time graph and the time axis gives the displacement of the object in that time interval.

6. Kinematic Equations for Constant Acceleration

For the motion of an object in a straight line with constant acceleration, the five physical quantities—displacement (s), time interval (t), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), and acceleration (a)—are related by three kinematic equations:

    v = u + at

7. Motion in a Plane & Uniform Circular Motion

Motion in a plane (like a vehicle making a turn) is called motion in two dimensions.

Uniform Circular Motion

When an object moves in a circular path with a constant (uniform) speed, its motion is called uniform circular motion. If an object takes time T to make one revolution in a circular path of radius R, its average speed (v) is given by:

v =
2πR T
Important

Is uniform circular motion accelerated? Yes! Even though the speed remains constant, the direction of velocity continuously changes at every point (acting along the tangent to the circle). Because velocity is a vector, this continuous change in direction means the motion is constantly accelerated.

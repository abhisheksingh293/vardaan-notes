Medieval Europe: Rise and Spread of Christianity

1. The Medieval Age & Socio-Political System

The term Medieval is associated with the Middle Ages, representing the transition period between ancient and modern times. The timeline for this era differs across the globe:

  • In Europe: The Medieval Age extends roughly from the 7th century to the 16th century.
  • In India: It extends from the 8th to the 18th century.

The transition was not sudden but happened gradually, bringing massive socio-economic and political changes.

Concept

Feudalism

During the medieval period, a new type of society and government system emerged in Western Europe known as Feudalism. It was a system where powerful military chiefs dominated large areas of land and played a vital role in the government. The king was essentially the most powerful feudal chief.

  • The main division in society was between Lords (who owned the land) and Peasants/Serfs (who worked on the land).
  • Serf: A peasant tied to his lord's estate who could not leave without permission.
  • The economic life was predominantly rural, and towns/trade played very little role.

2. Advent of Christianity

Two major religions, Christianity and Islam, arose and spread during this period, shaping the political, cultural, and economic activities of the world.

Roman Conquest of Palestine

In 63 BCE, Pompey the Great's campaigns led to the Siege of Jerusalem. His conquest spelled the end of Jewish independence, and Judea became a part of the Roman Empire.

Life of Jesus Christ

Christianity was founded by Jesus Christ. He was born in Bethlehem, a small village near Jerusalem. His father, Joseph, and mother, Mary, were Jews.

  • Jesus spent his early life as a carpenter but went about preaching his message at the age of 30.
  • He was known as a great healer and spoke in simple language, which earned him a large following.
  • His teachings emphasizing one God, love, and brotherhood angered the rich and powerful Romans.
  • They complained to Pontius Pilate (the Roman Governor of Palestine), accusing Jesus of encouraging rebellion. Jesus was arrested and crucified.
Fact

The Bible & Holy Days

The holy book of Christians is the Bible, divided into the Old Testament (history of the Jews) and the New Testament (life and teachings of Christ, including the four Gospels by Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John).

  • Christmas: Celebrates the birth of Jesus Christ.
  • Good Friday: The day Jesus is believed to have been crucified.
  • Easter (Resurrection): Celebrated to mark Jesus rising from the dead on the third day after his crucifixion.

3. Teachings of Jesus Christ

Jesus chose twelve disciples to help him in his mission of spreading love and compassion. His core teachings included:

  • God is One: He emphasized a personal relationship with a just and benevolent God.
  • Repentance: Sinners can always repent and be saved by God.
  • Compassion: We should hate the sins, not the sinners. Compassion for fellow human beings is essential.
  • Charity: People must show compassion to the poor, feed the hungry, heal the sick, and give shelter to the homeless.

4. Spread of Christianity

After the death of Jesus, his followers—primarily Peter, Paul, and John—zealously spread Christianity. St. Paul carried the teachings to Greece and Rome.

Initially, Christians were hated, persecuted, and considered disloyal by the Romans. However, the religion slowly gained massive popularity.

Important

The Role of Emperor Constantine

The persecution of Christians was officially ended by the Edict of Toleration (311 CE) by Galerius. Shortly after, Roman Emperors Constantine I and Licinius issued the Edict of Milan (313 CE), completely legalizing Christianity across the Roman Empire.

  • Emperor Constantine embraced Christianity, making it the official religion.
  • He introduced laws supporting Christian morals and started the custom of observing Sunday as a holy day (Sabbath).
  • The Bishop of St. Peter's Church in Rome came to be considered the representative of Christ on earth and was called the Pope.

5. Decline of the Ancient Roman Empire

The Roman Emperor Constantine moved his capital to Byzantine, which was renamed Constantinople. This new Eastern capital flourished in trade and commerce. Meanwhile, the Western Roman Empire faced a steady decline due to several reasons:

  • Political corruption, constant wars, and infighting among soldiers.
  • Slave revolts and the failure of slave labor to support agriculture and industry.
  • Barbarian Invasions: Repeated attacks by northern Germanic tribes such as the Goths, Vandals, and Franks finally destroyed the Western Roman Empire.

Impact of the Decline: It created immense confusion in Western Europe, leading to the rise of small states. Trade declined, people moved to rural areas to survive, and education/Latin learning suffered heavily.

The Byzantine Empire

While the West fell, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire) with its capital at Constantinople survived. It preserved the Greco-Roman civilization, acted as a link to the Arab world, and thrived until it was conquered by the Turks in 1453 CE.

6. Emergence of Turks & The Crusades

The capture of Constantinople by the invading Ottoman Turks in 1453 CE sent shockwaves through Europe. The city's name was changed to Istanbul, marking the definitive end of the Roman Empire.

The Crusades

The Crusades were a series of religious wars fought between the Christians and the Muslims from the 11th to the 13th centuries. The primary aim of the Christians was to win back their holy land of Jerusalem from the Turks.

Impact of the Crusades

  • Knowledge Transfer: Crusaders learned Science, Mathematics (Algebra), art, and architecture from the advanced Arab and Greek civilizations, paving the way for the Renaissance in Europe.
  • Decline of Feudalism: Crusaders learnt the use of gunpowder and guns, which eliminated the role of traditional knights.
  • Economic Growth: Trade relations between Europe and Asia expanded. New trade routes improved Europe's economy.
  • Rise of Monarchies: Kings received more tax revenue from traders, making them more powerful than feudal lords.

7. Monasteries and their Significance

A crucial development during this period was the building of Monasteries—institutions where monks lived and devoted their lives to prayer and public welfare.

  • Contributions: Monks cleared land, developed animal husbandry, preserved ancient manuscripts, and founded schools/hospitals. They excelled in stained glass painting and woodcutting.
  • Nuns: Served the society actively as teachers and preachers.
  • Conflicts: Over time, some monasteries became exceptionally wealthy and started acting like feudal lords. This led to internal disputes and conflicts with rulers who resented the political power of the Church and Popes.

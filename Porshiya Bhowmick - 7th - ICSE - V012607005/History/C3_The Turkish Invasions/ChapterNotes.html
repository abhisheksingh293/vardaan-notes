The Turkish Invasions

1. The Arab Invasions

The Arabs were the first Muslim conquerors of India. Their early attempts laid the foundation for future cultural and trade contacts.

  • In the beginning of CE 712, Sindh was attacked by the Arabs for the first time.
  • Muhammad-bin-Qasim, an Umayyad General, successfully conquered the regions of Sindh and Punjab.
  • As a result of this invasion, valuable trade and cultural contacts were established with Central Asia.
  • However, the Arabs could not extend their rule deeper into India at that time because of the strong resistance put up by the Rajputs.

2. Rise of the Turks

From the 8th century onwards, Northern India gradually became weaker due to constant internal wars among its rulers. This made it vulnerable.

  • Between CE 1000 - 1200, massive changes occurred in Central and West Asia.
  • The Turks became a powerful force following the decline of the Abbasid empire.
  • They established two major independent kingdoms: Ghazni and Ghor.

3. Mahmud of Ghazni

Mahmud of Ghazni was the first major Turkish invader who attacked India from the north-western side. He was primarily interested in plundering wealth rather than setting up an empire in the Indian subcontinent.

Key Campaigns & Conquests

  • He needed a lot of money to organize a well-equipped army to fulfill his desire of conquering Central Asia.
  • The weak political condition of India attracted him. He did not face any unified, tough resistance.
  • He defeated Jayapala, the Hindu ruler of Punjab, in 1001 CE, and later defeated Anandapala in 1008-1009 CE.
  • His cavalry played a massive role in his swift victories. He never suffered a defeat during his Indian campaigns.
  • He annexed Punjab, which completely opened the road for future invasions into India. He died in CE 1030.
Important

17 Raids & The Somnath Temple

During his thirty-three years of reign, Mahmud of Ghazni carried out seventeen raids on India between CE 1001 and CE 1025. He heavily targeted rich temple towns such as Multan, Alwar, Mathura, Nagarkot, and Thaneshwar.

His most famous and devastating plunder was the attack on the Somnath Temple in CE 1025, which brought him immense wealth. He used this wealth to beautify his capital city, Ghazni, with museums, libraries, and mosques.

Fact

Literature & Scholars

Mahmud of Ghazni was a great patron of art and learning. Several prominent scholars flourished in his court:

  • Alberuni: A renowned scholar who spent about 10 years in India. He wrote the book Tahqiq-i-Hind (Kitab-ul-Hind), which is one of the most valuable sources of information regarding Indian civilization of that time.
  • Firdausi: He wrote the famous Persian epic 'Shahnama', which serves as a biography of Mahmud of Ghazni.

4. Muhammad Ghori

After 150 years of Mahmud's invasions, Muhammad of Ghor (Muhammad Ghori) attacked India. Unlike Mahmud, Ghori wanted to establish a permanent empire in India.

  • He ascended the throne at Ghazni in CE 1173.
  • At the time of his invasion, Northern India was divided among five powerful Rajput kingdoms: the Gahadavalas (Kanauj), Chalukyas (Gujarat), Chandellas (Bundelkhand), Parmars (Malwa), and Chauhans (Delhi & Ajmer).
  • He rapidly captured Multan, Gujarat, Punjab, Peshawar, and Sialkot by 1190.
Concept

The Battles of Tarain & Chandawar

Muhammad Ghori's most crucial conflicts were against Prithviraj Chauhan, the brave Rajput ruler of Ajmer and Delhi.

  • First Battle of Tarain (CE 1191): Prithviraj Chauhan successfully defeated Muhammad Ghori.
  • Second Battle of Tarain (CE 1192): Ghori returned better prepared and defeated Prithviraj Chauhan.
  • Battle of Chandawar (CE 1194): Ghori defeated Raja Jaichandra of Kanauj. Jaichandra had previously refused to unite with Prithviraj Chauhan.

These victories made Muhammad Ghori the true founder of Turkish rule in India. He died in CE 1206.

5. Causes of the Turkish Success

Although the Rajputs were extremely brave soldiers and skilled swordsmen, they suffered defeat against the Turkish invaders due to several crucial reasons:

  • Lack of Political Unity: The Rajputs were constantly busy fighting with each other. Kings like Prithviraj Chauhan and Jaichandra could not unite against a common foreign enemy.
  • Internal Jealousy: The Rajput chiefs were deeply jealous of each other's power and territories.
  • Feudal Army Structure: The Rajputs had no strong standing army of their own. They had to rely on feudal lords for contingents, which lacked centralized coordination.
  • Military Tactics: The traditional Rajput fighting style was no match for the quick, swift, and highly mobile cavalry of the Central Asian warriors.

6. Impact of These Invasions

The continuous interactions and invasions led to significant long-term effects on the Indian subcontinent:

  • Exchange of Knowledge: Even before the conquests, Arabs came as travelers. Books on Indian mathematics and astronomy were taken to and studied in Baghdad.
  • Emergence of a Composite Culture: The mingling of Turkish, Arab, and Indian traditions led to a unique composite culture. This fusion became clearly visible in various forms of art, architecture, food, dress, music, and language.
  • Migration: These invasions permanently opened the doors of the Indian subcontinent to the migration of people from foreign lands.

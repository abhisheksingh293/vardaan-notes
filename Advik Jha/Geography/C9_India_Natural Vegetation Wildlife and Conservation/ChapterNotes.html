1. MATTER

1. Introduction to Matter

Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the study of various kinds of matter, which includes their structure, composition, and physical and chemical properties. If we look around us, everything from stones, sand, water, plants, animals, to the air we breathe is considered matter.

Definition

Matter is defined as anything which has mass, occupies space (volume), and can be perceived by our senses.

Historical Perspective on Matter

  • Greek Philosophers: Believed matter is made up of four elements: water, air, fire, and earth.
  • Ancient Indian Philosophers: Believed matter is made up of five elements (Panch Tattva): air, fire, water, sky, and earth.
  • Maharshi Kanad: An Indian philosopher who was the first to suggest that matter is made up of very tiny particles called parmanu.
  • Democritus: A Greek thinker who named these tiny particles as atoms.
  • John Dalton: Later suggested that all kinds of matter are made up of extremely small particles called atoms.

2. Composition of Matter

Matter is composed of extremely small, discrete particles. These particles are incredibly small and cannot be seen with the naked eye or even with an ordinary microscope. For instance, a single drop of water contains roughly 1021 particles of water.

3. Kinetic Theory of Matter

To understand the different states of matter, we study the Kinetic Theory of Matter. This theory explains the existence of the three states of matter, their characteristic properties, and the interconversions from one state to another.

Main Postulates of the Kinetic Theory:

  • Composition: Matter is composed of very small particles called atoms and molecules. The constituent particles of a specific kind of matter are identical in all respects but differ from the particles of another kind of matter.
  • Constant Motion: Particles of matter are always in a state of random motion and possess kinetic energy. This energy increases with an increase in temperature and vice-versa.
  • Interparticle (Intermolecular) Space: The particles of matter have spaces or gaps between them. These spaces are known as interparticle or intermolecular spaces.
  • Intermolecular Force of Attraction: There exists a force of attraction between the particles of matter which keeps them together. The magnitude of this force varies from one type of matter to another (e.g., chalk breaks easily, but iron does not).
Important

Brownian Motion: The continuous, zig-zag, random motion of suspended particles on the surface of a liquid or in air is called Brownian motion. It was first observed by scientist Robert Brown using pollen grains suspended in water.

Diffusion: The intermixing of two or more substances due to the motion of their particles in order to get a uniform mixture is called diffusion. The rate of diffusion is fastest in gases and slowest in solids, and it increases with an increase in temperature.

4. States of Matter

Based on the magnitude of intermolecular force of attraction, intermolecular space, and random motion, matter exists primarily in three states:

1. Solid State

In solids, particles are very closely packed. The intermolecular force of attraction is the strongest, making the intermolecular spaces almost negligible. The molecules are not free to move; they only vibrate about their mean positions.

  • Have a definite shape, size, and volume.
  • Highly rigid and difficult to compress.
  • Possess the lowest kinetic energy.

2. Liquid State

In liquids, particles are less closely packed. The intermolecular forces are weaker than in solids, resulting in larger intermolecular spaces. Particles can move freely and randomly.

  • Have a definite volume but no fixed shape (they take the shape of the container).
  • More compressible than solids and possess higher kinetic energy.
  • Can flow downwards (fluids).

3. Gaseous State

In gases, intermolecular forces of attraction are the weakest (almost negligible). The particles lie far apart, resulting in very large intermolecular spaces. Particles are completely free to move in the entire space available.

  • Neither a definite shape nor a definite volume.
  • Highly compressible and have the highest kinetic energy.
  • Flow easily in all directions.
Did You Know?

Apart from solid, liquid, and gas, scientists have proven the existence of two more extreme states of matter: Plasma and Bose-Einstein Condensate.

5. Interconversion of States of Matter

The phenomenon of change of one state of matter into another and then back to the original state, without any change in its chemical composition, is called Interconversion of the states of matter.

Changes in the state of matter are caused primarily by altering two factors: Temperature and Pressure.

Important Terminology for Interconversion:

  • Melting or Fusion: The process by which a substance changes from solid state to liquid state on heating. The fixed temperature at which this occurs is the Melting Point (e.g., Ice melts at 0°C).
  • Evaporation or Vaporisation: The process by which a substance changes from liquid state to vapour (gas) state. When a liquid is heated to a fixed temperature where it starts rapidly changing into gas, that temperature is called the Boiling Point (e.g., pure water boils at 100°C).
  • Condensation or Liquefaction: The process by which a gas changes into its liquid state upon cooling. The Condensation Point is the exact temperature at which this occurs. Liquefaction can also be achieved by applying high pressure and lowering temperature (e.g., LPG in cylinders).
  • Freezing or Solidification: The process by which a liquid changes into a solid state. The fixed temperature at which this happens is the Freezing Point (e.g., water freezes at 0°C).
  • Sublimation: The process by which certain solid substances change directly into the gaseous state on heating, without passing through the liquid state. Examples include Camphor, Iodine, Naphthalene, Ammonium Chloride, and Dry Ice.
  • Deposition: The exact reverse of sublimation, where a gas changes directly into a solid state upon cooling without becoming a liquid. The resulting solid is called a sublimate.

6. Law of Conservation of Mass

During physical changes (like melting) or chemical changes (like burning), you might wonder what happens to the total mass of the substance. This is explained by the Law of Conservation of Mass, proposed by the French chemist Antoine Lavoisier.

Concept

The Law of Conservation of Mass states: "Matter can neither be created nor be destroyed in a chemical reaction. It may only change from one form to another."

In simpler terms: Total mass of Reactants = Total mass of Products.

Experimental Verification

This law can be verified in a laboratory using a closed container (to prevent gases from escaping).

  • Reactants: Barium chloride solution (BaCl₂) + Sodium sulphate solution (Na₂SO₄)
  • Products: Barium sulphate (White precipitate) + Sodium chloride (NaCl solution)
  • Process: The solutions are kept in a sealed conical flask but separated using a suspended test tube. The entire apparatus is weighed before the reaction. The flask is then tilted to mix the solutions, causing a chemical reaction (white precipitate forms). The apparatus is weighed again.
  • Result: The mass remains exactly the same before and after the reaction, proving the Law of Conservation of Mass.

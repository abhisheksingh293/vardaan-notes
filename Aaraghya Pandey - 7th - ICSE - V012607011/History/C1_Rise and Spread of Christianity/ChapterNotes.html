The Mesopotamian Civilisation

1. Evolution of the Stone Age

Before diving into civilisations, it is essential to understand the progression of early human history through different ages:

  • Neolithic Age (New Stone Age): Began around 7000 BCE. Revolutionised by the onset of agriculture. Humans transitioned from hunter-gatherers to living in organised communities. (Important settlements: Mehrgarh, Burzahom, Gufkral, Chirand, and Utnur).
  • Chalcolithic Age: Lasted from 2100 BCE to 700 BCE. Marked by the use of copper along with stone.
  • Bronze Age: Began around 3000 BCE. Bronze (stronger and more durable) replaced stone for making tools and weapons, leading to surplus food production and the rise of early civilisations.
Important

The Four Major Bronze Age River-Valley Civilisations:

  • Mesopotamian Civilisation: Banks of Tigris and Euphrates rivers (3500 BCE to 600 BCE).
  • Egyptian Civilisation: Banks of River Nile (3000 BCE).
  • Harappan / Indus Valley Civilisation: Banks of River Indus (2500 BCE to 1500 BCE).
  • Chinese Civilisation: Banks of Hwang-Ho river (3000 BCE).

2. Features of a Civilisation

Concept

Civilisation: It is a state of human society that is highly developed and organised. It originates from the Latin word 'civitas' meaning 'city'. A civilisation implies a group of people sharing common languages, customs, and ways of life.

The emergence of civilisations brought several key features:

  • Rise of towns and cities.
  • Social and economic divisions in society.
  • Trade relations and exchange of goods/services.
  • Emergence of a centralised form of government.
  • Division of labour (agriculture, pottery, architecture).
  • Development of a highly developed written language.
  • Establishment of religious systems and technological advancements.

Why Along Rivers? Rivers provided fertile banks for growing crops, abundant water, and transportation for trade.

3. Introduction to Mesopotamia

The Mesopotamian Civilisation was the first one to emerge. It flourished between 3500 BCE and 600 BCE.

  • The word "Mesopotamia" comes from the Greek language and means "the land between the rivers".
  • It was located between the rivers Tigris and Euphrates in present-day Iraq and Kuwait.
  • It is often called the "Cradle of Civilisation" because it is where people first gathered in large cities and created governments.

Major Mesopotamian Civilisations:

  • Sumerians: First to form a civilisation. Invented writing, ploughing, and the sailboat. Organised into independent city-states.
  • Assyrians: A warrior society that ruled a major part of the Middle East.
  • Akkadians: Lived in northern Mesopotamia; lacked unity and often fought each other.
  • Babylonians: Most powerful under Hammurabi's rule. A major trade centre on the Euphrates.

4. Society and Life in Mesopotamia

Social Structure

The society was clearly divided into three classes:

  • Upper Class: The King, his family, and priests. The king was the head priest, made laws, and was extremely powerful. Priests lived in temples and collected taxes.
  • Middle Class: Craftsmen, merchants, civil servants, farmers, and labourers.
  • Lower Class: Slaves (captured in battle), bought and sold by the upper class.

Family Life

The family was patriarchal (father as the head). Boys went to school; girls learned household work. Women were respected but not considered equal to men and needed their husband's permission for decisions.

Town Planning & Architecture

Cities like Ur were well-planned and divided into three main parts:

  1. The Sacred Area: Housed the temple tower called the Ziggurat, dedicated to the patron God of the city.
  2. The Walled City: The central living area with houses around courtyards, built with sandstone and sun-dried bricks.
  3. The Outer Town: Where farmers and artisans lived.

5. Occupations and Economy

  • Agriculture: The main occupation. Grew barley, wheat, vegetables, and fruits. Built an elaborate irrigation system using canals, dykes, and aqueducts to control floods. Invented the ox-drawn plough.
  • Pottery: Initially made by hand, later revolutionized by the potter's wheel.
  • Art and Craft: Mixed tin and copper to make bronze. Stone masons carved intricate cylinder seals used as signatures.
  • Trade: Abundant food grains were the main export. Imported stone, timber, and metal. Traded with the Indus Valley Civilisation (evidence: Mesopotamian seals found in Indus sites).

6. Achievements of the Mesopotamians

Writing: Cuneiform

The Sumerians developed the first form of writing around 3200 BCE, known as cuneiform (meaning "wedge-shaped"). Scribes pressed a reed stylus into soft, moist clay tablets, which were then baked in fire to create permanent records.

Fact

Hammurabi's Code: Babylonian King Hammurabi compiled one of the earliest known codes of law (1792-1750 BCE). Consisting of 282 articles carved on a massive stone pillar, it operated on the principle of "An eye for an eye, and a tooth for a tooth".

Science, Math & Technology

  • The Wheel: Oldest wheel discovered here (3500 BCE for pottery, 3200 BCE for chariots).
  • Mathematics: Used a sexagesimal scale (base 60). This gave us the 60-second minute, 60-minute hour, and 360-degree circle.
  • Knew advanced math including fractions, geometric formulas, and potentially the Pythagoras Theorem before Pythagoras!

Architecture: The Hanging Gardens

The Hanging Gardens of Babylon is considered one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, built by Nebuchadnezzar II. They were built on terraces supported by columns, making the plants look like they were hanging.

7. Decline of the Civilisation

The civilisation eventually declined due to several factors:

  • Invasions and Wars: The last Sumerian dynasty fell in 2000 BCE causing chaos. The Amorites and later the Hittites invaded the region. (Sargon the Great also conquered Sumerian city-states).
  • Environmental Issues: Riverbeds built up silt, causing water levels to rise above the plains. When water evaporated, it left behind mineral salts. By 2300 BCE, the soil became too salty to support crops, leading to agricultural collapse.

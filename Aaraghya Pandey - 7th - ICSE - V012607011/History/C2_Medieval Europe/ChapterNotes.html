Chapter 2: Medieval Europe

The Medieval Period in Europe lasted for about 1000 years (from the 5th century to the 15th century). This era witnessed the fall of the Roman Empire, the rise of feudalism, the glory of the Byzantine Empire, and the widespread influence of Christianity and the Crusades.

1. Decline of the Roman Empire

In 395 CE, the Roman Empire became too large to govern efficiently and was divided into two parts: the Eastern Roman Empire and the Western Roman Empire. Several factors led to the eventual fall of the Western Roman Empire:

  • Effect on the Economy: The Western Empire had a large trade deficit. They purchased luxury goods from the East but had nothing to offer in return, leading to inflation and a shortage of money.
  • Military Attacks: Army generals fought for the throne, leading to political instability. Furthermore, due to a scarcity of funds, the government hired cheaper Germanic soldiers to fight, transforming the military into a mercenary army lacking loyalty to Rome.
  • Size of the Empire: The vast size of the empire made it incredibly difficult to manage and govern, increasing corruption.
  • Barbarian Invasions: Germanic barbarian tribes (Visigoths, Vandals, Angles, Saxons, and Franks) swept through the empire. The Angles and Saxons settled in Britain, while the Franks settled in France.
Fact

In 476 CE, Romulus, the last emperor of the Western Roman Empire, was overthrown by a Germanic leader named Odoacer. This event marked the final collapse of the Western Roman Empire.

2. The Rise of Feudalism

The disintegration of the Roman Empire and the subsequent chaos led to the birth of Feudalism in Europe.

Concept

Feudalism was a socio-political system where people worked for kings and nobles in exchange for protection. Kings granted land (called a fief) to nobles in exchange for military service and loyalty. The person receiving the land was known as a vassal.

The Feudal Hierarchy:

  • The King: Owned all the land and distributed it to high-ranking nobles.
  • Lords/Nobles: Dukes, barons, and counts who received land from the king and provided knights for the king's army.
  • Knights: Professional soldiers who protected the lords and the king.
  • Serfs and Peasants: Located at the very bottom of the hierarchy. Serfs were bound to the land and could not leave without the lord's permission. They worked the land to produce food for the entire community.

3. Rise of the Byzantine Empire (Eastern Roman Empire)

While the Western Empire fell, the Eastern Roman Empire, also known as the Byzantine Empire, flourished for another 1,000 years.

  • Constantinople: The capital city, located on a strait, made it highly secure from barbarian attacks and a major hub for trade.
  • The Justinian Period (527 CE): Justinian I (Justinian the Great) was the most notable emperor. He expanded the empire, defeated the Vandals and Ostrogoths, and introduced a stable gold coinage.
  • Corpus Juris Civilis: Justinian's most enduring legacy was the codification of Roman law into a single, organized collection.
  • Hagia Sophia: Built between 532 and 537 CE by architects Isidore of Miletus and Anthemius of Tralles, this magnificent church stood as a symbol of the empire's wealth and religious devotion.

4. Emergence of the Turks

The ancient Turks were nomadic people from the Altai Mountains. Over the centuries, the Byzantine Empire weakened due to constant attacks from Goths, Huns, Arabs, and Vikings. Finally, in 1453 CE, the Ottoman Turks captured Constantinople, marking the end of the Byzantine Empire.

5. The Crusades

The Crusades were a series of holy wars during the Middle Ages between the Christians of Europe and the Muslims, fought primarily to take control of Jerusalem and the Holy Land. There were eight major expeditions between 1095 and 1291.

  • First Crusade (1095-1099): The most successful for the Christians, who drove out the Turks and took control of Jerusalem.
  • Third Crusade (1189-1192): Fought after the Muslim leader Saladin recaptured Jerusalem. Led by kings like Richard the Lionheart.
  • Children's Crusade (1212): Tens of thousands of children marched to the Holy Land, but none made it; many died or were sold into slavery.
Important

Impact of the Crusades: Despite being wars, the Crusades had a positive impact on Europe. They acted as a catalyst for the Renaissance. They stimulated trade (silk, spices, glass), improved ship-building and transportation, and led to a massive exchange of cultural and scientific knowledge between Asia and Europe.

6. Spread of Christianity, Monks, and Monasteries

Early Christians were persecuted because their monotheistic beliefs clashed with traditional polytheistic Roman religion. However, the religion eventually grew and transformed Europe.

  • 313 CE: Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan, granting Christianity legal status.
  • 325 CE: Council of Nicaea established formal orthodox beliefs (the Nicene Creed).
  • 380 CE: Emperor Theodosius issued the Edict of Thessalonica, making Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire.

Monks and Monasteries

A monastery is a building where people dedicate their lives entirely to God. Men who live there are called monks, and women are called nuns (living in convents).

  • The Vows: Monks took strict vows of Poverty (renouncing worldly goods), Chastity (avoiding all pleasures of the flesh), and Obedience (obeying the Church and God).
  • Role in Society: During the Middle Ages, monks were among the few people who knew how to read and write.
  • Impact: Monasteries served as the centers of education. Monks hand-copied Latin manuscripts, preserving ancient Greek and Roman knowledge. They also provided medical care, fed the poor, and offered shelter to travelers.

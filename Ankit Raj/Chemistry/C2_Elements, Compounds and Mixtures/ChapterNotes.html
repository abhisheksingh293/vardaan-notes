2. Elements, Compounds and Mixtures

Concept

Pure Substance: A kind of matter which consists of the same type of particles that cannot be separated into its components by any physical means. Elements and Compounds are pure substances.

Impure Substance: A substance formed by the combination of two or more elements or compounds mechanically mixed together in any ratio. Mixtures are impure substances.

1. Elements

Elements are pure substances which can neither be broken down into further simpler substances nor formed from two or more simpler substances by any physical or chemical means.

  • All substances on Earth are chemically made up of one or more elements.
  • There are 118 known elements. Out of these, 92 occur naturally in the earth's crust, and the rest have been artificially created by scientists (e.g., radioactive elements).
  • Solid elements: gold, silver, iron, copper, lead, aluminium, zinc, sodium, carbon, iodine.
  • Liquid elements: mercury and bromine.
  • Gaseous elements: oxygen, nitrogen, hydrogen, chlorine, fluorine, helium, neon.

Characteristics of Elements

  • It is a pure substance.
  • It is made up of only one kind of particles (atoms).
  • Elements may occur naturally or be prepared artificially.
  • Elements exist in all three physical states (solid, liquid, gas).

2. Classification of Elements

Based on their properties, elements are classified into four main groups:

Metals

Generally hard solids at room temperature (except mercury, which is a liquid). They have a lustrous (shiny) surface, are malleable (can be beaten into thin sheets), and ductile (can be drawn into wires). They have high boiling and melting points, and are good conductors of heat and electricity.

Examples: Silver, gold, zinc, platinum, iron, calcium.

Non-metals

Have a dull surface (except graphite and iodine, which are shiny). They are non-malleable, non-ductile, and generally have low melting and boiling points. Non-metals are usually bad conductors of heat and electricity (except graphite, which conducts electricity). They are generally solids or gases (except bromine, which is a liquid).

Examples: Hydrogen (the lightest element), oxygen, sulphur, nitrogen.

Metalloids

A group of elements that lie in between metals and non-metals. They show some metallic and some non-metallic properties.

Examples: Boron, silicon, germanium, arsenic, antimony.

Noble or Inert Gases

These are gaseous elements that do not react chemically with other elements and are highly stable.

Examples: Helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon.

3. Symbols of Elements

A chemical symbol represents one atom of the element. The modern system of symbols was developed by the Swedish chemist J. J. Berzelius. The rules for writing symbols are:

  • First Letter: Used for many common non-metals. (e.g., Hydrogen = H, Carbon = C, Oxygen = O)
  • First Two Letters: Used when names share an initial letter. The first letter is capital, the second is lowercase. (e.g., Helium = He, Neon = Ne)
  • First Letter + First Consonant: Used when the first two letters are identical to another element. (e.g., Magnesium = Mg, Manganese = Mn, Chlorine = Cl)
Important

Symbols derived from Latin names:
• Sodium = Na (Natrium)
• Potassium = K (Kalium)
• Iron = Fe (Ferrum)
• Copper = Cu (Cuprum)
• Silver = Ag (Argentum)
• Gold = Au (Aurum)
• Lead = Pb (Plumbum)
• Mercury = Hg (Hydrargyrum)

Fact

Common Elements in Nature:
• Universe: Hydrogen and Helium.
• Earth's Crust: Oxygen, Silicon, Aluminium, and Iron.
• Atmosphere: Nitrogen.
• Human Body: Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen.

4. Compounds

Compounds are pure substances formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a fixed proportion by weight.

  • Salt (Sodium chloride): Formed by sodium (a highly reactive solid) and chlorine (a poisonous gas). The resulting compound is safe to eat and totally different from its parent elements.
  • Water (H2O): Formed by hydrogen (a combustible gas) and oxygen (a gas that supports combustion). Water is a non-combustible liquid.
  • Sugar: Formed by Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen.

Characteristics of Compounds

  • It is a pure, homogeneous substance.
  • It has distinct properties because the original components lose their individual properties.
  • Elements in a compound cannot be separated by physical methods (e.g., you cannot separate iron from iron sulphide using a magnet).
  • Energy is released or absorbed during the formation of a compound.

5. Atoms and Molecules

Atoms

An atom is the smallest particle of an element. It possesses all the properties of that element but may or may not have an independent existence. Atoms of most elements are very reactive. They maintain their properties through chemical changes.

Molecules

A molecule is the smallest particle of an element or a compound that has an independent existence and shows all properties of that substance. Atoms in a molecule are held together by attractive forces called chemical bonds.

Atomicity

Atomicity is the number of atoms present in a single molecule of an element.

  • Monoatomic (1 atom): Inert gases (He, Ne) and metals (Na, K, Fe).
  • Diatomic (2 atoms): Hydrogen (H2), Oxygen (O2), Nitrogen (N2), Chlorine (Cl2).
  • Triatomic (3 atoms): Ozone (O3).
  • Polyatomic (>3 atoms): Phosphorus (P4), Sulphur (S8).

6. Mixtures

A mixture is an impure substance formed when particles of two or more elements or compounds are mechanically mixed together in any proportion. The components of a mixture retain their original properties.

Examples: Air (mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide), soil, sea water, milk, blood.

Characteristics of Mixtures

  • Components are mixed mechanically in any proportion (no fixed ratio).
  • The individual components retain their own original properties.
  • The components can be separated by simple physical processes like winnowing, sieving, filtration, evaporation, etc.
  • Formation of a mixture does not involve any energy exchange.

Types of Mixtures

  • Homogeneous Mixture: Components are distributed uniformly throughout the mixture. You cannot easily see the different parts. (e.g., Sugar solution, air, brass, steel).
  • Heterogeneous Mixture: Components are not distributed uniformly. You can often see the different parts. (e.g., Mixture of iron filings and sulphur powder, sand and water, oil and water).

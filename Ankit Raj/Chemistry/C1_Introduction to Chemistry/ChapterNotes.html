1. Introduction to Chemistry

1. What is Chemistry?

Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the study of the structure, composition, and properties of substances, and the changes they undergo.

It helps us identify substances based on physical properties like colour, state, taste, odour, solubility in water, and combustibility. Understanding the relationship between the structure and properties of substances is highly useful in our daily lives.

Concept

Chemicals & Matter:
A chemical is the substance that makes up the materials of the world. Naturally occurring substances are called elements. Different elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio to make compounds. Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.

2. Different Branches of Chemistry

Because chemistry is a vast subject, it is divided into many branches based on how we deal with substances:

  • Analytical Chemistry: The science of processing and communicating information about the composition and structure of matter. It determines what matter is made of and how much of it exists.
  • Biochemistry: Deals with the study of chemical processes within and relating to living organisms.
  • Inorganic Chemistry: Deals with the synthesis, reactions, structures, and properties of compounds of elements found in non-living materials like rocks, soil, and atmosphere.
  • Organic Chemistry: The study of the structure, properties, composition, reactions, and preparation of carbon-containing compounds, which are primarily present in living things.
  • Physical Chemistry: The branch that studies the relationship between physical properties of substances and their chemical composition and transformation.

3. Historical Perspectives of Chemistry

In many ways, the history of civilization is the history of chemistry—the study of matter and its properties. Early potters, brewers, and metal crafters were practicing early forms of chemistry.

Alchemy: Many modern chemical sciences were influenced by a practice called alchemy. Alchemists believed they could refine base metals (such as iron) into precious metals (such as gold) using a mythical substance called the philosopher's stone. They also believed everything in the world was made of four elements: air, earth, fire, and water.

Important Fact

The Iron Pillar of Delhi: The iron pillar near the Qutub Minar was made around 400 BC by Indian iron workers. They developed a process that prevented wrought iron from rusting by forming a thin film of magnetic oxide on the surface through special heating and quenching treatments.

4. Importance of Chemistry in Daily Life

Chemistry is often known as the central science because it connects to almost all other fields. Its applications benefit us in numerous ways:

  • Agriculture: Production of fertilisers (Urea, Superphosphate), insecticides (DDT, BHC), fungicides, and herbicides to protect and improve crop yields.
  • Food: Development of food processing methods and preservatives. Aspartame, an artificial sweetener for diabetic patients, is 100 times sweeter than cane sugar.
  • Medicines: Creation of life-saving drugs like penicillin, paracetamol, and aspirin, as well as antiseptics and diagnostic testing chemicals.
  • Cosmetics: Production of items like talcum powder, which is made from a mineral called talc (magnesium, silicon, oxygen).
  • Petroleum: Refining of petroleum to produce petrol, kerosene, wax, and perfumes.
  • Clothing: Development of synthetic fibres like nylon, rayon, terylene, and flame-proof textiles.
  • Transport & Communication: Creation of fuels, strong carbon fibre for vehicle bodies, silicon chips, PVC wires, and plastics for modern communication devices.
  • Industry: Boosting sectors like pharmaceutics, soaps, dyes, paper, and rubber.

5. Famous Chemists and Their Discoveries

  • John Dalton (1766–1844): Proposed the Atomic Theory of Matter; stated that all atoms of an element are exactly the same.
  • Jöns Jakob Berzelius (1779–1848): Developed the modern system of chemical nomenclature (symbols and formulae) and discovered selenium, silicon, and thorium.
  • Joseph Priestley (1733–1804): Discovered oxygen (called it "dephlogisticated air") and was the first to dissolve carbon dioxide in water.
  • Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794): Known as the Father of Modern Chemistry. Showed oxygen's role in combustion and breathing, and developed the chemical naming system.
  • William Ramsay & Lord Rayleigh: Discovered inert gases in the air. Ramsay won the Nobel Prize in 1904.
  • Henry Cavendish (1731–1810): Discovered Hydrogen gas (called it "inflammable air") and proved that burning it in oxygen produces water.
  • Ernest Rutherford (1871–1937): Discovered the atomic nucleus, forming the basis of the modern atomic structure model.
  • Louis Pasteur (1822–1895): Discovered microbes, developed pasteurisation, and created vaccines for rabies and anthrax.
  • Marie Curie (1867–1934): Discovered the radioactive elements Radium and Polonium (Nobel Prize in 1911).
  • Dmitri Mendeleev: Arranged elements in increasing order of atomic weights to create the Periodic Table (1869).
  • Henry Moseley: Modified Mendeleev's work to arrange elements by increasing atomic numbers, creating the Modern Periodic Table.
Did you know?

Kevlar is a synthetic chemical used in making bullet-proof materials. Poly-acrylic acid is a polymer used in baby diapers because it can absorb huge amounts of water.

6. Apparatus Used in the Chemistry Laboratory

Experiments require special equipment to handle chemicals safely. Common apparatus include:

  • Test Tube & Stand: Used to hold, mix, or heat small quantities of liquids.
  • Beaker & Flask: Containers for storing, mixing, or heating liquids. Conical flasks are great for swirling solutions.
  • Tripod Stand & Wire Gauze: Used to support and uniformly spread heat to glassware during heating.
  • Funnel & Filter Paper: Used for filtering insoluble solid particles from liquids.
  • Tongs: To hold hot apparatus safely.
  • China Dish: Made of porcelain, used to heat or evaporate liquid substances.
  • Measuring Cylinder: Used for accurately measuring volumes of liquids.
  • Thistle Funnel: A long tube with a broad inlet to allow entry of reactants into a flask.
  • Bunsen Burner & Spirit Lamp: Primary heat sources for laboratory experiments.

7. Safety Rules in the Chemistry Laboratory

A laboratory can be dangerous if precautions are not taken. Always follow these rules:

  • Follow all written and verbal instructions carefully and never work alone.
  • Always wear a lab coat (apron), goggles, and gloves.
  • Do not eat food, drink beverages, or chew gum in the laboratory.
  • Never taste or inhale odorous chemicals without permission.
  • Keep your hands away from your face and wash them with soap and water after experiments.
  • Tie back long hair and avoid wearing loose clothing.
  • If a chemical splashes in your eyes or on your skin, immediately flush with running water for at least 20 minutes.
  • Never return unused chemicals to their original container to prevent contamination.

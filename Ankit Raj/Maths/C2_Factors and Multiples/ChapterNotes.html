Chapter 3: Whole Numbers

In our daily lives, we use numbers primarily to count objects. In this chapter, we will transition from basic counting numbers to understanding the complete set of Whole Numbers and their fundamental operational properties.

1. Natural Numbers and Whole Numbers

Definitions
  • Natural Numbers: The numbers used to count things are called natural numbers or counting numbers. They start from 1. The set of natural numbers is: 1, 2, 3, 4, ...
  • Whole Numbers: When we include the number 0 in the collection of natural numbers, we get the set of whole numbers. The set of whole numbers is: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...
Fact Check
  • The smallest natural number is 1.
  • The smallest whole number is 0.
  • There is no greatest natural or whole number because the list is endless (infinite).
  • All natural numbers are whole numbers, but not all whole numbers are natural numbers (because 0 is a whole number but not a natural number).

✏️ Practice Problems: Subtopic 1

  1. Are all whole numbers also natural numbers? Justify your answer.
  2. Write the smallest whole number. Is there any largest whole number?
  3. Write the first five natural numbers and the first five whole numbers.

2. Successor and Predecessor

Every whole number is connected sequentially to the numbers before and after it.

  • Successor: The whole number immediately greater than a given number by 1 is its successor.
    Formula: Successor = Given Number + 1
  • Predecessor: The whole number immediately less than a given number by 1 is its predecessor.
    Formula: Predecessor = Given Number - 1
Important Exception

Every whole number has a successor, and every whole number other than 0 has a predecessor. The whole number 0 has no predecessor in the set of whole numbers because there is no whole number less than 0.

3. Representation of Whole Numbers on a Number Line

Whole numbers can be visually represented on a straight horizontal line called a number line. To draw one:

  1. Draw a straight line. Mark a point O on it and label it 0.
  2. Select a uniform unit of length. Mark points A, B, C, etc. to the right of O at distances of 1 unit, 2 units, 3 units, etc., respectively.
  3. Label the points A as 1, B as 2, C as 3, and so on.

4. Fundamental Operations and their Properties

There are four fundamental operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Understanding how these operations behave with whole numbers simplifies complex calculations.

Property Name Addition & Multiplication (Holds True) Subtraction & Division (Does NOT Hold)
Closure Property Sum/Product of two whole numbers is always a whole number.
Ex: 12 + 5 = 17 (whole)
Ex: 4 × 8 = 32 (whole)		 Subtraction/Division of whole numbers may not give a whole number.
Ex: 7 - 9 is not a whole number.
Ex: 5 ÷ 2 is not a whole number.
Commutative Property The order of numbers doesn't change the result.
a + b = b + a
a × b = b × a		 Order strictly matters.
Ex: 10 - 4 ≠ 4 - 10
Ex: 12 ÷ 3 ≠ 3 ÷ 12
Associative Property The grouping of numbers doesn't change the result.
(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)
(a × b) × c = a × (b × c)		 Grouping strictly changes the result.

Distributive Property

This is one of the most powerful properties for mental math. It connects multiplication with addition or subtraction.

  • Over Addition: $a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c)$
  • Over Subtraction: $a \times (b - c) = (a \times b) - (a \times c)$
Formula

The Division Algorithm:
If we divide a whole number (Dividend) by a smaller non-zero whole number (Divisor), yielding a Quotient and Remainder, the relationship is:
Dividend = (Divisor × Quotient) + Remainder

5. Observing Patterns in Whole Numbers

Observation of number patterns can heavily speed up the simplification of operations.

Adding or Subtracting 9, 99, 999...

  • Addition: Instead of adding 9, add 10 and subtract 1.
    Example: $128 + 9 = 128 + 10 - 1 = 138 - 1 = 137$.
  • Subtraction: Instead of subtracting 999, subtract 1000 and add 1.
    Example: $3754 - 999 = 3754 - 1000 + 1 = 2754 + 1 = 2755$.

Multiplying by 5, 25, 125...

Convert the multiplier into a fraction of 10, 100, or 1000.

  • Multiply by 5: Multiply by 10, then divide by 2.
    Example: $32 \times 5 = 32 \times (10 \div 2) = 320 \div 2 = 160$.
  • Multiply by 25: Multiply by 100, then divide by 4.
    Example: $12 \times 25 = 12 \times (100 \div 4) = 1200 \div 4 = 300$.

Endocrine System and Adolescence

1. Introduction to the Endocrine System

In multicellular animals, body functions are regulated and coordinated by two systems: the Nervous System (neural coordination) and the Endocrine System (chemical coordination).

The Endocrine System consists of a group of specialized glands that secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream.

Concept

Exocrine vs. Endocrine Glands:
• Exocrine Glands: Have ducts through which they pour their secretions (enzymes, sweat, saliva) to specific target areas (e.g., salivary glands, sweat glands).
• Endocrine Glands: Are ductless glands. They secrete hormones directly into the blood, which carries them to target organs across the body (e.g., Thyroid, Pituitary).

2. Major Endocrine Glands and their Hormones

Thyroid Gland

Located on either side of the windpipe (larynx) just below the voice box. It secretes the hormone thyroxine, which contains iodine. Thyroxine controls the metabolic rate, protein synthesis, and growth and development in children.

  • Simple Goitre: Caused by a lack of iodine in the diet, leading to the abnormal enlargement of the thyroid gland.
  • Cretinism: Impaired physical and mental development (stunted growth and mental retardation) in children due to severe iodine deficiency during pregnancy.
  • Myxoedema: A severe form of hypothyroidism characterized by swelling and thickening of the skin, especially on the face and lower legs.
  • Hyperthyroidism: Over-secretion of thyroxine, causing weight loss, rapid heartbeat, insomnia, and anxiety.

Pancreas

The pancreas is an elongated, flattened gland located behind the stomach. It acts as both an exocrine and an endocrine gland. Its endocrine part, called the Islets of Langerhans, secretes two main hormones:

  • Insulin: Lowers the blood sugar level by stimulating cells to absorb and burn glucose. Insufficient secretion causes a disease called Diabetes Mellitus (high concentration of sugar in the blood).
  • Glucagon: Raises blood sugar levels by stimulating the liver to break down stored glycogen back into glucose.

Adrenal Glands

A pair of cap-like glands located just above the kidneys (suprarenal glands). They have two parts: the outer cortex and the inner medulla.

  • Adrenaline (from Medulla): Known as the "emergency hormone". It prepares the body for stressful or emergency situations (fight or flight) by increasing the heartbeat, blood pressure, and releasing extra glucose into the blood.
  • Cortisol & Aldosterone (from Cortex): Regulate protein metabolism and help maintain the salt and water balance in the body.
Fact

The Pituitary Gland (Master Gland):
A pea-sized gland located at the base of the brain. It is called the "Master Gland" because it secretes hormones that regulate and control the functioning of all other endocrine glands in the body (e.g., Growth Hormone, Thyroid Stimulating Hormone, ACTH, and Gonadotropins like FSH and LH).

3. Adolescence and Puberty

Adolescence is defined as the transitional phase of physical and mental growth and development between childhood and adulthood. This period roughly covers the age of 11 to 19 years (the "teens").

The physical changes that occur during adolescence mark the onset of puberty. Puberty is the period when the human body undergoes changes leading to reproductive maturity. These changes are brought about by the secretion of sex hormones.

4. Physical and Psychological Changes

Physical Changes (Secondary Sexual Characters)

The changes brought about by sex hormones that help distinguish males from females are called secondary sexual characters.

  • Increase in Height: The most visible change; bones of the arms and legs elongate. Girls generally grow faster than boys initially, but both reach their maximum height by the end of adolescence.
  • Change in Body Shape: Boys develop broader shoulders and wider chests. In girls, the region below the waist (hips) becomes wider and more curved.
  • Voice Change: In boys, the voice box (larynx) enlarges and protrudes out in the neck, visible as the Adam's apple, causing a deep, hoarse voice. Girls usually maintain a high-pitched voice.
  • Activity of Sweat and Sebaceous Glands: Increased secretion from these glands often leads to acne and pimples on the face.
  • Development of Sex Organs: Complete development and maturation of male and female reproductive organs.

Psychological and Emotional Changes

  • Adolescents start thinking more independently and may experience mood swings and emotional instability due to fluctuating hormone levels.
  • They often feel the need for acceptance and can be easily influenced by peer groups and friends rather than family members.
  • Increased self-consciousness about their body and physical appearance.

5. Personal Hygiene and Stress Management

Importance of Personal Hygiene

Maintaining basic hygiene is crucial for preventing infections, especially given the increased activity of sweat and sebaceous glands during adolescence.

  • Take a bath regularly to clean away excess sweat and germs.
  • Wear clean, washed, and comfortable clothes (especially undergarments).
  • Wash hands properly before and after meals and after using the toilet.
  • Eat a balanced, healthy diet and avoid unhygienic junk food.
Important

Managing Stress:
Stress is the body's natural physical and mental response to life's changes or challenges. To manage stress effectively during adolescence:
• Engage in regular physical exercise or outdoor sports to boost mood.
• Practice meditation and focus on deep breathing in a quiet place.
• Pick up hobbies like reading, listening to music, or painting to divert the mind from negative thoughts.
• Ensure proper sleep and rest, and avoid bad habits like smoking or consuming alcohol.

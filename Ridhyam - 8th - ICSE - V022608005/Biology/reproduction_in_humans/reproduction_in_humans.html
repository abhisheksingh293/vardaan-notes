Reproduction in Humans

1. Introduction to Reproduction

Reproduction is the biological process by which living organisms replicate themselves or give rise to new individuals of their own kind. It is one of the most important processes ensuring the continuity of life. Both plants and animals exhibit this ability.

Concept

Asexual vs. Sexual Reproduction:
• Asexual Reproduction: The phenomenon of the production of new individuals from a single parent. No specialized sex cells are involved.
• Sexual Reproduction: The process where a male and female produce specialized reproductive cells (gametes) that fuse to create a new individual resembling its parents.

2. Asexual Reproduction in Animals

Asexual reproduction is common among lower, single-celled organisms and some simple multicellular animals.

  • Fission (Binary and Multiple): Fission is the splitting of a parent organism into two or more daughter cells.
    • Binary Fission: The parent cell divides into exactly two identical daughter cells. Example: Amoeba.
    • Multiple Fission: The nucleus divides repeatedly to produce many daughter cells simultaneously. Example: Plasmodium (malarial parasite).
  • Budding: A new individual develops from a small bud that protrudes out from the parent's body. The bud grows, detaches, and lives independently. Examples: Hydra, Yeast.
  • Regeneration & Fragmentation: The ability of some animals to generate lost body parts or grow a whole new organism from a severed fragment. Examples: Sponges, Planaria.

3. Sexual Reproduction in Animals

In sexual reproduction, specific reproductive organs produce sex cells called gametes. The male produces sperms, and the female produces eggs (ova). The fusion of a sperm and an ovum is called fertilisation, which results in a zygote. The zygote divides to form an embryo, which eventually develops into an adult.

4. Human Reproductive System

Humans have distinct male and female reproductive systems that become fully mature during puberty. These systems are responsible for producing gametes and supporting the development of offspring.

The Male Reproductive System

The male reproductive system consists of the following organs:

  • Testes (Testicles): A pair of oval structures where sperms (male gametes) and hormones (like testosterone) are produced. They are located outside the abdominal cavity in a sac called the scrotum, which provides a lower, optimum temperature for sperm production.
  • Epididymis: A coiled tube in close proximity to the testes where sperms mature and are stored.
  • Vas Deferens (Sperm Ducts): Tubes that transport mature sperms from the epididymis to the urethra.
  • Urethra: A common passage for both urine and sperm. It extends through the penis.
  • Penis: The muscular organ that delivers sperm into the female's vagina during fertilisation.
Fact

Accessory Glands and Semen:
The male reproductive system includes accessory glands: the seminal vesicles, prostate gland, and Cowper's gland. Their secretions provide a medium for sperm transport and nourishment. The mixture of these fluids and sperms is called Semen. A single ejaculation contains about 20 to 40 million sperms!

The Female Reproductive System

The female reproductive system is designed to produce eggs and carry a foetus to full term. Its main parts are:

  • Ovaries: A pair of whitish, oval structures situated in the abdominal cavity. They produce the female gametes called ova (eggs) and female sex hormones. Typically, one egg is released alternatively every month.
  • Oviducts (Fallopian Tubes): Paired, narrow muscular tubes where the egg enters after release. This is the site where fertilisation occurs.
  • Uterus (Womb): A hollow, pear-shaped muscular organ found in the pelvic cavity. The growth and development of the embryo take place here.
  • Vagina: A wide muscular tube connecting the uterus to the outside of the body. It receives the sperm during mating and serves as the birth canal.

5. Fertilisation, Pregnancy, and Birth

Fertilisation

Fertilisation is the process of fusion of a male gamete (sperm) and a female gamete (egg). In humans, this typically takes place inside the fallopian tube. Out of millions of sperms, only one successfully penetrates the egg. The head of the sperm fuses with the nucleus of the egg, forming a single-celled zygote.

Implantation

After fertilisation, the zygote travels down to the uterus while dividing repeatedly to form a ball of cells called a blastocyst. The sinking and attachment of this embryo into the endometrium (inner wall of the uterus) is called implantation. A disc-like structure called the placenta develops to connect the embryo to the mother's uterine wall, providing nutrients and oxygen.

Important

Growth and Birth:
• Growth: The embryo develops body parts and is eventually called a foetus. Growth occurs continuously throughout the gestation period.
• Gestation Period: The full term of embryo development in the uterus lasts for about 280 days (or 9 months) in humans.
• Parturition: The physical act of giving birth to a baby through the rhythmic contractions of the uterine muscles.

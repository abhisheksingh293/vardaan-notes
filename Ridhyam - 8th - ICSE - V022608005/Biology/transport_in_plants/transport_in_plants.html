Transport in Plants

1. Introduction to Transportation in Plants

All living organisms, including plants, need food, water, and oxygen for their survival. Green plants synthesize their own food through photosynthesis using carbon dioxide, water, and sunlight. Transportation in plants refers to the carrying of substances like food, water, and minerals from one part of the plant to another.

  • In single-celled organisms like Amoeba and some Algae, materials move by diffusion.
  • In higher multicellular plants, transportation is carried out by a specialized vascular system or conducting system consisting of two main tissues: Xylem and Phloem.

2. The Conducting or Transporting Tissues

The transport system consists of specialized pipe-like vessels divided into two distinct tissues:

Xylem (Wood)

Xylem is a complex tissue primarily meant to conduct water and dissolved mineral salts upward from the roots to the leaves (aerial parts). It also provides mechanical strength to the plant. Xylem consists of mostly dead cells and contains four elements:

  • Tracheids: Elongated dead cells with tapering ends and lignified (thickened) walls. They provide mechanical support and transport water.
  • Vessels: Cylindrical tube-like structures placed end to end to form a continuous channel. They are non-living with lignified walls.
  • Xylem Parenchyma: The only living cells in the xylem. They store food and help transport water laterally.
  • Xylem Fibres (Sclerenchyma): Lignified, thick-walled narrow cells that provide mechanical strength to the plant.

Phloem (Bast)

Phloem is a complex permanent tissue responsible for the transportation of synthesized food from the leaves to various storage organs (bidirectional flow). It consists of four elements:

  • Sieve Tubes: Elongated, cylindrical, thin-walled cells placed end to end. The end walls have pores forming sieve plates. They lack a nucleus at maturity but are living.
  • Companion Cells: Living cells adjacent to sieve tubes that supply energy and help in the functioning of the sieve tubes.
  • Phloem Parenchyma: Living cells that help in the storage of food.
  • Phloem Fibres: The only dead cells in phloem; they provide mechanical support.
Concept

Xylem vs Phloem:
• Xylem transports water and minerals unidirectionally (upwards), consists mostly of dead cells, and provides mechanical support.
• Phloem transports prepared food bidirectionally (up and down), consists mostly of living cells, and distributes nutrients throughout the plant.

3. Water Absorption by Roots

Roots fix the plant into the soil and absorb water and minerals through microscopic structures called root hairs. Root hairs have a thin, semi-permeable cell membrane filled with watery cell sap.

Adaptations of Root Hair for Absorption:

  • They provide a large surface area for maximum absorption.
  • The cell membrane is semi-permeable (allows specific particles to pass).
  • The concentration of cell sap is higher than that of the surrounding soil water, triggering osmosis.

Methods of Absorption:

  • Diffusion: The spontaneous movement of molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration down the concentration gradient.
  • Osmosis: The movement of water (solvent) molecules from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration through a semi-permeable membrane.
  • Active Transport: The movement of minerals against the concentration gradient (from lower to higher concentration) using energy.
Fact

Ascent of Sap: The upward passage of water from the roots to the tips of the leaves is called the ascent of sap. It happens due to two major forces: Root Pressure (hydrostatic pressure pushing water up) and Transpirational Pull (suction force created by the evaporation of water from leaves).

4. Transpiration

Transpiration is the process by which a plant loses water in the form of water vapour through its aerial parts (mainly leaves via stomata). It is an essential process that facilitates water transport and keeps the plant cool.

Factors Affecting the Rate of Transpiration:

  • Light: Stomata open in the presence of light. Transpiration is maximum during the day and minimum at night.
  • Temperature: High temperatures increase the rate of evaporation, increasing transpiration.
  • Relative Humidity: Humidity is inversely proportional to transpiration. High humidity reduces the rate of transpiration.
  • Wind Speed: Faster winds move water vapour away from the leaf surface, increasing the transpiration rate.
  • Water Availability: Scarcity of water causes stomata to close, thereby decreasing transpiration and causing wilting.
Important

Significance of Transpiration:
1. Creates a transpirational pull (suction force) that helps in the ascent of sap.
2. Provides a cooling effect to the plant during hot summers.
3. Helps maintain turgidity of the cells, keeping the plant upright.
4. Assists in the excretion of excess water.

5. Importance of Minerals

Minerals are absorbed from the soil as ions (e.g., Nitrates, Phosphates). They are essential for sustainable plant growth, flowering, and fruiting. They are categorized based on the quantity required:

  • Macro-minerals (Macro-nutrients): Required in larger amounts. Examples: Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulphur (S).
  • Micro-minerals (Micro-nutrients): Required in trace (small) amounts. Examples: Iron (Fe), Manganese (Mn), Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn), Boron (B), Molybdenum (Mo), Chlorine (Cl).

Common Symptoms of Mineral Deficiency:

  • Nitrogen deficiency: Poor growth, yellowing of older leaves (chlorosis).
  • Phosphorus deficiency: Wilting, poor growth, purpling of veins, delayed flowering.
  • Potassium deficiency: Drying of leaf edges, reduced fruit yield.
  • Iron deficiency: Interveinal chlorosis in younger leaves.

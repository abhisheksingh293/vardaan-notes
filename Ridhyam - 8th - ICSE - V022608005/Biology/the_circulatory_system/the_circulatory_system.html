The Circulatory System

1. Introduction to the Circulatory System

The circulatory system, also known as the cardiovascular system, is one of the most vital organ systems in the body. It is responsible for transporting blood, oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products to and from the body's cells. The human circulatory system comprises three main components: Blood, Heart, and Blood Vessels.

Concept

Types of Circulatory Systems:
• Open Circulatory System: Blood flows through open body cavities (haemocoel), and the organs are directly bathed in blood. Found in insects (e.g., cockroach).
• Closed Circulatory System: Blood flows strictly inside closed blood vessels under pressure. Found in all vertebrates, including humans.

2. Blood and its Components

Blood is a fluid connective tissue that circulates throughout the body. It consists of a liquid part called Plasma (55%) and a solid part called Blood Cells (45%).

  • Plasma: The liquid component of blood. It is a thick, pale-yellow fluid mostly made up of water (about 90%) and contains dissolved proteins (albumin, globulin), nutrients, and waste.
  • Red Blood Cells (RBCs / Erythrocytes): Biconcave, non-nucleated, disc-like cells. They contain a red pigment called haemoglobin, which binds to oxygen and transports it to all cells.
  • White Blood Cells (WBCs / Leucocytes): Irregularly shaped cells that protect the body against infections and diseases by destroying pathogens.
  • Platelets (Thrombocytes): Tiny cell fragments that play a crucial role in the clotting of blood at the site of a wound or injury.

Functions of Blood:

  • Transports respiratory gases (Oxygen and Carbon dioxide).
  • Transports nutrients to the cells and metabolic wastes to the excretory organs.
  • Transports hormones from endocrine glands to target organs.
  • Maintains and regulates body temperature.

3. The Heart: The Pumping Organ

The human heart is a highly muscular organ located in the chest cavity, slightly tilted towards the left side. It is about the size of a human fist. It is protected by the rib cage and enclosed in a double-layered membrane called the pericardium.

Structure of the Human Heart

The heart is divided into four chambers. The right and left sides are separated by a muscular wall called the septum, which prevents the mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.

  • Atria (Auricles): The two upper chambers (Right and Left Atrium). They have thin walls and receive blood.
  • Ventricles: The two lower chambers (Right and Left Ventricle). They have thick muscular walls because they pump blood out of the heart.

Valves inside the Heart

Valves ensure the unidirectional (one-way) flow of blood and prevent its backflow:

  • Tricuspid Valve: Between the right atrium and right ventricle.
  • Bicuspid (Mitral) Valve: Between the left atrium and left ventricle.
  • Pulmonary Valve: Between the right ventricle and pulmonary artery.
  • Aortic Valve: Between the left ventricle and aorta.
Fact

Heart Beat and Sounds:
The rhythmic contraction (Systole) and relaxation (Diastole) of the heart muscles is known as a heartbeat. A normal human heart beats about 72 times per minute. The heartbeat produces a characteristic "lub-dub" sound due to the closing of the valves. It is heard using an instrument called a stethoscope.

4. Blood Vessels

Blood vessels are pathways or tubes through which blood flows. There are three main types:

  • Arteries: Carry oxygenated blood away from the heart to body parts (except the pulmonary artery). They have thick, elastic walls to handle high blood pressure and do not have valves.
  • Veins: Carry deoxygenated blood towards the heart from body parts (except the pulmonary vein). They have thin walls, carry blood under low pressure, and possess valves to prevent backflow.
  • Capillaries: Extremely thin, single-cell thick vessels where the actual exchange of gases, nutrients, and waste takes place between blood and tissues.

5. Double Circulation

In humans, blood flows twice through the heart to complete one cycle. This is called Double Circulation. It consists of:

  • Pulmonary Circulation: The flow of deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs (for oxygenation) and back to the left atrium.
  • Systemic Circulation: The flow of oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to all body parts and the return of deoxygenated blood to the right atrium.

6. The Lymphatic System

The lymphatic system is closely associated with the circulatory system. It consists of lymph, lymph vessels, and lymph nodes.

  • Lymph: A clear, colourless fluid (interstitial fluid) that contains plasma and white blood cells (lymphocytes) but lacks RBCs.
  • Functions: It transports nutrients to areas where blood cannot reach, absorbs fats from the intestine, and defends the body against infections.
Important

Blood Groups (ABO System):
Discovered by Karl Landsteiner. Human blood is categorized into four groups based on antigens present on RBCs: A, B, AB, and O.
• Group O is the Universal Donor (can give blood to any group).
• Group AB is the Universal Recipient (can receive blood from any group).

7. Heart Related Conditions & Maintenance

  • Hypertension (High Blood Pressure): A condition where blood flows through the arteries at a higher pressure than normal, which can damage organs over time.
  • Heart Attack (Myocardial Infarction): Caused by the interruption of blood supply to the cardiac muscles, usually due to a blockage (plaque) in the coronary artery.
  • Cardiac Arrest: A sudden and severe condition where the heart unexpectedly stops beating.

Keeping the Heart Healthy: Avoid smoking, exercise daily (30-60 mins), eat a healthy and balanced diet, maintain a healthy weight, and ensure adequate sleep.

Physical and Chemical Changes

1. Introduction

In our daily life, we constantly observe various changes in our surroundings. These changes happen in states, shapes, sizes, and chemical compositions of matter. Common examples include making a sugar solution, the formation of curd from milk, the rusting of iron, the ripening of fruits, and changing weather.

2. Broad Classification of Changes

Changes happening around us can be primarily divided into four categories based on how they occur, their duration, and their reversibility.

A. Natural and Man-Made Changes

  • Natural Changes: Changes that take place on their own in nature without any human interference. Examples: Growth of a tree, change of seasons, volcanic eruptions.
  • Man-Made Changes: Changes that happen due to human actions or under the control of human beings. Examples: Cooking of food, burning of fuel.

B. Periodic and Non-Periodic Changes

  • Periodic Changes: Changes that repeat themselves after a fixed interval of time. Examples: Day and night, beating of a human heart, movement of a pendulum.
  • Non-Periodic Changes: Changes that do not repeat after a fixed interval of time and occur randomly. Examples: Rusting of iron, volcanic eruptions, earthquakes.

C. Slow and Fast Changes

  • Slow Changes: Changes that take a long time (days, months, or years) to complete. Examples: Formation of curd from milk, rusting of iron, growth of a seedling into a tree.
  • Fast Changes: Changes that take place in a very short span of time (seconds or minutes). Examples: Burning of a matchstick, bursting of a cracker.

D. Reversible and Irreversible Changes

  • Reversible Changes: A change that can be reversed to obtain the original substance easily. Examples: Stretching of a spring, melting of ice into water (which can be frozen back into ice).
  • Irreversible Changes: A change that cannot be reversed to get the original substance back. Examples: Curdling of milk, burning of paper.
Important

Note: A particular change can fall into multiple categories at once. For example, the rusting of iron is a natural change, a slow change, a non-periodic change, and an irreversible change all at the same time.

3. Physical Changes

Matter undergoes physical changes depending on whether a new substance is formed or not.

Concept

Physical Change: A temporary and reversible change in which no new substance is formed. Only the physical properties (like state, shape, color, or size) of the substance undergo a change.

Examples of Physical Changes:

  • Change of State: Melting of ice into water, and boiling of water into vapour. Cooling the vapour reverses it back to water and then ice.
  • Dissolving Sugar: Sugar dissolved in water is a physical change because heating the solution evaporates the water, leaving the original sugar crystals behind.
  • Sublimation: Heating solid camphor or iodine changes it directly into a vapour state without becoming a liquid. On cooling, the vapour turns back into a solid.
  • Expansion of Metals: A wooden wheel of a bullock cart is fitted with an iron rim. The iron rim is made slightly smaller than the wheel. When heated, the iron expands and fits onto the wheel. Pouring cold water causes the iron to contract and fit tightly.
Fact

Key Characteristics of a Physical Change:
1. It is a temporary and generally reversible change.
2. No new substance is formed.
3. The chemical composition remains exactly the same.
4. Usually, there is no net gain or loss of energy (heat or light).
5. There is no change in the mass of the substance.

4. Chemical Changes

Unlike physical changes, chemical changes alter the fundamental structure of the matter involved.

Concept

Chemical Change: A permanent change in which the chemical composition and properties of a substance are changed, resulting in the formation of one or more new substances.

Examples of Chemical Changes:

  • Rusting of Iron: Iron reacts with oxygen and moisture in the air to form a reddish-brown flaky substance called iron oxide (rust).
  • Curdling of Milk: Adding a few drops of lemon juice or a small amount of curd to warm milk changes it permanently into curd, which has entirely different properties from milk.
  • Reaction of Metals with Acid: Adding dilute sulphuric acid to a magnesium ribbon produces a new substance (magnesium sulphate) and releases hydrogen gas (which burns with a 'pop' sound).
  • Heating Compounds: Heating green copper carbonate produces two entirely new substances: black copper oxide and carbon dioxide gas.
  • Burning of Sugar: Heating sugar in a porcelain dish melts it, turns it brown, and finally leaves a black powdery substance (charcoal) while releasing water vapour.
Fact

Testing for Gas Evolution (A sign of Chemical Change):
When baking soda reacts with vinegar or lemon juice, it produces a fizz. The gas evolved is carbon dioxide ($CO_{2}$). The presence of carbon dioxide is confirmed by passing it through lime water, which turns milky due to the formation of calcium carbonate.

Important

Key Characteristics of a Chemical Change:
1. It is a permanent and irreversible change.
2. New substances with completely new properties are formed.
3. A large amount of energy (heat/light) is either absorbed or released.
4. The mass of the individual substance may change because matter is either added or removed during the reaction.

5. Simultaneous Physical and Chemical Changes

In some cases, physical and chemical changes occur side-by-side at the exact same time.

  • Burning of a Candle:
    • Physical Change: The melting of solid wax into liquid wax, and liquid wax back into solid wax upon cooling.
    • Chemical Change: The liquid wax turning into wax vapour and burning in the air to produce carbon dioxide, water vapour, heat, and light.
  • Cooking an Egg:
    • Chemical Change: The protein in the egg white denatures when heated.
    • Physical Change: The egg white solidifying.

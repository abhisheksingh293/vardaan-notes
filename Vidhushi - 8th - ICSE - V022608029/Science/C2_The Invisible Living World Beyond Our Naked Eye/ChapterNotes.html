THE INVISIBLE LIVING WORLD: BEYOND OUR NAKED EYE

1. Cells: The Fundamental Units of Life

Concept

Cell: The most basic, structural, and functional unit of life. Just as bricks are the building blocks of houses, cells are the microscopic building blocks of all living organisms. Every living body is a complex organization of these cells.

The Discovery of the Cell

  • In 1665, British scientist Robert Hooke examined a slice of cork under a simple self-made microscope. He observed tiny honeycomb-like boxes and named them "cells" (meaning 'a little room' in Latin).
  • Later, a Dutch microbiologist named Anton van Leeuwenhoek used a more powerful microscope to become the first human to view living cells (like human blood and bacteria).
Fact

The Electron Microscope: Designed by Knoll and Ruska (Germany) in 1932, it can magnify objects up to half a million times! India's first electron microscope was built by Prof N.N. Dasgupta between 1946 and 1948 at the Saha Institute of Nuclear Physics.

Size, Shape, and Types of Organisms

  • Size: Cells are measured in microns (µ) or micrometres (µm). 1 µm = 0.001 mm. The average size ranges from 3 µm to 30 µm.
  • Shape: Shape depends on function. Amoeba and White Blood Cells (WBCs) change shape frequently. Nerve cells are long; Red Blood Cells (RBCs) are circular; muscle cells are spindle-shaped.
  • Unicellular Organisms: Made of a single cell that performs all life processes. Examples: Amoeba, bacteria, paramecium, yeast, and Chlamydomonas.
  • Multicellular Organisms: Made of many cells (e.g., an adult human has about 100 trillion cells). Examples: Humans, animals, birds, and trees.

Structural Hierarchy in Multicellular Organisms

In complex organisms, cells work together in a structured hierarchy to maintain life:

Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems → Organism

2. Inside the Cell: Cell Organelles

A typical cell consists of three main components: an outer covering (cell membrane), a dense central structure (nucleus), and a jelly-like fluid (cytoplasm). The specialized structures floating inside the cytoplasm are known as cell organelles.

Important

Selectively Permeable Membrane: The cell membrane acts as a smart guard. It controls the entry and exit of materials, allowing only specific nutrients and water to pass while blocking harmful or large waste molecules.

Key Cell Organelles and Their Functions

  • Nucleus: The control centre of the cell. It controls all activities, stores genetic information (chromatin), and directs protein synthesis.
  • Cytoplasm: A jelly-like fluid containing carbohydrates, proteins, fats, and minerals. It holds organelles in place and is the site for most life processes.
  • Mitochondria: Small, double-membrane organelles. They are the powerhouses of the cell, producing energy during cellular respiration.
  • Ribosomes: Tiny, non-membrane structures responsible for protein synthesis.
  • Lysosomes: Found mostly in animal cells. They act as the cell's clean-up system by using digestive enzymes to break down waste and worn-out parts.
  • Vacuole: A membrane-bound sac that stores water, food, and waste. Plants have a large central vacuole for rigidity; animals have small, numerous ones.
  • Plastids: Found only in plant cells. Chloroplasts carry out photosynthesis; chromoplasts give colour to flowers/fruits; leucoplasts store starch and proteins.
  • Cell Wall: A rigid outer layer made of cellulose found only in plant cells, providing strength and shape.

3. The World of Microorganisms

Microorganisms (Microbes) are tiny organisms visible only under a microscope. The study of microbes is called microbiology.

They survive in extreme environments—from hot deserts and active volcanoes to polar ice caps and the ocean floor. To survive adverse conditions, many form a hard protective covering called a cyst.

The Five Major Groups of Microorganisms

  • Bacteria: Unicellular organisms found everywhere. Classified by shape: Bacilli (rod), Cocci (spherical), Spirilla (spiral), and comma-shaped.
  • Algae: Green, plant-like organisms capable of autotrophic nutrition (photosynthesis) using chlorophyll. They live primarily in water. Examples: Chlamydomonas, Spirogyra.
  • Fungi: Plant-like organisms that lack chlorophyll. They display saprophytic (feeding on decaying matter) or parasitic nutrition. Examples: Yeast, bread mould, mushrooms, Penicillium.
  • Protozoa: Unicellular organisms with animal-like characteristics. They move and capture food (heterotrophic/holozoic). Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena, Plasmodium.
  • Viruses: The smallest microbes. They lack a true cellular structure and can only reproduce inside a living host cell. Because they are inactive outside a host, they are considered a link between living and non-living things.

4. Useful Microorganisms

While some microbes cause diseases (pathogens), many are highly beneficial to humans, agriculture, and the environment.

In the Food and Beverage Industry

  • Curd and Cheese: The bacterium Lactobacillus multiplies in lukewarm milk, converting it into curd.
  • Bread Production: Yeast reproduces rapidly and breaks down sugar to produce carbon dioxide gas and alcohol. This process is called fermentation. The trapped CO₂ causes bread dough to rise and become fluffy.
  • Vinegar Production: In the presence of air, bacteria like Acetobacter convert fermented alcohol into acetic acid (vinegar).

In Medicine and Healthcare

  • Antibiotics: Medicines prepared from specific bacteria and fungi that kill or inhibit the growth of disease-causing microbes. A famous example is Penicillin, extracted from the Penicillium mould.
  • Vaccines: Preparations containing dead or weakened microbes. They teach the immune system to fight diseases, developing immunity against ailments like polio, cholera, and tuberculosis.

In Agriculture and the Environment

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Bacteria like Rhizobium (living in root nodules) and blue-green algae convert atmospheric nitrogen into nitrates, naturally increasing soil fertility.
  • Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi decompose dead plants and animals into simpler inorganic substances, recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem.
  • Producing Clean Fuel: Microbes break down organic waste and animal dung through anaerobic decomposition to produce Biogas (primarily methane).
  • Oxygen Production: Tiny marine algae called Phytoplankton produce at least half of the Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis.
Concept

Symbiotic Microbiota (Good Bacteria): The human gut is home to trillions of helpful microbes. These good bacteria coexist with us symbiotically—they help digest complex foods while our bodies provide them with a safe environment to live.

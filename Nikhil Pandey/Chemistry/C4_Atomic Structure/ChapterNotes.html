ATOMIC STRUCTURE

1. Introduction to the Atom

Concept

An Atom is the fundamental building block of matter. Initially thought to be indivisible (according to Dalton's Atomic Theory), modern science proved that the atom is indeed divisible into much smaller subatomic particles: Electrons, Protons, and Neutrons.

2. Subatomic Particles

Atoms are composed of three primary subatomic particles. Each particle has specific characteristics and a unique location within the atom.

  • Electrons (e-): Discovered by J.J. Thomson. They are negatively charged particles revolving around the nucleus in specific paths. Their mass is considered negligible (about 1/1837 of a proton).
  • Protons (p+): Discovered by E. Goldstein (and named by Rutherford). They are positively charged particles located inside the central nucleus. Their mass is considered as 1 atomic mass unit (amu).
  • Neutrons (n0): Discovered by James Chadwick. They are neutral particles (having no electrical charge) located inside the nucleus. Their mass is almost equal to that of a proton (1 amu).

3. Structure of an Atom

The structure of an atom is broadly divided into two distinct regions:

  • The Nucleus: The dense, extremely small, and positively charged center of the atom. It contains protons and neutrons, which are collectively called nucleons. Because protons are positive and neutrons are neutral, the entire nucleus bears a positive charge.
  • Extra-nuclear Region (Orbits/Shells): The vast empty space surrounding the nucleus where electrons constantly revolve in fixed circular paths. These paths are called shells or energy levels (represented as K, L, M, N...).
Important

Neutrality of an Atom: A normal atom always has an equal number of protons and electrons. Because the positive charges perfectly balance the negative charges, the atom is electrically neutral as a whole.

4. Atomic Number and Mass Number

Every element is uniquely identified by the number of particles present in its atoms.

  • Atomic Number (Z): The total number of protons present in the nucleus of an atom. For a neutral atom, it is also equal to the number of electrons.
    Atomic Number (Z) = Number of Protons
  • Mass Number (A): The sum of the number of protons and neutrons present in the nucleus of an atom.
    Mass Number (A) = Protons (p) + Neutrons (n)

To find the number of neutrons in an atom, you can easily use the formula: Neutrons = Mass Number (A) - Atomic Number (Z).

5. Electronic Configuration

The systematic arrangement and distribution of electrons in various shells (orbits) around the nucleus is called electronic configuration.

Fact

The Bohr-Bury Scheme: The maximum number of electrons that can be accommodated in any shell is given by the formula 2n2, where 'n' is the shell number. However, the outermost shell can never hold more than 8 electrons (the Octet Rule).

Based on the Bohr-Bury scheme, electrons fill the energy levels as follows:

  • K shell (n=1): Can hold a maximum of 2 × (1)2 = 2 electrons.
  • L shell (n=2): Can hold a maximum of 2 × (2)2 = 8 electrons.
  • M shell (n=3): Can hold a maximum of 2 × (3)2 = 18 electrons.

6. Valency and Ions

Atoms constantly strive to achieve stability by having a full outermost shell (usually 8 electrons). The combining capacity of an element to achieve this stable configuration is called its valency.

  • Cations (Positive Ions): Formed when metal atoms lose electrons from their valence shell. (E.g., Sodium loses 1 electron to form Na+).
  • Anions (Negative Ions): Formed when non-metal atoms gain electrons into their valence shell. (E.g., Chlorine gains 1 electron to form Cl-).

7. Isotopes and Isobars

Not all atoms of the same element are perfectly identical in mass, and sometimes atoms of different elements can share the exact same mass.

  • Isotopes: Atoms of the same element having the same atomic number but different mass numbers (due to a differing number of neutrons). Example: Hydrogen has three isotopes - Protium, Deuterium, and Tritium. They exhibit identical chemical properties.
  • Isobars: Atoms of different elements having the same mass number but different atomic numbers. Example: Argon (Atomic No. 18, Mass No. 40) and Calcium (Atomic No. 20, Mass No. 40). Because they are completely different elements, they exhibit vastly different physical and chemical properties.

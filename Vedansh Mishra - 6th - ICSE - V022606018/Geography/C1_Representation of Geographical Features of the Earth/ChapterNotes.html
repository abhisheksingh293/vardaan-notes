Representation of Geographical Features of the Earth

1. Introduction to Geography and The Globe

The word 'Geography' is derived from two Greek words—Geo (meaning Earth) and Graphien (meaning to write). It means to write about or to describe the geographical features of the earth and their influence on human beings.

Concept

The Globe: A globe is a three-dimensional true model of the earth. It shows the exact shapes of oceans, continents, parallels of latitudes, and meridians of longitudes.

Limitations of a Globe

While a globe gives us the truest possible information about the earth, it has several drawbacks:

  • It is very expensive.
  • At one time, we can only see one part of the earth.
  • It is heavy and awkward to carry from one place to another.
  • It does not show small detailed information (e.g., studying a specific city like New Delhi is difficult).

2. Understanding Maps

To overcome the limitations of a globe, maps were developed. A Map is the representation of the earth's surface or a part of it on a flat surface, such as a piece of paper, drawn to a specific scale.

Fact Track

A collection of maps of the world, countries, and continents is called an Atlas. A person who draws maps is called a Cartographer, and the study and practice of map making is known as Cartography.

2.1 Types of Maps

Based on their purpose, maps can be broadly categorized into three types:

  • Physical Maps: These maps show the natural physical features of the earth, such as mountains, valleys, plains, and rivers. They are generally small-scale maps.
  • Political Maps: These maps are used to demarcate political boundaries. They show countries, states, districts, cities, and capitals.
  • Thematic Maps: These maps focus on specific themes. They are based on certain information, such as rainfall distribution, population density, roads, industries, etc.

3. Components of Maps

There are four major components of a map. Without these components, a map cannot be studied effectively. They act as the storehouse of information for a map:

3.1 Title

The title shows the name of the map and tells the reader what the map is all about (e.g., "Rainfall Distribution in India").

3.2 Scale

Scale is the ratio between a unit distance on the map and the proportionate actual distance on the ground.

  • Statement Scale (Verbal Scale): Uses simple words to describe the scale. Example: 1 cm = 200 km.
  • Linear Scale: A straight line divided into lengths that represent specific distances on the earth.
  • R.F. (Representative Fraction) Scale: The ratio is shown as a fraction without mentioning units. The numerator is always 1 (e.g., 1:50,000). This means 1 unit of length on the map represents 50,000 of the same units on the ground.

Small Scale vs Large Scale Maps:

  • Small Scale Maps: Represent large areas (like the world or continents) and show fewer details. Atlas maps are small-scale maps.
  • Large Scale Maps: Represent small areas (like a city or road network) and provide plenty of details.

3.3 Direction

A course along which someone or something moves. Most maps show direction with an arrow marked 'N' (North) in the corner. Directions are divided into:

  • Cardinal Directions: North (N), South (S), East (E), West (W).
  • Intermediate Directions: North-East (NE), South-East (SE), North-West (NW), South-West (SW).

3.4 Conventional Symbols

Maps have limited space, so it is impossible to draw actual shapes of features like temples or bridges. Instead, universally accepted Conventional Symbols are used to represent them. These include Line symbols (roads), Point symbols (buildings), and Area symbols (vegetation).

Standard Colors Used in Maps:

  • Brown: Land features like Mountains.
  • Black: Roads, Railways, Boundaries.
  • Blue: Water bodies (Oceans, rivers, lakes).
  • Red: Settlements, construction features.
  • Green: Vegetation and Plains.

4. Grid, Sketch, and Plan

Definition

Grid: A grid is a set of horizontal lines (parallels of latitudes) and vertical lines (meridians of longitudes) intersecting each other. It is used to find the exact location of a place on a map.

4.1 Sketch and Plan

  • Sketch: A rough drawing or layout of a place made from memory or observation, without using any scale.
  • Plan: A large-scale map that shows a small area with highly detailed information, such as the technical layout of a building. It is strictly based on a scale.

Fundamental Rights, Duties and Directive Principles

1. Introduction

After a long period of slavery and exploitation at the hands of the British, the people of India aspired for a life of dignity, equality, and freedom. To transform the lofty ideals of our Preamble into reality, our Constitution framers guaranteed specific protections and guidelines to ensure the holistic development of the nation. In this chapter, we explore the essential pillars that safeguard citizens and guide the state.

2. Fundamental Rights

Fundamental Rights are considered essential for the physical, intellectual, and spiritual development of every individual and to preserve human dignity. Because we were denied basic civil and political rights by the British, ensuring these rights became a priority. These rights are justiciable, meaning one can approach the court if they are violated.

The Constitution of India provides six Fundamental Rights to its citizens:

  • Right to Equality: Ensures equal protection before the law. Abolishes untouchability, untouchable practices, and titles. It ensures equal opportunities in public employment.
  • Right to Freedom: Guarantees freedom of speech and expression, freedom to assemble peacefully, form associations, move freely, reside anywhere in India, and practice any profession.
  • Right against Exploitation: Prohibits human trafficking, forced labor (begar), and the employment of children under 14 years in hazardous jobs like factories and mines.
  • Right to Freedom of Religion: India has no state religion. Every person has the right to freely profess, practice, and propagate their religion.
  • Cultural and Educational Rights: Protects the rights of minorities to conserve their distinct language, script, or culture, and allows them to establish educational institutions.
  • Right to Constitutional Remedies: Empowers a citizen to move the Supreme Court or High Courts for the enforcement of these rights.
Important Heart and Soul of the Constitution:

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called the Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32) the "Heart and Soul of the Constitution" because, without it, all other rights would be meaningless. It guarantees the protection of the remaining rights.

Fact The Right to Property:

Originally, there were seven Fundamental Rights. However, the Right to Property was removed from the list of Fundamental Rights by the 44th Amendment Act in 1978 and was made merely a legal right.

3. Fundamental Duties

Rights and duties are two sides of the same coin. A right without a corresponding duty is meaningless. The Fundamental Duties were not originally in the Constitution; they were added later by the 42nd Amendment Act in 1976 (inspired by the Constitution of Russia/USSR).

Key Fundamental Duties

  • To abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals, institutions, the National Flag, and the National Anthem.
  • To cherish and follow the noble ideals which inspired our national struggle for freedom.
  • To uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India.
  • To defend the country and render national service when called upon to do so.
  • To value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture.
  • To protect and improve the natural environment, including forests, lakes, rivers, and wildlife.
  • To safeguard public property and to abjure violence.

4. Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP)

The framers of our Constitution wanted India to become a Welfare State. To achieve this, they included certain guidelines for the government to follow while framing laws and policies. These are called the Directive Principles of State Policy, a concept borrowed from the Constitution of Ireland.

Nature of DPSP

Unlike Fundamental Rights, the Directive Principles are non-justiciable. This means that if the government fails to implement them, a citizen cannot go to court to enforce them. They are moral obligations on the government.

Important Directives

  • To provide adequate means of livelihood for all citizens (men and women equally).
  • To ensure equal pay for equal work for both men and women.
  • To prevent the concentration of wealth in the hands of a few.
  • To provide free and compulsory education for children up to 14 years of age (later made a Fundamental Right under Article 21A).
  • To promote the educational and economic interests of Scheduled Castes (SCs), Scheduled Tribes (STs), and other weaker sections.
  • To organize village panchayats and endow them with power.
  • To improve public health and prohibit the consumption of intoxicating drinks and drugs.
Concept The Welfare State:

A welfare state is a concept of government in which the state plays a key role in the protection and promotion of the social and economic well-being of its citizens. The Directive Principles are the primary tools given to the Indian government to transition India from a simple democracy to a full-fledged welfare state.

Mensuration

1. Introduction

In previous classes, you have learned to find the perimeter and area of standard plane figures (2D shapes) like squares, rectangles, triangles, and circles. Mensuration is the branch of mathematics that deals with the measurement of geometric figures and their parameters like length, area, surface area, and volume.

Concept

Recap of Basic 2D Areas:

  • Square: Area $= \text{side} \times \text{side} = a^2$
  • Rectangle: Area $= \text{length} \times \text{breadth} = l \times b$
  • Triangle: Area $= \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}bh$
  • Parallelogram: Area $= \text{base} \times \text{height} = bh$
  • Circle: Area $= \pi \times \text{radius}^2 = \pi r^2$

2. Area of a Trapezium

A trapezium is a quadrilateral with exactly one pair of opposite sides that are parallel.

Fact

Formula for Area of a Trapezium:

$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{Sum of parallel sides}) \times (\text{distance between them}) $$

If $a$ and $b$ are the lengths of the parallel sides, and $h$ is the perpendicular height (distance between them), then:

$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times (a + b) \times h $$

Derivation Concept: We can find this area by drawing a diagonal that splits the trapezium into two separate triangles. Both triangles will share the same height $h$, but one will have base $a$ and the other base $b$. Adding their areas ($\frac{1}{2}ah + \frac{1}{2}bh$) gives the formula!

Practice Problems: Trapezium

  • Q1. Find the area of a trapezium whose parallel sides are $12 \text{ cm}$ and $20 \text{ cm}$ respectively, and the perpendicular distance between them is $8 \text{ cm}$.
  • Q2. The area of a trapezium is $480 \text{ cm}^2$, the distance between two parallel sides is $15 \text{ cm}$ and one of the parallel sides is $20 \text{ cm}$. Find the length of the other parallel side.

3. Area of a General Quadrilateral

We can find the area of any general quadrilateral by splitting it into two triangles using a diagonal.

Important

General Quadrilateral Formula:

If you draw a diagonal of length $d$, and drop perpendiculars (altitudes) $h_1$ and $h_2$ from the remaining two opposite vertices onto this diagonal, the area is the sum of the two triangles formed.

$$ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times d \times (h_1 + h_2) $$

Area of Special Quadrilaterals (Rhombus)

A rhombus is a special quadrilateral where the diagonals bisect each other at right angles ($90^\circ$). If the lengths of its diagonals are $d_1$ and $d_2$, we can use the general quadrilateral formula where the first diagonal is $d_1$ and the two altitudes sum up exactly to the length of the second diagonal $d_2$.

$$ \text{Area of a Rhombus} = \frac{1}{2} \times d_1 \times d_2 $$

Practice Problems: Quadrilaterals & Rhombus

  • Q1. The diagonal of a quadrilateral shaped field is $24 \text{ m}$ and the perpendiculars dropped on it from the remaining opposite vertices are $8 \text{ m}$ and $13 \text{ m}$. Find the area of the field.
  • Q2. Find the area of a rhombus whose diagonals are of lengths $10 \text{ cm}$ and $8.2 \text{ cm}$.

4. Area of a Polygon

There is no single formula for a complex, irregular polygon (like a pentagon or hexagon). We calculate its area by triangulation.

Method: We split the given polygon into a combination of known, non-overlapping shapes—usually triangles, rectangles, and trapeziums. We calculate the area of each individual part and sum them up to find the total area of the polygon.

Practice Problem: Polygon

  • Q1. A pentagonal park is divided into three triangles by drawing two diagonals from one vertex. If the base and heights of these three triangles are given as $(b_1=5, h_1=4)$, $(b_2=6, h_2=5)$, and $(b_3=4, h_3=3)$ respectively (all in meters), find the total area of the park.

5. Surface Area of Solid Shapes (3D)

Solids are three-dimensional (3D) shapes. The Surface Area of a solid is the total area of all its exposed faces. We look at two types: Lateral/Curved Surface Area (LSA/CSA) which excludes the top and bottom bases, and Total Surface Area (TSA).

Concept

1. Cuboid (length $l$, breadth $b$, height $h$)

  • Total Surface Area (TSA): $$ 2(lb + bh + lh) $$
  • Lateral Surface Area (LSA): Area of 4 walls $= 2(l + b) \times h$

2. Cube (side $l$)

  • Total Surface Area (TSA): $$ 6l^2 $$
  • Lateral Surface Area (LSA): $$ 4l^2 $$

3. Right Circular Cylinder (radius $r$, height $h$)

  • Total Surface Area (TSA): $$ 2\pi r(r + h) $$
  • Curved Surface Area (CSA): $$ 2\pi rh $$

Practice Problems: Surface Area

  • Q1. Rukhsar painted the outside of a cabinet of measure $1\text{ m} \times 2\text{ m} \times 1.5\text{ m}$. How much surface area did she cover if she painted all except the bottom of the cabinet?
  • Q2. A closed cylindrical tank of radius $7 \text{ m}$ and height $3 \text{ m}$ is made from a sheet of metal. How much sheet of metal is required?

6. Volume and Capacity

Volume is the amount of space occupied by a three-dimensional object. Capacity refers to the quantity that a container can hold (usually measured in Liters or mL).

Fact

Formulas for Volume:

  • Volume of a Cuboid: $$ V = l \times b \times h $$
  • Volume of a Cube: $$ V = l^3 $$
  • Volume of a Cylinder: $$ V = \pi r^2 h $$
Important

Crucial Unit Conversions:

To solve capacity problems, you must memorize these conversions:

  • $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$
  • $1 \text{ Liter (L)} = 1000 \text{ cm}^3$
  • $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1,000,000 \text{ cm}^3 = 1000 \text{ Liters}$

Practice Problems: Volume

  • Q1. A cuboidal godown is $8 \text{ m}$ long, $6 \text{ m}$ broad and $3 \text{ m}$ high. How many cubical boxes of side $2 \text{ m}$ can be stored in it?
  • Q2. The circumference of the base of a cylindrical vessel is $132 \text{ cm}$ and its height is $25 \text{ cm}$. How many liters of water can it hold? (Use $\pi = \frac{22}{7}$)
  • Q3. Find the height of a cuboid whose volume is $275 \text{ cm}^3$ and base area is $25 \text{ cm}^2$. (Hint: Volume = Base Area $\times$ height)

7. Chapter Summary (You Must Know)

  • The magnitude of a plane region is called its area.
  • Area of a trapezium $= \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{sum of parallel sides}) \times (\text{distance between them})$.
  • Area of a general quadrilateral $= \frac{1}{2} \times d \times (h_1 + h_2)$.
  • We find the area of any complex polygon by splitting it into simpler shapes like triangles and rectangles.
  • Surface area of a solid is the sum of the areas of all its outer faces.
  • The amount of 3D region occupied by a solid is called its volume.
  • Remember that $1 \text{ m}^3$ is equivalent to $1000 \text{ Liters}$.

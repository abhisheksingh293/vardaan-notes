3. Exponents and Radicals

In the previous classes, we studied the exponents of rational numbers and their foundational laws. In this chapter, we extend our understanding from integer exponents to rational numbers as exponents (fractions) and explore the laws governing them.

1. Recall of Exponent Laws

Concept

Zero Exponent: For any non-zero rational number $a$, we define $a^0 = 1$.

Negative Integral Exponent: Let $a$ be any non-zero rational number and $n$ be a positive integer, then $a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$.

Fundamental Laws of Exponents: If $a$ and $b$ are any two rational numbers and let $m$ and $n$ be integers, then we have:

  • (i) $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$
  • (ii) $\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$, where $a \neq 0$
  • (iii) $(a^m)^n = a^{mn} = (a^n)^m$
  • (iv) $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$
  • (v) $\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}$, where $b \neq 0$

2. Rational Numbers as Exponents

We know that $4^2 = 16$ and inversely $\sqrt{16} = 4$. This relationship can also be expressed using a fractional exponent:

$\sqrt{16} = 4$ can be written as $16^{\frac{1}{2}} = 4$ (Sixteen raised to the power half is four).

Similarly, $\sqrt[3]{64} = 4$ can be written as $64^{\frac{1}{3}} = 4$.

Important Terminology

When a quantity has a rational (fractional) exponent, its numerator denotes the exponent (power), while its denominator denotes the radical (root) of the quantity.

Formula: $a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}$

  • Radicand: $a^m$
  • Radical: $\sqrt[n]{a^m}$
  • Index: $n$ (Index of a radical is always a positive integer)
  • Radical Sign: $\sqrt{}$

Examples:

  • $(225)^{\frac{3}{2}}$ means the square root of $(225)^3$.
  • $(216)^{\frac{2}{3}}$ means the cube root of $(216)^2$.

3. Positive and Negative Rational Exponents

Positive Rational Exponents

If $a$ is a positive rational number and $\frac{m}{n}$ is a positive rational exponent, we define $a^{\frac{m}{n}}$ as the $n^{th}$ root of $a^m$.

Formula: $a^{\frac{m}{n}} = (a^m)^{\frac{1}{n}}$

Example: Evaluate $(125)^{\frac{2}{3}}$

$(125)^{\frac{2}{3}} = [(125)^2]^{\frac{1}{3}} = \sqrt[3]{125^2} = \sqrt[3]{125 \times 125} = \sqrt[3]{5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5} = 5 \times 5 = 25$.

Negative Rational Exponents

If $a$ is a non-zero positive rational number and $\frac{m}{n}$ is a positive rational number in lowest form, then a negative exponent means the reciprocal.

Formula: $a^{\frac{-m}{n}} = \frac{1}{a^{\frac{m}{n}}} = \frac{1}{(a^m)^{\frac{1}{n}}}$

Example: Evaluate $(27)^{\frac{-2}{3}}$

$(27)^{\frac{-2}{3}} = \frac{1}{(27^2)^{\frac{1}{3}}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt[3]{27 \times 27}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt[3]{3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3}} = \frac{1}{3 \times 3} = \frac{1}{9}$.

4. Laws of Rational Exponents

The rules for rational exponents are identical to the laws for integer exponents. Let $x$ and $y$ be positive rational numbers and let $a$ and $b$ be rational exponents:

Fact File: The 5 Laws
  • Law I (Product Law): $x^a \times x^b = x^{a+b}$
  • Law II (Quotient Law): $x^a \div x^b = x^{a-b}$ (where $a > b$)
  • Law III (Power Law): $(x^a)^b = x^{ab} = (x^b)^a$
  • Law IV (Power of a Product): $x^a \times y^a = (xy)^a$
  • Law V (Power of a Quotient): $\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)^a = \frac{x^a}{y^a}$

Example (Applying Law I): Find the value of $6^{\frac{1}{2}} \times 6^{\frac{3}{2}}$

$6^{\frac{1}{2}} \times 6^{\frac{3}{2}} = 6^{\frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{2}} = 6^{\frac{4}{2}} = 6^2 = 36$.

Example (Applying Law III): Evaluate $(0.000064)^{\frac{5}{6}}$

$(0.000064)^{\frac{5}{6}} = \left(\frac{64}{1000000}\right)^{\frac{5}{6}} = \frac{(2^6)^{\frac{5}{6}}}{(10^6)^{\frac{5}{6}}} = \frac{2^5}{10^5} = \frac{32}{100000} = 0.00032$.

5. Exponential Equations

An exponential equation is an equation where the unknown variable appears in the exponent. To solve these equations, we generally try to make the bases on both sides equal.

Important

Rule for Solving: If the bases on both sides of an equation are the same (and not equal to 0, 1, or -1), we can equate their powers/exponents.

If $a^x = a^y$, then $x = y$.

Example 1: Solve $7^x = 343$

Since $343 = 7 \times 7 \times 7 = 7^3$, the equation becomes: $7^x = 7^3$.

Since the bases are the same, we equate the powers: $x = 3$.

Example 2: Solve $2^{x-3} = 1$

We know that any non-zero number raised to the power 0 is 1. Thus, $1 = 2^0$.

The equation becomes: $2^{x-3} = 2^0$.

Equating powers: $x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow$ $x = 3$.

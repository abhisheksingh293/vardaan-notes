6. Compound Interest

In Class VII, we learnt about Simple Interest. We know that when money is borrowed on simple interest, the interest is calculated uniformly on the actual principal throughout the loan period. However, banks, post offices, insurance corporations, and other financial institutions use a different, more profitable method of computing interest called Compound Interest.

1. Understanding the Difference: Simple vs. Compound Interest

Concept

Simple Interest (S.I.): Interest is calculated only on the initial principal. The formula is $S.I. = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$.

Compound Interest (C.I.): In this method, the borrower and the lender agree to fix up a certain time interval (like one year, half-year, or a quarter). At the end of the first interval, the interest is calculated and added to the original principal. This new amount becomes the principal for the next time interval. This process repeats, meaning you earn "interest on interest".

Conversion Period: The time period after which interest is calculated and then added to the principal each time to form a new principal.

Example of the Process: If you invest â‚¹100 at 10% per annum for 2 years:

  • Under Simple Interest: First year interest is â‚¹10. Second year interest is â‚¹10. Total interest = â‚¹20.
  • Under Compound Interest: First year interest is â‚¹10. New Principal for the second year becomes â‚¹110. Second year interest is 10% of â‚¹110 = â‚¹11. Total interest = â‚¹10 + â‚¹11 = â‚¹21.

2. Formula for Compound Interest (Compounded Annually)

Calculating compound interest year-by-year can become very tedious for long periods. Thus, mathematicians derived a direct formula.

Core Formula

Amount ($A$) = $P\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$

Compound Interest ($C.I.$) = $A - P = P\left[ \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n - 1 \right]$


Where:

  • P = Principal (initial amount borrowed/invested)
  • R = Rate of interest per annum
  • n = Number of conversion periods (years, if compounded annually)

3. Interest Compounded Half-Yearly and Quarterly

Interest isn't always calculated yearly. Sometimes banks calculate it every 6 months (half-yearly) or every 3 months (quarterly).

Modifying the Formula

If compounded Half-Yearly:

  • The rate of interest becomes half: $\frac{R}{2}$ per half-year.
  • The time period becomes double: $2n$ half-years.
  • Formula: $A = P\left(1 + \frac{R/2}{100}\right)^{2n}$

If compounded Quarterly:

  • The rate of interest becomes one-fourth: $\frac{R}{4}$ per quarter.
  • The time period becomes four times: $4n$ quarters.
  • Formula: $A = P\left(1 + \frac{R/4}{100}\right)^{4n}$

4. Applications of Compound Interest Formula

The compound interest formula is not just used for money. It is a general formula used to calculate the future value of any quantity that grows or decays at a constant percentage rate over time.

A. Population Growth or Bacterial Growth

When a quantity increases over time (like population of a city or bacteria in a culture), we use the standard compound interest formula.

Formula: $P_{new} = P_{initial}\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$

B. Depreciation

When a quantity decreases over time (like the value of a car, machinery, or electronic goods), it is called depreciation. Since the value is reducing, the plus sign in the formula becomes a minus sign.

Formula: $Value_{new} = Value_{initial}\left(1 - \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$

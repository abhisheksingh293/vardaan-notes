Combustion

1. Introduction to Combustion

We use various materials for producing heat energy in our daily lives, such as wood, coal, LPG, and petrol. When a piece of paper or a magnesium ribbon is heated, it catches fire and produces heat and light.

The chemical process in which a substance burns in air (or oxygen) with the release of heat and light energy is called combustion.

Combustible vs. Non-Combustible Materials

  • Combustible Substances: Materials that catch fire easily and undergo combustion to produce heat and light (e.g., paper, wood, kerosene, LPG). These are also called fuels.
  • Non-Combustible Substances: Materials that do not catch fire or burn in the presence of air (e.g., glass, iron nails, stone, sand).
Fact

Food is a Fuel! Just like petrol provides energy to a car through combustion, the food we eat undergoes a slow combustion process in our body cells (respiration) reacting with oxygen to release the energy we need to survive.

2. Conditions Necessary for Combustion

Combustion cannot happen on its own. For any fire to start and sustain, three essential conditions must be met simultaneously, often referred to as the Fire Triangle:

  1. Presence of a Combustible Substance (Fuel): There must be something that can burn.
  2. Presence of a Supporter of Combustion (Oxygen): Air contains about 21% oxygen, which is essential for burning. If you cover a burning candle with a glass jar, the flame goes out once the oxygen is consumed.
  3. Attainment of Ignition Temperature (Heat): A fuel must be heated to a certain minimum temperature before it catches fire. This lowest temperature at which a substance catches fire is called its Ignition Temperature. A matchstick does not catch fire on its own at room temperature; it must be struck against a rough surface to generate enough friction (heat) to reach its ignition temperature.
Concept

Inflammable Substances: Substances that have a very low ignition temperature and can easily catch fire with a small spark are called inflammable substances. Examples include petrol, alcohol, and LPG. Special care must be taken while storing and transporting them.

3. How Do We Control Fire?

To stop a fire, we simply need to break the "Fire Triangle" by removing one or more of the three essential conditions:

  • Removing the Fuel: If possible, move combustible materials away from the fire. However, in building fires, the building itself is the fuel, so this is rarely possible.
  • Cutting off the Air Supply (Oxygen): Throwing sand or soil over a fire cuts off oxygen. Covering a person whose clothes have caught fire with a heavy woolen blanket extinguishes the flames by blocking air.
  • Lowering the Temperature: Pouring water cools the combustible material below its ignition temperature. Water vapor also surrounds the material, helping to cut off the air supply.
Important

When NOT to use water:
1. Electrical Fires: Water is a good conductor of electricity. Pouring water on burning electrical equipment can cause severe or fatal electric shocks.
2. Oil/Petrol Fires: Oil is lighter than water. If you pour water on an oil fire, the water sinks to the bottom and the burning oil floats on top, spreading the fire further.
For these fires, a Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$) Fire Extinguisher is the best choice. $CO_2$ is heavier than oxygen, forming a blanket over the fire and cutting off contact with oxygen.

4. Types of Combustion

Depending upon the speed and nature of the reaction, combustion is classified into different types:

  • Rapid Combustion: When a substance burns rapidly to produce large amounts of heat and light. Example: Turning on a gas stove (LPG) and lighting it with a matchstick.
  • Spontaneous Combustion: When a substance suddenly bursts into flames on its own, without the application of any apparent external heat. Example: White phosphorus catches fire spontaneously at room temperature. Coal dust in mines can also ignite spontaneously, causing dangerous disasters.
  • Explosion: A sudden and very fast combustion reaction that produces a large amount of heat, light, and a loud sound, along with the release of a massive amount of gas. Example: Bursting of firecrackers during festivals.

5. Flame and its Structure

A flame is the visible, glowing part of a fire. Only those substances which vaporise (turn into gas) during burning produce flames. For example, wax and kerosene melt, vaporise, and burn with a flame. Charcoal and coal do not vaporise, so they merely glow red without producing a flame.

Structure of a Candle Flame

A typical candle flame has three distinct zones based on the amount of oxygen available:

  1. Outermost Zone (Non-Luminous Zone): This zone has a complete supply of oxygen, leading to complete combustion. It is blue in color and is the hottest part of the flame. Goldsmiths use a metallic blowpipe to blow the outermost zone of a flame onto gold or silver to melt them.
  2. Middle Zone (Luminous Zone): This zone gets a limited supply of oxygen, leading to incomplete combustion. Unburnt carbon particles glow brightly, giving this zone a yellow color. It is moderately hot.
  3. Innermost Zone (Dark Zone): This area surrounds the wick and consists of unburnt wax vapors because there is no oxygen here. It is black in color and is the least hot (coolest) part of the flame.

6. Fuel Efficiency and Calorific Value

We use different fuels for different purposes. An ideal fuel is one that is cheap, readily available, easy to transport, burns easily at a moderate rate, and produces a large amount of heat without leaving harmful residues (like ash or toxic gases).

The efficiency of a fuel is measured by its Calorific Value. The calorific value is the amount of heat energy produced on completely burning 1 kilogram (1 kg) of a fuel. It is expressed in a unit called kilojoules per kilogram (kJ/kg). Gaseous fuels like Hydrogen, LPG, and CNG have very high calorific values.

7. Environmental Hazards of Burning Fuels

The increasing consumption of fuels has harmful effects on the environment:

  • Incomplete Combustion: Burning fuels like wood and coal in a limited supply of oxygen produces Carbon Monoxide (CO) gas. It is a highly poisonous gas that can kill a person sleeping in a closed room with a coal fire. It also releases unburnt carbon particles that cause respiratory diseases like asthma.
  • Global Warming: The combustion of most fuels releases Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$). Increased concentration of $CO_2$ in the atmosphere traps the earth's heat, leading to global warming, which causes the melting of polar ice caps and coastal flooding.
  • Acid Rain: Burning coal and diesel releases Sulphur Dioxide ($SO_2$) gas. Petrol engines release Nitrogen Oxides. These gases dissolve in rainwater to form acids (sulphuric acid and nitric acid). This acid rain is extremely harmful to crops, buildings (like the Taj Mahal), and aquatic life.

Stars and Solar System

1. The Universe and Galaxies

If we look up into the sky on a clear dark night, we can see a faint band of light running across the heavens. This is the Milky Way, our own Galaxy. It contains about two hundred billion stars and countless other objects. The stars of this galaxy spread out from its centre like a spiral, which is why it is known as a spiral galaxy.

The universe as a whole contains billions of such massive galaxies. Therefore, our Milky Way Galaxy is just a very tiny part of the vast universe.

2. Stars and Constellations

The Stars

Stars are huge spheres of extremely hot gases that produce their own heat and light. They appear to twinkle when viewed from the earth. The Sun is the nearest star to the Earth. The next nearest star to Earth is Alpha Centauri.

The Pole Star (Dhruva Tara): Most stars appear to move from East to West because the Earth rotates from West to East. However, the Pole Star appears stationary in the night sky because it is situated directly above the geographic North Pole of the Earth's axis of rotation.

Concept

Measuring Distances in Space (The Light Year): Stars are millions of kilometers away from us. It is inconvenient to express these vast distances in kilometers. Therefore, astronomers use a much larger unit called a Light Year. A light year is the distance travelled by light in one year (approximately $9.46 \times 10^{12}$ km). The sun is about 8 light minutes away, and Alpha Centauri is about 4.3 light years away.

Constellations

Ancient people looked at the night sky and imagined groups of stars forming recognizable shapes resembling animals, humans, or objects. A group of stars forming a recognizable pattern is called a constellation.

  • Ursa Major (The Great Bear / Saptarishi): One of the most famous constellations, visible in the summer sky. It looks like a big ladle or a question mark. It can be used to easily locate the Pole Star.
  • Orion (The Hunter): Visible in winter. It has three prominent stars forming the "belt" of the hunter, which can be used to locate Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky.
  • Cassiopeia: A prominent constellation in the northern sky visible during winter, resembling a distorted letter 'W' or 'M'.

The Sun and all the celestial bodies that revolve around it due to its gravitational pull form the Solar System. This includes 8 planets, their moons (natural satellites), asteroids, comets, and meteors.

The Sun

The Sun is the central and most massive body of the solar system. It acts as the ultimate source of almost all the heat and light energy for all the planets.

4. The Planets

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A simple 2D vector illustration on a pure white background comparing two planets side-by-side. Left: A bright blue and green Earth with white clouds. Right: A slightly smaller, dusty rust-red Mars with distinct dark patches. Flat educational style.

Planets are large celestial bodies that revolve around the Sun in fixed elliptical paths called orbits. The word 'planet' means 'wanderer'. Unlike stars, planets do not have their own light; they shine by reflecting sunlight.

  • Mercury (Budh): The smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It has no moon.
  • Venus (Shukra): The hottest and brightest planet. It is often called the Morning or Evening star because it is visible just before sunrise or just after sunset.
  • Earth (Prithvi): The only planet in the solar system known to support life. From space, it appears blue-green due to the reflection of light from its vast oceans and landmasses. It has one natural satellite (the Moon).
  • Mars (Mangal): Often called the Red Planet because its surface is covered with reddish iron-rich dust. It has two small moons.
  • Jupiter (Brihaspati): The largest planet in the solar system. It is so massive that about 1300 Earths could fit inside it! It rotates very rapidly on its axis and has many moons.
  • Saturn (Shani): Famous for its beautiful, prominent system of rings made of ice and dust. Saturn is the least dense among all planets (it is less dense than water!).
  • Uranus (Arun) & Neptune (Varun): These are the outermost, incredibly cold giant planets of the solar system. Uranus has a highly tilted rotational axis, making it appear to roll on its side as it orbits the sun.
Fact

A Unique Rotation: While most planets (including Earth) rotate on their axis from West to East, Venus and Uranus rotate from East to West. This means that if you were standing on Venus, you would see the Sun rise in the West and set in the East!

5. Other Celestial Bodies

Asteroids

Asteroids are a large swarm of rocky, irregular-shaped objects that revolve around the sun. They are mostly found occupying the large gap between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter (known as the Asteroid Belt).

Comets

Comets are "dirty snowballs" made of ice, dust, and frozen gases that revolve around the sun in highly elliptical orbits. When a comet approaches the sun, it gets heated and develops a glowing head and a long, bright tail. The tail of a comet always points away from the sun due to solar winds. The most famous is Halley’s Comet, which appears once every 76 years.

Meteors and Meteorites

A meteor is a small piece of rock or debris entering the Earth's atmosphere at very high speeds. Due to extreme friction with the air, it heats up, glows brightly, and evaporates quickly. We commonly call these "shooting stars".

Important

Difference between Meteor and Meteorite: Most meteors completely burn up into ash in the atmosphere. However, if a meteor is exceptionally large, a part of it may survive the fiery journey and actually crash into the Earth's surface. This surviving piece of space rock that hits the ground is called a Meteorite. Scientists study meteorites to understand the materials that formed our solar system.

6. Artificial Satellites

While the Moon is Earth's only natural satellite, there are many man-made objects orbiting the Earth. These are called Artificial Satellites. They are launched from Earth and orbit much closer than the Moon.

Uses of Artificial Satellites: They are used for weather forecasting, transmitting television and radio signals, telecommunications, GPS, and gathering crucial scientific data about Earth and space.

India's Space Achievements: India has built and launched several artificial satellites. Aryabhata was the very first Indian satellite. Other well-known Indian satellites include INSAT, IRS, and Kalpana-1.

Sources of Energy

1. Introduction to Energy and Fuels

Energy is defined as the capacity of a body to do work. We need energy for all our daily activities, from baking chapatis and cookies to sending astronauts into space. Energy exists in various forms such as mechanical, heat, chemical, electrical, light, sound, and nuclear energy.

Substances that produce heat and light energy upon burning in the air are called fuels (e.g., wood, coal, petrol).

Concept

Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed; it can only be converted from one form to another. All forms of energy that we use every day are simply stored in different ways in various sources.

2. Classification of Sources of Energy

Because there are many different sources of energy, they are broadly classified based on their occurrence, physical state, and availability.

A. On the Basis of Occurrence

  • Natural Sources: Energy sources that are made available to us directly by nature. Examples include solar energy (from the sun), wind energy, and hydro energy (from flowing water).
  • Synthetic Sources: Energy sources that use man-made materials. For example, the chemical energy stored inside the batteries used in our calculators and watches is a synthetic source of energy.

B. On the Basis of Physical State

Fuels can exist in three different physical states:

  • Solid Fuels: Firewood, charcoal, agricultural waste, and coal.
  • Liquid Fuels: Kerosene, petrol, and diesel.
  • Gaseous Fuels: Petroleum gas (commonly used as LPG - Liquefied Petroleum Gas), natural gas (commonly used as CNG - Compressed Natural Gas), and biogas.
Fact

Calorific Value: Not all fuels are equally good. The efficiency of a fuel is measured by its calorific value—the amount of heat energy produced on completely burning 1 kg of that fuel. Gaseous fuels like CNG and LPG have very high calorific values and produce less pollution.

3. Fossil Fuels (Non-Renewable Energy)

Fossil fuels are exhaustible, non-renewable natural resources. They were formed over millions of years from the buried remains of dead plants and animals deep under the earth's crust, subjected to extreme heat and pressure.

  • Coal: A hard, black, combustible mineral mainly composed of carbon. It is heavily used in thermal power plants to produce electricity.
  • Petroleum: A dark, thick, oily liquid pumped out from deep underground wells. It is a complex mixture of many hydrocarbons.
  • Natural Gas: Found trapped above the petroleum reservoirs underground. It is an extremely clean-burning fuel.

4. Petroleum Refining and its Components

Crude petroleum extracted from the earth cannot be used directly. It must be refined (separated) into useful components in large industrial plants called oil refineries, using a process called fractional distillation.

Important components obtained from petroleum and their uses:

  • LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas): Used as a smokeless fuel for cooking in homes and industries.
  • Petrol: Used as a fuel for light motor vehicles like cars and motorcycles.
  • Diesel: Used as a fuel for heavy motor vehicles (trucks, buses) and electric generators.
  • Kerosene: Used as aviation fuel in jet aeroplanes and in domestic stoves.
  • Bitumen: Used for making road surfaces and damp-proofing roofs.
  • Paraffin Wax: Used to make candles, ointments, and vaseline.

5. Alternative and Renewable Sources of Energy

Since fossil fuels take millions of years to form and are depleting rapidly, it is crucial to shift towards inexhaustible (renewable) and pollution-free alternative energy sources.

  • Solar Energy: Energy harnessed directly from the sun using solar panels. It is used in solar water heaters, solar cookers, and to generate electricity.
  • Wind Energy: The kinetic energy of high-speed winds is captured by tall wind turbines to turn electric generators.
  • Hydro Energy: The energy of fast-flowing water in rivers or dams is used to rotate turbines in hydroelectric power plants.
  • Biomass/Biogas: Animal dung and plant waste are decomposed by bacteria in the absence of oxygen to produce biogas, an excellent domestic fuel.

6. Environmental Impact and Nuclear Energy

The Problem with Fossil Fuels

Burning fossil fuels releases large amounts of harmful gases, such as carbon dioxide ($CO_2$), sulphur dioxide, and nitrogen oxides. The excessive release of carbon dioxide (a greenhouse gas) traps the earth's heat, leading to Global Warming. The other gases mix with rain to cause harmful acid rain.

Nuclear Energy

Nuclear power plants use radioactive metals like Uranium to produce tremendous amounts of energy from very small quantities of fuel, without emitting carbon dioxide. However, they pose a high risk because they produce dangerous radioactive waste that is difficult to safely dispose of, and any accident or leak can be devastating to human populations and the environment.

Important

Energy Conservation: Since our energy demands are constantly increasing, every individual must take steps to conserve energy. Simple actions like switching off lights and fans when leaving a room, using energy-efficient LED bulbs, and maximizing the use of public transport or carpooling can make a huge difference to the society at large.

3. Whole Numbers

In our daily lives, we use numbers primarily to count objects. This chapter explores the foundational concepts of counting numbers, introducing the concept of zero, and establishing the properties of arithmetic operations that apply to them.

1. Natural Numbers and Whole Numbers

The numbers we use for counting are the most basic types of numbers.

Concept

Natural Numbers: The counting numbers {1, 2, 3, 4, ...} are called natural numbers. The smallest natural number is 1. There is no greatest natural number since counting is endless.

Whole Numbers: The collection of all natural numbers along with the number zero (0) forms the set of whole numbers. {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...}

Fact

Every natural number is a whole number, but 0 is a whole number which is NOT a natural number.

The smallest whole number is 0.

  • Q2: Are all whole numbers also natural numbers? Explain with an example.

2. Successor and Predecessor

The order of numbers is sequential, meaning every number comes exactly before or after another number.

Important

Successor: Given any whole number, the whole number just greater than it (obtained by adding 1) is its successor. (e.g., Successor of 15 is 15 + 1 = 16).

Predecessor: The whole number just less than the given number (obtained by subtracting 1) is its predecessor. (e.g., Predecessor of 20 is 20 - 1 = 19).

Note: Clearly, every whole number has a successor. However, every whole number except 0 has a predecessor in the set of whole numbers.

3. Representation of Whole Numbers on a Number Line

Whole numbers can be visually represented on a straight line known as a number line.

Steps to draw a number line:

  1. Draw a straight line. Mark a point O on it and label the point as 0 (zero).
  2. Select a unit of length. Let it be 1 cm. Mark a point A on the line to the right of O such that OA = 1 unit. Label the point A as 1 (one).
  3. Mark another point B on the right of A such that AB = 1 unit. Label point B as 2.
  4. Continue this process to mark points C, D, E, ... and label them 3, 4, 5, ... respectively.

4. Fundamental Operations on Whole Numbers

Understanding the properties of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division makes mathematical calculations faster and more logical.

A. Closure Property

If a and b are any two whole numbers, then their sum (a + b) and their product (a Ã— b) are always whole numbers. This means whole numbers are "closed" under addition and multiplication.

Note: Subtraction and Division are NOT closed under whole numbers. (e.g., 5 - 7 is not a whole number).

B. Commutative Property

The order in which you add or multiply two whole numbers does not change the result.

  • Addition: a + b = b + a (e.g., 15 + 10 = 10 + 15 = 25)
  • Multiplication: a Ã— b = b Ã— a (e.g., 5 Ã— 4 = 4 Ã— 5 = 20)

Multiplication distributes over addition. This is very helpful for mental math and simplification.

Formula: a Ã— (b + c) = (a Ã— b) + (a Ã— c)

Example: 5 Ã— (10 + 2) = (5 Ã— 10) + (5 Ã— 2) = 50 + 10 = 60.

Important

5. Patterns in Whole Numbers

Observation of patterns can help us in simplifying operations on numbers without doing long calculations.

Pattern Examples for Addition & Subtraction:

  • To add 9, 99, 999: Add 10 - 1, 100 - 1, 1000 - 1.
  • e.g., 117 + 9 = 117 + 10 - 1 = 127 - 1 = 126.
  • To subtract 9, 99, 999: Subtract 10 - 1, 100 - 1, 1000 - 1.
  • e.g., 117 - 9 = 117 - 10 + 1 = 107 + 1 = 108.

Pattern Examples for Multiplication:

  • To multiply by 9, 99, 999: Multiply by (10 - 1), (100 - 1), (1000 - 1).
  • e.g., 84 Ã— 9 = 84 Ã— (10 - 1) = 840 - 84 = 756.
  • To multiply by 5, 25, 125: Multiply by 10/2, 100/4, 1000/8.
  • e.g., 96 Ã— 5 = 96 Ã— (10/2) = 48 Ã— 10 = 480.

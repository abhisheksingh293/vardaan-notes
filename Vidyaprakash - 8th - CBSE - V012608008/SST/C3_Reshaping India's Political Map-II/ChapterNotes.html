Reshaping India's Political Map - II

1. The Foundation of the Mughal Empire

A. Babur (1526-1530)

Babur, a ruler from Central Asia, laid the foundation of the Mughal Empire in India. He was invited by nobles like Daulat Khan Lodi to defeat Ibrahim Lodi. Babur achieved a major victory in the First Battle of Panipat (1526), effectively ending the Delhi Sultanate.

B. Humayun (1530-1540 & 1555-1556)

Humayun succeeded Babur but faced immense difficulties, especially from Afghan nobles. He was defeated by Sher Shah Suri, who established the short-lived Sur Empire (1540-1555). Humayun spent years in exile but successfully recaptured Delhi in 1555, restoring the Mughal dynasty.

2. The Reign of Akbar (1556-1605)

Akbar, who ascended the throne at a very young age, became one of the greatest Mughal emperors. Early in his reign, his guardian Bairam Khan won the crucial Second Battle of Panipat (1556) against Hemu.

A. Expansion & Policies

  • Conquests: Akbar massively expanded the empire by conquering Gujarat (1572), Bengal, Bihar, Kabul, and Kandahar.
  • Rajput Policy: He entered into matrimonial alliances (such as marrying Jodha Bai) and appointed Rajputs to high posts, respecting their internal freedom.
  • Religious Tolerance: He adopted a liberal religious policy, removing discriminatory taxes and treating subjects of different faiths equally.
Important

Fatehpur Sikri: Akbar built this magnificent new capital near Agra. Important architectural monuments inside include the Buland Darwaza (built to commemorate the Gujarat victory), the Panch Mahal, Jodha Bai's Palace, and the Tomb of Sheikh Salim Chishti.

3. Jahangir and Shah Jahan

A. Jahangir (1605-1627)

Formerly known as Prince Salim, Jahangir consolidated his father’s vast empire. He expanded Mughal influence by conquering Kangra but faced strong resistance in the Deccan from Malik Ambar, the capable military commander of Ahmadnagar.

B. Shah Jahan (1628-1658)

Known as Prince Khurram before taking the throne, Shah Jahan conducted extensive military campaigns in the Deccan against Ahmadnagar, Bijapur, and Golconda. His reign is most famous for extraordinary achievements in art and architecture.

Fact

Shah Jahan's architectural legacy is unparalleled. He built the world-famous Taj Mahal in Agra, and the magnificent Red Fort and Jama Masjid in his newly constructed capital of Shahjahanabad (Delhi).

4. Aurangzeb and Mughal Administration (1658-1707)

Aurangzeb was an orthodox and god-fearing ruler. He spent a significant part of his reign engaged in the Deccan Wars, eventually annexing Bijapur (1686) and Golconda (1687). His policies, however, led to prolonged conflicts, notably with the Marathas led by Shivaji Maharaj.

Mughal Administration System

  • Central Administration: Key officials included the Wazir (Prime Minister), Mir Bakhshi (Head of Military), and Qazi-ul-Quzat (Chief Judge).
  • Provincial Administration: The empire was divided into provinces called Subas, governed by Subadars. Subas were further divided into Sarkars and Parganas.
  • Revenue System: Raja Todar Mal, Akbar's finance minister, established a highly organized land revenue system which became the main source of income.

5. Resistance to Mughal Rule

Throughout the 16th and 17th centuries, the Mughals faced severe resistance from various regional powers and communities across India.

Concept

Forms of Resistance

Opposition ranged from widespread peasant revolts protesting high taxation, to organized military resistance by powerful regional kingdoms and tribal groups asserting their independence.

  • The Sikhs: Resistance grew alongside the rise of Sikhism, founded by Guru Nanak. The spiritual movement eventually took on political and military dimensions against Mughal oppression.
  • The Gonds: Rani Durgavati of Garh Katanga fiercely resisted Akbar’s forces in 1565, choosing to die fighting for her kingdom rather than submit.
  • The Ahoms: A tribal community in present-day Assam (Kamarupa). Under the leadership of commanders like Lachit Borphukan, they successfully fought off Mughal expansion, leading to the Treaty of Ghilajhari Ghat.
  • The Rajputs: Despite Akbar's alliances, rulers like Maharana Pratap of Mewar continuously fought the Mughals, notably at the Battle of Haldighati (1576). In Marwar, fierce resistance was sustained under the leadership of Durgadas Rathore.

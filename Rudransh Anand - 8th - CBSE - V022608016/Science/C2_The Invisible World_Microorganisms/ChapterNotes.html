1. Cells: Fundamental Units of Living Organisms

Concept

Cell: The most basic, structural, and functional unit of life that all living things are made of. Just as bricks are the building blocks of houses, cells are the building blocks of all living organisms.

Discovery of the Cell

  • In 1665, a British scientist named Robert Hooke observed a slice of cork under a simple self-designed microscope.
  • He saw a pattern resembling a honeycomb consisting of tiny box-like units and named them "cells" (meaning 'a little room' in Latin).
  • Later, a Dutch microbiologist named Anton van Leeuwenhoek used a more powerful microscope and became the first human to view living cells (like human blood and bacteria).
Fact

Microscope: An optical tool that uses lenses to magnify tiny objects. The invention of the Electron Microscope in the 1930s allowed scientists to magnify things up to half a million times, enabling the observation of complex cell structures.

Size and Shape of a Cell

  • Size: Most cells are microscopic. Their size is measured in microns (µ) or micrometres (µm). (1 µm = 0.001 mm). The average cell size ranges from 3 µm to 30 µm.
  • Shape: Cells vary in shape depending on their function.
    • Some change shape frequently: Amoeba and White Blood Cells (WBCs).
    • Some have a definite shape: Nerve cells (long), Red Blood Cells (RBCs) (circular/spherical), and muscle cells (spindle-shaped).

2. Unicellular vs. Multicellular Organisms

Unicellular Organisms

Organisms that are made up of only one cell. This single cell performs all essential life processes like movement, respiration, digestion, and excretion.

  • Examples: Amoeba, bacteria, paramecium, euglena, and yeast.

Multicellular Organisms

Organisms that are made up of more than one cell. They have a complex body structure with specialized organs to perform specific functions.

  • Examples: Humans, animals, birds, and trees.

Organization in a Multicellular Organism

In complex bodies, life is organized in a hierarchy:

Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism

  • Tissue: A group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function.
  • Organ: Tissues combined together to perform a specific task (e.g., Stomach, Heart).
  • Organ System: Different organs working together (e.g., Digestive system).

3. Cells and Cell Organelles

A typical cell comprises three main parts: an outer covering (cell membrane), a dense structure inside (nucleus), and a jelly-like fluid (cytoplasm). The small, specific parts inside a cell are called cell organelles.

Important

Selectively Permeable Membrane: The cell membrane acts as a guard. It allows only specific nutrients and water to pass through while blocking harmful or large waste molecules.

Key Cell Organelles

  • Cell Membrane: A thin, flexible boundary that controls what enters and leaves the cell.
  • Nucleus: The control centre of the cell. It controls all activities, stores genetic information, and directs protein synthesis.
  • Cytoplasm: A jelly-like substance that fills the cell, holds organelles in place, and is the site for most life processes.
  • Mitochondria: The powerhouses of the cell. They produce energy during cellular respiration.
  • Ribosomes: Non-membrane structures that are the sites of protein synthesis.
  • Lysosomes: Found mostly in animal cells. They act as the clean-up system by breaking down waste and worn-out cell parts.
  • Vacuole: A sac-like structure that stores water, food, and waste.
  • Plastids: Found only in plant cells. Example: Chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis).
  • Cell Wall: A rigid outer layer found only in plant cells (made of cellulose) that provides strength and shape.

Comparing Plant and Animal Cells

  • Plant Cells: Usually larger, have a cell wall, contain plastids (chloroplasts), have fewer but larger vacuoles, and usually lack lysosomes.
  • Animal Cells: Usually smaller, do not have a cell wall, lack plastids, have numerous but small vacuoles, and contain lysosomes.

4. Microorganisms

Microorganisms (Microbes): Tiny organisms that can only be seen under a microscope. The study of microorganisms is called microbiology.

They can survive in all types of environments: hot deserts, polar ice caps, sea floors, active volcanoes, and inside the bodies of living organisms. During adverse conditions, they form a hard covering called a cyst to survive.

Types of Microorganisms

  • Bacteria: Unicellular organisms. Found everywhere. Classified by shape: Bacilli (rod), Cocci (spherical), Spirilla (spiral), and comma-shaped.
  • Algae: Plant-like, mostly green organisms capable of photosynthesis (autotrophic nutrition). They have no roots, stems, or leaves. Usually live in water.
  • Fungi: Plant-like but do not have chlorophyll. They feed on decaying organic matter (saprophytic nutrition) or live as parasites. Examples: Yeast, bread mould, mushrooms.
  • Protozoa: Unicellular organisms with animal-like characteristics. They can move, capture, and digest food. Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium.
  • Viruses: The smallest of all microorganisms. They do not have a cellular structure and can only reproduce inside a living host cell. They act as a link between living and non-living things.

5. Useful Microorganisms

Fact

Good Bacteria: The human body is home to trillions of microbes. Many are found in the intestines and help in digestion. They live symbiotically with us.

In the Food and Beverage Industry

  • Curd and Cheese: The bacterium Lactobacillus multiplies in lukewarm milk and turns it into curd or cheese.
  • Bread Production: Yeast reproduces rapidly and breaks down sugar to produce carbon dioxide gas during fermentation. This gas causes dough to rise and become fluffy.
  • Wine and Vinegar: Yeast is used to ferment fruit juices into alcohol. Bacteria like Acetobacter can convert alcohol into vinegar (acetic acid).

In Medicines and Vaccines

  • Antibiotics: Medicines prepared from bacteria and fungi that kill or stop the growth of disease-causing microbes. Example: Penicillin (extracted from Penicillium mould).
  • Vaccines: Made from dead or weakened microbes. They teach the human body how to fight diseases, developing immunity against ailments like cholera, polio, and tuberculosis.

In Agriculture and the Environment

  • Soil Fertility: Bacteria like Rhizobium (in root nodules) and blue-green algae convert atmospheric nitrogen into nitrates, naturally increasing soil fertility.
  • Cleaning as Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi decompose dead plants and animals into simpler inorganic substances, recycling nutrients back into the soil.
  • Producing Clean Fuel: Microbes act on organic waste and animal dung to produce Biogas (mostly methane) through anaerobic decomposition.
  • Oxygen Production: Phytoplankton (a type of algae in the ocean) produces at least half of the Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis.

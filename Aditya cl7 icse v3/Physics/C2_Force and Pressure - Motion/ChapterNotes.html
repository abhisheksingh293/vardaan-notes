Force and Pressure: Motion

1. Rest and Motion

Definition

Rest: A body is said to be at rest when its position does not change with time with respect to its surroundings.

Motion: A body is said to be in motion when its position changes with time with respect to its surroundings.

Rest and motion are relative terms. An object can be at rest relative to one group of objects while in motion relative to another. For example, a passenger sitting in a moving bus is at rest with respect to fellow passengers, but is in motion with respect to the trees and houses outside.

2. Types of Motion

2.1 Translatory Motion

The motion in which all particles of a body move through the same distance in the same time. It is of two kinds:

  • Rectilinear Motion: When a body moves along a straight line (e.g., a train moving on a straight track, a freely falling stone).
  • Curvilinear Motion: When a body moves along a curved line (e.g., a car passing over a curved bridge, a ball thrown upwards at an angle).

2.2 Rotatory and Circular Motion

  • Rotatory Motion: A motion in which a body moves about a fixed axis without changing its position (e.g., a spinning top, a ceiling fan, a potter's wheel).
  • Circular Motion: A special kind of curvilinear motion where a body moves in a circular path around a fixed point (e.g., the Earth revolving around the sun, a girl whirling a stone on a rope).
Spotlight

In Rotatory Motion, the axis of rotation passes through a point within the body itself (e.g., the Earth rotating on its own axis). In Circular Motion, the axis of revolution passes through a point outside the body (e.g., the Earth revolving around the sun).

2.3 Oscillatory and Vibratory Motion

  • Oscillatory Motion: The "to and fro" or "back and forth" motion of a body as a whole along the same path without any change in its shape (e.g., the pendulum of a clock, a boy on a swing, the needle of a sewing machine).
  • Vibratory Motion: A kind of oscillatory motion where a part of the body always remains fixed while the rest moves rapidly to and fro, leading to a change in shape and size (e.g., a plucked string of a sitar, a beaten drum, breathing chest expansion).

2.4 Periodic, Non-periodic, and Random Motion

  • Periodic Motion: A repetitive motion that repeats itself after a fixed interval of time (e.g., a swinging pendulum, a heartbeat 72 times a minute).
  • Non-periodic Motion: A repetitive motion that repeats itself, but not at fixed intervals of time (e.g., a football player running, motion of a bouncing ball under gravity).
  • Random Motion: When an object suddenly changes its motion from one kind to another without a specific path (e.g., a flying mosquito, a deflating balloon).

2.5 Uniform, Non-uniform, and Simultaneous Motion

  • Uniform Motion: When a body covers equal distances in equal intervals of time along the same straight line (e.g., an aeroplane flying at a constant speed).
  • Non-uniform Motion: When a body covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time along the same straight line (e.g., a cyclist on a busy street).
  • Simultaneous Motion: When an object possesses two or more types of motions at the same time (e.g., a drill cutting through metal possesses both translatory and rotatory motion).

3. Speed and Velocity

3.1 Speed

Speed is the rate of change of motion. It tells us how fast a body is moving without specifying the direction.

  • Formula: Speed = Distance Covered / Time
  • SI Unit: Metre per second (m/s). For large distances, it is measured in km/h.
  • Uniform Speed: Covering equal distances in equal intervals of time.
  • Non-uniform (Variable) Speed: Covering unequal distances in equal intervals of time.
  • Average Speed: Total distance covered divided by the total time taken.

3.2 Velocity

Velocity is the rate of change of motion in a specified direction.

  • Formula: Average Velocity = Total Displacement / Total Time
  • SI Unit: Metre per second (m/s).
  • Uniform Velocity: Covering equal distances in equal intervals of time in a specified direction.
  • Variable Velocity: Covering unequal distances in equal intervals of time in a specified direction, OR covering equal distances but continuously changing direction (like in circular motion).
Key Difference

It is possible for a moving body to have an Average Velocity of Zero (e.g., completing one full round on a circular track where displacement is 0), but its Average Speed will not be zero.

4. Mass and Weight

Mass and weight are often confused in daily life, but they are very different concepts in physics.

Points of Difference Mass Weight
Definition The amount of matter contained in a body. The force with which the earth pulls a body towards its centre (force of gravity).
Variability It is a constant quantity. It does not change with position or location. It is a variable quantity. It depends on the acceleration due to gravity (changes with location, e.g., max at poles, min at equator).
Measuring Instrument Found with the help of a physical balance or beam balance. Found with the help of a spring balance.
SI Unit Kilogram (kg). Newton (N). [Also measured in kgf or gf].
Zero Value The mass of a body can never be zero. The weight of a body can be zero if acceleration due to gravity is zero.

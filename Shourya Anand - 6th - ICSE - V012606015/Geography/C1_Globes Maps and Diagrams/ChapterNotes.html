Chapter 3: Water Bodies of the World

Concept

The Hydrosphere: The zone of water surrounding the surface of the Earth forms the liquid domain called the Hydrosphere. Water occupies about 71% of the Earth's surface, making it the "Watery Planet." Only 29% is covered by land.

1. Global Water Distribution

Water is found in all three forms on Earth: solid (ice), liquid (water), and gaseous (vapour). Most of this water is unusable for direct human consumption.

  • 97.2% of Earth's water is salty/brackish and is found in the oceans and seas.
  • Only about 2% is fresh water, found in rivers, ponds, lakes, underground, and trapped as glaciers/ice caps.

2. The Five Great Oceans

The large continuous bodies of salt water on the surface are inter-connected and are called oceans. The average depth is around 3,800 meters. The five great oceans are:

  • Pacific Ocean: The largest ocean, covering about 1/3 of the Earth. It is circular in shape. It contains the Mariana Trench (11,033 m deep), the deepest point on Earth. It is bordered by a zone of highly active volcanoes known as the Ring of Fire.
  • Atlantic Ocean: The busiest ocean for trade. Its most striking feature is the Mid-Atlantic Ridge (a 14,000 km long submarine mountain range). It has deep basins like the Labrador Basin and Brazil Basin.
  • Indian Ocean: Bounded by Asia, Africa, and Australia. Its floor has fewer irregularities compared to other oceans. The Sunda Trench is its deepest point.
  • Arctic Ocean: The smallest and shallowest ocean. Located around the North Pole, it remains frozen for most of the year.
  • Southern Ocean: Added to the list of oceans in the year 2000. It surrounds the continent of Antarctica, comprising the southernmost waters of the Pacific, Atlantic, and Indian Oceans.
Fact

Except for 44 landlocked countries, all nations have access to open navigable water. Almost 40% of the world's population lives in coastal regions!

3. Seas

A sea is a large body of saline water smaller than an ocean. They are categorized into:

  • Marginal Seas: Large shallow bodies of water along continental edges, partially cut off from the open ocean by an island or peninsula (e.g., Arabian Sea, Bering Sea, Baltic Sea).
  • Inland Seas: Seas completely surrounded by land (e.g., Caspian Sea, Dead Sea, Aral Sea).

4. Rivers and Their Courses

A river is a channel of water flowing from a highland to a lowland, eventually draining into a sea or lake.

The Three Courses of a River:

  • Upper Course (Youth): The river originates at its source. It flows swiftly down steep mountain slopes, deepening its valley and forming features like V-shaped valleys and waterfalls.
  • Middle Course (Maturity): The river enters the plains. The slope is gentler and the speed decreases. The river starts depositing its load, twisting and turning to form loops called meanders and ox-bow lakes.
  • Lower Course (Old Age): The land is almost flat. The river splits into many small channels called distributaries. It deposits fine silt (alluvium) to form a triangular piece of land called a delta before meeting the sea at its mouth.
Important

Cradles of Civilization: Major rivers like the Tigris, Euphrates, Nile, Indus, and Hwang Ho were the birthplaces of ancient human civilizations. They provided drinking water, transportation, and highly fertile flood plains for agriculture.

5. Lakes and Other Water Bodies

Lakes

Lakes are completely enclosed bodies of water. They can be freshwater or saltwater. Notable examples include:

  • Caspian Sea: Despite its name, it is the largest lake in the world by area.
  • Lake Superior: The largest freshwater lake by surface area (part of the Great Lakes in North America).
  • Lake Baikal: The deepest lake in the world and the largest freshwater lake by volume.

Gulf, Bay, Strait, and Lagoon

  • Gulf: An inlet of the sea, surrounded by land on three sides (e.g., Persian Gulf, Gulf of Mexico).
  • Bay: Similar to a gulf but generally with a wider mouth and less enclosed (e.g., Bay of Bengal, Hudson Bay).
  • Strait: A narrow stretch of water that separates two landmasses and joins two large water bodies (e.g., Palk Strait separating India and Sri Lanka).
  • Lagoon: A shallow body of water cut off from the sea by a sandbar or coral reef (e.g., Chilika Lake in India).

6. Water Pollution

When physical, chemical, biological, or radioactive matter enters fresh or ocean waters, it degrades the water quality. This is a severe threat to the ecosystem.

Major Causes of Water Pollution:

  • Industrial Sector: Chemical toxins are discharged into rivers. (e.g., Minamata Disease in Japan caused by mercury poisoning).
  • Agricultural Sector: Chemical fertilizers and pesticides wash off from fields into rivers, damaging aquatic life.
  • Domestic Waste: Sewage and soaps rich in chemicals decrease the water's quality and increase its BOD (Biological Oxygen Demand).
  • Thermal Pollution: Hot water released by power plants raises the water temperature, causing large-scale death of aquatic organisms.
  • Oil Spills: Accidental leaks from ships kill marine life.
  • Plastic Waste: By 2050, it is estimated that plastic will outweigh all fish in the sea. It breaks down into microplastics and suffocates or entangles marine animals.

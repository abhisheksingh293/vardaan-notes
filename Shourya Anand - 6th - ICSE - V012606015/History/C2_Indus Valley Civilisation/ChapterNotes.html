Chapter 2: Indus Valley Civilisation

1. Discovery and Extent

The Indus Valley Civilisation existed from around 2500 to 1500 BCE. It was contemporary to the Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and Chinese civilisations. Until the 1920s, nothing was known about it.

  • Harappa: Discovered by Dayaram Sahani in 1921. It was the first site to be excavated, hence it is also called the Harappan Civilisation.
  • Mohenjodaro: Discovered by Rakhaldas Bannerjee in 1922 in the Larkana district of Sind (Pakistan).
  • Other important sites: Lothal, Ropar, Kalibangan, Dholavira. It is sometimes called the Sindhu Saraswati Civilisation.
Fact

The word Mohenjodaro literally translates to the "mound of the dead" in the Sindhi language.

2. Town Planning

The excavations reveal that cities like Harappa and Mohenjodaro were carefully planned. The streets ran at right angles, creating a grid-like layout (similar to modern-day Chandigarh).

Concept

Each city was divided into two distinct parts:

  • The Citadel: Built on a raised platform. It was protected by thick walls and watchtowers and housed public buildings like granaries, town halls, and likely the administrators.
  • The Lower Town: Below the citadel, this was the residential area where merchants, artisans, and craftsmen lived.

Important Public Buildings

  • Great Granary (Harappa): A large building within the citadel used for storing surplus grain (collected as tax) to be used during emergencies like floods.
  • The Great Bath (Mohenjodaro): Looked like a large swimming pool. It shows advanced engineering skills and was likely used for a ritualistic bath.
  • Town Hall (Mohenjodaro): A massive building with 20 burnt-brick pillars that may have served as a meeting place or cultural venue.
  • Dockyard (Lothal): A brick structure connected to the sea, indicating overseas trade and ship-repair facilities.

Houses and Drainage System

Houses were single or double-storeyed, made of baked bricks. Each had a courtyard, well, and bathroom. The drainage system was remarkable; every house had drains connecting to street drains, all covered with stone slabs, showing a high civic sense regarding sanitation.

3. Social Life, Economy, and Trade

People belonged to different occupational groups like farmers, weavers, potters, toy-makers, and traders. There was likely a central authority or king.

  • Agriculture: Main occupation. They grew wheat, barley, cotton, maize, and millets. Ploughs and sickles were common tools.
  • Animal Husbandry: They reared oxen, buffaloes, goats, pigs, sheep, and camels. However, the horse was unknown to them.
  • Dress and Ornaments: They were probably the first to grow and wear cotton. Both men and women wore ornaments (bangles, necklaces) made of bone, metal, shells, and beads.
  • Pottery: Baked reddish-brown clay with geometric patterns in black paint.
  • Trade: They conducted overland and overseas trade. Harappan seals have been found in Mesopotamia, confirming their trade links. They used standardized weights and measures.
Important

Seals: More than 2000 seals made of terracotta and steatite (a soft stone) have been found. They were mostly rectangular and used by merchants for stamping consignments. They shed light on the religion and economy of the time.

4. Religion and Script

No temples or shrines have been found. We learn about their religion through seals and terracotta figures.

  • Pashupati (Lord Shiva): A famous seal depicts a male figure surrounded by animals (tiger, rhino, buffalo, elephant), believed to be an early form of Shiva.
  • Mother Goddess: Clay figures suggest the worship of a Mother Goddess, symbolizing fertility.
  • Nature Worship: Animals (like bulls) and trees (especially the pipal tree) were considered sacred.
  • The Script: They used a pictographic script (around 400 picture-like signs) written from right to left. It has not yet been deciphered.

5. Decline of the Civilisation

Around 1500 BCE, the civilisation began to decline. The exact reasons are unknown, but historians suggest several possible causes:

  • Natural disasters like floods or earthquakes.
  • A change in the river's course, leading to migration.
  • Epidemics or large fires.
  • Invasions by foreign groups (e.g., the Aryans).
  • Deforestation and ecological damage that made the area unfit for living.

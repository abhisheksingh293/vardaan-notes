Matter

1. Introduction to Matter

Everything around us is composed of materials. These materials are scientifically termed as matter. Our senses of sight, hearing, taste, smell, and touch help us perceive matter. Most of the universe, whether living or non-living, is composed of matter.

Concept

Matter is defined as anything which occupies space and has mass. The smallest particle of matter that exhibits all its properties is called a molecule, which is further made up of basic units called atoms.

Volume and Mass

  • Volume: The space occupied by an object is called its volume. For example, a wooden block with dimensions 4 cm × 3 cm × 5 cm will have a volume of 60 cubic cm.
  • Mass: The quantity of matter in an object is called its mass.
Important

Difference Between Mass and Weight:

  • Mass: It is the quantity of matter contained in a body. It is always constant regardless of location. The SI unit is kilogram (kg) and it is measured using a beam balance.
  • Weight: It is a measure of the Earth's pull of gravity on an object. It changes from place to place (e.g., your weight on Jupiter would be 2.5 times more than on Earth). The SI unit is Newton (N) and it is measured using a spring balance.

2. States of Matter

Matter mainly exists in three physical states: solid, liquid, and gas.

A. Solids

Solids are highly rigid matters. They have a definite shape and a definite volume because their particles are very closely packed.

  • Rigidity: Highly rigid.
  • Compressibility: Incompressible because particles are tightly packed. They only expand or contract slightly when heated or cooled.
  • Shape & Volume: Definite shape and definite volume.
  • Fluidity: They do not flow.

B. Liquids

This state of matter is less rigid than solids. It has a definite volume but no definite shape.

  • Rigidity: Less rigid compared to solids.
  • Compressibility: Can be compressed to a small extent.
  • Shape & Volume: Has a definite volume but takes the shape of the container.
  • Fluidity: Tendency to flow. Particles can slip, slide, or glide due to less intermolecular force of attraction.

C. Gases

Gases are the least rigid state of matter and are highly compressible.

  • Rigidity: Least rigid among all states.
  • Compressibility: Highly compressible due to a lot of empty space between molecules.
  • Shape & Volume: No definite shape and no fixed volume. It occupies the entire space of a closed container.
  • Fluidity: Maximum fluidity because molecules move freely in all directions.
Fact

Recent scientific studies have shown that there are actually five states of matter: Solid, Liquid, Gas, Plasma, and Bose-Einstein Condensate.

3. Arrangement of Molecules and Forces

The differences in the states of matter arise from how their molecules are arranged and the forces acting between them.

  • Intermolecular Space: The empty space existing between the molecules of a substance. It is minimum in solids, more in liquids, and maximum in gases.
  • Intermolecular Force of Attraction: The force by which molecules are held to each other. It is strongest in solids (holding them in fixed positions), weaker in liquids, and almost negligible in gases.
  • Cohesive Force: The attractive force between similar molecules (e.g., between water-water molecules).
  • Adhesive Force: The attractive force between molecules of different materials (e.g., between water molecules and a silica/glass surface).
  • Brownian Movement: The continuous random movement of microscopic particles suspended in a liquid or gas, caused by collisions with molecules of the surrounding medium.
  • Diffusion: The process of mixing up of substances with each other on their own. It occurs readily in gases because molecules move at high speeds with large intermolecular spaces.

4. Interconversion of States of Matter

Matter can change from one state to another when its temperature or pressure is altered.

  • Melting (or Fusion): The process of changing a solid into a liquid by heating. The constant temperature at which this occurs is called the melting point.
  • Vapourisation (or Evaporation): The process of changing a liquid into its gaseous state by heating. The temperature at which boiling starts is called the boiling point.
  • Condensation (or Liquefaction): The process of changing a gas into a liquid on cooling. The temperature at which this starts is the condensation point.
  • Freezing (or Solidification): The process of changing a liquid into a solid on cooling. The temperature at which this starts is the freezing point.
  • Sublimation: The process where a solid changes directly into vapour (gas) on heating, without passing through the liquid state.

5. Properties of Matter

Matter can be identified by its physical and chemical properties.

Physical Properties

Properties observed without changing the basic identity of the matter. Examples include:

  • Shape: Solids can be crystalline (definite geometrical shapes like salt/sugar) or amorphous (no definite shape like talcum powder/flour).
  • Colour: Different substances have distinct colours (e.g., gold is yellow, chlorine gas is greenish-yellow).
  • Solubility: Can be water-soluble (sugar, salt) or water-insoluble (sand, sawdust).
  • Conductivity: Good conductors allow heat and electricity to pass through them (e.g., copper, iron).

Chemical Properties

These properties describe how matter behaves when it changes into new substances through a chemical reaction. The original substances are called reactants, and the new substances formed are called products.

6. Effects of Heat on Matter

Applying heat to a body results in several notable changes:

  • Increase in Temperature: Adding heat increases the kinetic energy of particles, making them move faster, which we measure as a rise in temperature.
  • Change of State: Heat separates particles, changing a solid to a liquid or liquid to gas. The heat absorbed or released during this state change (without changing the temperature) is called latent heat.
  • Chemical Change: Heat can cause permanent and irreversible changes where the original substance loses its identity (e.g., burning of wax produces carbon dioxide, water vapour, and carbon).
  • Expansion: As particles gain energy and vibrate, they force each other further apart.
    • Linear Expansion: Increase in length (1D).
    • Superficial Expansion: Increase in area (2D).
    • Cubical Expansion: Increase in volume (3D). Note: Liquids and gases only exhibit cubical expansion as they have no definite shape.

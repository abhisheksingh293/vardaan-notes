Reproduction in Animals

1. Introduction to Reproduction

Reproduction is the ability of living organisms to produce young ones similar to themselves. It is one of the most fundamental and important characteristics of life. Without reproduction, no species of plants, animals, or other living organisms could continue to exist over generations.

In animals, reproduction broadly takes place in two different ways:

  • Asexual Reproduction
  • Sexual Reproduction

2. Asexual Reproduction

The production of young ones by a single parent, without the formation and fusion of gametes (sex cells), is called asexual reproduction. Because there is only one parent, the new individuals formed are genetically identical to the parent. This is a primitive and simpler method of reproduction.

Methods of Asexual Reproduction:

  • Binary Fission: Usually observed in unicellular organisms like Amoeba and bacteria. The parent cell simply divides into two equal daughter cells, and each grows into an independent individual.
  • Multiple Fission: The repeated division of a single parent cell into numerous daughter cells simultaneously (e.g., Plasmodium).
  • Budding: Observed in organisms like Hydra and yeast. A small bulb-like projection, called a bud, appears on the parent's body. It grows, develops body parts, and finally detaches itself to become a new, independent individual.
Fact

Amoebae Multiplication: If nothing stopped them, Amoebae would multiply by binary fission so fast that they would cover the entire planet! However, nature keeps them in check through predators and competition for food and space.

3. Sexual Reproduction in Animals

Sexual reproduction is a more complex process that involves two parents (a male and a female). It involves the formation of male and female sex cells, called gametes, and their fusion to create a new individual.

Male Reproductive System

The male reproductive organs include a pair of testes, two sperm ducts, and a penis. The testes produce the male gametes, which are called sperms. Millions of sperms are produced by the testes. Although a sperm is extremely small, it is a single cell with three distinct parts: a head, a middle piece, and a long tail (which helps it swim).

Female Reproductive System

The female reproductive organs include a pair of ovaries, oviducts (also known as fallopian tubes), and the uterus. The ovary produces the female gametes, called ova (or eggs). Like the sperm, an ovum is also a single cell. The uterus is the part where the development of the baby takes place.

4. Fertilisation and Development of Embryo

Fertilisation

The first step in sexual reproduction is the fusion of a sperm and an ovum. When a sperm comes in contact with an egg, their nuclei fuse together to form a single, fertilized cell called a zygote. This process is called fertilisation. The zygote represents the very beginning of a new individual.

  • Internal Fertilisation: Fertilisation that takes place inside the female body. Examples: humans, cows, dogs, and hens.
  • External Fertilisation: Fertilisation that takes place outside the female body, usually in water. The female lays hundreds of eggs, and the male releases sperms over them. Examples: frogs and fish.

Development of Embryo

The newly formed zygote begins to divide repeatedly to form a ball of cells. These cells then start forming groups that eventually develop into different tissues and organs of the body. This developing structure is termed an embryo. The embryo travels down and embeds (implants) itself in the wall of the uterus for further development. Once the embryo has developed all recognizable body parts (like hands, legs, head, eyes), it is called a foetus.

Concept

Cloning: Cloning is the production of an exact, identical copy of a cell, a body part, or an entire living organism artificially. Dolly the sheep was the first mammal to be successfully cloned by scientists.

5. Oviparous and Viviparous Animals

Animals give birth to their young ones in two different ways:

  • Viviparous Animals: Animals which give birth directly to young ones are called viviparous animals. Examples include humans, cows, lions, and dogs.
  • Oviparous Animals: Animals which lay eggs are called oviparous animals. The development of the embryo takes place inside the eggshell outside the mother's body. After the embryo is fully developed, the egg hatches (e.g., hens, frogs, birds, lizards).

6. Metamorphosis (From Young to Adult)

In some animals, the young ones that hatch from eggs look completely different from the adult animals. These young ones must go through several drastic bodily changes to become adults.

The transformation of the larva into an adult through drastic physical changes is called metamorphosis.

Examples of Metamorphosis:

  • Frog: Egg → Tadpole (larva with gills and a tail) → Adult Frog (with lungs and legs).
  • Silk Moth: Egg → Larva (caterpillar) → Pupa → Adult Moth.
Important

Human Development: Unlike frogs or insects, human beings do not undergo metamorphosis. The body parts present in adult humans are also present in babies from the time of their birth; they simply grow in size as the person ages.

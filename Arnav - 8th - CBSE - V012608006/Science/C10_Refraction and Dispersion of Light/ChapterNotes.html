Refraction and Dispersion of Light

1. Introduction to Refraction of Light

In previous classes, we learned that light normally propagates (travels) along a straight path when moving through a single, uniform transparent medium. We also learned that it gets reflected when it falls on a polished or shiny surface like a mirror.

However, when a ray of light traveling in one transparent medium (like air) obliquely enters another transparent medium (like glass or water), it changes its direction at the boundary separating the two media. This phenomenon of "bending of light" when it travels from one transparent medium to another is called Refraction of Light.

Concept

Cause of Refraction: Why does light bend? Light travels at different speeds in different transparent materials. For example, it travels faster in air than it does in water or glass. The bending of light is fundamentally caused by this change in the speed of light as it moves from one medium to another.

2. Optically Rarer and Denser Mediums

To understand exactly how light will bend, we classify media based on their optical density (how much they slow down light):

  • Optically Rarer Medium: A medium in which the speed of light is more (e.g., Air).
  • Optically Denser Medium: A medium in which the speed of light is less (e.g., Glass, Water).

Rules of Bending (Refraction)

When light travels obliquely across a boundary:

  • From Rarer to Denser (e.g., Air to Glass): The light ray slows down and bends towards the normal (the imaginary perpendicular line).
  • From Denser to Rarer (e.g., Glass to Air): The light ray speeds up and bends away from the normal.
  • Note: If light enters exactly perpendicular (at 90 degrees along the normal), it goes straight through without bending.

3. Spherical Lenses

A lens is a piece of transparent material (like glass or plastic) bound by two surfaces, of which at least one is spherical. Lenses use the principle of refraction to magnify or shrink images. They are widely used in spectacles, cameras, microscopes, and telescopes.

Types of Lenses

  • Convex Lens (Converging Lens): It is thicker in the middle and thinner at the edges. When parallel rays of light pass through a convex lens, they bend inward and meet (converge) at a single point. It is generally used as a magnifying glass.
  • Concave Lens (Diverging Lens): It is thinner in the middle and thicker at the edges. When parallel rays of light pass through it, they spread out (diverge) as if they are coming from a single point.

4. Key Terms Related to Lenses

To understand how images are formed by lenses, we must know a few important terms:

  • Optical Centre (O): The central point of the lens. A ray of light passing through the optical centre goes straight without suffering any deviation.
  • Principal Axis: An imaginary straight line passing through the optical centre and the centers of curvature of both spherical surfaces of the lens.
  • Principal Focus (F): A point on the principal axis where light rays parallel to the principal axis actually converge (in a convex lens) or appear to diverge from (in a concave lens) after refraction.
  • Focal Length (f): The distance between the optical centre (O) and the principal focus (F) of the lens.
Fact

Real vs. Virtual Images: A Convex lens can form both Real images (which can be caught on a screen, like a projector) and Virtual images (which cannot be caught on a screen, like a magnifying glass). A Concave lens always forms virtual, erect, and diminished (smaller) images.

5. Dispersion of White Light

In the 17th century, Sir Isaac Newton performed a famous experiment. He allowed a narrow beam of sunlight (white light) to fall on a triangular glass prism. He observed that the white light, after passing through the prism, split into a beautiful band of seven colours on a screen placed behind it.

This phenomenon of the splitting of white light into its constituent seven colours is called Dispersion of Light.

The band of seven colours obtained on the screen is called the Spectrum. The order of colours from bottom to top is Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, and Red, easily remembered by the acronym VIBGYOR.

Important

Why does Dispersion happen? White light is actually a mixture of seven different colours. When they travel through the air, they travel at the same speed. However, when they enter a glass prism, different colours travel at slightly different speeds and thus bend at different angles. Red light bends the least (as it is the fastest in glass), while Violet light bends the most (as it is the slowest in glass).

6. Formation of a Rainbow

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A simple, cheerful 2D flat vector illustration on a pure white background. A large, stylized blue raindrop acts as a tiny prism. A yellow beam of sunlight enters it, bends inside, and a beautiful mini-rainbow emerges from the other side. Minimalist lines, bright flat colors.

A rainbow is a spectacular natural phenomenon caused by the dispersion of sunlight. It is usually seen in the sky after a rain shower, always in a direction opposite to the sun.

After it rains, millions of tiny water droplets remain suspended in the atmosphere. These water droplets act like tiny natural glass prisms. When sunlight enters a water droplet, the droplet first refracts and disperses the white light into seven colours. It then reflects the colours internally and finally refracts them again as they come out of the drop. This combined effect across millions of droplets creates the beautiful curved band of seven colours we call a rainbow!

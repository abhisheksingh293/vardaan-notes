Conservation of Plants and Animals

1. Deforestation and Its Causes

India is a large developing country known for its diverse forest ecosystems. Forests are essential as they serve as a home to a large variety of plants and animals. However, this critical ecosystem is under severe threat.

The indiscriminate and large-scale cutting down of trees in the forests, clearing the land for other purposes, is known as deforestation.

Causes of Deforestation

  • Man-made Causes:
    • Procuring land for agricultural cultivation due to a growing population.
    • Building houses, roads, and large factories (urbanisation and industrialisation).
    • Using wood as fuel (firewood) or for making furniture, paper, pulp, and rayon.
  • Natural Causes: Severe droughts and massive forest fires can also naturally destroy large areas of forest land.

2. Consequences of Deforestation

Deforestation profoundly affects both the local environment and the entire globe.

Domestic Consequences

  • Disrupted River Flow & Soil Erosion: Roots of trees bind the soil together. Without trees, the top fertile layer of soil is easily washed or blown away (soil erosion). This decreases rainfall and affects the natural flow of rivers.
  • Flooding: Trees absorb large amounts of rainwater. A lack of trees reduces the water-absorbing capacity of the soil, causing rainwater to rush over the surface, leading to severe floods.
  • Loss of Wildlife Habitat: Destroying forests means destroying the homes of countless animal species, leading to their decline or extinction.
  • Scarcity of Forest Products: A shortage of wood, medicines, fodder, and other essential minor forest products.

Global Consequences

  • Global Warming: Trees absorb Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$) for photosynthesis. Deforestation increases the level of $CO_2$ in the atmosphere. Since $CO_2$ is a greenhouse gas, it traps the heat rays reflected by the earth, leading to a dangerous increase in global temperature.
  • Desertification: As soil erosion removes the fertile topsoil, the hard, rocky lower layers are exposed. Over time, fertile lands convert into lifeless deserts. This process is called desertification.
  • Droughts: Global warming disturbs the water cycle, causing a severe reduction in rainfall, which leads to prolonged droughts.
Concept

Ecological Balance: The complex interaction and harmony between living organisms (plants, animals, microbes) and their physical environment (air, water, soil) is called ecological balance. Deforestation severely disturbs this delicate balance.

3. Conservation of Forests and Wildlife

To protect our flora and fauna and their habitats from destruction, the government has created special protected areas where cutting trees, hunting, poaching, or grazing cattle are strictly prohibited.

  • Wildlife Sanctuaries: Areas where animals are protected from any disturbance to them and their habitat. Limited human activities (like collecting firewood or medicinal plants) are allowed if they don't interfere with wildlife.
  • National Parks: Vast reserves that are large and diverse enough to protect whole sets of ecosystems. They strictly preserve flora, fauna, landscapes, and historic objects. No human activity is allowed here.
  • Biosphere Reserves: Very large areas of protected land meant for the conservation of wildlife, plant and animal resources, and also for preserving the traditional life of the tribal groups living in the area. A biosphere reserve may contain other protected areas within it.

4. Flora, Fauna, and Endemic Species

  • Flora: All the different plants naturally occurring in a particular area.
  • Fauna: All the different animals naturally occurring in a particular area.
Important

Endemic Species: These are species of plants and animals which are found exclusively in a particular, restricted geographical area. They are not found naturally anywhere else. For example, the Giant Squirrel and the flying squirrel are endemic to the Pachmarhi Biosphere Reserve. If their specific habitat is destroyed, they will become extinct.

5. The Red Data Book and Extinction

Depending on their population levels, species are categorised into different risk groups:

  • Extinct Species: Species that no longer exist anywhere on earth (e.g., Dinosaurs, Dodo bird).
  • Endangered Species: Animals whose numbers are diminishing to a critical level, meaning they might face extinction very soon if not protected (e.g., Tigers, Snow Leopards).

The Red Data Book is a globally recognised sourcebook that keeps a comprehensive record of all the endangered animals, plants, and other species, helping authorities track and protect them.

Fact

Project Tiger: Launched by the Indian Government on a massive scale to ensure the survival and maintenance of the tiger population in India, which was rapidly decreasing due to illegal poaching.

6. Migration

Migration is the phenomenon where a species of birds or animals moves from its own natural habitat to some other far-off habitat for a particular time period every year. They usually migrate to escape extreme, freezing weather conditions in their homeland, or to find a safe place to lay their eggs and breed. Once the season changes, they fly back to their original homes.

7. Recycling of Paper and Reforestation

Recycling Paper

Did you know it takes about 17 full-grown trees to make just one tonne of paper? By saving, reusing, and recycling paper, we not only save trees but also save the massive amounts of energy and water needed to manufacture paper. Furthermore, recycling reduces the release of harmful chemicals used in paper making into the environment.

Reforestation

The permanent solution to deforestation is reforestation. This is the restocking of the destroyed forests by planting new trees. Ideally, we should plant at least as many trees as we cut down, and we should plant the same species of trees that were originally found in that forest to maintain the ecosystem.

Crop Production and Its Management

1. Introduction to Agriculture

All living organisms need food for growth and sustenance. Plants can prepare their own food through photosynthesis (autotrophs), while humans and animals depend on plants (heterotrophs).

Around 10,000 years ago, prehistoric humans were nomads, wandering from place to place gathering raw fruits and hunting animals. Eventually, they discovered they could grow 'food-giving plants' by planting seeds near water sources. This monumental discovery changed humans from 'food gatherers' to 'food producers'.

The practice of cultivating land and raising crops and livestock is known as agriculture. When plants of the same kind are cultivated at one place on a large scale, it is called a crop (e.g., a crop of wheat means all plants grown in that field are wheat).

2. Cropping Patterns in India

India is a vast country with rich diversity in climate, temperature, humidity, and rainfall. Despite this diversity, crops in India are broadly classified into two main cropping patterns based on the seasons:

  • Kharif Crops: The crops which are sown in the rainy season. In India, the rainy season is generally from June to September. Since these crops require a lot of water, they cannot be grown in winter. Examples: Paddy (Rice), Maize, Soyabean, Groundnut, and Cotton.
  • Rabi Crops: The crops grown in the winter season, generally from October to March. These crops require a cool climate to grow and less water. Examples: Wheat, Gram, Pea, Mustard, and Linseed.

3. Agricultural Practices

To raise a successful crop, a farmer has to perform a series of systematic activities over a period of time. These activities are known as agricultural practices. They are:

  1. Preparation of Soil
  2. Sowing
  3. Adding Manure and Fertilisers
  4. Irrigation
  5. Protecting from Weeds
  6. Harvesting
  7. Storage

4. Preparation of Soil and Sowing

Preparation of Soil

The first step before growing a crop is to turn the soil and loosen it. The process of loosening and turning the soil is called tilling or ploughing. This is done using tools like a plough, hoe, or modern tractor-driven cultivators.

Benefits of loosening soil: It allows roots to penetrate deep and breathe easily. It brings nutrient-rich soil to the top. It also promotes the growth of earthworms and friendly microbes, which further turn the soil and add humus (acting as "friends of the farmer").

Sowing

Sowing is the process of putting seeds in the soil. Farmers must select good quality, clean, and healthy seeds of a high-yielding variety. Sowing can be done manually or using a tool called a seed drill, which sows the seeds uniformly at equal distance and depth, saving time and protecting seeds from being eaten by birds.

5. Adding Manure and Fertilisers

Continuous cultivation of crops makes the soil poor in nutrients. To replenish the soil, substances are added in the form of nutrients, called manure and fertilisers.

  • Manure: An organic substance obtained from the decomposition of plant or animal wastes. It enhances the water-holding capacity of the soil, makes the soil porous for gas exchange, and improves soil texture.
  • Fertilisers: Man-made chemicals produced in factories that are very rich in specific plant nutrients like Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium (NPK). While they guarantee high yields, excessive use of fertilisers makes the soil less fertile over time and causes severe water pollution.
Concept

Organic Farming: A farming system that strictly avoids or largely excludes the use of synthetically compounded fertilizers, pesticides, and growth regulators. It relies heavily on crop rotation, animal manures, and biological pest control to maintain soil productivity.

6. Irrigation

Living beings need water to survive. In plants, water is essential for the germination of seeds and the absorption of dissolved nutrients from the soil. The supply of water to crops at regular intervals is called irrigation. Sources include wells, tube wells, ponds, lakes, rivers, dams, and canals.

Modern Methods of Irrigation

  • Sprinkler System: Useful for uneven land where sufficient water is not available. Water is sprayed on the crops through rotating nozzles, almost as if it is raining.
  • Drip System: The best technique for watering fruit plants, gardens, and trees in regions with water scarcity. Water falls drop by drop directly near the roots, ensuring absolutely no water is wasted.

7. Protection from Weeds and Pests

In a field, many other undesirable plants may grow naturally along with the crop. These are called weeds. The removal of weeds is called weeding. Weeds compete with the main crop for water, nutrients, space, and light, thus affecting crop growth.

Farmers remove weeds manually or by using chemicals called weedicides (e.g., 2,4-D), which kill the weeds without damaging the crops.

Fact

Biological Control of Pests: Nature has its own pest control system! Animals like frogs, lizards, owls, and bats play a crucial role in agriculture by naturally eating large quantities of crop-destroying insects and pests, keeping their populations in check without the need for toxic chemicals.

8. Harvesting and Storage

The cutting of crop after it is mature is called harvesting. In India, this is either done manually using a sickle or by a machine called a harvester. The process of separating the edible grain seeds from the chaff is called threshing.

Storage

Proper storage of the harvested crop is crucial to protect it from moisture, insects, rats, and microorganisms. If freshly harvested grains (which contain more moisture) are stored without drying, they may get spoilt or attacked by fungi. Large scale storage of grains is done in silos and granaries. At home, dried neem leaves are often used to protect stored food grains from pests.

9. Food from Animals (Animal Husbandry)

Just like plants, animals also provide us with different kinds of food (like milk, eggs, meat, and honey). Animals reared at home or on large farms have to be provided with proper food, shelter, and care. When this is done on a large scale, it is called animal husbandry.

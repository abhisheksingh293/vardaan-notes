Force and Pressure

1. Introduction to Force

An object at rest does not start moving on its own; some 'effort' is needed to make it move. From everyday experience, we know we have to push or pull objects to change their position. In science, a push or pull acting on an object is known as a force.

A force comes into play only when there is an interaction between at least two objects. If there is no interaction, no force acts between them.

Effects of Force

Force cannot be seen, but we can see and feel its effects. A force can produce the following changes:

  • Change the state of motion: A force can make a stationary object move, or make a moving object stop (e.g., kicking a resting football, or a goalkeeper catching a moving ball).
  • Change the speed: If force is applied in the direction of motion, the speed increases. If applied opposite to the motion, the speed decreases.
  • Change the direction: A batsman hitting a cricket ball changes the direction of the moving ball by applying force.
  • Change the shape or size: Squeezing a sponge, stretching a rubber band, or crushing an empty can changes their shape and size.

2. Magnitude and Direction of Force

Force is a physical quantity that has both strength (magnitude) and direction. The strength of a force is expressed by its magnitude. The SI unit of force is the Newton (N).

Concept

Net Force: When multiple forces act on an object, the overall effect is called the net force.
- If two forces act in the same direction, the net force is the sum of the two forces.
- If two forces act in opposite directions, the net force is the difference between the two forces, and the object will move in the direction of the stronger force.

3. Types of Forces

Forces are broadly classified into two categories based on whether the interacting objects are in physical contact or not.

A. Contact Forces

Forces that act only when two objects are in direct physical contact with each other.

  • Muscular Force: The force resulting from the action of muscles in our body. We use muscular force to walk, lift objects, and chew food. Animals also use muscular force to carry loads or pull carts.
  • Frictional Force: The force that acts along the two surfaces in contact and opposes the relative motion of the objects. Friction always acts in the direction opposite to the motion of the object (e.g., a rolling ball eventually stops because of friction).

B. Non-Contact Forces

Forces that can act between objects even from a distance, without any physical contact.

  • Magnetic Force: The force exerted by a magnet on magnetic materials (like iron) or other magnets. A magnet can pull an iron nail from a distance.
  • Electrostatic Force: The force exerted by a charged body on another charged or uncharged body (e.g., a plastic comb rubbed on dry hair attracts tiny bits of paper).
  • Gravitational Force: The attractive force exerted by the Earth (and all other massive bodies in the universe) on objects. This invisible pull makes an apple fall from a tree to the ground.
Fact

Gravity vs. Gravitational Force: Every object in the universe, whether small or large, exerts a pulling force on every other object. This universal force is called gravitational force. 'Gravity' specifically refers to the gravitational pull exerted by the Earth on objects near its surface.

4. Understanding Pressure

It is easier to push a sharp nail into a wooden plank than a blunt nail. Why? Because the effect of a force depends on the area over which it acts. The force acting on a unit area of a surface is called pressure.

Formula: Pressure = Force / Area

The SI unit of pressure is the Pascal (Pa), which is equivalent to 1 Newton per square meter (N/m²).

From the formula, we understand an inverse relationship: if the area is smaller, the pressure produced by the same force is larger; if the area is larger, the pressure is smaller.

Important

Applications of Pressure in Daily Life:
1. School Bags: Have broad shoulder straps to increase the area of contact, reducing the pressure on the child's shoulders.
2. Cutting Tools: Knives and axes have sharp edges to decrease the area, increasing the pressure to easily cut objects.
3. Heavy Vehicles: Trucks and tractors have broad, double tyres to distribute their heavy weight over a larger area, reducing pressure so they don't sink into the ground.

5. Pressure Exerted by Liquids and Gases

Just like solid objects, fluids (liquids and gases) also exert pressure.

Liquid Pressure

  • Liquids exert pressure on the walls and the base of the container they are kept in.
  • Depth Dependence: The pressure exerted by a liquid increases with depth. A hole near the bottom of a water tank will spurt water much farther and with greater force than a hole near the top.
  • Equal Pressure at Same Depth: A liquid exerts equal pressure in all directions at the same depth.

Gas Pressure

Gases (like the air we breathe) also exert pressure on the inner walls of their containers. When you inflate a balloon, it is the pressure of the trapped air pushing outwards in all directions that keeps it round and tight.

6. Atmospheric Pressure

The envelope of air surrounding our Earth is known as the atmosphere. Since air has weight, it exerts a downward push on the surface of the Earth. The pressure exerted by the weight of this air is known as atmospheric pressure.

We do not get crushed under this massive atmospheric pressure because the pressure inside our bodies (blood pressure and other fluids) is exactly equal to the atmospheric pressure outside, canceling it out.

Measuring Atmospheric Pressure

The Italian physicist Evangelista Torricelli invented an instrument called the Barometer to measure atmospheric pressure. At sea level, the standard atmospheric pressure is equal to the pressure exerted by a 76 cm high column of mercury.

Applications of Atmospheric Pressure

  • Drinking Straw: When you suck air out of a straw, the air pressure inside the straw decreases. The higher atmospheric pressure acting on the surface of the liquid pushes the drink up into your mouth.
  • Rubber Suckers: When you press a rubber suction cup against a smooth wall, you push out most of the air between the sucker and the wall. The strong atmospheric pressure from the outside then holds the sucker firmly to the wall.
  • Syringes: Pulling the plunger of a syringe creates a low-pressure area inside the tube, allowing the external atmospheric pressure to push the liquid medicine into the syringe.

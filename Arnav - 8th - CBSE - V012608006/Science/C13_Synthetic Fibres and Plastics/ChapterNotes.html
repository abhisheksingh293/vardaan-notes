Synthetic Fibres and Plastics

1. Introduction to Fibres and Polymers

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A simple, minimalist 2D flat vector graphic on a pure white background. On the left, show a single bead (Monomer). An arrow points to the right, showing a long necklace made of many identical beads joined together (Polymer). Clean educational textbook style.

In previous classes, we learned about fibres like jute, cotton, wool, and silk. Since these are obtained from natural sources like plants and animals, they are called natural fibres. They are used extensively for making clothes, ropes, and carpets.

However, the global demand for fabrics is so massive that natural fibres alone cannot fulfill it. To meet this ever-increasing demand, scientists developed artificial fibres in laboratories and factories, known as synthetic fibres or man-made fibres.

Concept

What is a Polymer? Just like a necklace is made by joining many small beads together, or a toy train is made by joining small wagons, a synthetic fibre is also a chain of small units joined together. Each small unit is a chemical substance called a monomer. Many such small units combine to form a large single unit called a polymer (from Greek: poly meaning many, mer meaning part/unit). The process of combining monomers to form a polymer is called polymerisation.

2. Types of Synthetic Fibres

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A clean 2D vector graphic on a pure white background showing four everyday items made from synthetic fibres, arranged horizontally: A shiny blue bedsheet (Rayon), a strong green climbing rope (Nylon), a crisp yellow shirt (Polyester), and a cozy red winter sweater (Acrylic). Minimalist, flat colors.

A. Rayon (Artificial Silk)

Silk is a beautiful, lustrous natural fibre obtained from silkworms, but it is very expensive. Scientists successfully created a fibre with properties similar to silk, called Rayon. Although it is man-made, it is obtained by the chemical treatment of a natural source: wood pulp (cellulose). It is cheaper than silk, can be dyed in many colours, and is often mixed with cotton to make bedsheets or with wool to make carpets.

B. Nylon

Nylon was the first fully synthetic fibre, prepared in 1931 without using any natural raw material (plant or animal). It was prepared from coal, water, and air. Nylon fibres are incredibly strong, elastic, light, lustrous, and easy to wash. A nylon thread is actually stronger than a steel wire of the same thickness! It is widely used to make socks, ropes, tents, toothbrushes, car seat belts, sleeping bags, and even parachutes and rock-climbing ropes.

C. Polyester (PET / Terylene)

Polyester is another extremely popular synthetic fibre. Fabrics made from this fibre do not get wrinkled easily, remain crisp, and are very easy to wash, making them highly suitable for making dress materials. Terylene is a popular type of polyester. PET (Polyethylene terephthalate) is a very familiar form of polyester used for making water bottles, utensils, films, and wires. Polyester is often blended with natural fibres to enhance quality, such as Polycot (Polyester + Cotton) and Polywool (Polyester + Wool).

D. Acrylic

Many of the sweaters, shawls, and blankets we use in winter are not made from natural wool, which is quite expensive. They are made from a synthetic fibre called Acrylic. It resembles wool but is much cheaper, highly durable, and available in a wide variety of vibrant colours.

Important

Danger in the Kitchen! Synthetic fibres have a major disadvantage: they melt on heating. If a synthetic fabric catches fire, it melts and sticks to the body of the person wearing it, causing severe burns. Therefore, we should never wear synthetic clothes while working in the kitchen or in a science laboratory. Always wear cotton!

3. Plastics and Their Types

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A simple 2D vector illustration on a pure white background comparing polymer arrangements in plastics. Top: 'Linear Arrangement' showing straight, unlinked chains of dots. Bottom: 'Cross-linked Arrangement' showing straight chains connected vertically and diagonally by other dots. Simple flat educational design.

Like synthetic fibres, plastic is also a polymer. However, the arrangement of the tiny monomer units is not the same in all plastics. In some, it is linear (straight chains), while in others, it is cross-linked (chains connected to each other).

Plastics are highly versatile—they can be easily moulded into any shape, recycled, reused, coloured, melted, rolled into sheets, or made into wires.

Types of Plastics

  • Thermoplastics: Plastics that get deformed easily on heating and can be bent easily are known as thermoplastics. Because their polymer chains are linear and loosely held, they soften when heated. Examples: Polythene and PVC. They are used for manufacturing toys, combs, and various types of containers.
  • Thermosetting Plastics: Plastics which, when moulded into a shape once, cannot be softened by heating again. Because they have strongly cross-linked polymer chains, they retain their hard shape.
    • Bakelite: A poor conductor of heat and electricity. Used for making electrical switches and handles of various utensils.
    • Melamine: A versatile material that resists fire and can tolerate heat better than other plastics. Used for making floor tiles, kitchenware, and fabrics for firemen.

4. Plastics as Materials of Choice

Today, if we think of storing food, water, milk, or pickles, plastic containers seem the most convenient. This is because of their special characteristic properties:

  • Plastics are Non-reactive: Unlike iron which rusts when exposed to moisture and air, plastics do not react with water and air. They are not corroded easily, which is why they are used to store various chemicals and foods.
  • Plastics are Light, Strong, and Durable: Since plastic is lighter, cheaper, and stronger than traditional metals, it is widely used in cars, aircraft, spacecraft, and household articles like buckets and mugs.
  • Plastics are Poor Conductors: They do not conduct heat or electricity. This makes them ideal for covering electrical wires, making handles of screw drivers, and handles of frying pans.

5. Plastics and the Environment

A material which gets decomposed easily through natural processes (such as the action of bacteria) is called biodegradable (e.g., peels of vegetables, paper, wood). A material which is not easily decomposed by natural processes is termed non-biodegradable.

Environmental Hazards of Plastics:

  • Plastics take several years (even hundreds of years) to decompose, making them highly non-biodegradable and a major cause of environmental pollution.
  • The burning process of synthetic materials is quite slow and does not burn completely easily. Burning plastics releases highly poisonous fumes and toxic gases into the atmosphere, causing severe air pollution.
  • When garbage is thrown improperly, animals like cows eat the food waste along with polythene bags. The plastic material chokes their respiratory system or forms a lining in their stomach, leading to a painful death.
  • Carelessly thrown plastic bags often find their way into drains and sewer systems, clogging them entirely.
Fact

The 5R Principle: As responsible citizens, we must adopt eco-friendly habits. Avoid using plastic bags; carry a cotton or jute bag for shopping. Separate biodegradable and non-biodegradable waste at home. Always remember the 5R Principle: Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Recover, and Refuse.

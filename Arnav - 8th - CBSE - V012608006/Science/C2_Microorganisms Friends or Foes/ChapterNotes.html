Microorganisms: Friends or Foes

1. Introduction to Microorganisms

Besides the plants and animals we see around us, there are countless tiny living organisms that are invisible to the naked eye. These are called microorganisms or microbes. The branch of science that deals with the study of microorganisms is known as microbiology.

The discovery of microorganisms was only possible after the invention of the microscope. Anton von Leeuwenhoek was the first person to establish the existence of bacteria by observing them through his simple microscope.

2. Major Groups of Microorganisms

Microorganisms are broadly classified into four major groups based on their cell structure:

  • Bacteria: Simplest and oldest single-celled organisms. Examples: Lactobacillus (rod-shaped), Streptococcus (spherical).
  • Fungi: Plant-like organisms that lack chlorophyll and cannot make their own food. They can be unicellular (Yeast) or multicellular (Bread mould, Mushrooms).
  • Algae: Simple, plant-like organisms containing chlorophyll that can photosynthesise. Examples: Spirogyra, Chlamydomonas.
  • Protozoa: Unicellular, animal-like organisms that can move and capture food. Examples: Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena.
Important

Are Viruses Microorganisms?
Viruses are also microscopic but are different from other microorganisms. They behave like non-living particles outside a body, but once they enter the cell of a living host (plant, animal, or bacterium), they hijack it to reproduce. Examples: Flu virus, Polio virus, HIV.

3. Where do Microorganisms Live?

Microorganisms are incredibly resilient and can survive in almost all types of environments, ranging from ice-cold climates to boiling hot springs, and from dry deserts to marshy lands. They are also found in the soil, in the air, in deep oceans, and even inside the bodies of animals and humans.

4. Microorganisms as Our Friends (Useful Microbes)

Many microorganisms play a highly beneficial role in our lives and the environment:

  • Making Curd and Bread: Lactobacillus is a bacterium that multiplies in milk and converts it into curd. Yeast is widely used in the baking industry for making bread, pastries, and cakes. It reproduces rapidly and produces carbon dioxide gas, which makes the dough rise and become fluffy.
  • Commercial Use: Yeast is used for the large-scale production of alcohol, wine, and acetic acid (vinegar).
  • Medicinal Use (Antibiotics): Certain bacteria and fungi are used to produce medicines that kill or stop the growth of disease-causing microbes. These medicines are called antibiotics (e.g., Penicillin, discovered by Alexander Fleming).
  • Vaccines: Dead or weakened microbes are introduced into a healthy body to trigger the immune system to produce antibodies. This protects the body from future infections. Edward Jenner discovered the vaccine for smallpox.
  • Agriculture and Environment: Certain bacteria (like Rhizobium) and blue-green algae fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil to increase fertility. Other microbes act as decomposers, breaking down dead plants and animals and cleaning the environment.
Concept

Fermentation: The process of conversion of sugar into alcohol by the action of yeast is known as fermentation. It was discovered by Louis Pasteur in 1857.

5. Microorganisms as Our Foes (Harmful Microbes)

Some microorganisms cause diseases in humans, plants, and animals. Such disease-causing microbes are called pathogens.

Diseases in Humans

Diseases that can spread from an infected person to a healthy person through air, water, food, or physical contact are called communicable diseases (e.g., Cholera, Common cold, Tuberculosis). Pathogens are often transmitted by insects and animals called carriers or vectors:

  • Housefly: Sits on garbage, picks up pathogens, and transfers them to uncovered food.
  • Female Anopheles Mosquito: Acts as a carrier for the Plasmodium parasite, which causes Malaria.
  • Female Aedes Mosquito: Acts as a carrier for the Dengue virus.

Diseases in Animals and Plants

  • Animals: Anthrax is a dangerous human and cattle disease caused by a bacterium. Foot-and-mouth disease of cattle is caused by a virus.
  • Plants: Microbes cause diseases like Citrus canker (bacteria) in citrus fruits, Rust of wheat (fungi) in wheat, and Yellow vein mosaic (virus) in okra (bhindi). This reduces crop yield.

6. Food Preservation

Microorganisms that grow on our food sometimes produce toxic substances. This makes the food poisonous, causing serious illness known as food poisoning. To prevent this, we must preserve food properly.

Common Methods of Food Preservation:

  • Chemical Method: Chemicals like sodium benzoate and sodium metabisulphite are used as preservatives in jams and squashes.
  • Preservation by Salt and Sugar: High salt (used for fish, meat, tamarind, raw mangoes) and sugar (used for jams, jellies) draw out moisture, inhibiting microbial growth.
  • Preservation by Oil and Vinegar: Bacteria cannot live in such an environment, so oil and vinegar are used to preserve pickles.
  • Hot and Cold Treatments: Boiling kills many microbes. Keeping food in the refrigerator slows down their growth.
  • Storage and Packing: Dry fruits and vegetables are sealed in airtight packets to prevent microbial attack.
Fact

Pasteurisation: Discovered by Louis Pasteur, this method involves heating milk to about 70°C for 15 to 30 seconds and then suddenly chilling and storing it. This prevents the growth of microbes, making the milk safe to consume without boiling.

7. Nitrogen Fixation and the Nitrogen Cycle

Our atmosphere contains 78% nitrogen gas, which is an essential part of proteins, chlorophyll, nucleic acids, and vitamins. However, plants and animals cannot take atmospheric nitrogen directly.

  • Nitrogen Fixation: Certain bacteria (like Rhizobium living in the root nodules of leguminous plants) and blue-green algae convert atmospheric nitrogen into usable nitrogenous compounds in the soil.
  • The Cycle: Plants absorb these compounds from the soil. Animals get nitrogen by eating plants. When plants and animals die, bacteria and fungi in the soil convert the nitrogenous wastes back into simple compounds to be used by plants again. Certain other bacteria convert some part of them back to nitrogen gas, which goes back into the atmosphere. As a result, the percentage of nitrogen in the atmosphere remains more or less constant.

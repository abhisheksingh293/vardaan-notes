Sound

1. Introduction to Sound and Vibrations

We live in a world full of sounds. Sound and music are a regular part of our everyday sensory experience. We hear sounds from humans, birds, vehicles, machines, and music systems. Sound plays an essential role in our lives as it enables us to communicate with one another.

In science, sound is a form of energy which produces the sensation of hearing in our ears.

How is Sound Produced?

Sound is produced by vibrating bodies. A vibration is defined as a rapid back-and-forth or to-and-fro motion of an object. When a tightly stretched rubber band is plucked, it vibrates and produces sound. When it stops vibrating, the sound completely stops.

Concept

Musical Instruments: In stringed instruments (like guitar and sitar), vibrating strings produce sound. In wind instruments (like flute), a vibrating column of air produces sound. In membrane instruments (like tabla and drum), a vibrating stretched membrane/skin produces sound.

2. Propagation of Sound

For sound to travel from the source to our ears, it requires a material medium. This medium can be a solid, a liquid, or a gas (air).

  • Sound in Solids: Sound travels the fastest in solids because the particles are tightly packed. For example, you can hear a train approaching from far away by putting your ear to the solid railway track.
  • Sound in Liquids: Sound travels faster in liquids than in gases. Dolphins and whales communicate over huge distances underwater.
  • Sound in Gases: Sound travels the slowest in gases (like the air around us).
Important

Sound Cannot Travel in a Vacuum: If there is absolutely no air or any other material medium (a vacuum), sound cannot propagate. This is why astronauts on the moon cannot hear each other directly and must use radios (electromagnetic waves) to communicate.

3. Sound Produced by Humans

In human beings, sound is produced by the voice box, also called the larynx. It is located at the upper end of the windpipe in the throat.

Inside the larynx, there are two vocal cords stretched across it in such a way that they leave a narrow slit between them for the passage of air. When the lungs force air through this narrow slit, the vocal cords vibrate, producing sound. Muscles attached to the vocal cords can make them tight or loose, altering the type and pitch of the voice produced.

4. The Human Ear (How We Hear)

The ear is a highly sensitive organ that helps us hear sounds. It converts sound vibrations into electrical signals that the brain can understand.

  • Outer Ear: The visible outer part is called the pinna. It acts like a funnel, collecting sound waves from the surroundings and directing them down the ear canal.
  • Middle Ear (The Eardrum): At the end of the ear canal is a tightly stretched, thin membrane called the eardrum (tympanic membrane). When sound waves hit it, the eardrum vibrates. These vibrations are then amplified by three tiny bones in the middle ear.
  • Inner Ear: The amplified vibrations reach the inner ear, where they are converted into electrical signals. These signals are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve, allowing us to perceive the sound.

5. Characteristics of Sound Vibrations

Every sound vibration (oscillation) has three major characteristics that determine how the sound is heard:

  • Amplitude: The maximum displacement of a vibrating body from its central resting position. Amplitude determines the Loudness of a sound. A larger amplitude produces a louder sound. Loudness is measured in a unit called decibel (dB).
  • Frequency: The number of complete oscillations or vibrations made in one second. Frequency determines the Pitch (shrillness) of a sound. A higher frequency produces a high-pitched, shrill sound (like a bird or a baby's cry). A lower frequency produces a low-pitched, deep sound (like a lion's roar or a drum). The SI unit of frequency is Hertz (Hz).
  • Time Period: The time taken by a vibrating body to complete exactly one oscillation. It is the reciprocal of frequency ($Time Period = 1 / Frequency$).

6. Audible and Inaudible Sounds

The human ear cannot hear sounds of all frequencies. Based on human hearing, sounds are classified into:

  • Audible Sound: Sounds with frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). This is the normal hearing range for humans.
  • Inaudible Sound (Infrasonic): Sounds with frequencies below 20 Hz. Humans cannot hear them, but animals like elephants, whales, and rhinos can produce and hear these very low-frequency sounds.
  • Inaudible Sound (Ultrasonic): Sounds with frequencies above 20,000 Hz. Animals like bats, dogs, and dolphins can hear these high-frequency sounds. Ultrasonic waves are heavily used in medicine (Ultrasound scans) and industry (cleaning hidden parts).

7. Noise and Noise Pollution

Music vs. Noise

Sounds that are pleasant to hear, produced by regular and periodic vibrations, are called musical sounds (e.g., sound from a sitar or a piano). Sounds that are unpleasant, harsh, and produced by irregular vibrations are called noise (e.g., sound of traffic, construction work).

Noise Pollution and Its Control

The presence of excessive or unwanted sounds in the environment is called noise pollution. Major causes include vehicles, explosions (firecrackers), machines, and loudspeakers.

Harmful Effects: Continuous exposure to loud noise can cause severe health-related problems, including lack of sleep, hypertension (high blood pressure), severe anxiety, and even temporary or permanent hearing loss.

Fact

Minimising Noise Pollution: We can reduce noise pollution by installing silencing devices (silencers) in vehicles and industrial machines, restricting the use of loudspeakers, minimizing the use of vehicle horns, and aggressively planting trees along roadsides and around buildings to absorb and cut down sound reaching residences.

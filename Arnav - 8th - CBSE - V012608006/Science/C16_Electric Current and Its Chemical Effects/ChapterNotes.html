Electric Current and Its Chemical Effects

1. Conduction through Liquids

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A simple, minimalist 2D flat vector illustration on a pure white background. It shows a basic electrical continuity tester: a small battery connected to a brightly glowing yellow LED bulb. The two wire ends are dipped into a transparent glass beaker filled with a blue liquid (saltwater). Clean educational style, no complex shading.

In previous classes, we learned that materials which allow electric current to pass through them easily are called conductors (like copper, aluminium, and most metals). Materials which do not allow electric current to pass through them easily are called insulators or poor conductors (like plastic, wood, and rubber).

But what about liquids? Do liquids conduct electricity? Yes, some liquids do conduct electricity. We can check this using a simple circuit containing a battery, wires, and a bulb (a continuity tester).

  • Good Conductors (Electrolytes): Liquids that contain dissolved salts, acids, or bases conduct electricity. Examples include tap water, lemon juice, vinegar, and salt solutions.
  • Poor Conductors (Non-Electrolytes): Liquids that do not have dissolved salts or ions are poor conductors. Examples include completely pure water (distilled water), vegetable oil, and honey.
Concept

Using LEDs in Testers: Sometimes the current flowing through a liquid is too weak to heat the filament of a traditional bulb enough to make it glow. In such cases, we use a Light Emitting Diode (LED) instead, as LEDs require very little current to light up.

2. Chemical Effects of Electric Current

📸 IMAGE PLACEHOLDER Prompt for Image Generator: Landscape orientation. A clean 2D vector diagram on a pure white background. Show a glass beaker filled with liquid. Inside the liquid are two dark grey rectangular blocks (carbon rods/electrodes) connected by wires to a battery symbol above. Tiny, distinct bubbles are rising from the surfaces of the rods into the liquid. Flat, vibrant colors.

When an electric current passes through a conducting liquid (an electrolyte), it does not just pass through silently; it causes a chemical reaction. The resulting effects are called the chemical effects of electric current.

Key Terms to Remember:

  • Electrolyte: The conducting liquid that undergoes chemical decomposition.
  • Electrodes: The solid rods or plates through which current enters and leaves the liquid.
    • Anode: The electrode connected to the positive (+) terminal of the battery.
    • Cathode: The electrode connected to the negative (-) terminal of the battery.

Common Chemical Effects Observed:

Depending on the electrodes and the liquid used, you might observe one or more of the following effects during the passage of current:

  1. Formation of gas bubbles on the electrodes.
  2. Deposits of metal forming on the electrodes.
  3. Changes in the colour of the solution.
Fact

Electrolysis of Water: In 1800, a British chemist named William Nicholson showed that if electrodes are immersed in water and a current is passed, bubbles of oxygen and hydrogen are produced. Oxygen bubbles form on the positive electrode (anode), and hydrogen bubbles form on the negative electrode (cathode).

3. Electroplating

A glass container filled with blue copper sulphate solution. On the left (+), a thick, rough copper plate. On the right (-), a shiny silver-colored spoon getting a smooth orange-brown coating of copper. Wires connect both to a battery. Minimalist and highly readable.

One of the most common and widely used applications of the chemical effects of electric current is electroplating.

Definition: The process of depositing a thin, even layer of any desired metal on another material by means of electricity is called electroplating.

How Does Electroplating Work?

Let's take the example of plating a metal spoon with copper:

  • The Electrolyte: A solution of the metal to be deposited is used (e.g., Copper Sulphate solution).
  • The Anode (+): A pure plate of the metal to be deposited (e.g., pure Copper plate) is connected to the positive terminal.
  • The Cathode (-): The object to be plated (e.g., the spoon) is thoroughly cleaned and connected to the negative terminal.

When the current is switched on, copper from the copper sulphate solution is drawn to the negative electrode (spoon) and gets deposited on it. To make up for this loss in the solution, an equal amount of copper dissolves into the solution from the pure copper plate (anode). Thus, the copper is effectively transferred from the pure plate to the spoon.

4. Applications of Electroplating

Electroplating is a very useful process widely used in industry for coating metal objects with a thin layer of a different metal. This is done for various reasons:

  • Protection and Shine (Chromium Plating): Chromium has a shiny appearance, does not rust, and resists scratches. Since making objects out of solid chromium is too expensive, objects like car bumpers, bicycle handlebars, wheel rims, and bath taps are made from cheaper metals (like iron) and electroplated with a thin layer of chromium.
  • Making Jewelry: Makers of cheap ornaments often electroplate a very thin layer of gold or silver onto less expensive metals (like copper or brass) to make them look luxurious and expensive.
  • Food Storage (Tin Plating): Tin is much less reactive than iron. 'Tin cans' used for storing food are actually made of iron that has been electroplated with tin. This prevents the food from coming into direct contact with the iron and getting spoilt.
  • Preventing Rust (Galvanization): Iron is widely used in making bridges and automobile parts because of its strength. To prevent it from rusting and corroding, a coating of Zinc is deposited on it.
Important

Environmental Concerns: The conducting solutions (electrolytes) used in electroplating factories often contain highly toxic chemicals and heavy metals (like chromium, which can damage organs). The safe disposal of these used chemical solutions is a major environmental concern, and factories must follow strict waste-disposal guidelines to protect land and water bodies.

8. Polynomials

In previous classes, you have learnt about algebraic expressionsâ€”their addition, subtraction, and multiplication. Recall that an algebraic expression is formed using variables and constants, combined with mathematical operations like addition, subtraction, and multiplication.

Consider the following examples:

  • (i) $x^{2}+2x+3$
  • (ii) $x^{2}+3x^{-1}+5$
  • (iii) $x^{2}+6xy+y^{2}+7$
  • (iv) $y^{3}+5\sqrt{y}-6$

In examples (i), (ii), and (iv), the algebraic expressions are in one variable, while example (iii) is in two variables ($x$ and $y$). In this chapter, we shall study special types of algebraic expressions in one variable called polynomials in one variable.

Concept

Polynomial: An algebraic expression in which the exponents of the variables are non-negative integers (whole numbers like 0, 1, 2...) is called a polynomial.

Notice that expression (i) is a polynomial. Expression (ii) is not a polynomial because it contains the term $x^{-1}$, where ($-1$) is not a non-negative integer. Expression (iv) is also not a polynomial because it contains the term $5\sqrt{y}$ (which is $5y^{\frac{1}{2}}$), and $\frac{1}{2}$ is not an integer.

1. Terms and Standard Form of a Polynomial

Classification by Terms
  • Monomial: Contains only one term (e.g., $5, 7x^2, 6xy$).
  • Binomial: Contains two terms (e.g., $5x+6, 7y^2-3y$).
  • Trinomial: Contains three terms (e.g., $x^2+2x+1, 3x-5xy^2+10xy$).
  • Constant Polynomial: Contains only a constant term (e.g., 4, 3, -7).

Note: A polynomial having one variable, say '$x$', is called a polynomial in the variable $x$ and is usually denoted by $p(x), q(x)$, etc.

Standard Form of a Polynomial

When the terms of a polynomial are written in the decreasing order of the powers of the variable, it is called a polynomial in the standard form.

Example: $x^{4}-4x^{3}+5x^{2}-x+1$ is in standard form.

2. Degree of a Polynomial

The highest power of the variable in a polynomial of one variable is called the degree of the polynomial.

Classification by Degree
  • Linear Polynomial: Degree is 1 (e.g., $2x+5$).
  • Quadratic Polynomial: Degree is 2 (e.g., $3y^{2}+5y-7$).
  • Cubic Polynomial: Degree is 3 (e.g., $p^{3}+9p^{2}+1$).
  • Biquadratic (Quartic) Polynomial: Degree is 4.

Note: The degree of a non-zero constant polynomial (like $-7$) is zero because $-7 = -7x^0$.

3. Division of a Polynomial by a Polynomial

The process of division is similar to integer division. It results in two cases: Zero Remainder (exact division) and Non-Zero Remainder.

A. Zero Remainder Case

1. Division of a Monomial by a Monomial

Quotient of two monomials = (Quotient of their coefficients) $\times$ (Quotient of their variables).

Example 1: Divide $18x^5$ by $(-3x^3)$
Solution: $\frac{18x^5}{-3x^3} = (\frac{18}{-3}) \times (\frac{x^5}{x^3}) = -6 \times x^{5-3} = -6x^2$

2. Division of a Polynomial by a Monomial

We divide each term of the polynomial by the given monomial, or take out a common factor.

Example 2: Divide $(6x^4 - 8x^3 + 10x^2 + 12x)$ by $2x$
Solution: $\frac{6x^4}{2x} - \frac{8x^3}{2x} + \frac{10x^2}{2x} + \frac{12x}{2x} = 3x^3 - 4x^2 + 5x + 6$

3. Division of a Polynomial by a Binomial

Method 1: Factor Method - Factorise the dividend so that one factor matches the divisor, then cancel it out.

Example 3: Divide $(x^2 + 5x + 6)$ by $(x + 2)$
Solution: $x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x + 3)(x + 2)$.
So, $\frac{(x + 3)(x + 2)}{(x + 2)} = x + 3$

Method 2: Long Division Method - Keep both polynomials in standard form. Divide the first term of the dividend by the first term of the divisor to get the first term of the quotient. Multiply, subtract, and repeat with the remainder.

Example 4: Divide $(6x^2 - 13x + 6)$ by $(2x - 3)$

  • Step 1: Dividend: $6x^2 - 13x + 6$, Divisor: $2x - 3$
  • Step 2: $6x^2 \div 2x = 3x$ (First quotient term).
  • Step 3: Multiply $(2x - 3) \times 3x = 6x^2 - 9x$. Subtract this from dividend. Remainder is $-4x + 6$.
  • Step 4: $-4x \div 2x = -2$ (Second quotient term).
  • Step 5: Multiply $(2x - 3) \times -2 = -4x + 6$. Subtract. Remainder is $0$.
  • Quotient: $3x - 2$
Important Rule

If a polynomial $p(x)$ is divided by $q(x)$ leaving remainder zero to give quotient $r(x)$, then:
$Dividend = Divisor \times Quotient$
Thus, $q(x)$ is a factor of $p(x)$.

B. Non-Zero Remainder Case

We continue the division process until we get a remainder with a degree strictly less than that of the divisor.

Example 5: Divide $9x^4 + 15x^3 - 2x^2 + 7x - 8$ by $3x - 2$
Solution steps summary:
1. First term: $9x^4 \div 3x = 3x^3$. Subtract $3x^3(3x - 2) = 9x^4 - 6x^3$. New dividend: $21x^3 - 2x^2 + 7x - 8$.
2. Next term: $21x^3 \div 3x = 7x^2$. Subtract $7x^2(3x - 2) = 21x^3 - 14x^2$. New dividend: $12x^2 + 7x - 8$.
3. Next term: $12x^2 \div 3x = 4x$. Subtract $4x(3x - 2) = 12x^2 - 8x$. New dividend: $15x - 8$.
4. Next term: $15x \div 3x = 5$. Subtract $5(3x - 2) = 15x - 10$. Remainder: $2$.
Quotient: $3x^3 + 7x^2 + 4x + 5$
Remainder: $2$

Division Algorithm

$Dividend = Divisor \times Quotient + Remainder$

The degree of the remainder is always less than the degree of the divisor.

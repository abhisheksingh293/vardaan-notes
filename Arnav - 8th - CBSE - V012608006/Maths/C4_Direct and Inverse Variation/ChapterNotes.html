4. Direct and Inverse Variation

In our daily lives, we frequently encounter situations where changes in one quantity bring about a predictable change in another. For instance, buying more chocolates increases the total cost, or increasing the speed of a car decreases the time taken to reach a destination. In mathematics, this relationship is known as variation.

Concept

Variation: If the values of two quantities are related in such a way that a change in one results in a corresponding change in the other, the two quantities are said to be in variation.

1. Types of Variation

Variation is primarily classified into two types: Direct Variation and Inverse Variation.

I. Direct Variation

If two quantities are related in such a way that an increase in one quantity results in a corresponding increase in the other (and vice-versa), then such a variation is called Direct Variation.

Example Situation:
2 chocolates cost â‚¹10. Therefore, 4 chocolates will cost â‚¹20. An increase in chocolates causes an increase in cost.

Mathematical Condition

Two quantities $x$ and $y$ are said to be in direct variation if $x$ and $y$ increase or decrease together in such a way that their ratio remains constant.

$\frac{x}{y} = k$ (where $k$ is a positive constant) or $x = ky$

Verification: If quantities $x$ and $y$ increase together, we must still check if the ratio $\frac{x}{y}$ remains perfectly constant to confirm it is a direct variation.

Word Problem Example: Cost of 6 mineral water bottles is â‚¹48. How many bottles can be bought for â‚¹120?
Let bottles for â‚¹120 be $x$.
$\frac{6}{48} = \frac{x}{120} \Rightarrow x = \frac{6 \times 120}{48} = 15$. So, 15 bottles can be bought.

II. Inverse Variation

If two quantities are related in such a way that an increase in one causes a corresponding decrease in the other (and vice-versa), then such a variation is called an Inverse Variation.

Example Situation:
With a speed of 40 km/hr, a distance is covered in 9 hours. If the speed is increased to 60 km/hr, the same distance is covered in 6 hours. Increase in speed causes a decrease in time.

Mathematical Condition

Two quantities $x$ and $y$ are said to be in inverse variation if an increase in $x$ causes a corresponding decrease in $y$ (and vice-versa) in such a manner that their product remains constant.

$x \times y = k$ (where $k$ is a positive constant) or $x = \frac{k}{y}$

Word Problem Example: If 52 men can do a piece of work in 35 days, in how many days will 28 men complete the same work?
Let 28 men do it in $x$ days. As men decrease, days will increase (Inverse Variation).
$52 \times 35 = 28 \times x \Rightarrow x = \frac{52 \times 35}{28} = 65$. They will finish in 65 days.

2. Time and Work, Time and Distance

Problems involving time, work, speed, and distance heavily rely on direct and inverse variation logic. Here are the core relationships:

  • Amount of work done by a person varies directly with the time taken by him/her to complete it.
  • Number of persons performing a particular work varies inversely with the time taken by them.
  • Speed of a moving body and distance travelled by it in a particular time vary directly with each other.
  • Speed of a moving body and time taken to cover a particular distance vary inversely with each other.
  • Distance travelled and time taken by a moving body (speed remaining constant) vary directly with each other.
Core Formulas

Speed (S) = $\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}$

Time (T) = $\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$

Distance (D) = $\text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$

Problems on the Motion of Trains

When solving train problems, the length of objects crossed plays a vital role:

Case 1: Crossing a stationary object of negligible dimension (like a pole, signal post, or a standing man).

  • The distance travelled by the train to cross it is equal to the Length of the train.

Case 2: Crossing a stationary object with some length (like a platform, bridge, or tunnel).

  • The distance travelled by the train to cross it is equal to the Length of the train + Length of the stationary object.
Important Conversion

Often, train lengths are in metres and speed is in km/hr. Always convert km/hr to m/sec before solving.

$1 \text{ km/hr} = \frac{1000 \text{ m}}{3600 \text{ s}} = \frac{5}{18} \text{ m/sec}$

Example: A train 400 m long is running at a speed of $72 \text{ km/hr}$. How much time does it take to cross a telegraph post?
Speed = $72 \times \frac{1000}{3600} = 20 \text{ m/sec}$.
Distance = Length of train = $400 \text{ m}$.
Time = $\frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Speed}} = \frac{400}{20} = 20 \text{ seconds}$.

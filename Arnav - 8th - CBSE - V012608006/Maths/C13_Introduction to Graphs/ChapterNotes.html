Introduction to Graphs

1. Introduction

We often see graphs in newspapers, television, and magazines. The primary idea of representation by a graph is to show numerical facts in a visual form so that they can be understood clearly and quickly.

While data can be presented in the form of a table, graphical presentation is much easier to interpret and analyze at a glance.

Fact

Why Use Graphs? A graph is a visual representation of collected data. It helps in spotting trends, comparing different quantities, and making predictions without analyzing raw numbers.

2. Linear Graph

A linear graph displays data that changes continuously over periods of time. To draw such a line, we need to locate a few points on the graph sheet and join them.

Concept

What is a Linear Graph? Unlike a bar graph or pie chart which shows discrete pieces of data, a linear graph is a single unbroken line that connects various data points to show a continuous relationship between two variables.

3. Cartesian Plane

To plot points to form a graph, we use a system called the Cartesian Plane. If you take a square grid or graph paper and mark a point $O$ on it, you can draw a horizontal line and a vertical line through $O$.

  • The point $O$ is known as the origin. Its coordinates are always $(0, 0)$.
  • The horizontal line $OX$ is known as the x-axis.
  • The vertical line $OY$ is known as the y-axis.
  • Together, the lines $OX$ and $OY$ are known as the coordinate axes.
  • The plane containing both the $x$-axis and $y$-axis is known as the Cartesian Plane.

Plotting a Point on a Cartesian Plane

Let $A$ be any point on the plane. To locate the position of point $A$, we measure its distance from the two axes:

  • The distance of a point from the y-axis is called its x-coordinate or abscissa.
  • The distance of a point from the x-axis is called its y-coordinate or ordinate.

These two distances combined give us an ordered pair $(x, y)$, where the x-coordinate always comes first and the y-coordinate comes second.

Important

Order Matters! The position of the x-coordinate and y-coordinate cannot be interchanged. If $x \neq y$, then the position of $(x, y)$ on the Cartesian plane is completely different from the position of $(y, x)$. For example, $(4, 5)$ is not the same point as $(5, 4)$.

Points on the Axes

  • If a point lies exactly on the x-axis, its y-coordinate is zero. The coordinates are written as $(x, 0)$.
  • If a point lies exactly on the y-axis, its x-coordinate is zero. The coordinates are written as $(0, y)$.

4. Construction of Graphs (Independent & Dependent Variables)

When constructing graphs, we typically analyze the relationship between two variables. One variable's value changes independently, while the other variable's value depends on it.

  • Independent Variable: The variable whose values do not depend on the other variable (e.g., Time, Number of days). Plotted on the x-axis.
  • Dependent Variable: The variable whose values depend on the independent variable (e.g., Amount of electric bill based on usage, Distance covered based on time). Plotted on the y-axis.

Example 1: Rainfall vs. Days

The following table gives information about rainfall on seven consecutive days in the month of July. Number of days is independent, Rainfall is dependent.

Day (July) 1 2 3 4 5 6 7
Rainfall (cm) 12 14 10 18 10 16 15

Example 2: Distance vs. Time

Arjun can drive a car continuously at a speed of $40\text{ km/hr}$. Let's observe the relation between time and distance.

Time (in hrs) 1 2 3 4
Distance Covered (km) 40 80 120 160

By reading the graph, we can find out how long it takes to cover intermediate distances (e.g., in $2.5$ hours, the distance covered is $100\text{ km}$).

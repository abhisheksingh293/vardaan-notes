The Modern Period

1. Transition to the Modern Period

In previous classes, we studied the Stone Age, ancient civilisations, and the rise of various empires in North and South India, including the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire. Now, we shift our focus to the Modern Period of Indian history.

The Modern Period in India began in the eighteenth century, after 1707, when the mighty Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb breathed his last. The crumbling of the old Mughal system gave place to new ideas, and many new powers emerged on the political horizon of India.

Concept

The Dawn of the Modern Period: The ever-changing political scenario heralded the dawn of the Modern Period, especially with the coming of the Europeans to India. During this era, India faced massive political, social, economic, and cultural exploitation.

2. Arrival and Expansion of the British

Initially, the East India Company was established strictly as a trading company. However, they soon realized the fragmented state of the Indian political landscape.

  • The Company took advantage of the political instability among regional rulers.
  • Gradually, through a mix of diplomacy and warfare, they took over the control of a large part of India.
Fact

In 1858, following the great uprising, the ruling power was officially transferred from the hands of the East India Company directly to the British Crown.

3. Consolidation of British Power in the 19th Century

The nineteenth century witnessed various systematic measures adopted by the British to extend and consolidate their control over India. These included:

  • Strict military action to suppress local rulers.
  • The introduction of railways and postal services to improve transportation and communication for British administrative and economic benefits.
  • Passing different Acts to legally bind and control the Indian population.
Important

The Revolt of 1857: This was a major, widespread event where Indians rose up against British exploitation. Unfortunately, the colonial power suppressed it ruthlessly. Though the British rule continued, the deep discontentment against them planted the seeds of nationalism and the eventual freedom struggle.

4. Sources of Information for Modern Indian History

How do historians know so much about the Modern Period? We rely on several primary and secondary sources that survived from the 18th and 19th centuries.

A. Official Records

The British believed that the act of writing was important. Every instruction, plan, policy decision, agreement, and investigation was clearly written up. They kept meticulous official records of their administration, including:

  • Detailed surveys and mapping of the Indian subcontinent.
  • Revenue records, census data, and administrative reports.

B. Letters, Writings, and Speeches

While official records tell us what the British officials thought and did, they do not always tell us how the common Indian people felt. For the Indian perspective, historians study:

  • Personal diaries of people and accounts of pilgrims and travelers.
  • Autobiographies of important personalities. A prominent example is Mahatma Gandhi's autobiography, "My Experiments with Truth".
  • Speeches by national leaders like Bal Gangadhar Tilak, who famously declared, "Freedom is our birthright."

C. Newspapers and Print Media

With the spread of the printing press, newspapers became a powerful medium to express public opinion and spread nationalist ideas. They were printed in English as well as vernacular (regional) languages. A famous example is the Amrita Bazar Patrika, a prominent Bengali newspaper.

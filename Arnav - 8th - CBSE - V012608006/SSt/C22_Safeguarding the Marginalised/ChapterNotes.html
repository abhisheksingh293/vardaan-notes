Safeguarding the Marginalised

1. The Concept of Marginalisation

Successive governments have aimed to eradicate poverty and social injustice, but unfortunately, even after over 70 years of independence, a significant majority of people in India remain socially, economically, educationally, and politically backward. These groups form the marginalised sections of society.

Concept Marginalisation:

It means the social process of discriminating and isolating an individual or a group to a lower or outer edge and treating them as 'inferiors'. The major marginalised groups in India are the Scheduled Castes (SCs), Scheduled Tribes (STs), Other Backward Classes (OBCs), and certain Minority groups.

To uplift these groups, it becomes the duty of the State to provide additional benefits—such as reservations and special safeguards—so they can stand on an equal footing with the rest of the country.

2. Steps Taken for the Upliftment of the Marginalised

Both the Central and State governments have framed extensive policies to improve the conditions of the marginalised classes. These include constitutional provisions as well as specific welfare programs.

A. Constitutional Provisions and Reservations

  • Fundamental Rights: Rights against exploitation, the abolition of untouchability, and the right to equality are powerful tools embedded in the Constitution.
  • Political Reservations: Seats are reserved for SCs and STs in the Lok Sabha, Vidhan Sabhas, and local bodies like Panchayati Raj institutions, ensuring their voices are heard in policy-making.
  • Job and Education Reservations: A certain percentage of seats are reserved in government educational institutions (like IITs and medical colleges) and government jobs for SCs, STs, and OBCs.
Fact Who are OBCs?

Even though SCs, STs, and OBCs are often clubbed together as 'Backward Classes', their nature of backwardness varies. OBCs are castes that are socially and educationally backward but do not fall under the SC/ST categories.

B. Economic and Welfare Schemes

The government implements numerous schemes, often through the Five Year Plans, focused on direct economic upliftment:

  • Providing free coaching to SC/ST students for competitive exams like medical, engineering, and civil services.
  • Constructing hostels for students belonging to these categories.
  • Distributing land to the landless and offering subsidized loans and agricultural tools.
  • Implementing schemes specifically targeting the eradication of extreme poverty and creating employment opportunities in rural areas.

3. Protective Laws and the Scourge of Manual Scavenging

While reservations and economic help are vital, direct protection from inhuman practices and violence is equally crucial.

The Manual Scavengers and Their Rehabilitation Act, 2013

One of the most degrading jobs forced upon the lowest strata of the caste system was manual scavenging (the removal of human waste/excreta using brooms, tin plates, and baskets). This inhuman practice forced people to work in highly unhygienic conditions, leading to serious health issues.

  • In 1993, the government passed an Act prohibiting the employment of manual scavengers.
  • However, the practice persisted. Therefore, a stricter law, The Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers and their Rehabilitation Act, 2013, was passed.
  • This act strictly bans manual scavenging and emphasizes the rehabilitation of the workers and their families by providing alternative livelihoods.
Important Swachh Bharat Mission:

The total eradication of manual scavenging is closely linked to sanitation. The construction of proper toilets across rural and urban India under the Swachh Bharat Mission aims to eliminate the very need for manual scavenging.

4. Protecting Marginalised Communities from Exploitation

Laws like the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 were enacted specifically to protect Dalits and Adivasis from upper-caste violence, land grabbing, and humiliation.

  • Adivasi Land Rights: The law provides strict penalties for anyone who wrongfully occupies or cultivates land owned by a member of an SC or ST. It supports the rights of Adivasis to their traditional forest lands.
  • Dignity of Women: The Act has specific provisions to severely punish anyone who assaults or uses force against an SC/ST woman with the intent to dishonor her.

5. The Role of Civil Society and the Judiciary

The fight for safeguarding the marginalised is not just the government's responsibility. It requires active participation from all pillars of democracy.

  • Activism: Organizations, NGOs, and activists belonging to these communities (like the Safai Karamchari Andolan) have filed Public Interest Litigations (PILs) to force the government to implement existing laws strictly.
  • Judiciary: The Supreme Court has repeatedly directed the central and state governments to identify manual scavengers, liberate them, and ensure their proper rehabilitation with financial assistance.

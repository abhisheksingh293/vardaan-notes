Social Justice and the Marginalised

1. Introduction to Social Justice

A democratic form of government presupposes a democratic form of society. Our Constitution aimed to create an environment where everyone can live with dignity and self-esteem. The Preamble clearly promises Justice, Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity to all its citizens. However, even after over 70 years of independence, social inequalities persist, especially for certain historically disadvantaged sections of society.

Concept Marginalised Groups:

Marginalisation refers to a situation in which individuals or groups are ignored, sidelined, and treated as insignificant. In India, marginalized sections generally include Scheduled Castes (SCs), Scheduled Tribes (STs), Other Backward Classes (OBCs), and Minorities, who have suffered social, economic, and educational deprivation for centuries.

2. Constitutional Safeguards for the Marginalised

The makers of the Indian Constitution recognized the need to protect the interests of these vulnerable groups. They introduced several provisions, policies, and laws aimed at uplifting them and ensuring social justice.

Protective Provisions (Fundamental Rights)

  • Abolition of Untouchability (Article 17): The practice of untouchability is strictly forbidden and made a punishable offence.
  • Right to Equality (Article 15): State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Also, all public places like shops, wells, and roads are open to all.
  • Equality of Opportunity (Article 16): Ensures equal opportunities in matters of public employment.
  • Protection against Exploitation (Article 23 & 24): Prohibits human trafficking, forced labor (begar), and child labor in hazardous occupations.

3. Reservation Policy

To overcome the historical disadvantages faced by the marginalized and to bring them into the mainstream, the government implemented the policy of reservation.

  • What is Reservation? It means keeping a certain percentage of seats reserved for specific deprived classes in educational institutions, government jobs, and legislatures.
  • Who benefits?
    • Scheduled Castes (SCs): 15% reservation
    • Scheduled Tribes (STs): 7.5% reservation
    • Other Backward Classes (OBCs): 27% reservation (implemented following the Mandal Commission report).
  • Political Reservation: Seats are also reserved for SCs and STs in the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies in proportion to their population.
Fact Mandal Commission:

The Mandal Commission (officially the Second Backward Classes Commission) was established in 1979 to identify the socially and educationally backward classes of India. Its recommendation to reserve 27% of government jobs for OBCs was implemented in 1990.

4. Laws for Protection of SCs and STs

Despite constitutional guarantees, discrimination and violence against Dalits (SCs) and Adivasis (STs) continued. To strictly curb this, the government enacted special laws.

The Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989

This powerful law was framed to prevent crimes against SCs and STs. It lists various offences, such as:

  • Forcing a member of an SC/ST to eat or drink any inedible or obnoxious substance.
  • Forcibly removing their clothes or parading them naked.
  • Wrongfully occupying or cultivating land owned by them.
  • Assaulting or using force against any woman belonging to an SC/ST with intent to dishonor her.

This Act ensures stringent punishment for those who commit such atrocities.

5. Safeguarding the Minorities

India is a land of diversity where people follow different religions and speak different languages. The Constitution ensures that the majority community does not impose its will on the minorities.

  • Religious Minorities: Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Parsis, and Jains are recognized as religious minorities at the national level.
  • Linguistic Minorities: Groups whose mother tongue is different from the majority language of the state they live in.
  • Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29 & 30): These articles specifically protect the rights of minorities to conserve their distinct language, script, or culture. They also have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice.
Important National Commission for Minorities:

Established by the Government of India, this commission evaluates the progress of the development of minorities, monitors the working of the safeguards provided in the Constitution, and looks into specific complaints regarding the deprivation of minority rights.

6. Conclusion: The Road Ahead

While the Constitution provides a robust framework for social justice, laws alone cannot change society. True equality requires a change in mindset, eradicating deep-rooted prejudices, and ensuring effective implementation of government schemes. Empowering the marginalized through quality education and economic opportunities is the key to building a truly democratic and inclusive India.

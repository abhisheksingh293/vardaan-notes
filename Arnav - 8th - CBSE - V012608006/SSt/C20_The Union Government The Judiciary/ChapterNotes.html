The Union Government: The Judiciary

1. Introduction to the Judiciary

You have previously studied about the Legislature (which makes laws) and the Executive (which enforces them). The Judiciary is the third major organ of the government. In a federal system, it is essential to have a judiciary that not only resolves disputes but also acts as the final interpreter of the Constitution.

Whenever a dispute arises regarding property, violation of rights, abuse of power, violence, or exploitation, the matter can be taken to a court of law to seek justice. A system of courts under an independent judiciary is the ultimate foundation of our democracy.

2. Independence of the Judiciary

Justice is possible only if the judiciary is independent, impartial, and unbiased. Our Constitution has various provisions to ensure the judiciary is kept completely free from the influence and pressure of the legislature and the executive branches.

  • Separation of Powers: Once appointed to office, judges are not subordinate to any political leaders or ministers.
  • Security of Tenure: Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts cannot be easily removed. They can only be removed through a very difficult process called Impeachment.
  • This independence allows the courts to play a crucial role in protecting the Fundamental Rights of the citizens.
Concept Judicial Review:

The judiciary has the power to strike down particular laws passed by the Parliament if it believes that these laws violate the basic structure of the Constitution. This ultimate power is called Judicial Review.

3. Structure of Courts in India

India possesses a single integrated judicial system. This means that the decisions made by the higher courts are binding on the lower courts.

  • The Supreme Court: The highest court of the land, located in New Delhi. It is presided over by the Chief Justice of India.
  • The High Courts: The highest judicial authority at the State level. While each state usually has its own High Court, two or more states can share one.
  • Subordinate Courts: These operate at the district or Tehsil level. People interact with these courts the most. They are presided over by District Judges.
Fact High Courts in India:

There are several shared High Courts in India. For example, the Punjab and Haryana High Court is located in Chandigarh and serves both states. Recently, three North-Eastern States—Manipur, Tripura, and Meghalaya—have had new High Courts set up.

4. Branches of the Legal System

The legal system in India is broadly categorized into two branches based on the nature of the crime/dispute:

A. Civil Law

  • Deals with any harm or injury to the rights of individuals.
  • Examples: Disputes related to the sale of land, purchase of goods, rent matters, marriage, and divorce.
  • A petition has to be filed before the relevant court by the affected party only.
  • The court gives specific relief requested (e.g., ordering a tenant to vacate a house and pay pending rent).

B. Criminal Law

  • Deals with conduct or acts that the law defines as offences.
  • Examples: Theft, harassing a woman to bring more dowry, murder.
  • It usually begins with the lodging of a First Information Report (FIR) with the police, who investigate the crime, after which a case is filed in court.
  • If found guilty, the accused can be sent to jail and/or fined.

5. Access to Justice & Public Interest Litigation (PIL)

In principle, all citizens of India can access the courts in this country. However, in reality, access to courts has always been difficult for the vast majority of the poor in India due to high legal fees, a lot of paperwork, and the immense time it takes.

Important Public Interest Litigation (PIL):

In response to the difficulties faced by the poor, the Supreme Court in the early 1980s devised the mechanism of PIL to increase access to justice. It allowed any individual or organization to file a PIL in the High Court or Supreme Court on behalf of those whose rights were being violated.

Example: The mid-day meals provided in government schools today were the direct result of a Public Interest Litigation!

6. Lok Adalats (People's Courts)

The judicial process in India is often plagued by the phrase "Justice delayed is justice denied." To combat the heavy backlog of cases and to provide quick, inexpensive justice, the system of Lok Adalats was established.

  • They act as alternative dispute resolution forums.
  • Disputes are settled amicably through compromise and mutual understanding.
  • They save time, money, and free the parties from lengthy legal procedures.
  • The very first Lok Adalat was held in Delhi.

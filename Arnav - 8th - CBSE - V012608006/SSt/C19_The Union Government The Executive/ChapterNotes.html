The Union Government: The Executive

1. Introduction to the Executive

Every government operates through three essential organs: the legislature, the executive, and the judiciary. The Executive is that organ of the government which enforces the laws made by the legislature, executes them, and effectively runs the administration of the country.

Because the Constitution of India provides for a parliamentary form of government, we have two distinct types of executives at the Union level: the nominal executive and the real executive.

Concept Nominal vs. Real Executive:

The President of India is called the Head of the State and acts as the nominal executive. All executive powers are vested in the President's name. However, the Prime Minister is called the Head of the Government and acts as the real executive. The President is bound to act on the advice of the Union Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.

2. The President of India

As India is a Republic, the highest office in the country is held by an elected President rather than a hereditary monarch.

Qualifications for Election

To be eligible for election as the President, a candidate must:

  • Be a citizen of India.
  • Be at least 35 years of age.
  • Be qualified for election as a member of the Lok Sabha.
  • Not hold any office of profit under the Government of India or any State Government.

Election and Tenure

The President is not directly elected by the citizens. Instead, they are elected indirectly by an Electoral College consisting of:

  1. The elected members of both Houses of Parliament (Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha).
  2. The elected members of the State Legislative Assemblies.

The President serves for a term of five years and is eligible for re-election.

Important Impeachment:

If the President violates the Constitution, they can be removed from office before the expiry of their term through a strict procedure called Impeachment. This process must be passed by a two-thirds majority in both Houses of the Parliament.

3. Powers of the President

Even though the President is a nominal head, the office holds significant constitutional powers categorised into various domains:

  • Executive Powers: The President appoints the Prime Minister, the Council of Ministers (on the PM's advice), State Governors, the Chief Justice, and judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts. The President is also the Supreme Commander of the Armed Forces.
  • Legislative Powers: The President can summon or prorogue the sessions of Parliament and can dissolve the Lok Sabha on the advice of the PM. No bill can become a law without the President's signature (assent).
  • Financial Powers: The annual Union Budget is presented in Parliament in the name of the President. Furthermore, a Money Bill can only be introduced in the Lok Sabha with the prior recommendation of the President.
  • Judicial Powers: The President has the power to grant pardon, reprieve, or reduce the sentence of any convicted person, including a death sentence.
  • Emergency Powers: The President can declare a National Emergency, State Emergency (President's Rule), or Financial Emergency to deal with extraordinary situations threatening the nation.
Fact Historic Firsts:
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad: The first President of independent India (belonged to Bihar).
  • Smt. Pratibha Patil: The first Woman President of India (belonged to Maharashtra).

4. The Vice President of India

The Vice President acts as the deputy to the President. They are elected by the members of both Houses of Parliament for a term of five years.

  • Role: The Vice President is the ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha.
  • Acting President: In case the office of the President falls vacant due to death, resignation, or illness, the Vice President takes over as the acting President until a newly elected President assumes charge.

5. The Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers

The Prime Minister is the real executive head and the most powerful political figure in the country. The President appoints the leader of the majority party (or coalition) in the Lok Sabha as the Prime Minister.

Categories of the Council of Ministers

The Prime Minister selects their team of ministers, which is officially appointed by the President. They are divided into three ranks:

  1. Cabinet Ministers: They hold key portfolios like Defence, Home Affairs, and Finance. They form the inner circle and make all major policy decisions.
  2. Ministers of State: They either assist Cabinet Ministers or hold independent charge of smaller ministries.
  3. Deputy Ministers: They are junior ministers appointed to assist Cabinet Ministers and Ministers of State.
Concept Collective Responsibility:

The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha. This means they function as a team. If a vote of no-confidence is passed against even a single minister in the Lok Sabha, the entire Council of Ministers must resign. They "swim and sink together."

6. The Civil Services (The Permanent Executive)

While ministers are the political (temporary) executive who change after every election, the day-to-day administration is run by trained career bureaucrats, known as the Civil Services.

  • They are selected through highly competitive exams conducted by the Union Public Service Commission (UPSC).
  • Examples include the Indian Administrative Service (IAS), Indian Police Service (IPS), and Indian Foreign Service (IFS).
  • They act as the permanent executive. Their main job is to advise the ministers, formulate policies, and effectively implement the laws passed by the Parliament at the ground level.

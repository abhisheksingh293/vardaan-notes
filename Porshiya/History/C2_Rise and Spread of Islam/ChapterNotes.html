Rise and Spread of Islam

1. Pre-Islamic Times: Conditions in Saudi Arabia

Before the advent of Islam, the socio-political conditions in Arabia were highly disjointed and tribal in nature:

  • Tribal Division: The people were divided into a number of tribes living mainly on breeding livestock.
  • Warfare: Various tribes were constantly involved in wars over the possession of land, particularly pastures (grazing lands).
  • Political Setup: Arabs were not politically organized. They only acknowledged the authority of their tribal Chiefs, and there was no central government to enforce law and order.
  • Religion: The religious beliefs were a mingling of polytheism, Judaism, and Christianity. People worshipped many idols.
  • Trade: During the first six hundred years of the Christian era, trade became vital. This brought Arabs into contact with new ideas from other lands.

2. Prophet Mohammad & His Early Life

Islam was propagated by Prophet Mohammad, who was born in Mecca in about CE 570.

  • Followers of Islam are called Muslims, meaning "people who give themselves to God". The word Islam means submission (to the will of Allah).
  • Until mid-life, Mohammad lived as a prosperous trader.
  • In CE 610, he had a spiritual experience and became a prophet. He declared himself the messenger of the supreme being, Allah.
  • He preached heavily against idol worship, which angered the ruling tribes of Mecca who worshipped many gods.
Important

Hijra & The Islamic Calendar

Due to heavy opposition to his teachings in Mecca, Prophet Mohammad was forced to leave and moved to Medina in CE 622 with his followers.

This emigration is known as Hijra (or Hijarat). It is highly significant because it marks the beginning of the Muslim Calendar (Hijri Calendar). It is a lunar calendar consisting of 354 days.

3. Teachings of Mohammad

Mohammad's teachings were incredibly simple. They succeeded in bringing the divided Arabs under a common political rule and united them under one faith.

  • Monotheism: There is only one God, Allah, and Mohammad is his Prophet.
  • Equality: All Muslims must regard themselves as brothers and equals.
  • Virtue: Followers should lead a life of virtue and benevolence.
  • Rules: Muslims must not lend money on interest and should not worship idols.

The Five Pillars of Islam

A Muslim has five primary religious obligations to fulfill in their lifetime:

  1. Shahada (Confession of Faith): Repeating the essential phrase, "There is no God but Allah, Mohammad is His Prophet."
  2. Salat (Prayer): Offering prayers (Namaaz) five times every day and on Friday afternoons in a mosque.
  3. Zakaat (Alms Giving): A religious duty to give alms and offer help to the poor.
  4. Sawm or Siyam (Fasting): Keeping a fast from dawn to dusk during Ramadan (the ninth month of the Islamic lunar year).
  5. Haj (Pilgrimage): Traveling to the holy city of Mecca at least once in a lifetime.
Concept

The Quran

The Quran (or Koran) is the holy book of the Muslims. Muslims believe that Allah dictated the Quran to Prophet Mohammad through the angel Gabriel. It contains the divine guidance and teachings for all Muslims.

4. Beginning of the Caliphate

Prophet Mohammad passed away in CE 632. By the time of his death, he was the leader of almost the entire Arabian Peninsula.

  • His successors were known as Caliphs or Khalifas. They acted as both the religious and political leaders of the Muslim community.
  • The first four Caliphs who succeeded him were Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali.
  • Within just a century, the Khalifas and their generals expanded the empire rapidly, conquering Iran, Syria, Egypt, Central Asia, North Africa, and Spain.

5. The Two Great Dynasties

The Umayyad Dynasty (661 CE – 750 CE)

After the rule of the first four Khalifas ended, power shifted to the Umayyads.

  • Capital: Damascus.
  • Expansion: They ruled over Spain and France in Europe, the Mediterranean coast of Africa, and places up to Sindh and Samarkand in Central Asia.
  • Culture & Knowledge: The Arabs were fast learners. They assimilated the best elements of Indian, Chinese, Greek, and Persian cultures.
  • Achievements: They enriched global knowledge in algebra, geometry, and astronomy. The art of calligraphy flourished, and they introduced new architectural features like the dome and minaret.

The Abbasid Dynasty (Post-750 CE)

The Umayyad rule was overthrown and replaced by the Abbasids.

  • Capital: Baghdad.
  • Key Ruler: The most famous Caliph was Harun al-Rashid.
  • Empire: For almost 150 years, the Abbasid Empire was one of the most powerful and flourishing empires globally, controlling vital trade routes connecting the Mediterranean with India.
  • Economy: They established the gold dinar and the silver dirham, which became standard trade currencies worldwide.
Fact

House of Wisdom (Bait-ul-Hikmat)

The Abbasid Caliph, al-Mamun, established the famous House of Wisdom (Bait-ul-Hikmat) at Baghdad. This was a massive intellectual center created to translate incredible learning from various civilizations—such as Iranian, Greek, Byzantine, Egyptian, and Indian—into the Arabic language.

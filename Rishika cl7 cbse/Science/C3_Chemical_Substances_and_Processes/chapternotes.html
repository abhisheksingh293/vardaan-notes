Chemical Substances and Processes

1. Basics of Matter

Matter exists in three states: solid, liquid, and gas. All matter is made up of incredibly small particles called atoms. When atoms combine, they form molecules.

Materials that are made up of the same kind of particles (having the same atoms or molecules) are called pure substances. Pure substances can be classified into two categories:

Concept
  • Element: A pure substance made up of atoms of the same kind. (e.g., Iron is an element made entirely of iron atoms).
  • Compound: A pure substance made up of molecules of the same kind. The molecule of a compound consists of atoms of two or more different elements combined in a fixed ratio. (e.g., Water is made of water molecules containing 2 Hydrogen atoms + 1 Oxygen atom).

Mixtures

In addition to elements and compounds, we have mixtures. A mixture contains two or more substances mixed in any proportion. The components are not chemically combined. Therefore, a mixture is not a pure substance.

2. Chemical Symbols and Formulae

Chemists represent chemical substances using symbols. Earlier chemists (Alchemists) used pictorial symbols, but today we use the letters of the English alphabet.

  • Hydrogen: H
  • Sodium: Na
  • Iron: Fe
  • Copper: Cu
  • Oxygen: O
  • Carbon: C

Chemical Formula

The chemical formula represents the chemical composition of a substance. It indicates the type and number of atoms of each kind present in that substance.

Fact To write a chemical formula, we criss-cross the charges (valencies) of the ions involved. For example, to write the formula for Sodium Oxide, we take the symbols Na⁺¹ and O⁻². Criss-crossing the numbers without the signs gives us the formula: Na₂O.

3. Chemical Changes and Equations

Substances can undergo different types of changes. A physical change alters state, shape, or size without forming a new substance. A chemical change, however, causes a substance to change into another substance entirely.

Substances which undergo a chemical change in a reaction are called the reactants. The new substances which are formed are called the products.

Chemical Equation

A chemical reaction can be represented by using appropriate symbols and formulae of the substances involved. This is called a chemical equation. We write the reactants on the left-hand side, the products on the right-hand side, and an arrow between them pointing towards the products.

The physical state is often written in brackets: (s) for solid, (l) for liquid, (g) for gas, and (aq) for an aqueous solution (dissolved in water).

Important Balancing of Chemical Equations:
A chemical equation should always be balanced. A balanced chemical equation has the same number of atoms of each element on both sides of the equation. To balance an equation, we do not change the chemical formulas; instead, we place a suitable coefficient (number) before the formula.

4. Types of Chemical Reactions

Depending upon the type of chemical change occurring, chemical reactions are classified into different types:

A. Combination Reaction

A chemical reaction in which two or more substances (reactants) combine to form a single new substance (product).
Example: Combination of hydrogen and oxygen gas yields water.
2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(g)

B. Decomposition Reaction

A chemical reaction in which more than one product is obtained from a single reactant.
Example: Breaking up of water into hydrogen and oxygen gases by passing an electric current.
2H₂O(l) → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)

C. Displacement Reaction

A chemical reaction in which one element displaces another element in a compound.
Example: An iron nail put in a blue copper sulphate solution changes the color to light green because iron displaces copper.
Fe(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu(s)

D. Neutralisation Reaction

A chemical reaction between an acid and a base, leading to the formation of a salt and water.
Example: Reaction between hydrochloric acid and sodium hydroxide (base) yields sodium chloride (salt) and water.
NaOH(aq) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)
